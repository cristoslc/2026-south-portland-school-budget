# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

After months of asking for real numbers, the administration finally delivered a comprehensive nine-scenario analysis matrix with specific FTE reductions, cost savings, and five-year projections. Option 1.7 (closing Skillin) saves $19.6M over five years versus $16.4M for option 1.3 -- the financial case is clear. The capital cost differential is enormous because Skillin needs HVAC replacement worth $2.96M over five years while other schools only need $316K-$430K. This is the kind of granular analysis that informs real decisions.

But here is what bothers me: they did all this detailed modeling for reconfiguration options and left the no-change scenario (option 1.2 -- cuts across all schools without reconfiguration) as "more analysis needed" with no financial modeling. Dr. Prince said it would save "at least $2 million" but nobody built the spreadsheet. That is not objective analysis -- that is steering the conversation toward a predetermined outcome. When the superintendent then gets up and says anything other than reconfiguration is "kicking the problem down the road," it stops feeling like neutral staff recommendations.

The good news is the board pushed back hard. Four members -- Dowling, Feller, Richardson, and DeAngelis -- publicly stated they will not support closing Skillin, creating a clear majority against the administration's top financial recommendation. That is appropriate oversight: elected officials making independent judgments informed by community input rather than rubber-stamping staff preferences.

Transportation costs are a significant gap in the analysis. The administration included only $60K-$125K for "transportation consultancy" but has no actual cost estimates for increased bus routes, longer rides, or driver hours despite proposing to completely reshuffle a thousand elementary students. The Pre-K expansion mandate -- 80-90 additional four-year-olds by September 2026, plus 80-90 three-year-olds the following year -- adds another unfunded variable. Enrollment projections showing future cohorts falling to or below 200 students reinforce the long-term case for reconfiguration but make the near-term implementation timeline even more critical to get right.
