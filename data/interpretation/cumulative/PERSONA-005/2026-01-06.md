# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

Reading January 6 from March 2026, I can see exactly how the machinery was being assembled. The city manager previews the February 10 joint budget guidance workshop — the same workshop where Councilor Walker would name Skillin's demographics as de facto segregation, where three budget scenarios would be presented without a single line of disaggregated impact data, where DeAngelo would call the council's refusal to set tax guidance "not sufficient, not adequate, not appropriate, not acceptable." All of that was still six weeks away. On January 6, it was just a calendar item, a procedural preview. And that is precisely the problem: the framing was already locked. "What's an acceptable tax increase." "Whether to add new positions or not." The questions being asked pre-determined the shape of the conversation to come. Nobody was asking which students would be most harmed by cuts. Nobody was asking the district to account for the ways that 78 positions map onto the children at Skillin or Kaler.

The January 22 citizen forum announcement compounds this. Asking the public to generate "cost saving measures" is not community engagement — it is laundering predetermined fiscal choices through the appearance of participation. I have been tracking the engagement failure pattern since the superintendent search in December, when nobody showed up to a public meeting; I have watched it deepen as immigrant families fear ICE enforcement, as forums fill with teachers and administrators while working families stay home. Now the district is asking those same communities — structurally excluded from the process — to come tell them how to cut. It is a cruelty dressed as democracy.

Mark Emmi's festival proposal is worth noting precisely because it is the only moment in the meeting where someone is thinking about public space and public programming as belonging to everyone. His explicit mention of partnering with the Human Rights Commission — the same body whose budget was just cut 75% in December — signals that he understands the connection between cultural access and equity. That connection is invisible in the budget discussions.

The state property tax task force is the detail I keep returning to. South Portland is "part of the conversation" on what drives property taxes and who's most affected. But that conversation, I already know, will not close the structural gap the district faces. By March, the cuts will be real.
