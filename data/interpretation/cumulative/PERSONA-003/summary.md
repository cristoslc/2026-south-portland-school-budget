# Cumulative Summary: Jess (PERSONA-003)

### Current Understanding

Jess has followed the South Portland school budget crisis across 18 meetings since December 1, 2025. The district faces a $7.2 million structural gap driven by a 23% collapse in elementary enrollment over four years and chronic state underfunding — the state covers roughly 20% of costs against a mandated 55%. The proposed response — eliminating 78 positions including 42 teachers (12% of staff) and reconfiguring five elementary schools into a grade-band model — is moving toward implementation, but the board has not committed to a specific reconfiguration option from the nine scenarios presented at March 2. Three of those scenarios involve school closures; board members have applied equity-based resistance to Skillin closure in particular. Even after all three "big ideas," a gap of over $3 million remains, and the finance director has confirmed that all budget scenarios now lead to deficit. The fund balance is depleted. Six finance directors in five years and recurring audit failures have compounded the structural crisis with institutional mismanagement.

Pre-K expansion is the most durable positive thread Jess has identified: state mandates require serving 80–90 four-year-olds by September 2026 with three-year-olds added the following year, physical space needs protect buildings from closure, and Head Start partnerships add external funding. This is her anchor for staying in South Portland.

The city and school district operate in confirmed, total silos — no civic venue at any level, including annual goal-setting, coordinates capital planning with school capacity. A potential triple tax stack (school budget increase, Mahoney bond, sewer project) has never been presented as a combined household number. State-mandated upzoning could add enrollment pressure no one is calculating. ICE enforcement destabilized community cohesion and introduced a category of fragility the school budget cannot address.

Jess has moved from acute alarm to weary watchfulness. She is staying to evaluate, uncertain about what elementary education will look like when her daughter ages out of pre-K.
