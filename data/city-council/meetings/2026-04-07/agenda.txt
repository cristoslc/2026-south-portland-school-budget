### Executive Session Pursuant to 1 M.R.S.A. § 405(6)(F) to Consider a Hardship/Poverty Abatement Application. Three-Fifths (3/5s) Vote of Councilors Present and Voting Required to Enter Executive Session.
Maine law allows any homeowner to apply for a full or partial waiver of property taxes owned due to hardship. What qualifies as a hardship is up to the elected officials.

 

Historically, South Portland has relied on the analysis of the City's Social Services Director, who meets with applicants and reviews their income and expenses and other resources that have been pursued.

 

On Tuesday night Council will consider a hardship waiver request from a resident. Social Services Director Chris Pupke will provide Council with background materials at that time. During the open session portion of the meeting, Council will need to vote to approve the request, approve a modified version of the request, or deny the request.

### Final Call for 26-28 Election Clerks Applications .
Serve Your Community as an Election Clerk (2026-2028)

We are incredibly proud of the strong tradition of civic service upheld by our Election Clerks. For many years, these dedicated individuals have been the bedrock of our local democratic process, ensuring every election is conducted with integrity, accuracy, and efficiency.

Our Election Clerks are not merely temporary employees; they are committed stewards of democracy. They embody the utmost professionalism, ensuring strict adherence to procedures and maintaining the trust of the community in the results. The passion for democracy and their meticulous attention to detail concerning all facets of election law. This level of commitment is what guarantees the fair and secure election.

A letter has been sent to local political party committees for nominations to serve as Election Clerks. As your City Clerk, I want to share, we are actively recruiting committed, impartial, and detail-oriented residents to serve a two-year term. You will be on the front lines of democracy!!

Key Responsibilities:

         Voter Assistance: Greeting and directing voters, verifying registrations, and issuing the correct ballots.

         Process Integrity: Maintaining accurate records, providing ballot security, and upholding all election laws and procedures.

         Polling Place Management: Assisting with the setup and closing of the polling place, ensuring a smooth and organized voting environment.

         Impartial Service: Performing all duties with strict neutrality, offering courteous and effective service to all members of the public.

Why Serve?

Serving as an Election Clerk is one of the most direct and rewarding ways to contribute to your community. The integrity and efficiency of our local democratic process depend on dedicated Election Clerks.

         Uphold Civic Duty: Play a vital and non-partisan role in securing the foundation of our democracy and instilling public trust in election outcomes.

         Community Connection: Work alongside fellow citizens, meet your neighbors, and foster a spirit of civic engagement.

         Training & Compensation: Receive paid, comprehensive training on election procedures and equipment, and earn compensation for your service on Election Day.

         Gain Valuable Skills: Develop skills in time management, detailed record-keeping, and public interaction under pressure—all highly valued in any professional setting.

State Law ( Title 21-A Sec. 503-A ) requires that the municipal officers consider all nominations and appoint election clerks for a two year term by May 1st of each even-numbered year. All nominations are received by April 1, 2026 shall be considered for appointment of qualified voters.

### ORDER #175-25/26 - Approving an Agreement between the City of South Portland and Southern Maine Community College for Maintenance of the Shoreway Trail. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

Since the late 1970s the City of South Portland has collaborated with Southern Maine Community College (then SMVTI) on what is known as the Spring Point Shoreway. The location of today’s trail was once overgrown with trees, shrubs, and vines, and gaining access to the ocean was not something easily achieved. A committee consisting of College and City employees who shared the same goals was established to oversee clearing of the campus shoreline, and ultimately establishing the trail that provides recreational opportunities and magnificent views of Casco Bay.

 

In those early years, the City and SMVTI entered into a lease agreement to allow for City investment and ensure public access, including designated public parking spaces. The original ten-year agreement was executed on May 5, 1981, with an option for an additional ten-year term. The agreement expired in 2001, and the City and the College were unable to agree on terms for the lease renewal, partly due to concerns raised at the college system level.

 

Grant funds have been used to maintain the trail over the decades, including the Land and Water Conservation Fund (LWCF). The City and College both have an ongoing obligation to preserve public access to the trail area, but since the lease expired, the City has not formally had the right and ability to maintain the trail.

 

During the past few years, the City and College have had ongoing dialogue about the trail and agreed to formalize the arrangement through a new trail maintenance agreement. This agreement is not a lease per se, but formalizes City-College collaboration related to the Spring Point Shoreway Trail, allows the City's Park Rangers to patrol the trail and enforce ordinances, addresses waste collection and maintenance, and preserves public access to the trail areas. Parking will not be reserved, but will be available to the public on an as-available basis.

 

Of note, while the agreement calls for the City to continue to maintain the trail surface, the City is not responsible for subsurface repairs or maintenance, such as erosion control.

 

Assistant City Manager Josh Reny will be available to answer questions regarding this item should the Council decide to remove it from the Consent Calendar.

### ORDER #157-25/26 - Amending Order #135-24/25 to Update the Charge of the Mahoney City Center Committee. Postponed from March 19, 2026. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

NOTE: This item was postponed from the March 3, 2026 and March 19, 2026 Council meetings.

 

On February 18, 2025, the City Council approved an Order that created the Mahoney City Center Committee (M3C). Following recommendations from the Mahoney City Center Committee and subsequent Council discussion during a February 10, 2026 workshop, guidance was given to staff and the design team to exclude the Library from the project, and to find a location for the Police Station other than the Mahoney property.

 

This Order serves to formally remove the Library from the Mahoney project and purview of the M3C, and to remove references to the Police Station. The Order has also been amended to remove reference to presenting Council with a "70% design" in July and instead replaces it with "schematic design". This is less than what a 70% design would be, but is less costly and still able to produce an accurate cost amount to present to voters. The original Order is attached for your reference.

 

Update (March 31, 2026): Over the past two meetings, the Council has changed its position on the Mahoney project. Council is now interested in bringing the Library back into the project, and has asked the Mahoney City Center Committee to explore public/private partnerships to help fund the project. In addition, Council has pushed the date of a potential referendum to November 2027, and has given guidance to staff to hold a November 2026 referendum for a new Police and Central Fire station.

 

Based on this new guidance, staff is proposing an alternative Order #157-25/26. This Order keeps some of the prior changes and makes new ones given recent developments. See attached.

 

Please note two specific changes: 1) the removal of the Police Chief and Fire Chief from the M3C, and 2) authorizing the committee, as part of their review of public/private partnership options, to consider scenarios that do not include all services within Mahoney, as some of that space may be needed to be provided to a developer in order for them to enter into a partnership. (Attached also is a red-lined version of the original order showing these alternative changes).

### ORDINANCE #20-25/25 - Amending Chapter 4, "Bicycles" of the Code of Ordinances to Update the Definition of a Bicycle and Add a Definition of Electric Bicycle to Better Align with State Definitions. First Read. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

On March 10, 2026, City Council held a workshop at the request of the Bicycle & Pedestrian Advisory Committee to allow electronic bicycles on City property.

 

As noted during the workshop, based in part on guidance from the City's legal counsel that City Council should expressly state whether electronic bicycles (e-bikes) are allowed on the City's Greenbelt (current interpretation is that they are not), on April 18, 2023, the City Council passed first reading of an ordinance amendment to Chapter 18 that would have specifically allowed them.

 

However, on May 2, 2023, the Council voted to withdraw the item from the agenda without prejudice due to concerns raised by Councilor West about the width of some portions of the Greenbelt not meeting the American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO) standard of 10 feet minimum. As such, e-bikes remained prohibited (although in practice, South Portland does not actively patrol the Greenbelt for e-bike enforcement).

 

Since that time, the City has conducted an in-house assessment of the Greenbelt to identify constraints and compile all existing easements. Public Works concluded that although some areas are close to the minimum width requirements, a full replacement of the entire Greenbelt should occur due to erosion, tree root issues, and pavement damage throughout the corridor. The next steps would be to secure funding for an inspection of the Anthoine Bridge and to begin preliminary design work. This work is estimated to cost approximately $200,000. Alternatively, funding could be pursued for the entire project, including construction, which is currently estimated at $6.5 million.

 

On October 14, 2025, the Bike Ped Committee once again requested a workshop on this topic (see attached). Based on input from Councilor West, at the March workshop City staff presented an alternative to what Council considered back in 2023. Staff are proposing to essentially align the City's definitions of a bicycle with the State's definition. Under the State's definition, Class 1 and Class 2 e-bikes are akin to traditional foot-powered bicycles, and thus would be allowed on the Greenbelt (and other City properties where pedal bicycles are allowed). Achieving this would require amendments to Chapters 4, 15, and 18 of the City's Code of Ordinances.

 

At the workshop, a majority of Councilors gave guidance to proceed with the necessary ordinance amendments. Attached is the first of three separate ordinance changes needed to better align City definitions with the State's, and effectively allow Class 1 and 2 e-bikes on City property such as the Greenbelt.

 

Parks & Recreation Director Karl Coughlin will be present for this discussion.

### ORDINANCE #22-25/25 - Amending Chapter Chapter 18 (Parks and Recreation) of the Code of Ordinances to Remove the Prohibition of Class 1 and 2 Electric Bicycles from Operating on City Property, and Other Miscellaneous Updates. First Read. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

On March 10, 2026, City Council held a workshop at the request of the Bicycle & Pedestrian Advisory Committee to allow electronic bicycles on City property.

 

As noted during the workshop, based in part on guidance from the City's legal counsel that City Council should expressly state whether electronic bicycles (e-bikes) are allowed on the City's Greenbelt (current interpretation is that they are not), on April 18, 2023, the City Council passed first reading of an ordinance amendment to Chapter 18 that would have specifically allowed them.

 

However, on May 2, 2023, the Council voted to withdraw the item from the agenda without prejudice due to concerns raised by Councilor West about the width of some portions of the Greenbelt not meeting the American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials (AASHTO) standard of 10 feet minimum. As such, e-bikes remained prohibited (although in practice, South Portland does not actively patrol the Greenbelt for e-bike enforcement).

 

Since that time, the City has conducted an in-house assessment of the Greenbelt to identify constraints and compile all existing easements. Public Works concluded that although some areas are close to the minimum width requirements, a full replacement of the entire Greenbelt should occur due to erosion, tree root issues, and pavement damage throughout the corridor. The next steps would be to secure funding for an inspection of the Anthoine Bridge and to begin preliminary design work. This work is estimated to cost approximately $200,000. Alternatively, funding could be pursued for the entire project, including construction, which is currently estimated at $6.5 million.

 

On October 14, 2025, the Bike Ped Committee once again requested a workshop on this topic (see attached). Based on input from Councilor West, at the March workshop City staff presented an alternative to what Council considered back in 2023. Staff are proposing to essentially align the City's definitions of a bicycle with the State's definition. Under the State's definition, Class 1 and Class 2 e-bikes are akin to traditional foot-powered bicycles, and thus would be allowed on the Greenbelt (and other City properties where pedal bicycles are allowed). Achieving this would require amendments to Chapters 4, 15, and 18 of the City's Code of Ordinances.

 

At the workshop, a majority of Councilors gave guidance to proceed with the necessary ordinance amendments. Attached is the third of three separate ordinance changes needed to better align City definitions with the State's, and effectively allow Class 1 and 2 e-bikes on City property such as the Greenbelt.

 

Parks & Recreation Director Karl Coughlin will be present for this discussion.

### ORDER #178-25/26 - Authorizing the City to Submit a Letter to the Maine Department of Environmental Protection Regarding the Renewal of Portland Pipe Line Corporation’s Oil Terminal Facility Licenses. Passage Requires Majority Vote.
POSITION PAPER OF THE CITY MANAGER

Pursuant to Council Rule 4, the Mayor has placed on the agenda a request to authorize submission of a comment letter to the Maine Department of Environmental Protection (DEP) regarding the Portland Pipe Line Corporation (PPLC) oil terminal facility license renewal. Note that this license is separate from their air emissions license.

 

PPLC has applied to renew its oil terminal facility licenses under the Oil Discharge Prevention and Pollution Control Act (38 M.R.S. §§ 541–560) and associated DEP rules (Chapter 600). The facility includes multiple tank farms and marine piers in South Portland, with a total storage capacity of approximately 3.83 million barrels of oil.

 

DEP’s draft order (see attached) finds the facility in compliance with applicable requirements and proposes license renewal through August 10, 2030. As part of the renewal process, DEP evaluates:

         Spill prevention and operational compliance

         Closure planning and cost estimates

         Financial assurance and liability coverage

 

While not opposing the proposed license renewal, the attached letter requests clarification from DEP on several items within the scope of its review. Key points include:

 

         Closure Costs and Financial Assurance

PPLC has submitted a closure estimate of $11.7 million with $12 million in financial assurance. The City requests clarification on how this amount was determined and whether it reflects potential remediation needs at a facility of this size and age.

         Inactive Tanks

A significant number of tanks are currently out of service, many for extended periods. The City requests DEP’s perspective on any associated risks and appropriate oversight.

         Site Conditions

The draft order references areas of known releases but provides limited detail. The City requests clarification on how these conditions are reflected in closure planning.

         Natural Hazard Risk

A Natural Hazard Risk Assessment was submitted as part of the application. The City requests additional information on identified vulnerabilities, including flooding and coastal risks.

         Future Transfer of Ownership

DEP rules require review of financial and technical capacity for any license transfer. The City requests notification and re-evaluation of financial assurance at the time of any transfer.

 

Council is being asked to authorize submission of the attached comment letter to the Maine DEP.
