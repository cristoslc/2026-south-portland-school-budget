### Reactions

The lead isn't the housing workshop, even though that was technically the biggest policy discussion. It's that ICE enforcement in Red Bank forced the school board to postpone a budget meeting that's been planned for weeks - that's the story that shows how federal policy is disrupting basic local democracy. When Pedro Vasquez says people are carrying papers and missing court dates because they're afraid to leave their homes, that's not just immigration news, that's civic dysfunction. The school board postponing their budget forum tells readers everything about where we are right now.

The housing stuff is actually more interesting than I expected - five councilors unanimously agreeing to basically deregulate multifamily development is significant, especially when Milan's presentation suggested this could affect virtually every property in the city. But the practical constraints are real too. West pointing out that fitting four units on 5,000 square feet with setbacks and parking is "very, very difficult" - that's the reality check that keeps this from being the development apocalypse some people fear. The 10% conversion rate to meet housing demand is a useful number to ground the discussion.

What strikes me is how the council handled both crises tonight - the immediate one of ICE enforcement and the longer-term one of housing costs - with remarkable unanimity. No drama, no dissent, just methodical problem-solving while community members described neighbors too afraid to leave their houses. That contrast between the procedural calm and the human fear described in public comment is the tension that defines local government right now.
