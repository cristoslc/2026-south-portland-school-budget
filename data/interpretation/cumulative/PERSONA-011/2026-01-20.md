# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

I've been pushing the January 22nd budget forum in every group chat for two weeks. It was the first formal public input opportunity on the cuts since the crisis began, the answer to a thread I'd had open since December 1st — when will these new leaders give parents a seat at the table? And then, two days before it happens, Chair DeAngelis walks to a city council podium and cancels it. Not indefinitely, but moved to February 4th. The reason: ICE activity confirmed at Scrub Dub car wash and the laundromat in Red Bank. Families are too scared to come to a school meeting.

That hit me differently than any of the budget numbers have. I've been tracking a financial crisis for six weeks — deficits, frozen classroom supplies, 78 positions on the chopping block — and now the meeting where parents were finally supposed to weigh in can't happen because some families don't feel safe leaving their houses. The two crises aren't parallel anymore. The community fear is directly interrupting the budget process.

Chief Ahern's statement is the piece my group chats need most: the city's 2017 resolution is still in effect, police won't enforce immigration laws, but residents should not try to intervene with federal agents. He made the point bluntly with the Cape Elizabeth example. That's quotable and I'm going to use it, because people are going to ask "what is the city doing?" and this is the answer — limited, but real.

The housing law workshop that filled the rest of the meeting was genuinely hard to follow while my phone was lighting up with ICE questions. But I stayed with it because it connects to threads I've had open since December. LD 1829 forces South Portland to allow up to four units on virtually any lot with city water and sewer starting July 1st, and planning board review is eliminated for those small projects. The density math Milan was walking through — 10% of single-family homes adding a unit to meet demand — is exactly the kind of figure that will surface in neighborhood Facebook arguments for the next decade. I noted it, but I'm not leading with it tonight. Tonight is about the cancellation and the ICE situation. The housing stuff keeps.

The thread I resolved tonight is also the one that hurts most to close: January 22nd was supposed to be the answer to "when do parents get to weigh in?" The new answer is February 4th — but the reason for the delay tells a story about who feels safe showing up to public meetings in this city right now, and that's a story I didn't have words for until tonight.
