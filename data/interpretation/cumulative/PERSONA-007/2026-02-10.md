# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026 -- 2026-02-10

### Interpretation

This was the budget meeting I've been dreading since the preliminary numbers started circulating in finance committee, and it was as stark as I feared. George laid it out plainly: Option A at 19% is obviously dead on arrival, but even Option B at 6% still leaves a $3.5M hole. The enrollment-versus-staffing chart -- 300 students lost, 82 positions added since 2017 -- is the smoking gun I've been trying to get administration to produce for months. It tells the whole story of how we got here, and it's going to be impossible for anyone to argue we don't have a right-sizing problem.

What really got to me was Ellen Sanborn's fund balance report. The school side is at $739K with $850K budgeted for use this year, which means we're functionally at zero. If the school runs a deficit -- which seems likely given Abigail's projections confirmed just last night -- that comes out of city fund balance temporarily. The city has $8.5M in excess fund balance, so there's a cushion, but the politics of city taxpayers bailing out school mismanagement is toxic for validation.

The most important moment was Rosemary DeAngelo calling out the council's historical "do your best" approach to budget guidance. She was absolutely right -- the school board can't make $7.2M in cuts without knowing what tax increase the council will actually support. The council's response was a 3-6% range, with most gravitating toward 4-6%. That's real numbers, finally, even if the range is still wide. Councilor Walker's willingness to support 6%+ as unpopular but necessary showed political courage. But Assistant Superintendent Prince's warning that 3% means 50+ additional staff cuts beyond what's already planned made the human cost of that low end viscerally clear.

The elementary reconfiguration saving roughly $2M is looking increasingly like the centerpiece of whatever budget we bring forward. I've seen similar consolidations work in other districts, and the data supports it operationally. But after last night's meeting where most community contacts opposed it, the political execution needs to be much better than our engagement so far. The middle school consolidation timeline is confirmed dead for FY27 -- $25K traffic study with 6-12 months of DOT permitting makes September implementation impossible.
