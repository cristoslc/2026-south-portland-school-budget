### Reactions

This meeting was devastating but not surprising. Seeing 78 positions on the chopping block - especially 42 teachers and 16 ed techs - I immediately started thinking about which kids will suffer most. You know those educational technicians are disproportionately supporting our students with disabilities, our English learners, our kids who need the most help. And there's no way those cuts are distributed equitably across schools.

What really gets me is we're spending $26,651 per student - the highest in our comparison group - but I guarantee you that money isn't reaching our most underserved schools. I need to see the school-by-school breakdown because I bet the predominantly white, affluent schools are getting renovations and enrichment while our Title I schools lose their reading specialists. The enrollment decline story is also telling - when families leave, which families are leaving? Are we pricing out working families and families of color with these tax increases?

The state funding crisis is infuriating but predictable - they promise 55% funding and deliver 20%, then act shocked when local districts implode. This forces communities to choose between educational equity and housing affordability, and guess who loses either way? The same families who always lose. We need to organize around this because individual districts fighting alone won't fix the systemic underfunding that's destroying public education for the kids who need it most.
