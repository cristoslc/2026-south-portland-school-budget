# Cumulative Summary: Meg (PERSONA-011)

### Current Understanding

As of April 2, 2026, Meg understands the South Portland school district to be operating under a structural budget gap that Councilor Matthews placed at $8.4 million on the March 19 council record — $1.2 million higher than the $7.2 million figure that had been the working number since December 17. The district has proposed eliminating 78 positions for FY27, including 42 teachers, 32 of whom are on a recall list; pink slips were issued March 18. The proposed FY27 budget carries a 6% tax increase translating to $257 per year for the average $514,000 home, but that budget failed a 5-2 board vote on March 30 and has not been adopted. The April 7 council presentation is the hard deadline; the board deferred to gather better information, with a joint council meeting Tuesday as the operative next decision point.

Elementary reconfiguration has been authorized: Option A places PreK–1 at Dyer and Small and grades 2–4 at Brown and Skillin, with Kaler School closed effective end of the 2025–26 year. The Kaler closure passed 6-1; DeAngelis cast the sole no vote on equity grounds, as Kaler is approximately 45% BIPOC and 30–35% multilingual learners. She voted yes on Option A. Attendance boundaries have not been published.

The district's structural problems compound each other: state funding covers roughly 20% of costs against a 55% statutory obligation; enrollment fell 23% at the elementary level between 2017 and 2022 while staffing grew by 82 positions; and the finance office has had seven directors in six years, with a documented history of mismanaged grants and repeat legal budget violations. The finance director warned in March that FY27 resets the path but does not solve the core problems — FY28 will be harder.

Property tax pressure extends beyond schools. The city approved an $87 million capital bond for police and fire facilities, targeting a 2027 voter referendum. Sewer rates will increase roughly 22% per year for three years beginning this fall. TIF arrangements on 170 Ocean Street and with Texas Instruments divert property tax revenue from the general fund for decades.

As of April 2, unconfirmed state funding of $300,000 to $1.05 million may be forthcoming from union lobbying at Augusta — the first reported movement on the state underfunding problem since the crisis began.
