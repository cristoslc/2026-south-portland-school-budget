### Reactions

I stayed up until midnight watching this thing and most of it genuinely wasn't for me — the fire station facade debate, who gets to pick the historic inventory, none of that touches my buildings or my kids. But then around 10:30 pm, Peter Cullen, this architect who shows up to everything, casually mentions "yesterday's news about going to having a four elementary school network versus five" like it's settled, like we all got the memo. And in the same breath he's suggesting Brown School become city hall, something about making a campus with the library. I rewound that section three times. Not a single council member reacted. They just kept talking about whether fire trucks can turn around on Ocean Street.

Which four schools? What's the timeline? Is Brown closing? I travel through Brown twice a week. I have students there on open MTSS referrals. I have specialist sessions on the books. And now I'm watching a retired architect throw the building into a city council conversation about municipal real estate like it's a warehouse that happened to have kids in it last year. This is exactly what the Boundaries and Configurations work was supposed to prevent — schools disappearing through the back door of a different process, without redistricting, without equity analysis, without any of the careful work that would actually fix what's broken. Closing one building and reshuffling the same segregated enrollment patterns isn't reconfiguration. It's just cutting.

The other thing I'm still thinking about is the early testimony from the families afraid to go to school. A woman reading someone else's statement described a family with a five-month-old, an eviction notice, a father detained. That's not a policy abstraction — that's a kid who has been out of my building for weeks and whose IEP timeline doesn't pause because his family is in crisis. And I watched the council have a genuinely decent conversation about a rent relief program, which fine, yes, good — but the instability those kids are living in doesn't end with a rent check. Lauren Shapiro said it directly and clearly: housing segregation is school segregation. She stood up at a city council meeting and said the thing I've been saying internally for years. And then everyone moved on to the square footage of fire truck bays. That's this city in a sentence right there.
