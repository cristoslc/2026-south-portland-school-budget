# General Upcoming Briefing
## Upcoming Meeting: 2026-04-02

## PUBLIC BRIEFING: South Portland School Board Special Budget Meeting
### Thursday, April 2, 2026 — 6:00 PM, South Portland High School

---

## Since Last Meeting

The March 30 special meeting produced two major decisions and one significant failure.

**What the board decided:** The board voted to authorize the Superintendent to file a school closing report for Kaler Elementary School with the Maine Commissioner of Education — meaning Kaler is set to close at the end of this school year. The board also voted 4-3 in favor of **Option A**, a full reconfiguration of the remaining four elementary schools: Dyer and Small would become "primary" schools serving PreK through grade 1, while Brown and Skillin would become "intermediate" schools serving grades 2 through 4. These two votes were separate from the budget and currently stand.

**What the board did not decide:** The FY27 operating budget failed 5-2. Only Members Smith and Risch voted in favor. The five opposing members cited different concerns: a last-minute change to the DEI director position that saves little to no money while eliminating the district's only senior BIPOC administrator; cuts to special education support positions; the absence of any fund balance in the budget; and a shared desire to hear from City Council before locking in the spending plan.

**What has happened since:** The district has moved quickly on reconfiguration planning. As of April 1, a community engagement schedule is posted: school-by-school staff meetings run April 7–14, family meetings run April 8–16 at each school, and an online ThoughtExchange survey is live for public input. The district is treating the reconfiguration decision as settled and is beginning transition work.

**The clock is running:** Under the budget process, the board must present its budget to City Council on April 7 — four days after this meeting. Without a board-adopted budget, that step is in jeopardy.

---

## What This Meeting Is For

Tonight is, above all, a second attempt to pass the school budget — and a chance to decide whether the board will formally ask City Council for help before doing so.

The budget before the board is the same $75.85 million proposal from March 30. It represents a 3.3 percent increase in overall spending and a 6 percent increase in what local taxpayers contribute. For the owner of a home at South Portland's average assessed value of $514,000, the school portion of the property tax bill would rise by roughly $257 per year. That is the school district's share; the city's other budget items will add to it separately.

The board is also being asked to vote on whether to formally meet with City Council to seek "budgeting guidance." Several members believe the city may have tools to help — potentially lending from city reserves, or allowing a higher tax increase than the 6 percent guidance already given — and want that conversation before locking in a final number.

A third, more procedural vote would approve up to four early-release days in May and early June. That time would be used for staff planning work required to carry out the reconfiguration before fall.

## Decisions Likely Tonight

**1. Adopting the FY27 budget (second attempt).** The board will vote again on the full superintendent's spending proposal. For it to pass, at least some of the five members who voted no on March 30 would need to change their votes. The budget includes closing Kaler, the Option A reconfiguration, eliminating approximately 70 positions (about 12 percent of the total workforce), and a 6 percent increase in the local tax share. It does not include a fund balance contribution, and it does not restore the percussion ed tech position at the middle school.

**2. Convening a joint meeting with City Council.** The board will vote on formally requesting a meeting with City Council to seek budget guidance. This is a process question as much as a financial one: even if City Council were willing to provide flexibility, what form would that take? The superintendent noted on March 30 that the city council "doesn't have money to give" — any support would be a loan from city reserves, which the district would eventually need to repay, adding to future-year budget pressure.

**3. Early release days for reconfiguration planning.** Up to four additional early release days are proposed: May 13, 20, 27, and June 3. These would give teachers and administrators structured time to plan the logistics of the reconfiguration — where students go, how classrooms are set up, how special education services continue across the new school structure.

## Key Public Questions

**Will the budget pass this time?** Five members voted no on March 30. None of the concerns they cited have been formally resolved since then. The budget text appears unchanged. It is not publicly clear what, if anything, would cause a "no" vote to become a "yes."

**What, specifically, would a City Council meeting accomplish?** Board members and residents have called for city involvement, but the exact ask remains vague. Is the board requesting a loan? A higher tax guidance number? The March 30 meeting surfaced that any city reserve funds would be a loan, not a gift — borrowing now would add to future costs. Clarity on what is actually being requested would help the public evaluate whether this is a realistic path.

**What happens if the budget fails again?** Maine law requires the board to put a budget before voters on June 9. The board must formally adopt a budget before it can be presented to City Council on April 7 and workshopped on April 14. If the board does not adopt a budget tonight, the district must either hold another emergency meeting very soon or miss the City Council presentation deadline. Under state law, a failed voter referendum would revert to the prior year's budget — approximately $2.5 million less than this proposal — which would trigger even deeper cuts than what is already proposed.

**Does the reconfiguration decision hold even if the budget fails?** The board voted on Kaler's closure and Option A separately from the budget. Those votes appear to stand. But if the budget is not adopted and the district cannot fund the transition work, it is unclear how reconfiguration would proceed in practice.

**What will community engagement actually produce?** The reconfiguration road map lists family and staff meetings at every school between April 7 and 16, plus an online survey. But the board approved reconfiguration *before* this outreach happens. Residents will likely want to know: what can change based on community input, and what is already decided?

**What is the status of the DEI director and percussion ed tech positions?** Member DeAngelis named the DEI position change as a reason for her "no" vote; Member Feller named the percussion ed tech. Neither change has been publicly reversed. Will either be addressed tonight?

## What To Watch For

**Whether the vote count changes.** Listen carefully to board members' statements before the vote. Do members who voted "no" on March 30 signal that anything has changed? Do they attach conditions to a "yes" vote?

**Whether anyone moves to amend the budget.** A board member could propose changing the budget before adopting it — for example, to restore the percussion ed tech, reverse the DEI position change, or add a fund balance contribution. Whether such a motion would be in order procedurally, and whether it would have the votes, is uncertain.

**Whether the City Council vote and the budget vote are treated as linked.** Several members have said they want to talk to City Council before adopting a budget. If the board votes to meet with City Council but *also* votes to adopt the budget tonight, those are consistent. But if members say they will only adopt the budget *after* hearing from City Council, that creates a direct conflict with the April 7 City Council presentation deadline.

**Whether the early release day vote passes.** This vote is procedurally routine, but a "no" would be a notable signal that support for the reconfiguration plan may be softer on the board than the 4-3 margin suggests.

**Any new financial figures from the administration.** On March 30, the finance director reported that the maximum health insurance rate dropped slightly — from 12 percent to 11.5 percent. That is a small but real change. Listen for any new financial information that might shift the overall spending picture.

**Whether anyone addresses the fund balance gap directly.** Multiple members and residents named the lack of a fund balance as a core structural concern. The March 23 and 30 presentations acknowledged that this year's budget "does not solve our core problems" and that FY28 will bring new cost pressures — more debt service, rising utilities, and further enrollment decline. Watch for whether anyone offers a concrete plan for rebuilding a reserve, or whether that question gets deferred again.

**Signals from the chair on timeline.** Board Chair DeAngelis has been explicit about the April 7 City Council deadline. If the budget fails tonight, listen for what she says the next step is — another emergency meeting, a delayed presentation, or something else. That will tell you how much runway the district believes it has left.
