# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

The 6% cap held. I need to sit with that for a moment, because it's the first time the school board has followed through on something they said they'd do since this whole thing started in December. The finance director confirmed it in plain English: 6%, no higher. She put a dollar figure on it too — $257 a year on a typical South Portland home. That's the number I've been waiting four months to hear. I finally have it.

But here's the thing. The same finance director who gave me that number also explained why it won't be enough. She used an analogy I'm going to borrow: paying off your credit card balance doesn't help if you're still spending more than you earn every month. Labor costs go up automatically through step increases and contract raises — more than 6% on their own. Utilities are climbing 13 to 14 percent. In FY28 they're going to start paying principal and interest on the athletic field bond. The fund balance is empty — not low, empty — and when a board member asked what happens if they get a bad storm or a lawsuit next year, the answer was: draw on the city's reserve and repay it by raising our taxes the following year. That's the plan.

I'll give credit where it's due: the finance director said out loud that she's the seventh person to hold this job in six years, and she said it as a cause, not an excuse. That's the most honest accounting I've heard from inside that district all winter. The diagnosis she laid out — 300 fewer students, 82 more staff positions, COVID money used to paper over the structural gap until it ran out — is exactly what I've been saying since December. It's something when the person getting paid to fix the problem tells you that you were right about what broke it.

What's sticking with me from public comment is the union president's testimony. Part-time lunch aides — people making near minimum wage — are out. Forty-two teachers are out. But the central office administrators and directors? Mostly still there. I've been asking about administrative bloat since December. When we have the highest per-pupil cost in comparable districts at $26,651, and the state's covering 20 percent of what it promised, and the people who kept their jobs are the ones furthest from the classroom, I need to see every admin line before I agree to anything else.
