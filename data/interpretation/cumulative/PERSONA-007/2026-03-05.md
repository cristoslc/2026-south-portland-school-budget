# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Looking back at March 5 from here — knowing what got formally voted at March 19 — this is the meeting where the public safety bond started finding its shape, even if no one could quite see that in real time. The Walgreens announcement was the one clean thing all evening: the council is under contract on a specific site for a specific purpose, and a November 2026 referendum is the stated target. Two weeks later I'd watch that P&S get voted unanimously and the scope get locked. On March 5, the direction was already set, and I felt the first genuine structural relief about the November collision I've been tracking since January.

The scope number is what I needed. Since January 13th, when Sanborn's $2.26-per-thousand figure made the first real dent in my modeling, I've been trying to calculate what homeowners are actually walking into. The $194M package broke apart in January, the Mahoney scenarios ran from $57M to $104M in February, and now March 5 produces $87M for police and fire combined. That's still a significant November ask sitting alongside our June validation. But it's a different political animal than the $194M shock. The scope has been collapsing toward something workable, and I can work with a known number even when the number is uncomfortable.

What I keep returning to is the library. Kevin put the case plainly: 13,000 visitors in two months, 27 per hour, asbestos behind every upgrade path. And the council postponed the orders that would formally separate the library from Mahoney — not because they have an alternative plan, but because they don't. I understand not wanting to sever an option before you have a replacement. But that's procrastination dressed up as governance, and those are real constituents with a building that's genuinely failing. Library needs are going to become someone's problem on a timeline that doesn't respect when it's convenient.

Natalie's fire station research was serious — the inundation data and response time analysis were real work, not performance. But raising structural site objections at this stage, with a November 2026 deadline requiring July-August council approval to even qualify, is a clock problem more than a policy problem. And the facilities director's testimony about weekly sewage backups and a preventive maintenance program that didn't exist until five years ago lands differently against the school district's own deferred maintenance history. We've both been running our physical infrastructure on hope.

The rental assistance testimony is the piece I connected to February's ICE-enrollment framing. $150K from undesignated fund balance, families describing displacement risk. By March 19 that becomes $100K to Project Home and Matthews naming 72 pink slips in City Council minutes. The draw-down of the city's $8.5M backstop — the number I didn't have until February 3 — is beginning. Not catastrophically, but it's beginning.
