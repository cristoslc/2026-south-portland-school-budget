# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: Joint Budget Workshop -- February 10, 2026 -- 2026-02-10

### Interpretation

The day after the board's unanimous vote, the administration went before the city council and made the reconfiguration proposal official in a joint setting. Interim Superintendent Entwistle presented a plan to split elementary schools into three intermediate buildings (grades 2-4) and two primary buildings (pre-K-1), claiming $2 million in savings. Hearing it laid out this formally, to both governing bodies simultaneously, made it feel much more like a done deal than a proposal under consideration.

What alarmed me most was the vagueness underneath the apparent specificity. When councilors asked perfectly reasonable questions -- why do you need five buildings if you're consolidating? -- Assistant Superintendent Prince couldn't give concrete answers, just vague references to special education mandates and potential future pre-K expansion. They're proposing to fundamentally reorganize how a thousand children experience school, and they can't clearly explain why the alternative configurations don't work. That's not a plan; it's a wish with a price tag.

The parent voices at this meeting were powerful and validated everything I've been feeling. Rose talking about the bike bus program captured exactly the kind of community connection that disappears when you treat schools as interchangeable containers for children. And the union president's intervention was critical again -- Sarah Gay pointed out that the administration still hasn't provided the central office staffing data promised six weeks ago, and asked why administrative cuts aren't being explored before classroom disruption. That question hung in the air unanswered.

The council's guidance landed in a 3-6% tax increase range, which is wide enough to mean almost anything. But critically, most councilors gravitated toward 4-6%, which would significantly reduce the cuts needed. If the tax increase ends up at 5-6%, the math changes enough that maybe -- maybe -- the reconfiguration isn't "necessary" anymore. That's the argument I need to make: there are revenue options that don't require upending children's lives. The administration's framing of this as inevitable is a choice, not a fact.
