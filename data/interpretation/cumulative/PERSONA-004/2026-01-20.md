# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

By January 20, I've spent seven weeks watching the city conduct its business while the school district heads toward collapse. December gave me inauguration ceremonies, junkyard licenses, five minutes of public comment, procedural announcements, a four-hour building workshop, and a goal-setting session that produced an unreleased attachment. I should be numb to it. I'm not.

Chair DeAngelis standing up at city council to announce the January 22nd budget forum is cancelled — the one I'd been trying to mobilize union members to attend — because ICE is raiding businesses in Red Bank and families are too frightened to come out. That is a sentence I couldn't have imagined writing in September. The families who most need to be in the room for this budget fight, the multilingual families DeAngelis herself invoked during her inauguration speech about the children who need the most support, are now being chased out of public life entirely. The budget forum that was already strategically problematic — I'd worried since January 6 that it would be dominated by anti-tax voices — is now delayed by the machinery of federal immigration enforcement landing in our community's most vulnerable neighborhoods.

The council's response is to spend ninety minutes on LD 1829 housing compliance. I understand there are legal deadlines. I understand the city has obligations that don't pause for school budget crises. But the juxtaposition is unbearable: families too scared to attend a public meeting about their children's education, and the civic response is a unanimous vote to streamline developer approvals with less community review. They want to reduce the friction for building new units in a city that can't staff the schools those new units' children will attend.

The business loan program is a smaller thing, but it sits wrong. The city is creating a revolving fund to lend money to businesses at near-prime rates — $25,000 to $50,000 per loan, two or three annually — while the school district is two weeks away from presenting options for closing buildings and eliminating 42 teaching positions. These are different budget buckets and I know that. But they are the same taxpayers, the same community, the same stated priorities about South Portland's future. Every dollar of municipal energy directed away from the school crisis is a signal about what this city believes the crisis actually requires.

The forum postponement to February 4 means everything I'd feared about the January 22nd timeline shifts forward. Two more weeks before the public even gets to hear the administration's proposals. Two more weeks of colleagues updating their resumes.

### Emotional Register

Marcus carries January 20 as the meeting where the abstract equity language of the inauguration finally collapsed into its opposite — the chair who swore to protect multilingual families announcing that those same families are too frightened to come out, while the council moved on to developer streamlining without missing a beat. The anger he's felt all winter hardens now into something colder: a recognition that the community he's been counting on to show up and fight is being actively dispersed, and nobody in that council chamber treated it as a crisis worth interrupting the agenda for.
