# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

If the February 4 budget forum was where the crisis became real, this meeting is where Maria realizes the board has already decided. Chair DeAngelis calling elementary reconfiguration "a fabulous idea" and "good for kids" before any formal proposal has been presented -- before the March 2 workshop, before parent survey, before any educational impact analysis -- tells Maria that the decision-making process she has tried to influence since January is largely performative. The chair's dismissal of neighborhood schools ("we don't really have them anymore") directly contradicts Maria's lived experience of walking her daughter to school, knowing every teacher by name, and building community through her PTA.

The unanimous board vote to request a formal reconfiguration proposal feels like a formality disguising a foregone conclusion. Maria tracks a painful pattern: in December, the board spent $30,000 it didn't have on a superintendent search while cutting classroom supplies; in February, they unanimously advance reconfiguration despite community opposition and teacher pushback. The teachers' union president was unambiguous -- educators do not support plans that eliminate colleagues' jobs. Over a hundred people attended budget forums saying "student needs first." Fifty-one data requests have been filed. And yet the board moves forward without hesitation.

There is one crack in the wall that gives Maria complicated hope. Board member Feller openly suggested a tax increase above 6% might be necessary to avoid "draconian cuts." This reopens a thread Maria has been carrying since the February 4 forum: if the 6% ceiling is not actually fixed, then perhaps the forced choice between reconfiguration and decline is a false dilemma. But it also raises a disturbing possibility -- the district might both restructure elementary schools AND raise taxes beyond 6%, delivering disruption without the savings supposedly justifying it.

The current-year deficit confirmation (FY26 now running deficits across all scenarios due to the nutrition fund shortfall) adds another layer of financial instability. Maria had already processed the current-year crisis from the December 8 spending freeze, but learning that deficits are now projected under every scenario -- not just worst-case -- deepens the sense that the financial foundation is crumbling in real time, not just in future projections.
