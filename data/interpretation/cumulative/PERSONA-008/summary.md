# Cumulative Summary: Rachel (PERSONA-008)

### Current Understanding

As of March 20, 2026, Rachel understands South Portland's school crisis as a system-wide institutional failure operating on an accelerating, near-final timeline. The budget deficit has grown from the $7.2 million figure she first encountered in December to $8.4 million. The district must cut the equivalent of 78 positions — 42 teachers, 16 ed techs — and 72 termination notices went out on March 18, explicitly described as "just the first wave." Nine formal elementary reconfiguration scenarios were presented at Budget Workshop I on March 2nd, including closures of specific named schools (Kaler, Dyer, Brown, Small, Skillin) and grade-band models splitting elementary into two primary buildings (pre-K/K/1) and three intermediate buildings (grades 2–4). Board Chair DeAngelos has declared this "fabulous," dismissed neighborhood schools as obsolete, and stated there will be no public referendum. Four board members opposed closing Skillin, but Skillin faces a $3M HVAC replacement that may reopen that question.

The crisis is not an isolated school-board problem. It sits atop a $25M state structural underfunding gap, competes with a $194M city hall project for the same property tax base, is complicated by ICE enforcement suppressing enrollment and civic participation, and is being managed by an institution with documented repeat audit non-compliance and six finance directors in five years. Transportation impacts of reconfiguration have not been modeled; redistricting mechanics remain unresolved. The teachers' union has not expressed unified support and bargaining is required. The decision timeline has accelerated: April 7 public hearing, April 14 school budget workshop at city council, May 5 council vote, June 9 referendum. Councilor Matthews's casual use of "closing schools" at the March 19th council meeting signals that closures are now institutional vocabulary at both governing bodies.
