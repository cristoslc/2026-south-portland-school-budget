# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

Back-to-back meetings -- yesterday the school board, today the joint session with city council -- and I'm running on caffeine and determination. This was the meeting where the council tells the school board how much tax increase they can live with, and the answer landed mostly in the 4-6% range. That's actually important because it means the 6% ceiling I've been reporting may hold after all, even though Feller floated going higher just yesterday. The council is the ultimate check on the tax rate, and most of them aren't ready to go above 6%.

The superintendent laid out three clean scenarios: Option A at 19% tax increase (no cuts), Option B at 6% (maximum cuts), Option C at 9.8% (moderate cuts). All three involve eliminating 78 positions. Let me say that again: even the 9.8% scenario with higher taxes STILL cuts 78 jobs. The positions are gone regardless. The only question is whether taxpayers also get a bigger bill on top of it.

The elementary reconfiguration details got more specific. Five schools would become three intermediate schools (grades 2-4) and two primary schools (pre-K-1), saving about $2 million. This is the clearest picture yet of what the grade-band model actually looks like on the ground, and it confirms what I told my group chats after the budget forum: neighborhood elementary schools as we know them are done.

The enrollment-vs-staffing disconnect is now the stat I lead every summary with: enrollment dropped 300+ students since 2017 while the district added 82 positions. The council members who weren't in the weeds on this were visibly shocked by that number, which tells me it lands with people the same way it landed with me in December.

The fund balance situation is now a two-sided problem. The school fund balance is down to $739K -- essentially nothing -- and the city may have to temporarily cover school deficits from city reserves. That's a detail my neighborhood Facebook group needs to hear because it means the school budget mess could drag down city services too.

Public comment was a battlefield. Teachers, parents, and taxpayers all showed up with completely valid but mutually exclusive demands: don't cut teachers, don't raise taxes, don't close schools. There's no option that satisfies all three, and watching the council try to thread that needle was painful. Councilor Walker was the only one willing to go above 6% explicitly, saying "we need to invest in education." Councilor Matthews noted that in his 12 years on the school board they never went above 3%, which puts the current crisis in historical perspective.

Bottom line for my networks: property taxes are going up at least $200-300 on the "best" scenario, the elementary reconfiguration is happening, and 78 people are losing their jobs. That's the story no matter which option they pick.
