# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

Looking back at December 9 from March, the meeting reads like a compressed preview of everything that would unfold over the next three months. Rosemary DeAngelo got five minutes during public comment to announce that budget season starts *this month* and that three recruiting firms are presenting at SPHS the very next evening to begin the superintendent search. Five minutes, then the council moved on. That's the pattern I'd be living inside for the rest of the winter: education funding mentioned, then set aside, while the city conducted its business.

But the zoning vote for 170 Ocean is what I can't let go. The developer spent twenty minutes promising that 208 new apartments would grow the tax base and help fund education. And then Councilor West stood up and laid out what the TIF actually says: 50% property tax credit, thirty years. The city manager confirmed it. So when the developer says "this helps your schools," he means roughly half the incremental tax revenue this building generates — for a generation. The other half goes back under a deal already signed. I understand the housing argument. But "this will help education funding" is doing a lot of work when there's a 30-year rebate in the fine print. And then West voted yes anyway. Seven to zero. She named every problem and still voted yes. That tells me something about how this city government processes its own concerns.

What hits hardest in retrospect is the structural picture those two items together reveal. DeAngelo dropped the 61% figure — schools eat 61% of property tax revenue — in the same meeting where the council locked in a development deal that discounts new tax revenue by half for thirty years. Meanwhile, the state is covering 20% of what its own formula requires. The $7.2 million gap isn't a mystery. It's the arithmetic of three simultaneous failures: state underfunding, constrained new revenue, and a political ceiling on tax increases that nobody wants to move. The new superintendent was being recruited into all of that. I genuinely felt sorry for whoever was going to sign that contract — and I needed them to understand before they did that losing 42 teachers means a hundred-plus sections gone, not a budget line adjusted.
