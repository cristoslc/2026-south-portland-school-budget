# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

The city's silence is cracking. After December 4 and December 9 — two full meetings where city council said nothing about schools while the budget crisis was already underway — January 6 brings the first real acknowledgment from the city side that something is coming. The mayor announces a citizen forum on January 22. The city manager describes a budget guidance workshop with the school board scheduled for February 10. The city has finally entered the conversation that Lila's family has been trapped inside for a month.

But knowing everything that followed, the framing is the thing. The January 22 forum is described as a place to share "ideas and feedback of some potential cost saving measures." Not "how do we protect our children's schools." Not "how do we find the resources we need." The first time the city invites the public into the school budget conversation, the conversation is already shaped around cuts. The adults are asking people what to take away, not asking whether there is something worth keeping.

The February 10 workshop — presented here as a routine planning step, the city manager asking calmly what "acceptable tax increase" looks like — is the same meeting that will end with the board chair publicly confronting the city council for their vague "3% to 6%" guidance. It doesn't sound explosive on January 6. It will feel that way afterward.

Mark's music festival at Bug Light is the one uncomplicated thing in the whole meeting. Trumpet player, five bands, one of them called Primo Banano, free admission in September. Lila holds onto September the way she holds onto the idea that school might still be okay. But September is also when she might be starting somewhere new — a building she's never walked through, trying to find which bathroom is the good one.

The mayor says property taxes are "everyone's favorite subject" with the same sarcastic tone her teacher uses when someone forgets their homework. She talks about seniors and people on "fixed incomes" struggling. Nobody ever talks about how it's hard for kids when the adults can't figure out the money. The city has finally said the words "school budget" out loud. It just took until January to get there — and the conversation started already pointed at cuts.
