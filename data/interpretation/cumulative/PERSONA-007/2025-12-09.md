# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

Twenty-four hours after Abigail laid out the $1.9M deficit with no fund balance to absorb it, I'm watching the city council vote 7-0 on a major development project while the developer tells the room his project "can help with every single element of this city's budget, whether it be education funding." Not for thirty years it won't.

The One 70 Ocean rezoning is probably the right call for long-term development. I'm not second-guessing the vote. What I'm still processing is the fiscal mechanics that came out in the middle of it, almost as a footnote. Josh, the assistant city manager, explained that outside a TIF district, the city only captures about fifty cents of every new dollar of property value created — because higher assessments reduce the state education subsidy and increase county obligations. He said it matter-of-factly. I don't think most people in that room registered what he'd just said. I did.

For years I've been sitting in finance committee watching our state subsidy come in at 20% of actual costs when the EPS formula is supposed to deliver 55%. The explanation I always heard was political — the legislature changes the formula, the targets shift, Maine underfunds education. All true. But this is the structural mechanism underneath it: every time South Portland's tax base grows and valuations rise, the state automatically reduces what it sends us. Development isn't just slow revenue relief — it's partially offset at the source. The city isn't keeping a dollar of every new dollar. And the project approved tonight gives 50% back to the developer for thirty years on top of that.

Then there's the taxable base context: $120 to $125 million in new valuation this year, versus $1.2 billion the year before. One-tenth. That's the revenue runway I'm working with for the budget I'll have to defend at validation. And commercial values dropped 2.5% while residential climbed 3%, which means homeowners are absorbing more of the tax burden again — making them exactly the voters who'll be most sensitive to the school budget line when we ask for more.

Rosemary stood up and said the right things: schools are 61% of property taxes, superintendent search starts tomorrow, budget season begins this month. The room had moved on. Looking back from March, that moment captures the entire problem: the right framing, said at the right time, to an audience already focused elsewhere. The gap between what development is supposed to deliver for schools and what it actually delivers — structurally, on our timeline — is going to be mine to explain.
