# Cumulative Summary: Ben (PERSONA-010)

### Current Understanding

Ben understands the South Portland school crisis as a four-month institutional failure with roots stretching back years. The district's $8M FY27 gap — grown from a December $1.9M figure — traces directly to a six-year finance leadership vacuum (seven directors in six years, self-reported by the current director), a no-fund-balance-policy that removed any early warning, and COVID relief funds that inflated staffing never brought back down after federal money expired. Elementary enrollment fell 23% while the district added 82 positions. The result: a $26,651 per-pupil cost, the highest among comparable districts.

Structural solutions remain unmade. The board voted 6-1 to close Kaler and 4-2 for Option A reconfiguration on March 30, but the FY27 budget itself failed 2-5 on fractured, incompatible objections. At April 2, new state money arrived mid-meeting — a likely $300K confirmed plus a possible $750K EPS formula change — preventing another vote. The budget goes to the April 7 council presentation as the superintendent's document, not a board-adopted one. September 2026 implementation is a physical impossibility given the current timeline.

The school crisis does not stand alone. The same South Portland homeowner faces sewer rate increases of ~22% annually through FY29, a November 2026 public safety bond (~$87M), and a November 2027 Mahoney bond ($70–76M). No elected body has assembled the combined household impact. Ben has the school-portion figure — $257/year on a median $514,000 home — but the full multi-variable number remains unassembled and unpublished. The TIF mechanics he traced in December confirm that new development provides almost no school budget relief on any timeline that matters for this referendum. The immigration crisis and school crisis are now officially linked, through Councilor Scott's enrollment argument and Councilor Matthews' lone no vote on Project Home funding.
