# Cumulative Summary: Maria (PERSONA-001)

### Current Understanding

As of April 2, Maria understands the South Portland school budget crisis as a compound failure spanning structural underfunding, multi-year financial mismanagement, and a governance process that has consistently absorbed parent opposition without changing course. The district faces a gap now cited at $8.4 million for FY27 — grown from the $7.2 million figure first quantified in February — with no fund balance remaining. Elementary enrollment has declined 23% in four years, from 1,401 to 1,080 students, a drop compounded by immigrant family displacement driven by ICE enforcement activity.

The board has voted to close Kayler Elementary and adopt Option A reconfiguration (PreK–grade 1 / grades 2–4 split across four remaining buildings), but as of April 2 the FY27 budget has not been adopted. The board is waiting for confirmed state funding figures — a projected $300,000 in new aid was announced mid-meeting — before a final vote, with the April 7 city council deadline as the binding constraint. Seventy-two employees received pink slips on March 18.

Maria understands the support infrastructure reductions as interlocking and severe: 16 educational technicians, four elementary MTSS positions, all seven lunch aides, and the district's sole general education behavioral strategist — a position covering nearly 60 students and more than 40 formal behavior plans across four buildings — are all proposed for elimination. These cuts land in the same September that hundreds of displaced Kayler students will arrive at unfamiliar schools with larger class sizes.

Attendance boundaries under Option A have not been drawn. Transportation plans have not been modeled. Families with children in multiple grade bands do not yet know whether they face two-building assignments. The community engagement process — thirteen listening sessions plus a digital survey — is underway, and the early removal of a proposed early-release day schedule, following parent pushback, gives Maria her first concrete evidence that public pressure can produce a visible reversal.
