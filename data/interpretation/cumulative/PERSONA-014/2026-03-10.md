# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

This meeting happens the day after Ms. Beland's resignation became official record — the day after a Dyer teacher made her decision based on an assumption about what Dyer is becoming. Lila is still holding that. The read-a-thon is scheduled. The bookmark-making is happening. And a teacher already left before any vote was taken.

The letters read aloud at city council added a fear Lila didn't anticipate. An elementary school. A lockdown. ICE agents near the school driveway. Children too scared to go to class. She has been tracking the immigration thread since January — Red Bank, then Brown School kindergartners, then families in darkened apartments, then a neighbor setting her alarm for 5:45 AM to drive a friend to work. Each time it has gotten closer. Now it is at a school driveway. That is the shape of her mornings. That is the walk she knows.

And then the school board member stood up. DeAngelis — the same person who formally requested the reconfiguration proposal in January, who set the nine scenarios in motion — stood at city council and said she could confirm: no ICE agents came to any South Portland school. No children taken from bus stops. She was kept informed. She knew. The relief is real, and Lila accepts it. But the family in the letter was already hiding before anyone showed up. Being confirmed-safe after the fact doesn't reach back and uncurl all that fear. The children in those letters were too scared to go to class whether or not ICE actually arrived at the driveway.

A city council member used the word grim. Councilor West said he listened to the school board and the situation is grim. Not "difficult." Not "challenging." Grim. Lila has known it was grim since December, when teachers couldn't buy laminating paper. She has watched the city use careful, cushioned words for a crisis that has felt nothing like careful. Hearing that word from that side of the room doesn't close the $7.2 million gap or bring Ms. Beland back. But it means someone there is finally looking at the same thing Lila has been looking at all winter.

Councilor Scott said something Lila couldn't quite place but couldn't stop thinking about: eighty families are eighty students who might not be in the school system next year. The immigration fear and the budget fear have been running side by side since January — two different kinds of losing a school. Now an adult is saying they're the same problem. Losing families to enforcement and losing students to declining enrollment are both ways the numbers get smaller. And smaller numbers, she knows by now, are what closes schools.

### Emotional Register

Lila holds this meeting in two registers at once: the partial, incomplete relief of being told that no ICE agent actually came through a school door — and the unshakeable knowledge that the children in those letters were frightened before anyone arrived, which is also exactly how she has been frightened about Dyer all winter, afraid of what is coming before it gets there. Hearing a city council member finally say "grim" about her school's money feels like being seen for the first time in months, and it still doesn't help.
