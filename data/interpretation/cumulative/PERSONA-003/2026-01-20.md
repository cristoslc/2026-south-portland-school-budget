# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

I'm watching this meeting a week after the January 13 Mahoney workshop — still processing the triple tax stack — and it opens with something I was completely unprepared for. The January 22 budget forum, the one I'd actually been planning to try to attend, got cancelled. Not rescheduled for logistical reasons. Postponed because immigrant families in South Portland are afraid to leave their homes. Chair DeAngelis announced it from the public comment microphone, just like she announced budget season back in December — two sentences, no elaboration — and then speakers confirmed what she was describing: ICE enforcement at Scrub Duds car wash and a laundromat in Red Bank, cell phone video, kids missing school, families staying inside.

This hit differently than the budget numbers. I've been doing financial math for six weeks straight, and now I'm sitting here thinking about the parents at December's city council inauguration who spoke in six languages while DeAngelis promised to listen. Those are the same families being kept home right now. The community stability I've been implicitly factoring into my "should we stay" calculation just got a variable I hadn't included.

The housing workshop that followed is another piece of the same puzzle, just from a different angle. The state is requiring South Portland to permit up to four units on 5,000 square foot lots across most of the city, with streamlined approval. I've now watched six weeks of city meetings where more housing and school contraction are moving in opposite directions — and the density changes being mandated here are larger than anything discussed before. The enrollment math I've been doing gets even harder when I don't know how many more children might be added to a system simultaneously cutting teachers and seats. Nobody in this room ran those numbers.

The childcare-in-residential-zoning change is genuinely good news for my family's timeline — more neighborhood childcare options, less distance to drive — and the business loan funds suggest the city is trying to support the economic fabric that makes South Portland worth staying in. But they're footnotes to a meeting that delivered two gut-punches: the community is more fragile than I understood, and the neighborhood I bought into is about to change in ways no one is coordinating with the school system.
