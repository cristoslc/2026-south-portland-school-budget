# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Goal Setting Workshop -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

Nine days after the city council finally said the words "school budget" out loud — nine days after the January 6 forum announcement felt like the adults were finally paying attention — the city held another meeting and didn't say Dyer's name again.

It was a goal-setting workshop. Lila's mom explained that goal-setting means making a list of the things you want to do. And Lila understands goal-setting. Her teacher does it at the start of every unit. But her teacher does it before anything goes wrong, not in the middle of a crisis. Why are the adults making lists of goals when teachers are losing their jobs and her school might close? Why does everything get turned into a process when there's already a problem right in front of them?

The numbers have been accumulating in Lila's understanding since December, and this meeting brings the whole picture into awful focus. The $7.2 million gap she first learned about in December. The forty-two teachers who might be gone — forty-two — a number she keeps trying to count out in her head and can't fully picture. The district's savings: gone. No cushion, no backup plan, nothing saved up for when things get bad. Her mom saves money for emergencies. The district didn't.

One piece feels almost comforting: the state is supposed to be paying 55 cents of every school dollar but is only paying 20. That means it isn't Dyer's fault. Dyer didn't do anything wrong. The problem is something bigger and further away — Augusta, lawmakers, people Lila has never seen and never will. It's not that her school failed. It's that the money that was supposed to come never arrived. That should feel like good news, but it doesn't change what's happening. It just means nobody is guilty — and nobody guilty means nobody fixes it.

The city council had a meeting with goals and an attachment nobody published. And Lila came home from asking her parents about it with the same answer she always comes home with: not yet. We don't know yet. They're still talking.

She's been waiting since December. It's January now. The waiting feels the same as it did before, except there's more to be afraid of.
