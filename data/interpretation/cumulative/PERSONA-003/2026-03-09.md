# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- March 9, 2026

### Interpretation

Three months into watching these meetings and I feel like I'm stuck in a loop. The same devastating numbers keep coming back: 78 positions cut, 42 teachers, 12% of staff, elementary enrollment down 23%, fund balance gone, $7.2M gap, 6% tax ceiling. I've heard all of this before. What's different now is that they presented nine reconfiguration scenarios at the March 2 workshop but haven't signaled which direction they're actually going. The board pushed back on closures last week, but this meeting didn't advance the conversation -- it felt like the board is circling the same territory without making decisions.

The school closure thread has gotten muddier, not clearer. The interpretation from this meeting flags "elementary school closures being considered" at threat level 5, but that conflicts with what I heard at the budget forum -- that buildings won't close because of pre-K space needs -- and with the board's own pushback against Skillin closure just a week ago. I think the reality is that some scenarios include closures and others don't, and the board hasn't committed to a path. For a parent trying to plan ahead, the ambiguity is almost worse than a clear bad answer.

The pre-K expansion thread remains the most consistently positive signal. The district will serve 80-90 four-year-olds for special education starting September 2026 with plans to expand to three-year-olds the following year. This has come up in every meeting since January and each time the details get more concrete. State mandates, physical space needs, partnership funding -- it has multiple structural supports that make it resistant to budget cuts. If my daughter is going to benefit from anything in this district, it will be early childhood programming.

What's crystallizing for me after nine meetings is that I'm watching two districts simultaneously: one that's contracting -- losing teachers, losing students, losing money, potentially losing buildings -- and one that's growing in early childhood services because state mandates and community demand are forcing expansion. My daughter is going to enter at the exact point where these two trends intersect. Whether that's a crisis or an opportunity depends entirely on decisions being made right now by people who can't tell me which school she'll attend. I'm no longer thinking about leaving South Portland -- the pre-K expansion has given me enough reason to stay and see -- but I am deeply uncertain about what comes after pre-K, and nobody in the system seems able to answer that question yet.
