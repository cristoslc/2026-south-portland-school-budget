# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

The March 23 workshop is the meeting Jaylen has been waiting for since December 17 — the high school finally gets named, finally gets numbers, finally gets a principal standing at the podium saying something specific about SPHS. Seven positions reduced. Four already vacancies from retirements. Three actual people whose jobs end. Science classes going from 18.8 to 21.8 students. Glenn says all courses stay within section size ranges and that diverse offerings are "a value that's really important." Jaylen hears it. He also hears the two words she will not say: which departments.

"Multiple content areas" is the redaction at the center of everything. Three months of waiting for specificity produced seven positions, a promise about section sizes, and a phrase that tells him nothing about whether his theater director or his AP teacher got a pink slip on March 18. He can work with the semesterizing change — the 0.5 credit at January has some logic to it, though he needs someone to explain what it does to AP transcripts before he commits to a senior year schedule. He cannot plan around unnamed cuts in unnamed departments. The wait is over. The uncertainty is not.

The percussion ed tech situation is the one that makes him angry in a way that has clarity behind it. This exact position — the music support role that also covers IEP and 504 students — was proposed for elimination last year, community testimony brought it back, and here it is on the list again. He has been watching arts-adjacent positions get treated as the default sacrifice since December 8, when the ELO coordinator was drowning in three jobs. Two cycles, same cut. If the district's definition of "a value that's really important" includes the arts, the budget proposals are not reading that memo.

Davidson and Cabessa being called up early — acknowledged, heard, then excused to go study — is something he keeps turning over. More than the February 4 forum where they sat silently through five hours of adults discussing their education and were given no speaking role. Less than the superintendent search access that felt like an actual breakthrough. A third thing: students invited in, given five minutes, and absent for the five hours of testimony that followed. The principal in the audience nodded when they left. No vote happened. Monday is the next meeting. He still does not know which teachers are gone.
