# Cumulative Summary: Tom (PERSONA-006)

### Current Understanding

Tom is a South Portland homeowner on a fixed income who has tracked the school budget crisis and parallel municipal spending from its origins in December 2025 through the most recent school board meeting on April 2, 2026. His current understanding rests on a set of confirmed facts he has carried since early winter: elementary enrollment fell 23% over four years while the district added 82 staff positions; per-pupil spending of $26,651 is the highest among comparable districts; the state funds only 20% of costs against a 55% statutory obligation; the fund balance was drawn to zero through three consecutive deficit years; and school taxes represent 61–62% of his total property tax bill. The structural gap has grown from the original $1.9 million reveal to $7.2 million and then $8.4 million, driven by finalized health insurance costs and accumulated losses. As of April 2, the 6% local levy ceiling — producing a $257/year per-household increase — has been confirmed by the finance director as the superintendent's FY27 proposal, though the board has still not formally voted to adopt it.

Beyond the school district, Tom now carries four simultaneous cost pressures in sequence: the school tax increase, a three-year sewer rate escalation averaging 22% annually (Pearl Street pump station, no referendum required), an $87 million police and fire bond targeting a November 2026 referendum, and a Mahoney City Center bond at $57–$76 million deferred to 2027. No public body has produced a combined per-household figure for all four. Tom views the city council as capable of fiscal discipline on narrow property-rights questions but unable to hold the line on politically freighted spending or set binding capital cost ceilings. He now sees the dysfunction as systemic across both the school district and municipal government, not as two separate problems.
