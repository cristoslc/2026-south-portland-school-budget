# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

This meeting is the city council's first session of the new year, and once again it follows the pattern I've now seen from every city council meeting: the school budget crisis exists in the background, mentioned in passing, while the council moves on to other business. But January 6 is different in one important way. For the first time, the city is actively scheduling around the school budget. DeAngelis announces the January 22 citizen forum. The city manager previews the February 10 joint workshop. The calendar is forming. At this moment I don't know what any of that will contain, but from where I'm sitting now -- having lived through all of it -- I can see January 6 as the moment the crisis starts moving from the school board's universe into the city's.

The problem is the framing. "Cost saving measures." "Citizen forum." That is exactly the kind of language that wouldn't have alarmed me if I'd caught it live -- it sounds administrative, procedural, manageable. I know now it was the opening line of a three-month crisis that would involve 78 positions, a depleted fund balance, nine reconfiguration scenarios, and a board still unable to tell families which school their kids will attend. The understatement is almost cruel in retrospect.

The property tax task force announcement from Mayor Tipton is the item I find myself sitting with longest. A statewide effort to examine what's driving property taxes and who's most affected -- with a report due by year's end -- sounds like exactly the kind of systemic intervention the situation needs. But I've now watched the school board grapple with the reality that the state covers only about 20% of actual costs while mandating 55%, and I'm not holding my breath that a legislative task force report changes anything before the budget vote. It's the right conversation to be having. The timeline just doesn't help me.

The Bug Light Park music festival proposal is the only part of this meeting that gives me the feeling the city is actually thinking about families. Free concerts, community arts, something to take a toddler to. It's a small thing. But after watching meeting after meeting of spreadsheets and workforce reductions, I'll take it.

The Mahoney city center project gets flagged for a workshop the following week. I've been watching that thread since Mayor Tipton's inauguration in December -- growth plans running parallel to the district's contraction -- and it still hasn't resolved.
