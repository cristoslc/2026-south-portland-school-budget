### Reactions

I walked into that meeting thinking I'd write about three vendors pitching search services. Instead, I watched a school board facing a $7.2 million budget crisis - one that requires cutting 78 positions - vote to spend $30,000-plus on the most expensive superintendent search option available. The room was completely empty of community members, which makes the board's decision to pay a premium for "community engagement" feel particularly tone-deaf.

The most telling moment came when Interim Superintendent Whistle - the guy who actually knows the numbers - warned them the real cost would hit $35,000 with travel expenses. Board Chair DeAngelo basically shrugged and said if they choose the wrong superintendent, it'll cost more than $30,000 in the long run. That might be true, but it's exactly the kind of reasoning that got them into this financial mess in the first place.

What's fascinating is how the board justified this. They kept talking about needing "consensus" and finding the right "match" for South Portland's diverse community. Those aren't wrong priorities, but when you're asking taxpayers to absorb an 18% tax increase while cutting nearly 80 jobs, spending three times more than necessary on a search firm because they promise better team-building feels pretty disconnected from reality. Two members - Holman and Rich - clearly got that, but they were outvoted by colleagues who seem convinced that expensive equals better.
