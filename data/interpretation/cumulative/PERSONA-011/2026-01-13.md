# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

I've been watching the property tax bill from the school side for seven weeks straight. Every meeting, every scenario, every deficit figure — all of it filtered through "what does this mean for my kids' schools and what do I tell my group chats?" Tonight I watched the other side of the same bill get its own crisis.

$194 million. That's what the Mahoney City Center committee came back with — a combined police station, rebuilt fire station, consolidated city hall and library at the old Mahoney site. Committee chair Mike Halsey opened by asking the council directly whether that number "changes the direction." Watching on my phone, already knowing what the school board is working toward for January 22nd, my answer was immediate: it changes everything taxpayers are being asked to absorb this year.

Finance Director Ellen Sanborn did the math I needed: $2.26 per thousand added to the mill rate, roughly $1,162 more per year on a home assessed at $514,000. She was careful to say that's the worst case — borrow it all at once, phase it and it comes down. Fine. But the school side is already adding 4-6% minimum to a portion that accounts for 61 cents of every property tax dollar. These numbers don't live in separate buckets. They land on the same bill.

The quote I'm leading with tonight is from SMRT architect Craig Piper. Councilor Matthews asked him directly: how confident are you in $193 million? His answer: "I am not confident." Five percent design completion, unknown soil conditions on an old dump site, structural unknowns in a 1925 masonry building, construction escalation risk. There is no floor on that number. That's what I'll tell my group chats.

The council is already backing away from the full ask — no one up there thinks $194M is going to voters this November. The actual outcome tonight is a pause: pull police and fire off the table, bring back Mahoney-only options for January 27, decide by early August if it goes to a November ballot. The project isn't dead. It's just smaller and later.

Back in December I understood that TIF deals were diverting property tax revenue from the general fund for decades on growing housing projects. That was one mechanism. Tonight I'm watching a potential nine-figure capital bond arrive in the same tax cycle as the biggest school cut package in this city's recent memory. The taxpayer math isn't just complicated anymore. It's becoming actively hostile, and my networks are about to feel it from two directions at once.
