# Cumulative Record: Kira (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 10, 2025 -- 2025-12-10

### Interpretation

Ten days in, and she watches the board do something she genuinely didn't expect: pick the right firm, for the right reasons, with the wrong safeguards.

She'd opened the concurrent search thread the night before — the fear that a leaderless district in crisis would default to whoever was cheapest rather than whoever was right for the community. What she got instead was three hours of the board pressing every presenter on equity and diversity in ways she hasn't seen elected officials do in years. Richardson asking each firm for data on non-white placements. DeAngelos pointing directly at MSMA's all-white team and naming limited lived experience as a problem, out loud, in the meeting. That is not nothing. That is the inauguration equity language cashing out into actual pressure.

And then Jeff Melendez said the sentence she will replay. Some families don't feel comfortable coming into school buildings right now — especially with some of the things that are happening — they're afraid. He named it without being asked, in front of elected officials, as a design constraint. A platform in 100 native languages. Going to where families actually are — Willow Pizza, a community center, wherever they'll come. That is the superintendent search her families deserve, and the one she's been making the case for in different language for years.

The 4-2 vote with a cost negotiation mandate is what she cannot put down. Rich and Holman's financial objection is honest — she lives inside that tension every day at three different buildings. But she knows which line items get negotiated away first: the ones most expensive and hardest to defend on a spreadsheet. The community engagement week. The thought exchange platform. The parts of the process that might actually reach the families who weren't in that room on a Wednesday night.

And the chair's framing of that empty room is what lands hardest. DeAngelos did the outreach, invited the council, publicized the meeting — and concluded: if that's the attitude of the public, we need to make the best decision we can. Kira has been tracking participation barriers since December 4. She watched two women pay forty dollars each in babysitting to speak at a different meeting. She knows why the room was empty, and it isn't attitude. That the chair would read absence as disengagement — even as pragmatic acceptance rather than judgment — is the sharpest warning she's received that the structural barrier she's been documenting may not be visible to the people who most need to see it.

Three hours of discussion. Not one mention of MTSS wait lists, intervention programming, or what the next superintendent actually inherits on the programmatic side. Her caseload was invisible in that room.
