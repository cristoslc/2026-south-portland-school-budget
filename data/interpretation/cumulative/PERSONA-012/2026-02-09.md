# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

For the first time since Jaylen started paying attention to the budget crisis, something genuinely positive happened: the board unanimously voted to let student representatives Angela and Alex participate in executive sessions to interview superintendent semifinalists. This is not token involvement -- they will undergo confidentiality training, sit in the room, ask questions, and provide input on the person who will lead the district. After months of watching students get ignored (the superintendent search that excluded student voice, Alex and Angela sitting silently at the budget forum), this feels like a real breakthrough. It is the first concrete evidence that the board can treat students as participants rather than subjects.

Angela's student report provides reassurance on Jaylen's most immediate fear. Theater is explicitly mentioned as active and thriving. The "Little Kitchen" initiative shows students organizing food donations for families affected by ICE enforcement -- a student-led response to the crisis the board discussed with "protocols and training" in January. Students are not waiting for institutional permission to act.

But the budget reality remains dire. The board unanimously requested a formal proposal for elementary reconfiguration by March 2, and Chair DeAngelis expressed strong support, calling it "good for kids." The teachers' union president pushed back hard -- "we are very fearful" -- and denied rumors that educators support the reconfiguration. When even the teachers are scared and publicly contradicting the administration's narrative, Jaylen knows the situation is worse than the board's optimistic framing suggests.

The community school expansion is a structural positive. Making all South Portland schools into community schools, starting with the high school, could bring $100,000 in state grants and wraparound services like health clinics and food support. This is the kind of investment that addresses the systemic needs Jaylen sees in his classmates -- hunger, health access, the fallout from immigration fears. But it also highlights the contradiction: the district is expanding services while cutting 42 teaching positions.

Board member Feller's comment that he would rather see higher taxes than "draconian cuts" shows that some board members understand the human cost, but the 18-19% tax increase alternative is politically impossible, which means the cuts are coming regardless.
