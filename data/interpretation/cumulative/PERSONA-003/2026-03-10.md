# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

This is the meeting where the ICE fear I've been tracking since January finally has a child's face on it. Not an abstraction, not a demographic statistic -- a parent's letter, read aloud, describing her kids too scared to walk into school because a neighbor reported seeing ICE agents in the driveway. I've documented this fear thread for nine weeks now, through the forum cancellation, through Cassie Moon driving a woman to work on back roads, through the six-year-old whose classmates were missing. But this letter lands differently because it's about the school building itself. Not the neighborhood around it. The building.

Then DeAngelis gets up and says no, there were no ICE activities at our schools, we were kept informed. And I believe her -- and I still feel worse. Because the fear was real enough that a parent genuinely didn't know whether federal agents were in her child's school driveway. That gap between what officials know and what families experience in the moment is exactly the gap I've been living in for four months. The process runs on one track; families run on another.

The Councilor Scott moment is the first time anyone in city government has said, on the record, what I've been doing in my head since January: 80 families equals 80 students who may not be in the school system next year. She made the enrollment math explicit as a justification for the rental assistance fund, and it got through. The $100,000 passes, no immigration status required. That's something. After watching the eviction moratorium fail 4-2 in February, I wasn't expecting it.

But I can't let that be the note I end on, because the school budget comments from two councilors who watched the board meeting the night before are sitting on top of everything else. "Grim." "Very cautious of every dime." Forty-two teachers. I now know from March 19 that the layoff notices actually went out on March 18 -- the day before that meeting, one week after this one. On March 10, nobody had connected those dots yet publicly. But the decision was already in motion. The transit portion of this meeting, the bus schedules and the high school ridership numbers, barely registered for me -- I can't think about whether Metro serves the high school well when I don't know if the high school will have the same teachers next year. The system my daughter is entering is being dismantled in real time while the city deliberates rental assistance and bus routes. I keep waiting for someone to talk about what comes after.
