# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

The February 3 meeting handed me two new analytical tools and a scheduling choice that made the council's priority ordering impossible to misread. I came in already knowing the TIF pattern, the engagement failures, the housing deferrals. What I did not know — what the audit revealed — is that the targeted grant funds designed specifically for English learners and students with disabilities have been mismanaged, overspent into ineligibility, and forced back into the general fund. Four hundred thousand dollars. A material weakness finding — and a repeat one. Six finance directors in five years at the school department, three of them interim. Thirty-four journal entries to correct the books when the auditor's comfort threshold is under ten.

This reframes the entire budget crisis for me. I have been tracking it as a story of state underfunding and political will — the 20% actual coverage against a 55% statutory obligation, the TIF votes, the choice not to attach affordability conditions. Those are real. But they are not the whole picture anymore. The district has also been failing, internally and for years, to steward the equity-designated funding it already receives. The grants that are supposed to supplement services for multilingual learners and students with IEPs are the ones that got mismanaged. Those students lose twice: once when the money is spent wrong, and again when the correction absorbs resources that should have served them.

The Texas Instruments TIF extension adds a third instance to the corporate subsidy pattern I have been cataloguing since December — but it brings something new with it. The sheltering mechanism. South Portland's TIF portfolio does not merely redirect potential equity investment into private development; it restructures what the state believes South Portland can contribute to education costs, potentially suppressing the state aid flowing to the district's schools. The city has been simultaneously claiming poverty on school funding and engineering the appearance of reduced wealth to extract more from state formulas. I want the numbers on this.

And then, at 9:25 PM, after nearly four hours of audit findings and corporate tax structuring, the council reached the immigration resolution. Families in Red Bank are sitting in the dark. A budget forum had to be rescheduled because immigrant parents were too frightened to attend. Community members are at the podium asking for an eviction moratorium, and the city discovers it has only $2 million in discretionary funds — after all the corporate incentives have been paid out. The resolution passed. That matters. The scheduling told me more.
