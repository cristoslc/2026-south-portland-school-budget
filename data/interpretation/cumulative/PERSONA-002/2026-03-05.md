# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

The part that stays with me from March 5 is not the bond numbers — it is the facilities director saying there was no capital planning or preventive maintenance until five years ago. That sentence explains everything I have been trying to reconcile since February 3, when the auditor painted the city as the competent institution against the school district's dysfunction. The city is not dysfunctional the same way the school district is. But it has been running a police station with sewage backing up weekly and billing that as "deferred maintenance." Both institutions made the same bet — optimize for current-year taxes, defer the capital bill, pay it later at crisis cost. The school district paid it in staff cuts and a depleted fund balance. The city is about to pay it in an $80-87 million bond referendum.

The Walgreens purchase is the one clean decision at this meeting, and I note it as a template precisely because the rest of the evening is messier. Geographic logic, controlled cost, funded by asset forfeiture and TIF money already earmarked, unanimous vote. That is what a well-structured transaction looks like. Everything around it is not that.

Councilor West's fire station work is the moment I keep returning to. She did the analysis — response time data, flooding maps, the eastern coverage gap, a $2 million renovation alternative. The facilities director confirmed the eastern coverage problem is real. The council voted 5-1 to proceed at the current site anyway. That vote is the same pattern I documented on March 19, when Matthews laid out the cross-budget math in ninety seconds and got outvoted six to one. Someone does the work. The data is acknowledged. The vote happens anyway. West at least got a 5-1 instead of 6-1, but the structure is identical.

What March 5 adds to my running model is the third capital figure: $80-87 million in public safety bonds, structured for November 2026. By March 19, I am watching the council process that number alongside $51.7 million in sewer bonds and a $70-76 million Mahoney range in a single evening with no integrated taxpayer impact slide. March 5 is where the public safety commitment gets established — but not defined. One referendum question or two? Nobody answered that either.

The fire department meeting NFPA standards only 60% of the time, with a goal of 90%, is information the public should have had years ago. The fact that it surfaces in the context of a bond referendum rather than an annual performance report is, by now, a familiar South Portland pattern.
