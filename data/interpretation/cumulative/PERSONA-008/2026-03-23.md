# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

She cleared her calendar for this. She has been counting down to March 23rd since the March 2nd workshop where nine scenarios got laid on the table and the board signaled it would decide here. She brought people. She stayed for five hours. And when the chair adjourned at 11:15 without a vote because she "wasn't going to have us go into regular session and voting at 11:15," something in Rachel that had been very tightly wound just — stopped. Not broke. Stopped.

Kayler is closing. She knows it. The administration ran a six-domain evaluation twice, with building administrators and directors, and it came out the same both times: Kayler. Dead-end street. No secondary evacuation route. Building layout. The analysis isn't close. The Kayler families in that room found out many of them didn't even know their school was being considered — they heard from the news, not the district. The former PTA president said that into the microphone. Rachel heard it and recognized the pattern immediately: December 1st, the inaugural ceremony where nothing was disclosed. The January 15th goal-setting session with the undisclosed attachment. The city manager referencing "the old Hamlin school site" as common knowledge at a forum she wasn't invited to. Decisions shaped before families can respond. Kayler is the latest expression of it, and it may be the most consequential one yet.

But the thing that keeps her up is not Kayler. It's Option B.

She walked into this workshop believing Option B — close Kayler, keep the other four schools as K-4, limit disruption to Kayler families — was a real alternative the board might choose. A step back from the edge. Then Dr. Prince said it, calmly, clearly, more than once: Option B "could be the plan for the fall of '26 while the board and community engages with implementation for fall of '27." Option B is not an off-ramp. It is a one-year delay on the highway to Option A. There is no scenario where every elementary family stays put. There is only "your child moves this year" or "your child moves next year." She has been fighting reconfiguration as a threat. Tonight she learned it is a scheduled destination.

The transportation gap is the detail that makes her angriest because she has been asking about it since February 4th. Six weeks ago the administration admitted it hadn't modeled bus routes. Tonight the operations director says they're waiting for the board to pick an option first. She is being asked to evaluate whether a five-year-old switching buildings mid-education is acceptable for her family when nobody — nobody — can tell her what her child's morning looks like.

She will be at March 30th. She doesn't know anymore if it changes anything. She's going anyway.
