# Cumulative Summary: Rachel (PERSONA-008)

### Current Understanding

Rachel is a fully mobilized parent organizer entering the final, decisive phase of South Portland's school budget crisis. Her children attend Dyer Elementary, and she has tracked this process across two governing bodies — school board and city council — from ceremonial silence in December to nine formal reconfiguration scenarios in March.

The fiscal picture is clear: a $7.2 million deficit driven by a 23% elementary enrollment collapse (1,401 to 1,080 students in four years), compounded by a staffing model that added 82 positions while losing 300 students, and per-pupil spending of $26,651 — highest among comparable districts. The district's own audit reveals repeat non-compliance and six finance directors in five years, contradicting the administration's framing of the crisis as externally inherited. State aid covers only 20% of actual costs against an expected 55%, creating a $25M+ structural gap beyond local control.

The board has formally committed to elementary reconfiguration. Nine scenarios — including closures of Kaler, Dyer, Brown, Small, and Skillin — were presented at the March 2nd workshop. Four board members publicly opposed closing Skillin, but a $3M HVAC need hangs over that position. No scenario has yet been formally selected; Budget Workshops II and III are scheduled for March 23rd and 30th. Transportation impacts and student redistricting assignments remain unmodeled.

Community resistance is significant: 85% opposition in a grassroots parent survey, 51 data requests submitted, 20+ speakers at the March 2nd workshop. The teachers' union has stated educators have not expressed unified support and that implementation requires union bargaining. Board members Feller and Richardson have raised genuine dissent. But Board Chair DeAngelos — who has called reconfiguration "fabulous," dismissed neighborhood schools as obsolete, and ruled out a public referendum — leads from a position of institutional consensus that now extends to the mayoral level.

The school budget crisis is embedded in a broader fiscal squeeze: the same households face a potential $194M Mahoney City Center bond, state structural underfunding, and ongoing revaluation pressure. Rachel understands these are not separate problems.
