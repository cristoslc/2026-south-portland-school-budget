# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

The Mahoney thread I've been watching since Mayor Tipton's inauguration speech on December 1 finally got concrete, and concrete is not the same as reassuring. Four hours of discussion about a $194 million project to turn the old Mahoney school into consolidated city offices, a library, and a police station. A former school. And the finance director laid out the math plainly: the average South Portland homeowner, assessed at $514,000, is looking at over $1,100 more per year in property taxes if this bond passes. Then, almost as an afterthought near the end, the city manager mentions there's also a $50 million sewer project coming that would be billed separately through sewer rates -- and nobody calculated what that adds to the monthly bill.

I've now watched six city council meetings and the pattern is identical to what I identified in December: the school budget crisis and the city's capital plans exist in completely separate universes. Seven-point-two million dollar structural gap. Forty-two teachers on the table. A quarter of elementary enrollment gone. In four hours of workshop, it never came up. Not once. They debated whether to include a rooftop garden. Meanwhile I'm doing mental math: if property taxes go up for the bond, and they go up for the school budget, and then they go up again for sewer, what does that stack actually look like for a family trying to stay in South Portland? I don't know, because nobody in that room was adding those numbers together.

The person who said out loud what I was thinking was a first-time homebuyer named Henry Silve, who'd just bought his house in December -- basically us, essentially our situation -- and he told the council that an 18% school tax increase alone already makes it too hard, and this would make it harder. The council received that and moved on. The housing ordinance, which was literally the reason I stayed up to watch, got ten minutes at the end of a four-hour meeting and no substantive discussion. The thread about state housing laws and what they mean for neighborhoods like mine: still unresolved.

The architect, when asked directly how confident he was in the $194 million figure, said: "I am not confident." That sentence is sitting with me. The number they spent four hours debating might not even be the real number.
