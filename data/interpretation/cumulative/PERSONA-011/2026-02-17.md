# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

The headline is: the eviction moratorium is dead. Failed 4-2 on first reading. Walker and Tipton voted yes; Coleman, Matthews, Pride, and Scott voted no. And the thing I keep staring at — three weeks ago, on February 3rd, that same council voted unanimously to ask the governor for a 60-day eviction moratorium. Unanimously. Tonight, when they had the actual power to create one themselves, the coalition cracked in half.

The West recusal is the detail I'm still chewing on. She disclosed at the start of the item that she and her husband own five residential rental units in two South Portland buildings — direct financial conflict, sat out. That means one of the seven council members who passed that unanimous immigration resolution on February 3rd was voting to ask someone else to do something that would have directly cost her money if it happened locally. I'm noting it and letting people draw their own conclusions.

I've been tracking the ICE situation since January 12th. Tonight the police chief said on the record: ICE left South Portland the Friday night before the snowstorm and hasn't come back. His words: "my information is that they're not here." That's real — SPPD got fewer than a handful of calls the whole time ICE was operating. But the physical departure of ICE doesn't undo what happened. A speaker tonight described a family with young children who spent the surge hiding in their bedroom, kept their kids home, and are now behind on rent because a parent missed work shifts. Julia Edwards said there's a 6-year-old in her son's class who still isn't coming to school. The children are still out even after the agents are gone. The moratorium was supposed to be the bridge that gave those families time to stabilize. Without it, Project Home is the only mechanism left — and a speaker testified tonight that Project Home is projected to run out of funds in 10 to 11 days from February 17th. That's around February 27th.

On Tipton: her yes vote on the moratorium complicates the read I've had since December. I've been framing her as housing-and-development-first, indifferent to the school funding and immigrant family side of this crisis. She voted yes tonight. I'm not revising everything, but I'm noting the vote.

The Mahoney workshop ran until almost midnight and produced no binding decision — options from $57 million to $104 million, no vote. The city attorney cleared Scott's participation at the February 10 school board meeting on the record — statutory threshold doesn't apply to a spouse's employment. That's the kind of thing I need when someone challenges me in a thread.
