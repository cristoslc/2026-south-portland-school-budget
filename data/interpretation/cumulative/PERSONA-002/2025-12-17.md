# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 17, 2025

### Interpretation

After the frustration of watching the board spend $30K on a superintendent search during a spending freeze, this workshop finally gave me the data-rich budget discussion I have been waiting for. The enrollment numbers are now on the table and they are stark: elementary enrollment dropped 23% in four years -- from 1,401 to 1,080 students -- while the district added 82 staff positions. That is the root of our structural imbalance, laid bare in simple arithmetic. The board acknowledged it, which is progress.

The fund balance depletion I flagged after the December 8 meeting is confirmed again here, but now we also have the per-pupil cost comparison: $26,651, reportedly the highest among comparable districts. That number demands scrutiny. If it holds up, we do not just have an enrollment problem -- we have a fundamental efficiency problem baked into our cost structure. I want to see the methodology behind that comparison before drawing conclusions, but the direction is concerning.

What actually encouraged me was the board setting a 6% property tax increase ceiling instead of the 18-19% that a no-change budget would require. That is fiscal realism. It forces roughly $7.2 million in cuts, which is painful, but it is the responsible approach. The breakdown of the 78 position cuts -- 42 teachers, 16 ed techs, 14 facilities/food/transport, 2 administrators, 4 non-bargaining -- shows they are spreading the pain across categories rather than gutting one area.

The state funding picture adds important context: only about 20% of actual costs are covered by state funding when the formula assumes 55%, pushing 61% of the tax burden onto local property taxes. That is a structural problem beyond the district's control, but it does not excuse the local mismanagement of enrollment-to-staffing ratios. We need to fix what we can control.
