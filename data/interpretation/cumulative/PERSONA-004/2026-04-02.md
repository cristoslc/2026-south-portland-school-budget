# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

I've been tracking numbers since December, and the one that defines April 2 is twenty to thirty thousand dollars. That's what the big administrative restructuring — the DEI director to strategist, the assistant special ed director to instructional strategist — actually saved per swap. Not per year. Per role. Less than it costs to run one classroom. Holman said exactly what I felt: she expected money to be liberated when they cut those director positions, and instead the savings were so small Feller had to stand up and say out loud that we can't bring back the computer science department because there's simply nothing to bring it back with. Three and a half months of watching this board workshop and deliberate and produce nine reconfiguration models, and the administrative cost-cutting that was supposed to soften the blow to teaching positions saved a rounding error.

But here's where April 2 breaks from everything that came before it: the board didn't vote. After watching this governance structure move in one direction since December 10 — decide first, inform the people who do the work second — they stopped. The deferred budget vote is the first unscheduled pause I've seen in this entire process, and it happened because Connie DeSanto walked up to that microphone and announced three hundred thousand dollars in state money that union members went to Augusta and fought for. And then Claire Richardson said on the record that she wants teachers to get that money — no director positions. I wrote that down the moment she said it.

The structural trap Ketchem named on March 23 got confirmed again tonight: labor costs outpace the 6% ceiling annually, automatically, and a depleted fund balance means any under-budgeted line item becomes a personnel cut the following year. That's the mechanism. That's how we keep arriving at the same cliff. But the $750,000 in potential FY28 EPS formula changes now floating suggests the state picture may be shifting in ways the district hasn't priced in yet — and the board is meeting with the council before they vote, which means the April 14 workshop is no longer just a presentation but a live negotiation.

The behavioral specialist elimination got a non-answer. The class size policy is already cracking in real classrooms this year. And the budget is still open. April 7 and April 14 are not epilogue — they are the fight.
