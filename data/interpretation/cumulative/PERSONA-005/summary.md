# Cumulative Summary: Priya (PERSONA-005)

### Current Understanding

South Portland's FY27 school budget crisis has produced a confirmed $8.4 million structural gap — no longer proposed but real, with 72 pink slips issued March 18, 2026. The crisis is driven by both state underfunding (20% actual coverage against a 55% statutory obligation) and years of internal financial mismanagement that degraded equity-designated federal grants for ELL students and students with disabilities through serial finance director turnover. The district's TIF portfolio, extended as recently as February 2026, shelters assessed value from state formula calculations — a second-order harm that actively suppresses the education aid the district would otherwise receive while generating no affordability conditions for the public. The 78 position eliminations — now in motion — include 42 teachers, 16 educational technicians (frontline support for students with IEPs), and 14 food service, transportation, and facilities workers, with only 2 administrator positions cut. De facto segregation across elementary schools is confirmed by hard data: Skillin serves 60% BIPOC, 54% free/reduced lunch, and 33% multilingual learners, while Small serves fewer than 20% BIPOC and 6% multilingual learners. The administration attempted to close Skillin under equity framing; four board members rejected it by naming the racial politics directly. The community school model has been approved district-wide, and both governing bodies have formally endorsed immigration protection resolutions, but non-biased hiring training has been postponed twice while major equity-impacting decisions proceed. At the city level, the council has demonstrated three distinct failure modes for equity arguments — erasure, inversion, and weaponization — documented across the Montgomery testimony, the moratorium vote, and Matthews' no vote on Project Home funding. ICE enforcement has been a documented suppressor of school attendance and civic participation since January. Financial burdens across school cuts, housing instability, and compounding sewer rate increases of 22% per year for three years are converging on the same households — primarily immigrant, low-income, and communities of color concentrated in Red Bank and the west side of the city — and Priya intends to document that convergence with geographic precision.
