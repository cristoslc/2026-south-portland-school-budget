# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

She walked in knowing the vote had happened. What she didn't know was how completely the chair would close the door in the first sixty seconds. DeAngelos said it before anything else: the budget vote is not a lever. The city council cannot override the board's structural decision. Reconfiguration is a separate, final action and it is done. Rachel has been tracking this institution for sixteen weeks, long enough to recognize that opening statement for what it is — not information delivery, but expectation management. The chair knew parents were coming in hoping that tonight's budget discussion was still a mechanism. She made sure they understood it wasn't.

And yet the early release days came off the agenda unanimously. Parents pushed back after Monday and the board listened, at least on that one specific thing. It's the smallest possible concession and Rachel registers it as real — the board can hear pressure and respond to it — while understanding immediately that it changes nothing structural.

What the rest of the meeting delivered was the specific shape of what "done" actually means when nobody has done the work yet. Feller called it "an absolute information vacuum" and asked directly whether families should expect their children bused across the city. Dr. Prince said she didn't want to get ahead of the listening sessions — meaning there are no boundaries, no assignments, no answers. Rachel has been asking some version of this question since February 4th, when she first learned transportation hadn't been modeled. It is now April 2nd. The vote is final. The answer is still: we don't know, come to a session.

The IEP exchange is what sits with her most heavily. A therapist described ending her day in a restraint with her OT already cut. A board member asked directly what the specific plan is for children with IEPs. The answer was: we plan to talk to educators and parents. Board Member Richardson called that insufficient from the dais. It is insufficient. For Rachel — and for every family whose child depends on consistency, on relationship, on a known environment — "we'll talk to people" is not a transition plan. It is the absence of one, described in active voice.

The potential state money surfacing at the end — $300,000 confirmed, maybe $750,000 more — gives her nothing she can hold. Numbers that aren't confirmed don't put her daughter in a classroom she can name. The fight to prevent has ended. The fight over implementation has no map yet, which is itself the problem.
