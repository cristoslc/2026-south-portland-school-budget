# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

The timeline is locked. Order 161 went through on consent without a word — April 7 hearing, May 5 council vote, June 9 referendum. That's the process working exactly as it should, and in another year I'd take it as an unambiguous good sign. Tonight it felt more like the starting gun on a race we have to run through a minefield.

The sewer presentation was the one I had been dreading since January, when I first started trying to calculate what the cumulative homeowner impact actually looks like. Fred Dillon and CDM Smith put numbers to it: 22% annual increases for three straight years — FY27, FY28, FY29 — to finance Pearl Street and the dewatering upgrades. The average residential bill goes from $56 to over $112 a month. The infrastructure case is real, the pump station is nearly fifty years old, and Sanborn made the regional comparison argument competently. None of that changes what residents are going to feel when they walk into the June validation booth: sewer bill up, school budget up, public safety bond on the horizon in November. Since January 13th I've been asking what the combined homeowner number is. Tonight gave me the sewer component. The figure is bad.

Mahoney is off the November ballot — the council reached informal consensus targeting 2027, and the city manager summarized it cleanly. This is the relief I thought I had in January and then lost in February when the council snapped back to November 2026. This time it appears to be real. One fewer competing ask. I'm marking it but not celebrating it.

What I'm still processing is Matthews. He stood up during the Project Home vote and put "72 people in the school department got their pink slips yesterday" into the City Council minutes. He voted no, alone, on a $100,000 appropriation that the other six passed. I'm not saying he was wrong to raise the tension — the tension is real. But that exact framing — "why did the city spend money on X while cutting teachers?" — is the validation argument we are going to hear in June, and we need a factual answer for it before April 7. The public safety bond is still tracking for November. The Walgreens purchase passed unanimously tonight. The fiscal environment around our June ask is not getting simpler. My finance committee homework before the April 7 hearing is to build the full cumulative picture — every line of it — so we're not caught flat-footed when someone else does it first.
