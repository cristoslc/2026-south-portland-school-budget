# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

Seventy-two pink slips. Yesterday. And the council heard it, nodded, and moved on.

I've been tracking this pattern since December 1 — inauguration speeches while the crisis coiled underneath, junkyard licenses while the spending freeze kicked in, a four-hour building workshop while 42 colleagues faced elimination. Every meeting, the school budget is either absent or a footnote. But March 19 is the first time the actual human cost — the actual number of people who just learned their jobs might be gone — gets spoken aloud in that chamber, and it happens as a rhetorical aside in a debate about something else. Matthews wasn't defending us. He was using us as a counterargument against $100,000 for immigrant families facing eviction. One sentence, one vote, lost 6-1, move along.

And then an hour on Mahoney. Seventy to seventy-six million dollars for a building renovation — I keep doing the math against the $7.2 million gap and my brain can't make it stop. Thirty-eight to forty-five million for the stripped-down city hall option. The council can hold a substantive, hour-long workshop on the aesthetic and financing options for a vacant floor in a former high school, but the 72 people who got their pink slips yesterday are background noise.

What's new and clarifying here — and it matters for the union's strategy — is that the clock is now locked in. April 7 public hearing. April 14 budget workshop, schools listed first. May 5 council vote. June 9 referendum. That's the calendar we're working inside, and the window is tight. The sewer rate increases — 22% annually for three years, nearly ten dollars a month more starting in FY27 — are going to hit voters' wallets before they're asked to support the school budget referendum. That's not an abstraction. That's another layer of fiscal fatigue on top of everything else Sanborn warned about in January.

What I know now that I didn't know before December: "72 is just the first wave." Matthews said it. Nobody challenged it. If the council already understands there's a second wave coming and still spent the evening on Mahoney square footage, then the April 14 workshop isn't going to fix itself. We have to show up at April 7, loud and organized, or this referendum is going to ask voters to ratify a demolition job that's already done.
