# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

Rachel comes into the new year already primed. Three school board meetings in December left her with a clear picture: $7.2 million in cuts, 42 teachers gone, a board chair who views parent involvement as a nuisance, and an enrollment collapse that makes every elementary building vulnerable. She is no longer a worried parent watching from a distance. She is someone who has been doing homework.

So when Rosemary DeAngelos appears at the January 6th city council meeting and announces a public forum for January 22nd to get "citizen feedback on cost saving measures in the schools," Rachel's response is not gratitude for the transparency. It's recognition of a pattern. She has watched this board select a $30,000 superintendent search firm specifically to avoid community input in candidate selection. She has heard "community engagement" framed as a pain point to manage. A public forum where citizens are invited to help figure out what to cut is not democracy — it's a process designed to be pointed to later. "We asked for community input." What it is not is a process where the community actually decides anything.

The February 10th guidance workshop announcement lands differently — and harder. This is the meeting where the city council tells the school board what tax increase level is politically acceptable, which means it's the meeting that sets the ceiling for how much financial pain the schools have to absorb on their own. Rachel now understands that the conversations happening at city council aren't just parallel to the school board fights she's been tracking — they're upstream of them. Whatever number comes out of February 10th constrains every decision that follows. If the council says 2%, there is no scenario where the schools survive this intact. If they say 5 or 6%, maybe the math changes enough that upending hundreds of children's lives isn't "necessary" anymore.

The Mahoney City Center project getting a "dollar tag" and council direction next week reads as another data point in a pattern she started noticing in December, when she heard the Hamlin school site casually described as a housing parcel at a prior forum she hadn't been invited to. The city has development priorities. She is watching to see how they weigh against the small human beings currently enrolled in her children's schools.

January 22nd is now on her calendar in permanent marker. And she is bringing everyone she knows.
