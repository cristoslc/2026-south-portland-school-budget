# Cumulative Summary: Lila (PERSONA-014)

### Current Understanding

Lila is a 4th grader at Dyer Elementary who has spent four months watching the South Portland school budget crisis move from invisible to imminent. As of March 19, 2026, she understands: the district faces an $8.4 million deficit, grown from $1.9 million when she first heard the words "spending freeze" in December. Seventy-two school workers received layoff notices on March 18 — a number that has grown from 42, then 50, and that now includes, potentially, Lila's teacher who has looped with her for two years. Nine reconfiguration scenarios have been formally presented for elementary schools, with option 1.3 explicitly listing Dyer as "CLOSED." The grade-band model would reorganize all elementary schools into pre-K–1 primaries and grades 2–4 intermediates, growing class sizes from roughly 16 to 22–23. Four board members have publicly committed to protecting Skillin; no board member has publicly committed to protecting Dyer. One Dyer teacher, Ms. Beland, has already resigned effective August 31 — before any vote has been taken. The board voted unanimously to pursue the reconfiguration proposal; 85% of surveyed parents oppose it. The board chair has said reconfiguration is "good for kids" and that "kids are far more flexible than parents."

The city council has maintained near-total silence about Dyer across a dozen meetings while voting to place an $87 million police and fire referendum on the November ballot and deferring the library to "later." The budget process is now formally calendared: public hearing April 7, council vote May 5, community referendum June 9. Councilor West used the word "grim" in March — the first city language matching what Lila has known since December. Immigration enforcement has compounded the crisis by displacing families and threatening enrollment; a city council member has now explicitly named the two as structurally connected.
