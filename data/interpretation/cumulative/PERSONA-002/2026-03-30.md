# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

The board made the two hardest structural calls of the year tonight — Kaler closure 6 to 1, Option A reconfiguration 4 to 2 — and then the budget itself failed, 5 to 2. That result is genuinely new failure terrain for this process. Every problem I have been tracking since December has been about the district's inability or unwillingness to make hard decisions: the ceremonial silence before the deficit, the $30K superintendent search during a spending freeze, the unmodeled no-reconfiguration alternative. Tonight the board made the hard decisions. They still could not ratify the budget. The failure mode has shifted.

The DEI coordinator-to-strategist swap is the specific finding that stays with me. DeAngelis walked the room through the arithmetic in real time: if the recall list places a senior SPTA member in that role, the total cost equals or exceeds the prior configuration. The administration confirmed it. The bottom line did not move from last week's proposal. This is the pattern I have been watching for since December 8 gave us a $1.9M deficit nobody had tracked — the headline looks like a reduction, the footnote says otherwise. I cannot give the 23% director unit cut full credit until I know who fills that position. The $8K in cited savings is contingent on a recall outcome nobody controls.

The fund balance recommendation is now on its third meeting without a motion. Ketchem named the absent minimum threshold policy as a primary guardrail failure in March 23's root-cause analysis. The March 30 slide deck repeats the recommendation. No dollar target, no timeline, no vote. Chair DeAngelis made the analytically correct point that city council supplemental funding is a loan requiring future tax repayment — it seeds the reserve, it does not erase the obligation. That argument is right. It did not land. We are heading into Thursday with a board that universally acknowledges the reserve needs to be rebuilt and has produced no mechanism to do it in three successive workshops.

Feller's per-pupil comparison — Gorham at $20,000, Windham at $21,600, Brunswick at $24,000, Portland with twice the enrollment spending less per pupil than South Portland — is the first time this figure has appeared in a meeting with peer data attached. The thread I opened in December is partially closed. But the methodology question is still live. I need to know what share of South Portland's premium over Gorham reflects structural costs — five buildings, the prior staffing ratio — versus programming choices. Without that decomposition, the number is a talking point. The direction is confirmed. The cause is not.

Thursday is the decision meeting. Six days after an emotionally exhausting five-hour workshop, with a city council presentation locked in for April 7 regardless of outcome, the board needs to find a landing zone that did not visibly exist when the meeting adjourned.
