### Interpretation

This meeting represents a governance crisis that cuts to the core of everything I've worked to build as a board member. We've gone from what the finance director called a "rosy scenario" just last month to facing a potential $1.9 million deficit, and the reason is inexcusable: we never properly budgeted for the teacher contract increases we negotiated and approved. This isn't just a financial miscalculation—it's a fundamental breakdown in the budget process I'm supposed to oversee.

The immediate spending freeze is necessary but painful, and what terrifies me most is learning we've exhausted our fund balance safety net. We're operating without any cushion for the unexpected costs that always come—special education needs, facilities emergencies, energy spikes. One major unplanned expense and we're in real trouble. This is exactly the kind of fiscal mismanagement that destroys public trust and makes validation votes fail.

But there are some bright spots emerging. The new board members, particularly Tyler Smith, are asking the right operational questions about how our decisions affect classrooms and teachers. They seem to understand that governance means digging into details, not just rubber-stamping recommendations. And that parent presentation about community resources opened my eyes to revenue opportunities we've been leaving on the table. We have $46,000 in organic donations flowing in, and she's essentially offering to help us tap into much more through coordinated foundation work.

The path forward requires both immediate damage control and longer-term strategic thinking. We need to understand exactly how our budget projections failed so catastrophically, implement better financial oversight processes, and simultaneously accelerate our community partnership development. We can't keep lurching from crisis to crisis while missing opportunities to diversify our revenue base.
