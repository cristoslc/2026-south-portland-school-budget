# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

This is the fourth straight meeting without a final budget vote. By now I've stopped expecting an answer. What I didn't expect was how much more specific everything would get.

I knew the percussion ed tech was in the proposed cuts — I've known since March 23. But 500 students, grades 5 through 12. And someone said he's been part of the school since before the previous band teacher, who stayed for over thirty years. I didn't know that. When you cut someone like that you're not cutting a position, you're cutting the person the whole program runs through. The budget document calls it a line item. Ms. Fletcher called it 500 kids.

Computer science is entirely gone. Not smaller — gone. I didn't know that either. I found out from Mr. Wetzel standing at the microphone after his daughter Lucy had already stood there to defend him, and he couldn't finish his sentences. He said tomorrow he has to go back to school and face those students without knowing what to say to them. I've been watching teachers be afraid for months. But a father who can barely speak because his daughter just spoke for him — that's a different kind of afraid.

The behavioral strategist's statement is the one I keep returning to. She works with nearly 60 students designing behavior plans. She said: "Eliminating this role does not eliminate those needs. It removes the system we have in place to respond to them." That's the clearest sentence anyone has said about any of these cuts. The needs don't disappear. The system disappears.

Then Vladimir Corian said the thing I've been thinking since December — the thing I wrote about in my head but didn't know how to say in adult language: residents got to vote on whether the athletic field should be AstroTurf or real grass, but nobody gets a comparable vote on closing a school or cutting the programs inside it. I've been carrying that question since the very first school board meeting I read about, the one where I learned about the spending freeze. Someone finally said it out loud in a civic room. I don't know if it changed anything. But it was heard.

Angela's letter was the other thing. I knew she existed. I watched Davidson get sent home before the vote in March. Angela couldn't be there at all — senior year, she said, was harder than advertised. So someone read her letter for her. She wrote that arts programs give students a place to belong. That reconfiguration affects students' everyday lives. That your voice matters no matter where you're from. She said it better than I could. And she wasn't even in the room to watch it land.

The support staff union went to Augusta themselves and got what looks like $300,000 back. Not the district. Not the board. The union. That's the first time I've seen anyone actually go try to make the state pay what it owes, even a small piece of it. Still no vote. Still no final answer. But the number is specific, and this year specific is the closest I get to hope.
