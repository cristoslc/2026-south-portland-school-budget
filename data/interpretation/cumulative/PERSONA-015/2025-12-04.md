# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

Three days after the inaugural warmth, Sheanna is back in the room — and the room has changed. This is not a ceremonial night. This is the city's actual operating machinery, and what she watches unfold is the gap between the values language and the budget reality closing fast.

Steinbrink's public comment lands on her the way the multilingual speakers did on December 1 — viscerally, personally. Someone stood up at a city council meeting and named the Somali American community's need to feel safe and at home, and she recognizes that energy as the same thing she is trying to build every day at three different buildings. It confirms something she felt but couldn't be sure about: this community's stated values aren't just for inaugurations.

But the committee budget debate is where December 4 becomes a different kind of meeting for her. On the surface it's procedural noise — how do volunteer committees request operating funds. In practice it is Councilor Walker flagging out loud that the Human Rights Commission, which has historically needed around $10,000 to run its equity programming, will now be capped at $2,500 per request unless it can justify more through a separate process. And then Matthews says it plainly: *things are tight, we may have to tell everybody no.* That sentence is the one she carries home. The city is already signaling constraint — out loud, on the record — in the same cycle where the school district is quietly building toward a vote on 78 positions. This is no longer a school problem. It is a city-wide fiscal posture, and the equity-facing bodies are already getting squeezed first.

The Zoom public comment discussion ties all of it together in the worst way. She understands the First Amendment argument. She doesn't dispute it. But what she sees underneath it is the participation gap her families live inside — the working second-shift parent, the multilingual family that took the bus from Portland, the mother who can't get childcare for a 6:30 meeting. The council names this problem clearly and then moves to the tree lighting. And now Sheanna has a sharper version of the Kaler thread she opened three days ago: it is not enough to worry whether Jean Tam's family knows about reconfiguration. The structural question is whether there is any pathway for them to show up and be heard even if they do.
