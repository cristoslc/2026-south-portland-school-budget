# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Goal Setting Workshop -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

The goal-setting workshop is where I expected South Portland's elected leadership to put their priorities on the record — and the publicly available agenda does exactly that, just not in the way anyone should be proud of. "Annual Goal-Setting Session." One item. An attachment I cannot see. No equity commitments. No mention of the 78 positions about to be eliminated. No mention of the students who will absorb those cuts. The city council is setting goals for the year and the word "equity" does not appear in the visible record. This is January 15. Children are already staying home because their families fear ICE enforcement. The budget crisis is already in motion. And the council's public-facing goal-setting document is a placeholder.

I keep returning to the number sixteen. Sixteen ed tech positions on the elimination list. These are not abstract line items — they are the people sitting next to kids with IEPs all day, the ones who make inclusion mean something beyond policy language. Lumping them into the same category as facilities staff treats disability equity infrastructure as interchangeable overhead. Fourteen food service and transportation positions are also on the list, which means the district is proposing to cut the school meal programs that are a primary food source for students experiencing poverty — and doing it without a word about which families depend on those services or what alternatives exist.

The state funding gap is the structural villain here, and it deserves naming: 20% actual coverage against a 55% statutory obligation is not a local management failure, it is a systemic abandonment of public education by Augusta. But the state's culpability does not absolve the district of the decisions it still controls — which positions to cut, which schools bear the burden, which students lose their support staff. Forty-two teacher positions represent the largest single cut category, and without a breakdown by school, grade level, or ELL endorsement status, I cannot assess the equity distribution. That data gap is not accidental. I have been watching this district avoid disaggregated analysis since December, and I know by now that the absence of data is itself a finding.

The per-pupil spending figure of $26,651 — highest among comparable districts — should be an asset to defend. But if that spending is not reaching Skillin's 60% BIPOC population or Kaler's multilingual families or the students with IEPs who are about to lose sixteen of their support staff, then it is a premium paid to maintain a system that was never built for them. I am filing public records requests this week.
