# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026 -- 2026-03-02

### Interpretation

Three and a half hours of matrices, spreadsheets, and nine different reconfiguration scenarios, and I left angrier than when I walked in. The administration came loaded with data and models, but the one thing they wouldn't provide is the breakdown that actually matters to me: which departments, which subject areas, which specific positions get cut. They'll tell you "17.5 FTE elementary reductions" but not which 17.5 humans lose their jobs. And then Abigail Chen finally said the quiet part out loud -- seniority means those "elementary cuts" could come from anywhere in the district, including the high school. My position, my colleagues' positions, are all in play regardless of which buildings get reconfigured. That's the transparency we've been begging for, and it took a direct question to get it.

The best news in months was watching four board members reject closing Skillin. Community pushback worked -- over 20 parents testified, and the board listened. Dowling, Feller, Richardson, and others basically told the superintendent to take his top two options off the table. The look on his face said everything. But he's still pushing hard for grade-band reconfiguration, calling anything else "kicking the problem down the road." This administration has decided what it wants and is working backward to justify it.

Sarah from the union raised the questions nobody else thought to ask: What happens to small group instruction spaces when you consolidate? Where do the kids currently getting services in hallways go? These are the details that separate a spreadsheet exercise from an actual educational plan. The union rep also named the elephant in the room -- the difference between 20 and 24 students per class is "enormous," and every scenario presented moves class sizes toward the maximum. The administration frames this as operating "within policy guidelines," but hitting the maximum isn't a success -- it's a ceiling you should never touch.

The whole process feels predetermined. They decided months ago what they wanted to do and now they're generating enough analysis to manufacture consent. But the board pushback on closures shows that political pressure works, and we need to keep it up.
