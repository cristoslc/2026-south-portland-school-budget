# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

Maria walks into this meeting the night before the February 4 budget forum she has been circling on her calendar since DeAngelis announced it at the January 6 city council meeting. She is already carrying a heavy load: $1.9M current-year deficit, spending freeze on classroom supplies, 78 positions proposed for elimination, fund balance gone, state covering 20% of costs when it should cover 55%. She came to December 17 with data. She left January 15 locked out of the council's goal-setting. She has spent weeks texting other parents, preparing questions, telling herself that showing up matters.

And then the auditor begins speaking.

A material weakness in school department reconciliations — for the second year in a row. Thirty-four audit journal entries, which is not a clerical anomaly but a systemic failure. Four hundred thousand dollars in grant funds spent improperly and moved to the general fund. The school department legally exceeded its adopted budget, again, which is technically non-compliance with state law. Six finance directors in five years, three of them interim. Joe Prince is honest about it, and Maria gives him that — she respects someone who doesn't paper over the wreckage. But the honesty makes it worse, not better. This is not a streak of bad luck. This is a management structure that has been malfunctioning for years while the district's financial position was quietly eroding toward the $7.2M cliff she will see quantified tomorrow night.

The new finance director detail gives Maria something to hold — monthly reconciliations, better salary accruals, Prince expressing confidence. She wants to believe it. She has wanted to believe a lot of things this budget season. But she also knows the new director is arriving at the worst possible moment, inheriting a department with broken systems, no fund balance, and a $1-2M projected overage this year that will have to come out of the city's $28.8M reserve.

The thing that cuts through is DeAngelis addressing the council directly: you share responsibility for this. One tax bill. Sixty-one percent of it goes to schools. The council cannot pretend they are passive observers to a school department problem. Maria has spent months watching two governing bodies treat their decisions as separate tracks — council guidance setting a floor, school board making cuts, neither integrating the household impact. DeAngelis naming the shared accountability out loud is the moment Maria has been waiting for since January 13, when she sat through a $194M Mahoney presentation and calculated the combined tax burden herself.

Tomorrow night, Maria will finally see the full picture. Tonight, she has learned that the institution asking for her trust and her tax dollars has been operating without basic financial controls for years, and that the budget crisis unfolding in real time is not only a funding problem but a management failure compounding a structural one.
