# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The budget still isn't adopted. I walked in expecting April 2 to lock everything in — two prior rejections already, March 23 adjourned without voting, March 30 failed five to two — and tonight the board defers again, this time unanimously, to seek council guidance before finalizing anything. Connie DeSanto announces during public comment, via a text received in the room, that South Portland is likely to receive approximately $300,000 in state funding for homeless and economically disadvantaged populations — money our people lobbied for in Augusta. Richardson immediately says it belongs to teachers and staff. Feller agrees. That's on the record now, and we'll cite it from here until June.

But $300,000 is less than four percent of the gap. What matters is the pause it bought, not the arithmetic it solves.

What crystallized tonight is something I've suspected since December 10, when the board paid $24,000 to a superintendent search firm that recommended keeping community out of candidate screening: the administrative right-sizing — the director restructuring framed all spring as institutional shared sacrifice — saved approximately $20,000 to $30,000. Finance Director Ketchen said it directly. Richardson and Holman both walked in expecting that director-level cuts would free money for classroom positions. They didn't. Forty colleagues on recall lists, and the administration reorganized some central office titles and produced the equivalent of one aide's salary. Richardson said she couldn't see any real concession at the director level. That's now on the public record, not just a suspicion shared in hallways.

Jenna Walsh's statement — read by someone else because Jenna herself received a RIF notice — quantified what the behavior strategist elimination means: sixty students directly supported, forty-plus formal behavior plans, daily presence across four elementary buildings. When the board pressed on who absorbs that work, the administration cited BCBAs and instructional strategists — categories, not people with daily multi-building presence. Stacy Lauren ended her day in a physical restraint with her OT as sole backup and told this board so directly. These are the conditions heading into next fall, with larger classes and fewer people to call.

Sarah Gay put the necessary thing on the record: reconfiguration has generated several dozen items requiring meet-and-consult, many requiring actual bargaining. That work must be compensated or the union will not accept responsibility for cost overruns that result from this administration's failure to plan. And Abby Anderson added the harder warning: restoring positions based on who lobbied loudest is not a process. We need defined criteria before any restoration. Our credibility in this fight depends on holding that line.
