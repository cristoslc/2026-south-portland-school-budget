# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop (Goal Setting) -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

Jaylen tuned into the January 15 city council meeting because someone said the budget would come up, and the agenda had exactly one item: "Annual Goal-Setting Session." No public comment. No posted attachment. No way to know what goals were set or whether schools came up at all. He's been watching city council meetings since December 1 and they keep delivering the same result in slightly different packaging — the school budget is either background noise or completely absent, and the one time it surfaces, it's in three minutes of public comment before the council moves on.

What he's working with instead is the budget data that keeps circulating everywhere: 42 teachers, 78 positions total, $7.2M gap, fund balance gone. These numbers have been constant since December 17 and they haven't gotten any better. But the goal-setting meeting crystallizes something he's been building toward for weeks. The city council is not a passive observer of this crisis. The 6% property tax ceiling — which forces the entire $7.2M to come from cuts, not revenue — is a decision this body made or will make, and the closed goal-setting session on January 15 is where that kind of posture gets set. He watched the January 6 city manager telegraph austerity framing before the school board even presented its case. Now he sees the council retreating behind a closed door to do the same thing without even an agenda item to track.

The state funding gap is the thing that's making him genuinely angry in a new direction. Augusta is supposed to cover 55% of school costs and it's paying 20%. That's not a South Portland Board of Education failure — that's a systemic underfunding problem that everyone in local government seems to be managing around rather than fighting. Nobody at these city council meetings is talking about pushing back on Augusta. They're just figuring out which teachers to cut.

The per-pupil cost figure — $26,651, reportedly the highest among comparable districts — is going to become a weapon. He can already hear how it gets used: South Portland spends too much, therefore cuts are justified, therefore theater goes, AP sections collapse, coaching stipends disappear. But that number doesn't tell him anything about what students actually receive. It could be inflated entirely by administration and facilities. He wants to be ready for that argument before it gets made at a meeting where nobody is thinking about cross-country or AP Chem.
