# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

The day after watching the school board splinter over the 6% ceiling, Ben attends the joint meeting where the city council weighs in -- and the political math gets even harder. Council guidance ranges from 3-6% school tax increases, with Councilor Walker alone supporting 6%+ while veteran Councilor Matthews recalls never exceeding 3% in his 12 school board years. The school board's already-cracking 6% consensus now faces external pressure to go lower, which means even deeper cuts.

Interim Superintendent Entwistle lays out the three options with a clarity that previous presentations lacked: Option A (status quo) means 19% tax increase; Option B (6% increase) still leaves a $3.5M gap; Option C (9.8% increase) still requires $1.1M in cuts. There is no option that avoids both significant tax increases and significant cuts. When Dr. Prince tells the council that the 3-4% range most councilors prefer would mean "upwards of probably 50 staff reductions," the human cost of the political consensus becomes concrete. Ben now has the framing that connects board politics to classroom consequences.

The fund balance situation is worse than Ben previously understood. School fund balance stands at just $739K against $850K budgeted, essentially zero in functional terms. And if schools run a deficit, city taxpayers will temporarily cover it from the city fund balance -- a detail Ben suspects most taxpayers do not know. The city's own budget pressures compound the problem: 10% health insurance increases, rising solid waste costs, and two ARPA-funded positions sunsetting, with department requests currently totaling a 15% tax increase before schools are even added.

The elementary reconfiguration -- now presented to the council as a $2M solution creating three intermediate and two primary schools -- draws pushback from parents who see through the euphemism. Ben's December 10th observation about a district that prioritizes aspirational framing over fiscal honesty continues to hold: "reconfiguration" is the word; "ending neighborhood schools" is the reality. Chair DeAngelo's frustration with council members who say "do your best" instead of giving concrete guidance captures the dysfunction of the entire system: everyone wants cuts, nobody wants to own which ones.
