# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- December 8, 2025 -- 2025-12-08

### Interpretation

Eight days into this budget season, Sheanna has now watched three consecutive meetings and the arc is becoming visible to her in a way it isn't to most people in those rooms. The inauguration gave her equity language from DeAngelos and a vague promise of shared purpose. The city council meeting confirmed the fiscal squeeze is municipal-wide and that participation gaps are real and structurally unaddressed. And now the school board — the body that actually controls her professional world and her children's school assignments — finally said the word she has been waiting years to hear.

Holman named redistricting. Called it by name, tied it explicitly to the Boundaries and Configurations Committee work, and the chair affirmed it in the same breath. For someone who drives between three buildings every day carrying the operational evidence of why reconfiguration matters — the MTSS wait lists, the specialist travel time, the uneven programming — this is not a small thing. She has been making this argument in letters and public comment while watching committees study and defer. Hearing a board member say *pick back up where we left off* and watching the chair point at a specific incoming member to carry it forward is the first concrete institutional signal that the structural conversation might actually happen.

But she cannot separate that from what DeAngelos said earlier in the same meeting: *equity is not about moving kids around to make things look even.* Sheanna has heard that framing weaponized before — as a way to do professional development instead of redistricting, to invest in teachers instead of changing enrollment patterns, to leave the inequitable geography intact while calling it a values choice. She genuinely doesn't know which version of that the chair means, and until the policy committee produces something concrete, she has to hold both possibilities simultaneously.

What she is certain about is the PTA conversation. No ambiguity there. A board member asked whether PTAs could fill the spending freeze gap, the superintendent said yes, and not one person in that room noted that PTA capacity is radically unequal across the district's buildings. Sheanna knows this firsthand — she works in those buildings — and the district just floated "ask your PTA" as an official response to a crisis without acknowledging it turns her observed inequities into codified policy. Matthews's "we may have to tell everybody no" from four days ago now has a district-level companion: *we may have to tell your PTA to handle it.* She is drafting a response tonight, because if nobody else has the cross-building view, she does.
