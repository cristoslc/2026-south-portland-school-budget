# Cumulative Summary: Linda (PERSONA-007)

### Current Understanding

Linda is a school board member who has watched South Portland's fiscal crisis compound from pre-crisis silence in December through a fully exposed structural emergency. The district faces a $7.2M structural deficit driven by a staffing-enrollment mismatch — 82 positions added while 300 students were lost — and the fund balance is functionally zero ($739K with $850K budgeted for drawdown). All FY26 scenarios now project deficits. The February 10 joint workshop finally produced concrete council guidance (3–6%, most gravitating toward 4–6%), but a $3.5M+ gap remains even under the 6% ceiling after the three "big ideas," board cohesion is fraying (Feller has publicly broken from the ceiling), and the health insurance projection could shift further before final adoption.

Linda's understanding of the revenue environment has been structurally revised since December. She now grasps that rising local valuations automatically reduce state education subsidy via the 50-cent capture rate, making development a much weaker relief valve than assumed, and that the state coverage gap (20% actual vs. 55% formula target) is structurally baked in rather than merely political. The November 2026 validation campaign faces a confirmed concurrent Mahoney city bond referendum, plus sewer rate increases and existing GO debt — and no one has publicly aggregated the combined homeowner impact.

Two late-emerging risks complicate planning further: LD 1829's state-mandated housing density could generate student population growth on a timeline that undermines the closure decisions currently being defended, and ICE enforcement is depleting enrollment from the most vulnerable communities, creating a per-pupil state subsidy pipeline risk that neither governance body has named publicly. The board has converged on closing Kaler/Dyer or Small rather than the administration-recommended Skillin, but implementation feasibility for September 2026 is unconfirmed, community engagement is badly lagging (only 12% of parents felt adequately informed), and special education staffing losses against an expanded Pre-K mandate are the emerging next crisis. Three more budget workshops in March and a city council workshop on April 14 define the path ahead.
