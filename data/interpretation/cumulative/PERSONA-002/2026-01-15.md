# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Goal-Setting Workshop -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

Two days after watching the council adjourn without giving the Mahoney architect a budget ceiling, I was looking at the January 15 agenda hoping to see whether the city's annual goal-setting would finally grapple with the fiscal reality the school district disclosed five weeks earlier. What I got instead was the most frustrating thing possible for someone who relies on primary sources: a one-line agenda item — "Annual Goal-Setting Session — *this item contains an attachment*" — and no attachment.

The goals the council sets here matter. They are not housekeeping. Whatever language they agree on tonight — whether it names fiscal discipline, tax ceiling targets, capital priorities, or community investment — will show up as the implicit frame in every subsequent budget presentation between now and June. The February 10 joint guidance session is twenty-six days away. The council is about to sit across from the school board and set the tax parameters that determine whether the district cuts $7.2 million or $9 million. The goals they established on January 15 are the foundation for that conversation, and I cannot see them.

This is the same pattern I have been tracking since December 1, but now it takes a specific form I have not encountered before. The prior silences — the inaugural ceremony one week before the deficit disclosure, the routine agenda on December 4, the ninety seconds of public comment on December 9 — were all omissions. Nobody said anything about the budget. This is different: the record explicitly acknowledges a document exists and then does not provide it. That is not a gap. That is a wall.

The workshop format compounds it. No formal comment period, thinner minutes, reduced documentation requirements. The council is setting the priorities that will constrain every hard budget decision downstream, and they are doing it in the format that produces the least public accountability. I am going to try a public records request, but the fact that I need to do that — for the goals a city council sets in January — is itself the finding. A district where 61% of property taxes go to schools, mid-crisis, should not have its governing framework set in a session the public cannot reconstruct. I need that attachment.
