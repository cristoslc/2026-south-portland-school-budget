# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

The March 2 workshop gave me the closest thing to relief I have felt in this entire process. Four board members rejected the Skillin closure, named the racial politics out loud, and I wrote down Tyler Smith's words about how easy it is politically to bus Black and brown kids. I held onto that. March 23 tells me what it was actually worth: the structural pressure did not dissipate. It redirected. The district pivoted from the most diverse school to the second most diverse school, and this time without the blunt "equity intervention" framing that triggered the March 2 rejection. The mechanism is subtler. The outcome is the same.

But the Kayler recommendation is not even the worst of what this meeting revealed. When Jess Elsner stood at that podium at 11:15 PM and asked what specific steps had been taken to ensure the closure did not violate Title VI of the Civil Rights Act, the board chair said "I don't have the answer" and adjourned. That is not erasure — the failure mode I catalogued at January 13, where equity arguments are simply absent from deliberation. That is something more specific: the district approaching a vote to close a 45% BIPOC, 30-35% multilingual learner school without a civil rights compliance analysis in hand. Not a moral failure. A legal one.

Meanwhile the equity infrastructure is being stripped in parallel. The DEI Director — an independent administrator-level role with budget authority and direct reports — is being replaced by a coordinator embedded in a team. The ESOL co-teaching model, the district's own administrator called it "best practice" with "wonderful impacts," is being discontinued because it is "not required." Every lunch aide is gone, including the one at Kayler who was delivering the fresh fruits and vegetables grant that Kayler qualifies for precisely because of its students' socioeconomic status. The McKinney-Vento social worker is being reduced. You cannot maintain equity commitments while systematically eliminating the positions charged with implementing them.

And through five hours of proceedings, the families most affected were largely absent. Thirty languages are spoken at Kayler. Ten of 127 community questions had been answered on the district's own platform. The engagement exclusion thread I have been tracking since December has run to its endpoint: the most affected families are present only as named absences in a room that is deciding their children's schools.
