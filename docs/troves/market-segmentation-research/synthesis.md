# Synthesis: Market Segmentation and Market Research Techniques

## Key Findings

### What Market Segmentation Is

Market segmentation divides a broad potential market into subsets of consumers who share common needs, interests, and priorities — enabling more targeted, effective communication and resource allocation. It differs from customer segmentation (which categorizes existing customers) by focusing on potential market opportunities. Every source agrees that segmentation's value lies in enabling organizations to understand specific audiences at a granular level.

### The Core Segmentation Types

All sources converge on a consistent taxonomy:

| Type | What it groups by | Best for |
|------|------------------|---------|
| **Demographic** | Age, income, education, family status | Broad audience profiling |
| **Geographic** | Location, region, climate | Locally differentiated offerings |
| **Behavioral** | Purchase patterns, usage, loyalty | Action-driven marketing |
| **Psychographic** | Values, lifestyle, motivations | Emotional and identity-based appeals |
| **Firmographic** | Company size, industry, revenue (B2B) | Business-to-business targeting |
| **Needs-Based** | Problems seeking solutions | Product-market fit |
| **Intent-Based** | Strong purchase signals | Conversion-stage marketing |

### The Research Process

Sources agree on a two-phase research approach:

1. **Qualitative first** — focus groups, in-depth interviews, and observation to understand motivations and identify the right variables
2. **Quantitative follow-through** — large-scale surveys (minimum n=1,200 for 6 segments) analyzed with factor analysis and cluster analysis to size and validate segments

A priori segmentation (predetermined factors: age, gender, usage rate) is fast and easy but less nuanced. Post-hoc segmentation (derived from analysis) is richer and more actionable but requires more resources.

### Analytical Techniques

- **Cluster analysis**: groups respondents who are most similar within segments and most different between segments
- **Factor analysis**: reduces many variables to underlying dimensions
- **RFM analysis** (Recency, Frequency, Monetary value): behavior-based segmentation for existing customers
- **Machine learning / AI**: increasingly used to process large datasets for dynamic, real-time segmentation

## Points of Agreement

- **Segment count**: 4–8 segments is the consensus ideal, with 5–6 being optimal. Fewer creates unwieldy groups; more creates impractical overlap.
- **Actionability requirement**: Segments must be targetable using real-world identifiers — a beautiful segment that cannot be reached is useless.
- **Naming matters**: Descriptive segment names (e.g., "budget-conscious first-time buyers") create mental clarity for implementation teams.
- **Hybrid methods**: Qualitative + quantitative together produce better results than either alone. Qualitative informs survey design; quantitative validates and sizes.
- **Test before scaling**: Pilot campaigns should validate segmentation before full rollout.

## Points of Disagreement / Nuance

- **How to weight CLV**: Prelaunch emphasizes customer lifetime value (CLV) heavily, noting a 25% profitability increase for CLV-focused segmentation. Omnisend and Brandspeak treat it as one consideration among many.
- **Privacy emphasis**: 2024–2025 sources increasingly emphasize privacy compliance and ethical data use as a constraint, while older frameworks treat it as a footnote. The newer guidance is that transparency and consumer trust are competitive differentiators, not just compliance obligations.

## Gaps

- **Non-commercial applications**: All sources assume a commercial context (selling products/services). The techniques are broadly applicable to civic, educational, and public-interest contexts — understanding which community members are most concerned about school budgets, for instance — but this application is not discussed.
- **Small-population segmentation**: Minimum sample sizes of n=200 per segment assume a large market. For small-community contexts (a city of 25,000), these methods require adaptation.
- **Longitudinal validity**: Sources describe point-in-time segmentation but do not address how often to re-segment as community values and circumstances change.

## Application to This Project

For a school budget communication project:
- **Demographic segmentation** can identify voters by presence of school-age children, property ownership, and income
- **Psychographic segmentation** reveals underlying values (community investment vs. tax burden concern)
- **Behavioral segmentation** identifies who attended budget forums, who contacted elected officials, and who voted in the last school budget referendum
- **Needs-based segmentation** helps identify which community members are concerned about specific programs (special education, arts, athletics, college prep)

Qualitative research (focus groups or interviews with community members) would reveal which concerns are most salient before designing survey instruments or communication strategies.
