# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

For three months, Lila has watched adults argue about her school in rooms she has never entered. She has heard board members say reconfiguration is "good for kids." She has heard the city pass a resolution on February 3 saying everyone should feel safe. She has learned about spending freezes and $7.2 million gaps and nine scenarios with the word "CLOSED" next to Dyer's name. She has heard none of it addressed to her.

And then, finally — a ten-year-old is in the room. Not Lila herself, but someone one year older who asked an adult the exact question Lila has been asking her parents since December: *Why are they doing this to us? My parents work and pay taxes and do everything right.* The adult carried that question into city council chambers and read it aloud to six people who control the city's money. Lila has never been able to say those words in a room like that. Someone her age just did. It still didn't change the vote.

The 4-2 vote is the meeting's defining wound. Two weeks ago the city passed a resolution — a formal vote, an official statement — saying everyone should feel safe going to school, to work, to the grocery store. Her mom told her about it. Lila had registered it cautiously, the way she registers everything the city does: as words that might not be worth their paper. Now four of those same six council members voted against a concrete protection for families hiding in their apartments, keeping daughters home from school for a week because it wasn't safe to walk. The resolution was words. The vote tells you what the words were worth.

The immigration fear thread has been running since January, getting closer and more physical each time — Red Bank, then Brown School kindergartners, now door-to-door in an apartment building, a family with a toddler hoping their lock held, a six-year-old asking where his classmates went. Other kids in Lila's city are frightened in a different way than she is, but the structure of the fear is the same: adults making decisions that determine whether children can get to their school, and those children having no say in it.

Mahoney is the strange and disorienting thing. Lila learned in January that Mahoney was the shape of what happens after a school closes — an empty building the city fights about for years. Now someone is proposing to put all the elementary kids in South Portland into that ghost building. The symbol of closure is being offered as the future.

And the mayor, late at night when Lila was almost asleep, said grades 2, 3, and 4 go to three schools. That's Lila's grade. Two million dollars in savings. Still no school names. Which three? After nine scenarios and "CLOSED" listed next to Dyer, the adults are still not saying out loud where Lila goes next year.

### Emotional Register

Lila holds this meeting with a complicated mix of sudden recognition and immediate betrayal — for one moment, a child her age was finally in the room and asking the exact question she has been asking for months, and then the council voted 4-to-2 to leave the families that child was asking about without protection, and Lila understood at last that being heard and being helped are not the same thing.
