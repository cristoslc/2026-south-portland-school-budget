# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

Five hours and seven minutes. Midnight, no Mahoney decision. But I have to give credit where it's due before I get to that.

The insurance switch was real money: a hundred thousand dollars up front plus fifteen thousand a year, just by comparing the state's PFML rate against a private insurer. That's what good fiscal management looks like. Small, but it happened, and it happened because someone actually shopped around.

The eviction moratorium vote was the other thing I'll hold onto. The city wanted to freeze all landlord-tenant legal proceedings citywide — not just ICE-affected cases, but every tenant and landlord — because writing it narrowly was legally "very difficult." The Rental Housing Alliance warned it would double an already eight-to-ten week eviction timeline and signal to housing investors that South Portland can set aside lease contracts whenever the council declares hardship. The city manager confirmed, when pressed, that nothing prevented this coming back in July if ICE returned. Four of the six voting councilors said no. Councilor Scott put it cleanly: you don't fix one group's problem by loading the cost onto someone who didn't cause it. That's exactly the principle I've been applying to the school budget all winter, and it's the first time I've heard it come back at me from the council floor.

That vote forces me to revise something I've been saying since January. The council is not uniformly a body that talks tough and then folds. On the moratorium — a direct property-rights question where the cost-shifting was explicit — four of them held the line. That's different from the Mahoney pattern. I should keep those two things separate in my head.

Then Mahoney. The council sent the design team back in January demanding bare bones alternatives. The answer came back: fifty-seven million dollars is the floor. No library, no community spaces, minimal build-out. Because there is no such thing as a bare bones approach — the moment you change a hundred-year-old building's use, ADA, life safety, structural, and energy codes all trigger simultaneously. You can't pick and choose. So the council's January verbal rejection of $194 million produced a $57-to-$105 million range, and they adjourned past midnight without binding themselves to any number in it. That pattern I know cold.

One man in the audience — Edward, Romano Road — said during the PFML discussion exactly what I've been saying all winter: Augusta passes mandates without counting what they cost property owners, calls South Portland a rich community, shortchanges our education funding, but the mandates land here the same as everywhere else. Nice to hear someone else say it to the council's face.
