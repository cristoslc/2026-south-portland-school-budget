# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 17, 2025

### Interpretation

The scale of what's happening has gotten much bigger and much scarier. What started as a $1.9 million shortfall and a spending freeze has now ballooned into a $7.2 million budget gap, 78 jobs being cut -- including 42 teachers and 16 ed techs -- and the possibility of an 18-19% tax increase if nothing changes. Lila can't comprehend numbers this large, but she can read the room: every adult who talks about these numbers looks worried, and that tells her everything she needs to know.

The enrollment decline -- 300 fewer elementary students in four years -- gives her a new way to understand why Dyer might close. It's not that there's something wrong with her school specifically. There are just fewer kids everywhere. Her class is smaller this year and now she understands why. But understanding the reason doesn't make it feel any better. If anything, it makes it worse -- this isn't a problem anyone can fix by trying harder or raising money. The kids just aren't there anymore.

The most frightening revelation is that there's no backup money left. Lila's mom always tells her to save her allowance for emergencies, and learning that the district didn't do that -- or couldn't -- shakes her sense that the adults have things under control. Combined with the board spending $30,000 they didn't have just a week ago, her fragile hope that donations might save Dyer now seems almost silly against a $7.2 million gap. The $780 from Hannaford and $934 from the photo company that she was clinging to as signs of hope are practically nothing compared to what the district needs.

The state funding gap -- covering only 20% when it should cover 55% -- is beyond her comprehension, but she picks up on the unfairness of it. The grown-ups keep talking about how the state isn't doing its part, and that frustrates her because it means the problem isn't just local. Nobody seems to be helping.
