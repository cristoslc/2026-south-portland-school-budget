# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

One day after the board granted student representatives access to superintendent interviews -- the single most encouraging development in months -- Jaylen watches a joint session where the budget crisis is presented to the city council, and the exclusion pattern snaps back into place. The same 78 positions and 42 teachers are discussed, the same elementary reconfiguration is explained, and once again nobody specifies what any of it means for theater, cross-country, AP classes, or any program a high school student would recognize. A community member explicitly called out the exclusion of students from the process, everyone nodded, and the discussion moved on.

The city council's budget guidance -- limiting increases to 4-6% -- effectively tells the school board to find millions more in cuts. This is a layer of government Jaylen barely knew existed making decisions that constrain his education. The school board cannot unilaterally choose higher taxes; the council sets the ceiling, and the ceiling is low. Jaylen is learning that the budget process involves multiple institutions, each with the power to limit what schools can do but none taking direct responsibility for the consequences at the student level.

The elementary reconfiguration is being rushed into a six-month implementation despite being a fundamental structural change, while the middle school schedule consolidation was dropped because it needs a year of traffic studies. The inconsistency in urgency further erodes Jaylen's trust in the process. Decisions that affect younger children are being fast-tracked; decisions that affect his grade levels are deferred. The pattern reinforces his sense that the system treats different groups of students with different levels of urgency.

The one genuinely reassuring data point is that SPHS has six social workers compared to two at neighboring high schools, and these positions are being maintained. This suggests some awareness within the district that high school students face mental health and support needs that cannot be cut. But it sits alongside the paradox of a district that maintains support staff while cutting the teachers who deliver the core educational experience.

The teachers' union president reiterated that staff are "nervous right now about losing their jobs," extending the anxiety reported at the February 9 meeting. Teachers already went through cuts last year -- over 20 positions -- and the current round is far larger. If Jaylen's teachers are anxious about their own employment, the quality of his classroom experience is already degrading before any formal cuts take effect.
