# Cumulative Summary: Amira (PERSONA-013)

### Current Understanding

Amira has watched her school district's budget crisis escalate from a supply freeze and a broken athletic field into a full-blown restructuring that threatens to reshape every level of her educational experience. The district faces an $8 million budget gap requiring 78 position eliminations (42 teachers, 16 ed techs, and other staff), elementary school closures or grade-band reconfigurations, and class size increases from 16 to 22-23 students. The leadership team's top recommendation is to close Skillin elementary -- the school serving many Somali and car-free families in Amira's community -- primarily to avoid a $3 million HVAC replacement, not for educational reasons. At least four board members publicly oppose this option.

Beyond the structural changes, Amira has experienced the crisis through concrete personal losses: two math teachers at Memorial Middle School retiring with no guaranteed replacements, music trips and robotics competitions now requiring special board approval instead of being automatic, and special education teachers resigning voluntarily. The school nutrition fund carries a $1.1 million deficit because state reimbursements fall far short of actual meal costs. The district spent $30,000 on a superintendent search firm while under a supply purchasing freeze. Property taxes are rising 6% despite all the cuts.

Amira has developed a more sophisticated understanding of why the crisis exists: state funding covers only ~20% of school costs versus a mandated 55%, but the district also contributed by adding 82 staff positions since 2017 while enrollment dropped by 300+ students. City council members add political pressure, with some arguing taxpayers cannot afford any of the proposed budget options. The budget must be adopted by June 9 for a referendum, compressing massive restructuring decisions into months.

Two bright spots stand out: high school students created "The Little Kitchen" to directly help families during the immigration enforcement crisis, demonstrating that students take action where adults hesitate; and the district's new "community school" designation promises wraparound services through community partnerships. Student board representatives will participate in superintendent interviews. But Amira's core frustration persists across all nine meetings: adults make decisions that reshape students' daily lives without asking students what they need, what they value, or what they're afraid of losing.
