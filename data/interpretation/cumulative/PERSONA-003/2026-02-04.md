# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: School Board Public Budget Forum -- February 4, 2026

### Interpretation

Now I know what "reconfiguration" means, and it's bigger than I feared. They're talking about scrapping the neighborhood school model entirely and replacing it with grade-band schools -- pre-K through second grade in one building, third and fourth in another. That means my daughter wouldn't just go to our neighborhood school; she'd be assigned to a primary campus based on grade level, then moved to a different building partway through elementary. Class sizes would jump from the current average of about 16 to 18-19 for K-2 and 22-23 for grades 3-4. Those numbers are within board guidelines, so they can do it, but it's a fundamentally different experience than what I imagined when we bought this house.

The financial math continues to get worse. Even if they implement all three "big ideas" -- elementary reconfiguration, middle/high school efficiency changes, and operational restructuring -- there's still a gap of over $3 million to reach the 6% tax ceiling. So reconfiguration is not the solution; it's just one piece of a much larger set of cuts that haven't been specified yet. That means more unknowns coming, probably affecting programs and services I haven't even thought to worry about.

But here's the thing that actually made me feel slightly better for the first time in weeks: they're not closing any school buildings. The reason is that new state requirements force them to serve 3-to-5-year-olds with special needs within two years, and they're moving toward universal pre-K, so they need the physical space. This is huge for me. It means the buildings stay open, the pre-K expansion I got excited about in January has physical infrastructure behind it, and there's a structural reason it can't be easily cut. The pre-K thread just became more durable.

Meanwhile the superintendent search rolls on in parallel with all of this. The interim superintendent acknowledged he's leaving and the assistant superintendent is retiring. So whoever takes the reins will inherit a completely restructured system -- or a system mid-restructuring -- which is its own kind of risk. I still can't picture what South Portland schools will look like in 2028, but at least the floor isn't dropping out from under the buildings themselves.
