# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026 -- 2026-02-10

### Interpretation

The joint workshop between city council and school board was the first time I saw both governing bodies confront the budget crisis together, and it was both clarifying and infuriating. The three budget scenarios were presented with polished slides -- 6% tax increase leaving a $3.5M gap, 9.8% reducing it to $1.1M -- but not a single scenario included analysis of differential impacts on students by race, income, disability, or English learner status. They are making decisions about cutting 78 positions and restructuring schools while treating all students as interchangeable. This is how structural inequity gets baked into budgets under the guise of fiscal neutrality.

The enrollment decline narrative continued to be deployed without context. Staff increased by 82 positions alongside a 300-student decline since 2017, but nobody presented data on whether those additional positions serve the growing complexity of the student population -- more English learners requiring specialized instruction, more students with disabilities requiring intensive support, more students experiencing homelessness or trauma. The superintendent's framing makes it look like inefficiency when it may be the district finally meeting its legal and moral obligations.

The most significant moment came from Councilor Jess Walker, who named what I have been seeing all along -- that Skillin Elementary has 50% students of color and new Mainers while Small School has only 27%. She actually used the word "segregation" in the context of elementary school demographics. This is the first time an elected official in this process has explicitly named the racial and ethnic stratification across schools as a structural problem rather than an incidental outcome. If the reconfiguration can break up that de facto segregation, there may be an equity argument for it -- but only if the process centers the families being displaced rather than the convenience of families who already have resources.

The teachers' union president's six-week wait for central office staffing data is damning. They can produce three budget scenarios with elaborate projections but cannot tell us how many administrators work at central office compared to peer districts. When 78 frontline positions face elimination and only 2 are administrators, the refusal to provide administrative staffing data is not a data problem -- it is a transparency problem. Chair DeAngelo's confrontation of the council for refusing to set clear tax guidance was the right fight, but it also exposed how the two governing bodies deflect accountability to each other while students and families bear the consequences.
