# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

This meeting is a smaller version of a pattern Tom has watched play out all winter: one councilor doing the math, the rest finding reasons not to. The rental assistance vote — $100,000 out of the city's undesignated fund balance — isn't a large number on its own. But Tom has been watching this fund balance since February, when the auditor's report showed the city had grown it by $5.8 million through disciplined management. That number was Tom's single piece of evidence that sound fiscal governance was possible in this city. Now a piece of it is walking out the door, and the city manager confirmed on the record what the cost is: next year's capital budget goes from $3 million to $2.85 million. Stated plainly. Voted past anyway.

Councilor Matthews said what Tom was thinking. The school board chair had called him the night before to say watch every dime, the school department is 62% of the total budget, and here the council is voting to draw down the reserve fund while 72 school employees are carrying pink slips home. Matthews was outvoted. West offered a middle ground at $20,000 that also went nowhere. This is the second time in a week Tom has watched the council spend out of the fund balance while the district burns — the March 19 meeting did the same thing, and now he has the March 10 version to compare it to. The principle, such as it is, appears to be: yes to spending, no to bikes.

The bike share rejection was right, and Tom knows it. Twenty thousand in matching funds plus ongoing subsidy, a geography that doesn't fit the model, and a local business owner pointing out what happens to her eleven employees when a national company moves in. Six of seven said no. That's the correct answer. But it lands next to a hundred thousand for rental assistance in the same meeting, and Tom cannot find the rule that produces both outcomes. Cost matters for bikes. Cost is a secondary consideration for the other vote. He left the meeting not knowing what the council's actual fiscal threshold is — only that it isn't consistent.

The Scarborough bus route is the quiet one. Fully funded for two years, Metro says. After that, South Portland picks up roughly 28% of the annual cost starting in 2028. Nobody asked what 28% of that route costs. Nobody will, apparently, until the bill arrives.

### Emotional Register

Tom leaves this meeting with a cleaner but grimmer understanding of the council's actual decision rule: they'll say no to twenty thousand dollars when the politics are easy and yes to a hundred thousand when they aren't, and the fund balance that represented his one data point for responsible city management is now being drawn down in parallel votes on back-to-back weeks while the school crisis runs its course one building over.
