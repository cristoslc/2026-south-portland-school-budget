# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: Regular -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

Two and a half hours of this meeting and the biggest bombshell wasn't even on the agenda. Turns out the nutrition fund -- the school lunch program -- has been hemorrhaging money for years. Over $715,000 in FY25, projected $1.1 million in FY26. And nobody knew because it was tracked separately from the main budget. Seven business managers in six years and none of them caught this? That's not a gap in oversight, that's a void. The finance director says the current year is now heading for deficit across all scenarios once you count the nutrition losses. So the crisis I've been watching unfold isn't just about next year -- they can't even finish this year in the black.

Board member Feller confirmed what I suspected after the budget forum: the 6% ceiling was never real. He flat-out said he'd be "more comfortable with something exceeding 6%" rather than making "draconian cuts." So five days after they told the public they're targeting 6%, a board member is already signaling he wants to go higher. And the chair admitted they're heading to the city council the next day to get "specific guidance" -- meaning the real number. The 6% was theater for the budget forum.

The early retirement buyout idea is still alive -- Feller calculated that if 20 of 41 eligible staff took buyouts, savings would exceed $1 million. That's the most sensible thing I've heard in months. They also approved studying elementary reconfiguration for a March 2nd presentation, despite most public feedback being negative. The union president warned that any staffing changes require contract renegotiation, which could delay everything. More friction, more cost, more time. I'll be at March 2nd to see what they actually propose.
