### Reactions

Look, I went into this meeting expecting some tough budget numbers, but what I saw was worse than I thought. The school side has a fundamental operational problem - they've added 82 positions while losing 300+ kids since 2017. That's not a budget problem, that's a management problem. When the interim superintendent put up that chart showing enrollment dropping while staffing climbed, it was like watching a slow-motion car crash.

The thing that really got me was when that teacher stood up asking where the historical admin data is - apparently they requested central office staffing comparisons six weeks ago and still don't have answers. How do you make $7 million in cuts without knowing where your money actually goes? And then we find out their fund balance is basically zero while they're potentially looking at a deficit this year. Meanwhile, the city has $8.5 million in excess fund balance. It's like one side of the house is on fire while the other side is doing fine.

The council basically told them to go back to the drawing board - they rejected both the 6% and 9.8% options and said keep it between 3-6%. That means they're looking at cuts closer to $9 million instead of the $3-7 million they presented. I get that taxpayers are hurting, but you can't just wish away the structural problems by demanding lower numbers. They need to figure out why they're spending $26,651 per kid when comparable districts do it for less.
