# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: School Board Public Budget Forum -- February 4, 2026

### Interpretation

This is the meeting where the budget crisis stopped being abstract numbers and became a plan that will reshape my kids' daily lives. The $7.2 million gap I've been warning my group chats about since December is now attached to specific proposals, and the biggest one -- elementary school reconfiguration -- would completely change how every elementary family in South Portland experiences school.

The headline I'm leading with: without cuts, our school portion of property taxes goes up 18.95%. The board capped it at 6%. That 13-point gap is $7.2 million in cuts. I've been reporting pieces of this since December, but this is the first time they've laid out the full picture with specific proposals. The three "big ideas" -- elementary reconfiguration saving ~$2M, middle/high school efficiencies saving ~$1M, and operational changes saving ~$1M -- only get them to about $4M. There's still over $3 million in cuts they haven't even identified yet, and that's the part that terrifies me.

The elementary reconfiguration would eliminate neighborhood schools entirely. Instead of each school serving its surrounding area for all elementary grades, they'd split into grade bands -- pre-K-2 schools and 3-4 schools. Class sizes would jump from about 16 to 18-19 for younger kids and 22-23 for grades 3-4. The district says that's "within guidelines" but any parent with a kindergartener knows the difference between 16 kids and 19 kids in a room.

I have two kids at different elementary buildings right now. If reconfiguration happens, they might both have to change schools, or one stays and one moves, and my morning routine goes from two drop-offs at nearby schools to potentially two drop-offs across town. Every parent in my group chats with multiple elementary-age kids is doing this same calculation right now, and I'm the one they're asking for details.

The fund balance being gone is confirmed for the third time now -- I first reported this December 8th and it keeps coming back as context for why there's no wiggle room. Health insurance jumped from the 8% estimate to 12%, which I reported at the December 17 workshop. The one genuine piece of good news: no school closures planned because they need the buildings for state-mandated pre-K expansion. I held that for the end of my group chat summary because people needed something to not panic about.
