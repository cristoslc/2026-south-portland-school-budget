# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

One night after watching the school board implement a spending freeze and confirm a potential $1.9 million deficit, I'm watching city council do routine business. Tax assessments, committee appointments, a zoning vote. And then Rosemary DeAngelo walks up to the podium.

She's there as the school board's liaison, and she drops two things: budget season officially starts this month — watch the district website — and tomorrow night, three superintendent search firms are presenting at SPHS, public welcome, three minutes to speak. One day after the worst school board meeting of the year, she's standing in front of the council telling everyone: show up, get involved, this is the moment. I appreciated the invitation in real time. I know how December 10 ended — the board picked Zeal for $35,000 in a 5-2 vote with no community screening committees, Holman calling it "bling." All that public access DeAngelo advertised didn't change the outcome. The mechanism was broken before anyone showed up.

The item that has grown in my mind over three months is the TIF deal on 170 Ocean Street. The council voted 7-0 to approve 208 market-rate apartments in Mill Creek, no affordable units, 1BRs reportedly running $1,800 a month per Zillow. That was the headline. But Councilor West buried the lead: the existing tax increment financing agreement gives the developer a 50% property tax rebate for thirty years. And that deal was made when the project was 124 units — not 208. The project got bigger, the terms stayed the same, and the council voted yes anyway. All seven.

Since the December 1 inaugural I've had an open question about whether Mayor Tipton's housing agenda would compete with school funding. I didn't have a mechanism. Now I do. DeAngelo says it every single time she stands at a podium: schools are 61% of property taxes. Half of a building's tax contribution is half of schools' share too. The city is structuring housing deals that divert property tax revenue from the general fund for three decades — the same property tax revenue that funds the district's budget — and doing it on a project that grew 67% larger without anyone renegotiating the terms. That's not a housing-versus-schools tension in the abstract anymore. That's a number.

The good news from tonight: the assessor reported a stable year. Values up $120 million instead of last year's $1.2 billion jump. Mill rate came in below projections. Homestead exemption is $341. That's the one piece I could drop into my group chats with something other than dread attached to it.

The virtual comment fight limped forward again. Two parents paid $40 in babysitting to attend together and make the case in person. The mayor said she'd look into options. No timeline, no vote. Same answer the council gave in December 4 when they blocked Zoom access over First Amendment concerns. These are the same parents who are going to need to weigh in on the biggest budget decision in a generation, and the city is making attendance expensive.
