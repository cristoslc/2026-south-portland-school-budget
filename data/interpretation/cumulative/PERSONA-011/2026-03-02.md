# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

Three and a half hours. Nine reconfiguration options. Every elementary principal presenting data. This was the meeting where abstract budget scenarios became blueprints with school names attached, and I was screenshotting slides as fast as I could because my group chats have been waiting weeks for this level of detail.

The lead story writes itself: the superintendent's top recommendation (Option 1.7) would close Skillin school, and four board members immediately shot it down. Dowling said he "cannot support an option or a model that closes Skillin." Feller and Richardson agreed. Then Chair DeAngelis delivered what might be the most memorable moment of this entire budget season -- she argued that closing Skillin would force every family "west of the Dairy Queen" to bus their kids across town, and that it would disproportionately burden the district's most vulnerable immigrant families. That quote is going to travel far beyond my group chats.

So Skillin is protected, but that constrains which options are left. The nine models ranged from minor tweaks to full grade-band reconfiguration with school closures, and now the politically viable ones are a narrower set. Option 1.7 saved the most over five years ($19.6M) but needed $540K in portable classrooms -- and it's dead. Whatever the board picks instead will save less money, which means either more cuts elsewhere or a higher tax increase than the 4-6% guidance the council gave three weeks ago.

Richardson called out what I've been thinking for weeks: there's no academic achievement data by school anywhere in the presentation. They're recommending closing and reconfiguring schools without showing which ones are actually performing well or struggling. She called it "such an obvious void" and she's right. How can I tell my group chats which option is best for kids when the district itself hasn't analyzed outcomes by building?

The parent survey results were devastating for the board's communication strategy. 85% of 245+ respondents oppose or strongly oppose reconfiguration. Only 12% felt adequately informed. Twelve percent. The board has been making the biggest structural change in a generation and almost nine out of ten parents feel they don't have enough information. That's a communication failure I've been trying to compensate for in my group chats, but I'm one person with a phone -- I can't substitute for an actual district engagement strategy.

The non-reconfiguration path was modeled too -- across-the-board class size increases and staff cuts saving ~$2.2M. The superintendent called it "kicking the problem down the road." I'm genuinely torn on this. Reconfiguration is disruptive and my networks hate it, but the alternative is bigger class sizes everywhere with no structural fix. There's no option where nothing changes and everything's fine.
