# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

Seven weeks after watching the council adjourn the Mahoney workshop without giving the architect a budget ceiling, and five days after hitting the wall of the missing January 15 goal-setting attachment, this meeting is the first city council session where David finds himself thinking mostly about the city's own structural challenges rather than the school budget. That shift matters. It is not relief — the school crisis hasn't changed — but it is a reorientation toward what else is loading onto the same property tax base.

The housing workshop is the entire meeting, substantively speaking. The state has effectively eliminated local control over small residential development: 5,000 square foot minimums citywide wherever public sewer exists, up to four units permitted by right, and planning board review stripped out of the process entirely for 2-4 unit projects. David's instinct here is to run the math rather than take a position on the politics. Milan's team did exactly that — if 10% of single-family parcels add one unit, regional housing demand is met, and current infrastructure handles it through normal capital planning. That is the kind of practical projection David can work with, though he wants to see the sewer and school capacity analysis behind it before accepting it as settled.

What actually registers with him is the school enrollment implication buried inside the housing projections. The district just lost 23% of elementary enrollment over four years while the housing stock was presumably not adding multi-family density at any significant rate. If state mandates now force meaningful densification — even at 10% conversion — the enrollment trajectory question gets more complicated. The reconfiguration planning assumes continued decline. A new source of younger households in a city that just eliminated barriers to adding units could change that model, or at minimum should be stress-tested against it.

The council's pragmatic posture toward implementation — streamline approvals, don't fight the state on design controls — is the right call fiscally. Bureaucratic resistance costs staff time and legal fees the city cannot afford right now. The working waterfront petition with 2,163 validated signatures is a different kind of signal: organized community opposition that doesn't align neatly with the housing mandate, and that David will watch to see whether it produces a ballot initiative or dissipates.
