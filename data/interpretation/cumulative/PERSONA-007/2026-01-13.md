# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

The night after our school board meeting confirmed 92% of funds expended at midyear — with a spending freeze already in place — I'm watching the city council discuss a $194 million bond referendum they want to put in front of voters in November 2026. The same November when our school budget needs to survive validation. I've spent months trying to quantify the fiscal environment our ask will land in. This meeting gave me the number I was missing, and it's worse than I'd modeled.

Ellen Sanborn put it plainly: the current city rate is $13.65 per thousand. Borrowing $194 million at once adds $2.26 in year one. That's a 16.5% increase on the city's share of the property tax bill — stacked directly on top of whatever we're asking for on the school side. And that's before the Pearl Street pump station, which the city manager confirmed is "probably just under $50 million" in sewer borrowing that doesn't need voter approval but still hits homeowners through rate increases. Nobody totaled all of it up at tonight's meeting. That's the kind of gap I keep having to fill myself.

The moment I'll carry forward is the architect telling Councilor Matthews, flat out, "I am not confident, Dickie" — on the $193 million estimate. Unknowns around structural reinforcement and soil conditions could push it higher before any referendum. We're potentially asking taxpayers to absorb a number that the people designing the project can't stand behind. And this is supposed to share a ballot with our school budget validation.

Then the council fractured. Multiple councilors declined to support the full package, and by the end they'd sent the design team back to develop scaled-down Mahoney-only scenarios for the January 27th committee. The city manager acknowledged that meaningful scope changes would likely blow past the July-August deadline required for a November 2026 ballot placement. That fracture is — quietly, probably — the best news I've heard for our validation campaign in weeks. Our ask cannot compete with a $194 million city bond in the same November window. If that referendum slips to 2027, we have one fewer catastrophic variable.

Since December 9th, when Josh explained the 50-cent capture rate on rising valuations, I've understood that the fiscal ceiling is structurally constrained regardless of development. Now I'm holding the other end of that picture: even the city's capital investment needs translate directly into GO debt that competes for the same homeowner tolerance we're counting on. The validation math doesn't get easier from here.
