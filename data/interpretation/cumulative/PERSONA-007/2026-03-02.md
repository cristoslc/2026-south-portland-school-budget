# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026 -- 2026-03-02

### Interpretation

Three and a half hours, and I think we learned more about what won't work than what will. The administration did their homework -- nine detailed reconfiguration scenarios with precise FTE reductions, five-year capital projections, and savings ranging from $10.5M to $19.6M over five years. Joe and Abigail deserve credit for the analytical rigor. But the minute they recommended closing Skillin as the top option, I knew the board would revolt.

And it did. Four of us -- Dowling, Feller, Richardson, and Chair DeAngelis -- explicitly rejected closing Skillin during the workshop. Rosemarie's argument about geographic equity was the most compelling: Skillin serves 33% multilingual learners versus 6% at Small, has the highest walkability rate at 72%, and closing it would effectively tell families west of Cash Corner that their side of town doesn't deserve a neighborhood school. Tyler was right to call it what it is -- we'd be busing our most vulnerable kids across town while keeping all the east-side schools open. That's not equity regardless of what the HVAC savings look like.

The public comment was brutal and revealing. Three hours of parents who clearly had no idea this was coming. Rachel Gallo's grassroots survey of 245+ parents showed only 12% felt adequately informed. That's a massive communication failure that compounds our already difficult validation campaign. Speakers repeatedly requested Spanish interpreters, and we still don't have them at these meetings when a quarter of our families speak languages other than English at home. We're making decisions about their children's schools without ensuring they can participate in the process.

The implementation timeline concerns are equally serious. Multiple board members questioned whether any reconfiguration could be properly executed by September 2026, and Dr. Prince couldn't commit to a redistricting plan date before budget approval. We're potentially committing to something we can't actually deliver, which would create chaos for families and staff. And Option 1.2 -- proportional reductions without closure -- was labeled "more analysis needed" with no financial modeling provided, which feels like the administration steering us away from a politically easier path without proving it's fiscally inferior.

I think we're converging on Options 1.3 or 1.6 -- closing either Kaler/Dyer or Small instead. Still saves substantial money, still allows reconfiguration benefits, but doesn't gut the west side.
