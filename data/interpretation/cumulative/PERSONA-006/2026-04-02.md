# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The 6% ceiling held. That's the headline, and Tom knows it's the only headline he gets to take home from this meeting — because everything underneath it is a mess. Since December he has been watching a board that couldn't manage its finances, couldn't account for 82 new positions, and couldn't pass its own budget. Tonight they finally held the line at the number the city council told them to hold. That's not a triumph. That's the floor.

What the finance director laid out in her presentation is the clearest accounting Tom has heard all winter of how the district got here — and it's damning in a specific, documented way. Not one or two bad years. Electricity under-budgeted by $138,000 to $368,000 every single year since FY23. Tuition reimbursements over-spent by $30,000 to $153,000 every year since FY23. Three consecutive deficit years in FY24, FY25, and FY26. She called it "optimistic budgeting." Tom has a less charitable phrase for it, but the mechanism is now on the record. This confirms and deepens what the auditor said at the February 3 city council meeting — two consecutive years of non-compliance with the legally adopted budget — and adds the structural pattern underneath it. This wasn't a surprise crisis. It was a series of known shortfalls that nobody corrected.

The fund balance is zero. Tom knew this going in, but tonight a board member said "we've been extremely negligent" out loud, and the finance director confirmed the district's own standard calls for 9–12% in reserve. The $52,000 in insurance savings that had been discussed as a possible start toward rebuilding the fund balance was redirected to the high school chimney stacks and absorbed into the operating budget — a decision made without deliberation. Tom doesn't dispute that deferred maintenance is real. He disputes that a decision of that kind gets made without a conversation.

And then the labor contract math, again. The finance director said it plainly: costs grow faster than 6% per year by contract, automatically. She said the district has to be "very precise and careful" in FY27 to hold the line. That's not a plan for FY28. That's a warning. The 6% ceiling Tom credits the city council for extracting in February is, structurally speaking, already broken for next year.

The state money news — $300,000 from lobbying, possibly $750,000 more from a formula change — would have been the most encouraging thing Tom heard all winter if anyone could confirm the numbers. Nobody could. The board spent the end of the meeting debating what to do with money that hasn't arrived, couldn't agree on whether it should restore cut positions or seed the fund balance, and left without voting on the budget. The superintendent wanted a clean vote. He didn't get one. Four and a half hours, and Tom drove home with a maybe.
