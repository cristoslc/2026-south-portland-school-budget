# Cumulative Record: Kira (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

This is the first moment Kira has to read the political landscape of the people who will actually vote on cuts that touch her work and her children. It's a ceremonial meeting, and she knows that. But she stays for all of it, and it gives her both more hope and more anxiety than she expected.

The multilingual community speakers are not abstractions to her. These are the families she crosses three buildings every day to serve — the ones on her MTSS caseload, the multilingual learners she advocates for when access is uneven between schools. Watching six people stand up and speak in Farsi, Kinyarwanda, Arabic, Khmer, Portuguese, and Somali and say *we want to be your friends* to a new school board is, for Kira, a direct mirror of everything she believes her professional work is for. Jean Tam's daughter attends Kaler specifically — a concrete detail that lodges in her mind, because Kaler is a named school, and reconfiguration is coming, and this family has no idea yet what that may mean for them.

DeAngelos's address is the most significant thing that happens. The new chair talks about power inequity, about kids who were never seen by their schools, about education as belonging rather than just academics. That is Kira's language. That is the argument she makes in board comment and in letters that she isn't sure anyone reads. Hearing it from the person who will run the meetings where 78 positions get voted on is meaningful — maybe more meaningful than anything she's heard from district leadership in years.

Tipton naming the middle school as proof this community can make hard changes with shared purpose gives her a jolt of recognition: that's her argument for reconfiguration, word for word, coming out of the incoming mayor's inaugural address.

And then the gavel drops and the budget was never mentioned. Four new members — Feller, Richardson, Rich, Smith — sat there and took their oath and she knows nothing about any of them. DeAngelos's equity framing is real, she believes it, but warmth alone doesn't protect the intervention slots that are already wait-listed at two of her buildings. She needs to know where these people stand, and this meeting left her with nothing.

### Emotional Register

Kira leaves this meeting split between genuine, tear-streaked hope — the multilingual families, DeAngelos's belonging language, Tipton's middle school invocation all felt like her own convictions reflected back from the front of the room — and a grinding, specific dread that the board just got handed a crisis and four of its seven members are complete unknowns to her.
