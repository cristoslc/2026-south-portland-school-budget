# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

The January 22 citizen forum that Maria had circled on her calendar since DeAngelis announced it at the January 6 city council meeting never happened. ICE raided the laundromat and car wash in Red Bank the same day the forum was scheduled, and the families who would have filled those seats — the immigrant families DeAngelis invoked at her December 1 inauguration, the same families Jean Tam from Kaler School represents — were hiding at home instead.

Maria has been cataloging participation failures since December 4: Zoom blocked over First Amendment constraints, three-minute comment limits, forty dollars in childcare just to speak for three minutes, an in-person-only format that prices out working parents. Those were institutional failures — frustrating, predictable, unaddressed. The ICE raids are categorically different. Federal enforcement activity doesn't fail to design around community needs. It doesn't inadvertently raise the cost of participation. It extinguishes participation directly, and it targets exactly the families whose children are most vulnerable to what the district does next.

DeAngelis came to the January 20 city council and made the announcement herself. Rescheduled to February 4. Said nothing more. Maria had been trying to reconcile the equity language of DeAngelis's inauguration speech with the governance choices that followed — choosing the most board-controlled superintendent search process, calling reconfiguration "fabulous" before any formal proposal existed, dismissing neighborhood schools as something the city "doesn't really have anymore." Now she adds this: the chair who invoked immigrant belonging and connection is also the person who stood at the podium to announce that immigrant families were too afraid to attend the meeting meant to give them voice in their children's future. It was information delivery, not moral reckoning.

Maria went to the February 4 forum. She knows how that story ends — $7.2M gap confirmed, reconfiguration the primary strategy, the machinery kept moving. The postponement changed the date. It did not change anything else.

The housing workshop is a secondary dissonance she cannot shake. State law now mandates lots as small as 5,000 square feet citywide — down from minimums reaching 80,000 in some zones — with no planning board review for two-to-four-unit developments. Councilor Walker admitted she was "vastly over" her skillset on the zoning details and voted anyway. Maria cannot stop doing enrollment math in her head: more housing density eventually means more children. South Portland is eliminating 42 teacher positions because there aren't enough children now. Nobody in either governing body appears to be running both calculations at the same time.
