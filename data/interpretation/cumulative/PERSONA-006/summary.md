# Cumulative Summary: Tom (PERSONA-006)

### Current Understanding

Tom is a South Portland homeowner on a fixed income who has tracked the school budget crisis from its first public disclosure in December 2025 through March 2026. His understanding has hardened from initial vindicated anger into a cold, arithmetic dread.

The school district faces an $8.4 million structural deficit — revised up from the $7.2 million figure that held all winter — driven by a 23% elementary enrollment drop over four years while staffing grew by 82 positions, a fund balance burned from $5.9 million to zero, state funding covering only 20% against a 55% statutory obligation, and two consecutive years of budget non-compliance confirmed by auditors. Seventy-two employees received layoff notices on March 18. School taxes represent 61% of his property tax bill, and the per-pupil cost of $26,651 is the highest among comparable districts. The school administration has still not produced a breakdown of the 82 added positions despite promising it to the city council in February.

Beyond schools, Tom now tracks four simultaneous cost pressures with a concrete calendar: a school property tax increase in FY27, sewer rates rising roughly 22% annually for three consecutive years (FY27–FY29) to finance the $51.7 million Pearl Street pump station with no voter approval required, a police and fire bond referendum targeting November 2026, and a Mahoney City Center bond referendum deferred to 2027 at a stripped-down estimate of $70–76 million. No one in the process has produced a combined per-household total.

Tom views the city council as intermittently capable of fiscal backbone — the 3–6% guidance at the February joint workshop and the 4–2 eviction moratorium defeat both demonstrated real restraint — but fundamentally unreliable on capital commitments, where verbal concern consistently fails to produce binding ceilings. The city's flagship TIF deal at 170 Ocean Street returns 50% of new tax revenue to the developer for 30 years. The state is simultaneously underfunding education and mandating citywide residential densification with no planning board review. The incoming mayor's expansive agenda — inaugurated while the school crisis was already building — set a spending-forward baseline that makes every subsequent moment of council restraint look like an exception rather than a pattern.
