# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

The February 3 meeting gives Dana something she has been building toward since December 8 without realizing it: independent certification. She has been covering the school department's fiscal chaos through the board's own admissions — the mid-year gap, the depleted fund balance, the 6% ceiling that math said was impossible. Now an outside auditor has come in and confirmed all of it on paper. Six finance directors in five years. Thirty-four journal entries, flagged as unusually high. A material weakness in reconciliations for the second consecutive year. Four hundred thousand dollars in grant money that had to be absorbed by taxpayers because the department overspent or filed ineligible claims. This is no longer the school board's self-reported crisis — it is a certified institutional failure. That distinction matters in a television segment because it removes the "he said, she said" framing and gives Dana an accountability anchor that doesn't require her to referee between the board and the administration.

But the city-school contrast is the editorial gift she did not see coming. She has been framing this story as two bodies locked in a resource dispute — each under pressure, each with legitimate claims, each making the other's situation harder. February 3 reveals something more complicated: the city received a clean audit opinion, posted a $5.8 million fund balance increase, and is sitting on approximately $28.8 million in reserves with $8.5 million available for projects. The school department has been in material weakness for two consecutive years. When DeAngelos stands at the council table in this same meeting and urges the council to "accept responsibility for the school as a department of the city" and demands "clear guidance" at the February 10 workshop — a confrontation Dana has already covered — she is now watching a fiscally healthy municipality being asked to rescue a demonstrably poorly-managed department. That is a harder story to tell than institutional warfare, and a more accurate one.

The immigration resolution is usable footage, but not for the resolution itself. The council buried the item at the end of four hours spent on corporate TIF calculations and business incentives. People who testified about living in darkness, afraid to turn on lights, waited the full length while their elected officials worked through semiconductor finance. The council passed something that amounts to asking the governor to act. Dana reads it as a punt, and the anger in public comment confirms the audience reads it the same way. She now has this council on tape from January 20 — briefed on canceled school forums and cell phone video from Red Bank — and on tape from February 3, burying the follow-up under corporate business. The pattern is becoming airworthy.

DeAngelos's warning seven days before the February 10 explosion is the setup she now has on tape. He gave them a week.
