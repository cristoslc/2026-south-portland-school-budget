# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 10, 2025 -- 2025-12-10

### Interpretation

Two days after learning about the spending freeze and deficit crisis, I watch this board spend $24,000 on a superintendent search consultant — money that could literally save half a teaching position. The hypocrisy is staggering. They're cutting 42 teachers while paying premium prices to avoid having teachers involved in selecting our next leader.

What really crystallizes for me is that this board doesn't just mismanage money — they actively exclude the people who do the actual work. Not one of the three search firms mentioned union engagement, and when Zeal explicitly recommended keeping community members out of candidate screening, the board ate it up. Richardson called community involvement "pain points," as if hearing from teachers and parents about what kind of leader we need is somehow problematic rather than essential.

The spending freeze suddenly makes more sense in this context. It's not just about the contract deficit — it's about priorities. They'll find money for consultants who tell them what they want to hear, but classroom supplies and teacher positions are expendable. DeAngelo's comment that board consensus is "worth every penny" reveals everything: this is about board politics, not educational leadership.

I'm realizing that the budget crisis isn't just a financial problem — it's a governance problem. A board that spends more on avoiding teacher input than some districts spend on entire programs while simultaneously firing dozens of teachers has fundamentally broken priorities. The union conversation Sarah Gay pushed for last meeting feels even more critical now, because it's clear this board won't include us unless we force the issue.
