# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- March 9, 2026 -- 2026-03-09

### Interpretation

This meeting was a study in contradictions. The board formalized two genuinely positive equity actions -- endorsing the city council immigration protection resolve and approving the community school model expansion district-wide -- while the elementary reconfiguration continued to unfold without the equity guardrails these decisions need. The community school model in particular could be transformative: integrating mental health services, social services, and family support directly into school buildings addresses root causes of educational inequity in ways that budget line items never will. But the recurring question remains -- which schools get resourced first, and will the communities with the greatest need actually receive priority?

The reconfiguration discussion revealed a troubling rhetorical pattern. District administrators described linguistic and cultural diversity as "an asset" and framed restructuring as "empowering all South Portland students," while Principal Hopkins stated that schools more closely reflecting overall district diversity "will benefit all learners." This is the right language, but after watching the administration recommend closing Skillin -- the most diverse school -- just one week earlier, I cannot take equity framing at face value anymore. The question is not whether diversity benefits learners; the question is whether the implementation plan protects the families who are being asked to absorb the disruption of school closures and longer bus rides.

Parents' concerns about losing neighborhood schools are legitimate equity concerns even when they do not use equity language. When a parent says it will be "harder for families to make drop-offs at the same time," that is a transportation equity issue that disproportionately affects single-parent households, working families, and families without reliable vehicles -- populations that overlap heavily with the communities of color and low-income families at Skillin and Kaler. The non-biased hiring training postponement from February 4 to March 18 is a small detail that carries symbolic weight: the district is restructuring schools that serve its most diverse populations while repeatedly delaying the training that would help staff serve those students equitably in their new settings.

The special education expansion for 3 and 4-year-olds remains an unfunded mandate arriving in the middle of a budget crisis, and the tension between expanding services for our youngest learners with disabilities while cutting the ed tech positions that support older students with the same needs has not been reconciled. The district seems capable of doing the right thing in discrete moments -- halal meals, immigration solidarity, community schools -- but struggles to apply a consistent equity framework to the large structural decisions that determine who gets what resources.
