# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

The day after the board's unanimous vote, Lila is hearing the reconfiguration plan described to a new audience -- the city council -- and it's becoming more specific. The interim superintendent laid out the model: two schools would become primary schools for pre-K through 1st grade, and three would become intermediate schools for bigger kids. Dyer could end up as either type, but either way, Lila's experience of her school would be unrecognizable. If it becomes a primary school, her little brother stays but she leaves. If it becomes an intermediate school, the little kids she knows leave and the building fills with unfamiliar faces.

The meeting exposed the fracture lines between the bodies that control Lila's school funding. The school board chair got visibly angry at the city council for not providing clear budget guidance, demanding they "tell the schools how much money they can have" rather than offering vague encouragement. The adults responsible for Lila's education are fighting with the adults responsible for funding it, and neither side seems to be winning. Meanwhile, council members offered a range of guidance from 3% to 6% increases -- numbers that mean nothing to a 9-year-old but clearly aren't enough to close a $7.2 million gap.

The staffing cuts got more specific: if the district receives only a minimal budget increase, up to 50 staff could lose their jobs, and the assistant superintendent said this would "hurt kids the most." Lila has been worried about her teacher for weeks now, and hearing that 50 positions might be cut -- with the explicit acknowledgment that children bear the cost -- confirms her worst fear. Her teacher, who has looped with her for two years and knows her better than any other adult at school, could be one of those 50.

One parent's testimony about a "bike bus" -- kids riding to school together from different neighborhoods -- painted a picture of exactly the kind of community connection that reconfiguration would destroy. Lila wishes she had something like that at Dyer. The irony is that she's hearing about it precisely at the moment these neighborhood-based connections are being dismantled.

The enrollment data was presented again -- 300 fewer students district-wide but 82 more staff hired -- which the adults seemed to think proved the district is inefficient. To Lila, this just means there are more grown-ups per kid, which sounds like it should be a good thing. She doesn't understand why having more helpers means you have to close schools.
