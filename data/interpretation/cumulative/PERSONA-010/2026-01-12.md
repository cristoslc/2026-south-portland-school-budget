# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- January 12, 2026

### Interpretation

After the data-heavy December 17th workshop, this regular meeting gives Ben something he values even more than numbers: tone shift and human stories. Chair DeAngelis opens by saying this will be "another difficult budget year" requiring "creative ways to really save money" -- language that is both more honest and more politically exposed than anything Ben heard in December. When Finance Committee Chair Holman reports that 92% of funds are expended or encumbered at the 50% fiscal year mark and says flatly "we are likely to be in the red," Ben has the quotable reality check that cuts through bureaucratic hedging.

But the more immediate story overtakes the budget: children are missing school because their families fear ICE enforcement. Multiple speakers, including union president Connie DeSanto, report kids staying home and staff feeling unsafe coming to work. This is the kind of story that exists at the intersection of federal policy and local impact -- the exact territory where a community paper can add value that the Sentry or the Press Herald will miss. Ben knows his readers include both immigrant families and lifelong residents who may not realize their neighbors' kids are afraid to go to school.

The teachers union positioning is strategically interesting. SPTA president Sarah Gay says staff will not offer specific budget recommendations but are "very eager to provide informed feedback" on board proposals. Ben reads this as the union planting a flag: they want to be consulted, not blamed. If the board makes cuts without meaningful engagement, the union has built its public record of good faith. Ben files this away for later -- it will matter when the cuts land and the political blame game begins.

The superintendent search costs continue to creep upward. The board approves $3,500 for national advertising and up to $1,595 per finalist for background checks on top of the Zeal contract, and Richardson rightly asks why these were not included in the base price. This is the kind of process failure that makes Ben's readers cynical about how the district handles money -- not because the amounts are large, but because the pattern of undisclosed costs reinforces the December 10th story about a board that consistently underestimates what things will actually cost.
