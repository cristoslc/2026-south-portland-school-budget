# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

Ben has been waiting for the meeting where somebody in an official capacity says the quiet part out loud — where the immigration story and the school budget story, which he has been covering as parallel tracks since January 12, collide in a single vote. Matthews gives him that moment. She casts the lone no vote on $100,000 for Project Home rental assistance, and her reason is not that immigrant families don't deserve help. Her reason is that 72 school employees got pink slips the day before. That's the lede he's been waiting for: the city is making real-time choices between two legitimate community crises, and the same depleted fiscal position that underlies the school crisis is now the argument against helping neighbors who can't pay rent because they're afraid to leave their houses. Six votes say yes anyway. Ben is not writing about a villain here — he's writing about a city that has run out of easy answers.

The number discrepancy needs a call before anything goes to print. Matthews said 72 and $8.4 million. Everything Ben has from the school board since March 2 says 78 positions and "closer to $8 million." His working theory is that 72 is the first formal wave of reduction-in-force notices and more follow, and that $8.4M reflects the gap at a tax ceiling lower than 6% — but he wants that on record from the superintendent's office, not assembled from inference.

The sewer rate presentation is the story he didn't know he needed. He has been building the combined-household-cost synthesis since the January 13 Mahoney meeting — school tax plus potential bond, all landing on the same $514,000 home. Now there's a third line: 22% annual sewer rate increases for three straight years. Nobody in that room added it up. Nobody in any room this season has added it up. The school referendum is June 9. A public safety bond is headed to voters in November. Mahoney is now targeted for November 2027. And in the background, sewer bills are roughly doubling by FY29. Ben has a specific call to make — city finance director, business office — and a specific question: what does a median South Portland household actually owe, all in, by fiscal year 2029?

The calendar is the one clean thing in this meeting. April 7 public hearing, April 14 school department presents to council, May 5 council votes, June 9 referendum. He knows where he needs to be.
