# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

The Mahoney number is finally on the table, and it is worse than the signals suggested. I have been waiting for it since Tipton's inaugural in December — when he mentioned Mahoney, housing development, and comprehensive planning with the same breezy confidence, one week before the district disclosed a $1.9 million deficit. Tonight the architect stood up in front of the council and, when pressed directly by a councilor, said he is "not confident" in the $193 million estimate. That is not spin. That is the designer of the project disclaiming the headline figure the city is proposing to take to a November referendum. The unknowns driving that number — foundation load capacity untested, unreinforced masonry requiring full steel reinforcement throughout — are priced at worst case because nobody has done the actual subsurface work. The estimate is either right or it is low. There is no upside scenario without additional design investment.

The thread I opened on December 1 — does Tipton's growth agenda create compounding fiscal pressure on top of the school crisis — is now resolved. Definitively and badly. A $193 million bond the designer disavows, layered on top of $7-9 million in school cuts, all hitting the same property tax base. That is not a fiscal policy, it is a collision. And it gets worse: buried near the end of the meeting, the city manager confirmed the Pearl Street pump station is roughly $48-50 million in separate revenue bond debt that does not require voter approval and does not appear anywhere in the Mahoney cost comparison. The combined municipal debt picture is somewhere north of $240 million, and most of the people in that room were not adding those numbers together.

The finance director's funding analysis was actually the most useful thing in the room. Ellen Sanborn walked every mechanism — grants, TIF, tax credits, naming rights, property sales, local option sales tax — and the answer was bonds, every time, with everything else arriving as post-referendum debt service offsets. The best non-bond option requires state legislation a sitting representative called extremely unlikely. So the ballot question is: do you trust this $193 million number enough to borrow it?

The council adjourned after three hours without giving the design team a budget ceiling. The architect asked for one. Nobody gave him a number.
