# Writing Opinion Columns and Op-Eds: Techniques, Voice, and Audience

*Sources synthesized from: Elon University Writers Syndicate, Journalism University (mastering column writing), and web search results on opinion column techniques*

## What Is an Opinion Column?

Writing a column is the art of expressing a personal viewpoint on a specific topic, backed by facts and analysis, using a unique voice to engage and persuade readers. Unlike a straight news story, which focuses on the "who, what, when, and where," a column dives deep into the **"why" and "so what."**

Today, "op-ed" is used broadly to represent a column expressing the strong, informed, focused opinion of the writer on an issue of relevance to a targeted audience.

## Types of Opinion Pieces

| Type | Characteristics |
|------|----------------|
| **Editorial** | Unsigned; institutional voice; collective board position |
| **Op-ed** | Signed; guest writer; author's personal view |
| **Column** | Recurring; signed; specific journalist's ongoing voice |
| **Letter to the editor** | Reader response; brief; community voice |

## Developing Your Voice

You can't have a compelling column without a compelling voice. The key is **authenticity** — your voice should be a natural extension of how you think and talk.

- Voice range: contemplative, conversational, descriptive, experienced, informative, introspective, observant, humorous
- A good method: **read your column out loud** to test the rhythm and naturalness of your voice
- "Columns are most typically conversational in tone, so you can imagine yourself having a conversation with your reader as you write"

## Structure and Core Elements

A column is typically **750–800 words** and has:

1. **Hook/opening** — The first line is crucial. May grab attention with a strong claim, surprising fact, metaphor, mystery, or counter-intuitive observation
2. **Topic + theme** — Topic is stated in the first paragraph; theme is the overarching idea of the column
3. **Evidence and argument** — Grounded in solid research: facts, quotations, citations, data, or personal observation
4. **Brief counterargument acknowledgment** — mention opposing views while offering a rebuttal; keep it brief
5. **Conclusion** — Don't just repeat your thesis; bring the reader somewhere

## Writing Techniques

- **Active voice over passive** — "I believe..." is clearer than "It is believed..."
- **Show, don't tell** — Use active voice and compelling verbs; show through description and anecdote
- **Name names** — Avoid vague references like "some people say." Name specific sources
- **Acknowledge critics ethically** — Represent opposing arguments accurately; attacking a straw man is cheap; defeating a strong, accurately-stated argument is true persuasion
- **Be ethical** — Even when dismantling an opposing argument, represent it accurately

## Knowing Your Audience

- Write with a particular audience in mind: who are you trying to convince? Why that specific reader?
- Readers may not be familiar with your discipline — avoid jargon
- "Our readers expect sophisticated analysis that respects their intelligence while remaining accessible"
- Consider appropriate level of technical language, background information, and contextual framing

## Building a Niche and Consistency

- Most successful columnists build reputation within a specific **beat or niche** (politics, technology, community issues, local education)
- A key feature of a column is **regularity** — appearing on a consistent schedule builds a powerful relationship with readers
- The relationship between columnist and reader, built over time, is what distinguishes a column from a one-off op-ed

## Serving Civic Purposes

- The most influential opinion writing educates, contextualizes, and invites readers to contemplate complex issues — an approach that serves democratic values and community development
- "Quality opinion journalism is built on a commitment to fact-based journalism that contextualizes current events for audiences"
- Opinion writers have a responsibility to provide insightful analysis grounded in verifiable facts, even when expressing viewpoint
