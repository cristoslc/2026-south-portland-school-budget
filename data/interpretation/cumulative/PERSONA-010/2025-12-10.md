## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 10, 2025 -- 2025-12-10

### Interpretation

Two days after discovering the district's $1.9 million deficit crisis, Ben watches the school board make a decision that crystallizes everything wrong with their financial management. The board votes 5-2 to hire the most expensive superintendent search firm available — Zeal Education Group at $30,000-plus — while facing what's now revealed as a $7.2 million budget deficit requiring 78 position cuts. The irony is staggering: they're paying premium prices for "community engagement" services while the community room sits completely empty, despite Chair DeAngelo personally inviting the public at a city council meeting.

This meeting transforms Ben's understanding from December 8th's "planning failure" narrative into something more systemic — a board culture that consistently prioritizes aspirational spending over fiscal reality. When Interim Superintendent Whistle warns them the true cost will hit $35,000 with expenses, the board essentially ignores their own financial expert. Board member Richardson's argument that they shouldn't choose "the least expensive option because of" their previous superintendent's financial neglect reveals the backwards logic: they're using past financial mismanagement to justify current financial mismanagement.

The split vote tells the real story for Ben's readers. Members Holman and Rich clearly grasp the disconnect — Holman's "fancy car versus Toyota" analogy perfectly captures the choice taxpayers face. But they're outvoted by colleagues who seem convinced that expensive equals effective, even as they prepare to ask residents to absorb an 18% tax increase. The Maine School Management Association, already providing mentoring services and knowing local context, gets dismissed as "loosey goosey" in favor of out-of-state consultants promising consensus-building.

For Ben, this meeting provides the perfect follow-up story: how a district in financial crisis chooses to spend its way out of trouble rather than face hard choices about priorities.
