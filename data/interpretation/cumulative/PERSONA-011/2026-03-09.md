# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- March 9, 2026

### Interpretation

After the three-and-a-half-hour marathon a week ago where they presented nine reconfiguration models and the board blew up the superintendent's top recommendation, tonight felt like the eye of the storm. Routine business: policy approvals, field trip authorizations, enrollment report. The budget barely came up beyond a brief mention of "FY26-27 budget development" in the superintendent's report. Everyone's holding their breath for March 23rd.

The two items my networks actually care about from tonight: two more elementary special education teachers resigned (one at Brown leaving this Friday, one at Dyer leaving in August), and the board approved a new public comment policy that tightens the rules for speaking at meetings and eliminates back-and-forth between the public and board members. The timing on that public comment policy is terrible. We're heading into the most consequential budget decisions in a generation, parents already feel uninformed (85% said so in the survey last week), and now the board is making it harder to engage? That's going to land badly.

The special ed resignations add to the staffing bleed I've been tracking. Seven retirements announced in January, 78 positions being cut for next year, and now two more departures in the area where kids are most vulnerable -- special education. Brown and Dyer are both elementary schools that could be affected by whatever reconfiguration option survives, so these teachers may be leaving positions that get eliminated anyway. But in the meantime, those kids need coverage.

Enrollment ticked up 6 students to 2,754 total. That's noise, not signal. The structural decline I've been reporting -- 300+ students lost since 2017, 23% elementary drop -- isn't reversed by 6 kids. But it's slightly less bad than the trajectory, so I'll note it.

The field trips getting approved -- robotics competitions and All-State music -- actually matter to me because they show that normal high school life continues even as the budget crisis dominates every other conversation. My networks need to hear that some things are still working.

The temperature policy on first read is a small win. Having actual written standards for building temperatures (65-80 degrees) instead of the current "whatever the aging HVAC system produces" is progress, even if it's minor compared to everything else.

Bottom line: quiet meeting, everyone waiting for March 23rd. The real action is happening behind the scenes as the administration figures out which reconfiguration option to bring forward now that Skillin is protected and 85% of parents oppose the whole concept.
