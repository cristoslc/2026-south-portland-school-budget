# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 17, 2025 -- 2025-12-17

### Interpretation

The December 17 workshop confirmed my worst fears from the previous meetings. We now have actual numbers on the scale of cuts -- 78 positions, including 42 teachers and 16 educational technicians -- and the scope is staggering. What really crystallized for me is the connection between these cuts and the equity concerns I have been tracking since the beginning. Those educational technicians are the people who directly support our students with disabilities, our English learners, our kids who need one-on-one attention. Losing 16 of them is not a line item -- it is dismantling the support infrastructure for our most vulnerable students.

The per-pupil spending figure of $26,651 -- highest among comparable districts -- should be a sign that we are investing well, but I know that number masks enormous disparities across schools. Without a school-by-school breakdown, we have no way of knowing whether that spending is reaching Skillin or Kaler or whether it is concentrated in buildings serving more affluent families. The enrollment decline of 23% at the elementary level alongside an 82-position staffing increase is being weaponized as evidence of bloat, but nobody is asking who those additional staff serve. If the district added special education positions, multilingual specialists, and social workers to meet growing student complexity, that is not bloat -- that is meeting legal and moral obligations.

The state funding crisis -- 20% actual versus 55% statutory obligation -- is the structural driver of everything happening here. It forces a false choice between educational equity and property tax affordability, and the people caught in the middle are always the same: low-income families, renters, immigrants, and the students whose needs cost more to serve. I came away from this meeting understanding that the budget crisis is not just a local management failure but a systemic abandonment of public education by the state, and that organizing across districts may be the only path forward.
