# Cumulative Record: Kira (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

Nine days in, four meetings, and Kira is watching patterns crystallize in real time. She went to December 9 for one reason: DeAngelos was speaking at city council, and she wanted to hear what the school board chair would say to a different room. What she got confirmed two things she already suspected and introduced a third she couldn't have fully anticipated.

The confirmation she needed: budget season is officially open, the superintendent search starts tomorrow night, and both are happening at the same time. DeAngelos said it plainly — *61% of property taxes, engage now* — and the council absorbed it and moved on. For Kira, that moment of institutional indifference is its own data point. A school board chair had to walk across the street to a city council meeting to announce budget season because the information vacuum in the public arena is real enough that it required intervention. She noticed that. And the superintendent vacancy layered on top of it is the kind of operational vulnerability she recognizes from her buildings: decisions made without stable leadership tend to default to whatever is easiest to defend, not what is best for students most in need.

The development vote was the math lesson she didn't want. She let herself sit with the developer's promise for a moment — more housing, more tax base, relief for the school budget — because she wanted it to be true. Councilor West's question closed that door. Fifty percent rebate, thirty years. The assistant city manager's explanation made fiscal sense at an abstract level; it did nothing for the kids on her MTSS wait list right now. The developer's public statement about education funding will get quoted in community conversations, and it is not true in any timeframe that matters to her work or her children.

What landed hardest was the babysitting testimony. Two women, over forty dollars each, paid to speak at a Tuesday evening meeting. Kira opened this thread on December 4 — she named participation barriers as structural, she connected them to reconfiguration hearings — and now she has faces and dollar amounts to attach to the argument. Those are her families. The kids on her caseload at two of her three buildings. If the fiscal decisions ahead are as consequential as everything she's watched accumulate over these nine days suggests, and the families with the most at stake need a babysitter to even enter the conversation, then the "community process" language is doing a lot of work it cannot actually bear.
