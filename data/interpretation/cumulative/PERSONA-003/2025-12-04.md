# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

This meeting sits four days before the December 8 school board meeting that cracked open the budget crisis for me, and looking at it now from the other side of three months of school board trauma, what strikes me is how completely ordinary it was. The city council was doing city council things -- committee budgets with $2,500 caps, board appointments, affordable housing ordinances -- while the school district was four days away from revealing a $1.9 million deficit and a 23% enrollment collapse. The two governments were operating in completely separate realities.

The affordable housing zoning change is the one item that reads differently now than it would have in the moment. They passed an ordinance giving density relief to projects like Mill Cove apartments, which sounds like good news for families trying to stay in South Portland. And I still want it to be good news. But I now know from Mayor Tipton's inauguration speech three days earlier that the city's growth ambitions and the district's contraction are moving in opposite directions -- and the council in this meeting wasn't connecting those dots either. More affordable housing means more families, potentially more children, entering a school system that as of four days from now will be publicly acknowledging it can't afford to keep the teachers it has. Nobody in this room was thinking about that.

The Zoom public comment discussion is the part that would have frustrated me most if I'd watched it live. They rejected remote commenting because of trolling incidents and First Amendment complications -- and I understand the argument, I do -- but I've now watched three months of school board meetings where parents like me are functionally excluded by 9 PM runtimes and bedtime constraints. The city council just made that problem worse, not better. The "call us, email us" suggestion they substituted feels like a polite way of saying participation is for people with time and confidence. I have neither, most evenings.

The tree lighting announcement is the only moment where the city feels like it's actually thinking about families with small children. That's a low bar, and it tells me something about how much of city governance is genuinely irrelevant to my situation.
