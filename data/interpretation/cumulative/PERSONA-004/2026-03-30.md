# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

The budget failed. Five to two. I've been watching this board since December — the superintendent decides, the analysis follows, the board acquiesces, and the union testifies into the void. And Monday night five board members said no. DeAngelis, Feller, Richardson, Holman, Dowling. I need to sit with that before I explain why it doesn't mean what I want it to mean.

Because reconfiguration passed first. Four to two. Before the budget. Before the April 2 meeting. Before a single real conversation with SPTA about teacher assignments. Dr. Prince said it himself: "We have not been able to start that conversation with our teacher association yet because we don't yet have a decision." And now there's a decision. Option A — the Primary/Intermediate grade-band split — is adopted with four months to plan and zero answers about which teacher goes to which building. The meet-and-consult with the teachers' association was scheduled for March 31. The morning after. Not weeks ago when the reconfiguration models were being drafted. The morning after they voted. That sequencing is not a coincidence. I've been watching this governance philosophy since December 10, when the board chose a superintendent search firm specifically because it recommended keeping community members out of candidate screening. The vote comes first. The people who do the work find out second.

Sarah Gay put the survey on the record — 80% of members who responded said too many certified positions are cut, 77% said the process was rushed — and the board heard it and voted for restructuring anyway, then scheduled the teacher conversation for the following morning. The administration built this plan without us. Now they want us to implement it.

The DEI reclassification is the other thing I can't let go. Two moves in two weeks — Director to Coordinator, then Coordinator to "instructional strategist" in our bargaining unit — for a net claimed savings of eight thousand dollars and the loss of the district's only BIPOC leadership role. DeAngelis said it in the room: more lives affected, no meaningful savings. And under Article 18, if it's in our unit, it goes through the recall list first. The savings could be zero. So we reorganized the org chart twice, gutted a director position, called it a 23% administrative reduction, and may have accomplished nothing financially except making a recalled teacher's assignment more complicated.

April 2 is Thursday. The door is not closed. But reconfiguration is locked in, and I need every person who testified on March 30 to walk back into that building on Thursday and make clear that Thursday is the last chance to undo some of what passed last night.
