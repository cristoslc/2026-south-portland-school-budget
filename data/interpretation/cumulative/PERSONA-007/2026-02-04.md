# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: School Board Public Budget Forum -- February 4, 2026 -- 2026-02-04

### Interpretation

This forum was both better and worse than I expected. Better because the process feels solid -- Johanna presented the numbers clearly, the three big ideas are reasonable starting points, and the community engagement format actually worked for once. People were asking the right technical questions about class sizes and busing costs, which tells me they're taking this seriously and engaging substantively rather than just venting.

The 6% tax ceiling guidance from December is holding, which is critical for maintaining the board's credibility. Dr. Prince confirmed it remains the target, requiring $7.2M in cuts to avoid the 18.95% increase. That consistency matters when we're asking people to trust the process. And the elementary reconfiguration proposal stays within our existing class size guidelines -- average sizes moving from about 16 to 18-19 for K-2 and 22-23 for grades 3-4. That's defensible.

But that health insurance jump from 8% to 12% without any board discussion really bothers me. That's a $400K+ swing in our budget assumptions, and Abigail changed the projection based on signals from other districts without bringing it to the board first. We won't know the actual figure until late March, after we've already adopted the budget. We're making final cut decisions based on an assumption we can't verify. And losing the middle school bell time consolidation because of DOT permitting timelines eliminates a savings option that was supposed to be part of our plan.

The elephant in the room is the math: even with all three big ideas fully implemented -- elementary reconfiguration at roughly $2M, grade 5-12 efficiencies at roughly $1M, and operational changes at roughly $1M -- we're still looking at over $3M in unidentified cuts. That's where the real pain lives. Central office, supply budgets, contracts -- those are all politically harder to defend than reconfiguration. And Joe's observation about forum composition was spot-on: the room was full of educators and engaged parents, but the taxpayers without kids in school who could sink us at validation weren't there.
