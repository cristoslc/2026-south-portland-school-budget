# Cumulative Summary: Tom (PERSONA-006)

### Current Understanding

Tom understands the South Portland fiscal situation as four simultaneous cost pressures that no public body has ever added up and said aloud. The school district's structural deficit has grown from the initially revealed $1.9M (December 8) to $7.2M (December 17) and now $8.4M (March 19). The underlying causes — a 23% elementary enrollment drop over four years against 82 added staff positions, complete depletion of the fund balance, state funding covering only 20% of costs against a 55% statutory obligation, and two consecutive years of budget non-compliance — are now framed as self-made operational dysfunction, not structural bad luck. Seventy-two employees received layoff notices on March 18. Stacked on top: sewer rates rising roughly 22% annually for three consecutive years (bill climbing from ~$56 to ~$90/month) to fund the $51.7M Pearl Street pump station, with no voter approval required; an $87M police and fire bond targeting a November 2026 referendum; and a Mahoney City Center project with a bare-bones floor of $57-76M, deferred to a 2027 referendum.

Tom's view of city leadership has hardened into a working taxonomy: the council can hold on cost-shifting when the politics are easy but defaults to words over binding commitments on capital projects and politically charged spending. Councilor West is tracked separately as a credible but isolated fiscal voice. The mayor's spending-forward posture, set at her inauguration while the school crisis was already building, has never been offset by any enforceable fiscal guardrail. Tom remains supportive of public education as a property-value protector but demands outcome data justifying $26,651 per-pupil spending — the highest among comparable districts. His defining grievance: nobody in any room has been willing to add all four cost pressures together and say the total number out loud.
