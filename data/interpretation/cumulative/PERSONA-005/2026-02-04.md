# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Public Budget Forum -- February 4, 2026 -- 2026-02-04

### Interpretation

The budget forum was my first look at the elementary reconfiguration proposal, and it confirmed every concern I have had about how this district handles equity when money gets tight. They want to consolidate schools and increase class sizes by 20-40% in some grades -- moving from 16 to 18-19 students in K-2 and up to 22-23 in grades 3-4 -- but presented zero data on which schools currently have which class sizes, which demographics they serve, or how the changes would affect different populations of students. When someone asked about transportation impacts, the administrators admitted they had not modeled it. This is a massive restructuring being planned without the basic equity homework.

The revelation that current class sizes range from 10 to 24 students across schools is a critical data point. That variation almost certainly correlates with school demographics -- I would wager the smallest classes are not at the schools serving our newest Americans or our families in poverty. If the reconfiguration moves those 10-student classes to 18-student classes while already-large classes stay roughly the same, the equity impact is regressive even if it looks "equal" on paper. This is exactly the pattern where equity gets redefined as equality to avoid hard allocation decisions.

Board member Joe's acknowledgment that attendees were "teachers, administrators, ed techs, parents" but "not people representative of the majority of our community" was bracingly honest. Combined with the engagement crisis I tracked in January -- where immigrant families are afraid to participate publicly -- this confirms that the district is making decisions about which kids get what resources based on input from an unrepresentative subset of the community. The people most affected by these cuts are systematically excluded from the process that determines them. Special education complexity is growing with new state mandates for 3-5 year olds, which means additional resource demands, yet the district is simultaneously reducing the support staff who serve these populations. The ed tech cuts are especially alarming given that these are the positions that directly support students with IEPs, 504 plans, and English language needs.
