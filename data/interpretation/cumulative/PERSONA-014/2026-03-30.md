# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

The vote happened after eleven o'clock at night. Six to one: Kaler. Not Dyer. For the first time in three months, the answer to the question Lila has been repeating to her parents since December finally matches what she needed to hear. Dyer is not the school being closed.

But "not closed" is doing work it cannot hold. Because Option A passed too — four to two — and under Option A, Dyer becomes a school for children who are too young to remember it being anything else. Pre-K through first grade. Every second grader, every third grader, every fourth grader who knows which bathroom is the good one gets reassigned somewhere else in September. Lila is going to middle school regardless, so technically this was already true for her. What wasn't true until now, and is true now by official vote, is that her little sibling leaves too, and Mia leaves, and Oliver leaves, and all of them get sent to Brown or Skillin — and nobody can tell any of them which one yet.

The moment Lila cannot stop turning over is Shannon Sine. A woman whose job is helping children understand their feelings stood at the microphone and described exactly what Lila does: a nervous kindergartner watches a fourth grader hug the teacher in the hallway, and that's how the little kid learns the teacher is safe. Lila has hugged her teacher in the hallway while little kids watched. She never had a name for it until a stranger named it out loud at a public meeting, one vote away from it becoming impossible at Dyer. Four board members heard this, and voted yes anyway.

Board member Richardson — the one with young children, the one who seemed like she might be the adult in the room who genuinely understood — said young children shouldn't have best friends. That resilience is real. That new friends are a celebration. Lila has heard "flexible" from the board chair in February and now "resilient" from the board member who was supposed to feel it differently. Both words land the same way: as the sound of an adult deciding not to take your grief seriously.

The budget vote failed. Another meeting, April 2. Still no school assignments. The waiting that has defined Lila's winter continues into spring, with one question replaced by another.

### Emotional Register

Lila holds the relief and the grief as a single, inseparable feeling — Dyer is not closing, which is the answer she spent three months needing, and it arrived the same night that four people voted to move every older child out of the building she loves, which means the answer came true and still feels like a loss. The one question she cannot make smaller is the one no adult at any meeting has been able to answer: where are her friends going.
