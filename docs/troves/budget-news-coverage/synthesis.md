## News narrative

Both articles report on the same event: a March 23, 2026 South Portland school board budget workshop at which district leadership proposed closing James O. Kaler Elementary School as part of the FY27 budget. The two outlets converge on the same core facts but offer different depth.

### The closure proposal

The school department presented Kaler closure as the preferred path to avoid additional staff cuts beyond the roughly 70 positions (including 30 teaching roles) already proposed in the $76 million FY27 budget — a 12% staff reduction. Keeping all five elementary schools open would require cutting 12-16 more positions. The closure is framed as a fiscal necessity driven by a goal of keeping the property tax increase below 6%.

### Two reconfiguration options on the table

The board is not yet committed to a closure, nor to which reconfiguration model to adopt. The two options presented:

1. **Grade-level reconfiguration** — Dyer and Small become pre-K/grade 1 schools; Brown and Skillin become grades 2-4. Saves $3.1 million in year one, $16.4 million over five years. Promotes demographic equity and grade-level collaboration but requires significant logistics and community adjustment.

2. **Redistricting only** — Keep K-4 at all four remaining schools, redraw attendance boundaries. Saves $2.1 million in year one, $12.8 million over five years. Less disruptive but less efficient, and leaves structural demographic disparities in place.

### Community response and equity concerns

The workshop was packed and emotional. Kaler's demographics surfaced as a central equity concern: the school is described as the district's second most diverse elementary school — more than 50% students of color, more than 50% free/reduced lunch eligible, 30-35% multilingual learners. Community members raised concerns that the process was rushed (the presentation was posted to the district website only three days before the meeting) and that Kaler had not been on the community's radar as a closure candidate until that moment.

The board had previously considered closing Skillin (reversed because it is the most diverse school) and Dyer (the proposal two weeks prior), before arriving at Kaler. This pattern of shifting targets is a recurring theme in community reaction.
