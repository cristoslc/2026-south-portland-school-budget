# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

The three testimonies read by Margot Kralik and Emily Hansen are where I keep landing when I think about this meeting. A detained man in a freezing room with shoes that did not fit, no medical care, an eviction letter waiting when he got out. A parent who watched her children fall apart from fear and lost income because she could not leave them home alone. A woman who spent rent money on a lawyer for her detained husband. These are South Portland residents. Their stories are in the public record. And they could not be there to tell them, because standing at a city hall microphone in your own city is not safe when federal enforcement is circling your neighborhood. That is the community engagement exclusion thread I have been tracking since December, fully arrived: not apathy, not logistics, but structural fear so complete that the most affected people need intermediaries to speak their own experiences into a city record. The meeting technically received their voices. It did not receive them.

The GA finding is the structural revelation of this meeting and I want to say it plainly: Chris Pupke confirmed on the record that the state told the city that missing work out of fear of ICE enforcement does not constitute just cause for General Assistance eligibility. Since February 3 I have been tracking the catch-22 — that any formal mechanism requiring institutional contact was structurally unavailable because visibility creates enforcement risk. March 10 adds a second, independent layer of exclusion: even if a family could safely apply, the state has pre-defined their harm out of the eligibility criteria. This is not one barrier. It is a system with redundant failures, each one positioned to catch whoever the previous one missed.

West's arrest-count framing is a precedent I am watching. "Less than five people were arrested in South Portland" as a measure of communal disruption is the same logic as requiring proof of harm from people who cannot safely produce documentation. The bike share vote is its twin: genuine national equity research — bike share schemes disproportionately serve young, white, higher-income men — deployed not to demand equity-centered design but to close the program and the question at the same time. That is now the fourth distinct council failure mode I have documented: erasure, inversion, weaponization, and now equity-as-termination. The city's commitment to no immigration status disclosure in the rental program is real, and I want it in writing before the March 19 vote. It is the most direct structural protection this body has offered since the moratorium failed. Knowing the $100,000 passed nine days later does not make the $68,000 gap — approximately 32 households — feel smaller.
