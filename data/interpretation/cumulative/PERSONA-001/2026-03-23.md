# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Five hours and thirty minutes in that room, and she left without knowing what school her daughter will attend in September.

The meeting finally did one thing Maria has been waiting for since the nine-option matrix arrived at March 2: it named a school. Kayler. The administration said they ran the analysis twice and both times it pointed to Kayler — safety egress, building layout, proximity. Maria expected the naming to feel like something. Clarity, maybe, or relief at having a target to argue about. What it actually feels like is grief. Kayler is not her daughter's school. But Kayler is 45% BIPOC and 30-35% multilingual learners, and a parent named Jess Elsner stood up near midnight and asked whether closing that school might violate Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. The board chair — the same chair who made immigrant belonging the centerpiece of her December 1 inauguration, the same chair who argued at March 2 to protect Skillin because of its immigrant families — said no one present had a legal answer. Deferred. No timeline.

Maria has been cataloging the gap between DeAngelis's equity rhetoric and her governance choices since December. The Kayler moment is not a new entry in that catalog. It is the catalog.

Two options survived: Option A, the grade-band campus plan; Option B, neighborhood K-4 structure at four schools. Kayler closes in both. What changes is how the remaining children are sorted. Class sizes go up in both. Maria already knew this — the February 10 joint workshop confirmed it — but hearing it confirmed again, with the caveat that uneven grade enrollment could push some classrooms to the policy ceiling, is not easier the second time. The support staff who might have managed that crowding are mostly gone: ed techs, behavioral support, and now all seven lunch aides, replaced on a one-in-five rotation by bus drivers.

Richardson asked the question Maria has been carrying all winter: can class size policy account for how many IEPs are in a room? The answer was yes, technically — but not without cutting something else or going above 6%. It is the first time one of the four new board members sworn in on December 1 has named the specific harm Maria tracks. She gives Richardson that. It is not enough, but it is real.

The board adjourned at 11 PM without a vote. March 30 is next. She has the date.
