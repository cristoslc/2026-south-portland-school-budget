# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

The March 30 meeting inverts March 23's structure. At the workshop, the board deliberated five hours and voted on nothing. Tonight the board voted on everything — closure, reconfiguration, budget — and the sequence is the story: the first two passed, the third fell apart two-to-five at eleven o'clock.

That's the lede, and Ben knows it. Kaler gets closed, six to one — DeAngelis the sole holdout. Option A gets approved, four to two — Feller and Richardson believe in reconfiguration but won't vote for a plan that doesn't exist yet. And then the budget itself fails, and everyone's reason is different: the DEI position change for DeAngelis, special education cuts for others, wanting a city council conversation first for still others. The fractures are real, and they're not pointing the same direction. The five-person no vote is not a unified opposition. It is a coalition of incompatible objections that happened to produce the same outcome.

Feller's comparison table is what Ben has been assembling in fragments since December 17 — South Portland at $26,651 against Gorham at $20,000, Windham at $21,600, Brunswick at $24,000, Portland at less per pupil with twice the enrollment and higher need. That data finally exists in quotable form, delivered by a board member during a school closure vote, not by the district in a budget presentation. The gap between what the administration chooses to surface and what a board member has to surface himself is now embedded in the record.

The DEI director situation is the one he cannot put down. Three demotions in three weeks — director to coordinator to teaching-unit strategist — with roughly $8,000 in marginal savings that the administration immediately redirected to facility reserves. Net fiscal gain: approximately zero. The person displaced is identified on the record as the district's only BIPOC person in a leadership role. The board that framed every reconfiguration discussion in equity terms across two months of public hearings ratified the effective elimination of its only equity-focused BIPOC leader at negligible savings. That's not Ben's framing. It's the record's own. And he needs to find this person before he can write it.

The chair's end-of-meeting correction resets something Ben watched accumulate through the night: the city has no money to give the district. The only mechanism is a short-term loan from a fund balance that has been drawing down since March 19. He already knew that. Half the room apparently did not. The April 7 council presentation is now the meeting that matters — featuring a district asking for guidance on a budget the board itself just voted down.
