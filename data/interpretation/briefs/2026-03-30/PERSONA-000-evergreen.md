# General Budget Briefing
## Current Budget Picture

## Budget Snapshot

The South Portland School Department has proposed a Fiscal Year 2027 (school year 2026–27) operating budget of **$75,850,000**, which includes approximately $75.6 million for K–12 programs and $250,000 for adult education. This represents a **3.3% overall increase** from the current year's budget, and is described by district finance staff as the smallest operating increase in at least five years.

The budget is at the **school board proposal stage**. The board held a major public workshop on March 23, 2026, and is scheduled to hold another on March 30. The proposal must then be presented to the City Council on April 7 and formally approved through a public referendum scheduled for June 9, 2026.

The most significant structural element of the proposal is **closing one elementary school**—Kayler School—to help close a financial gap created by declining enrollment, depletion of the district's savings reserve, and rising labor and utility costs. The district's finance director described the situation as reaching a "financial cliff," driven by a long-term misalignment between staffing levels and declining enrollment, the end of federal COVID-relief funding, and leadership instability (seven finance directors in six years).

Key confirmed numbers from the budget:
- **Local property tax revenue increase:** 6.0% (the cap set by City Council)
- **Estimated annual tax impact:** approximately $257 per year for the owner of a home at South Portland's average assessed value of $514,000
- **Total staff reduction:** approximately 12% of the district workforce
- **32 teachers** are currently on a recall list (no position in FY27 but eligible for future openings); **11 additional teachers** have been offered a different position than they currently hold
- The district projects a **FY26 deficit** in the current school year; no savings reserve ("fund balance") currently exists

## What Changed

Several elements of the proposal shifted between earlier budget discussions and the March 23 workshop:

**Pre-K expansion dropped from the local budget.** An earlier version of the superintendent's proposal included expanding locally-funded pre-kindergarten. That has been removed. The FY27 budget maintains 64 locally-funded pre-K seats—the same as the current year. However, the district says it expects to add 40 additional pre-K seats through non-local funding: 8 seats through the state's Child Development Services program, 16 through a United Way off-site classroom, and 16 through a Head Start partnership. Those additions would not draw on local tax dollars.

**The school recommended for closure shifted to Kayler.** Earlier in the budget process, Skillen School was the starting point for closure analysis due to its documented long-term facility needs. After community and board feedback, the analysis expanded to all five buildings. Brown and Small were removed from consideration because their closure would require more use of portable classrooms. The district then conducted a formal analysis of Dyer and Kayler on six domains and ran it twice; both analyses pointed to Kayler. The change from an earlier period when Dyer appeared under discussion generated significant community reaction.

**The curriculum and instruction leadership structure changed.** The Director of Curriculum, Instruction, and Assessment position is being eliminated. Ellen Ferrick, who held that role, resigned effective June 30, 2026. The district is replacing the position with a "curriculum council" led by the assistant superintendent. This change was not in earlier iterations of the proposal.

**DEI director role is under review.** The proposal includes potentially converting the Director of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion position to a "strategist" role embedded with a team of content experts rather than serving as a standalone director. This was described at the March 23 meeting as still under consideration, not yet finalized.

## Major Decisions Ahead

**1. Whether to close Kayler School — and authorize the superintendent to file a school closing report with the Maine Commissioner of Education.** This is a required formal step under state law. The board had this decision on its March 23 special meeting agenda.

**2. Which of two elementary grade-configuration options to adopt for fall 2026:**
- **Option A (Primary/Intermediate model):** Two schools would serve pre-K through grade 1; two schools would serve grades 2–4. This is described by district leadership as better for equity and resource efficiency but involves a compressed implementation timeline and more transitions for some families.
- **Option B (Full grade band model):** All four remaining elementary schools would serve K through grade 4 (with two also offering pre-K). This involves fewer changes for most families immediately, but district leadership says it does not advance equity goals and may require further restructuring in future years.

District leadership supports moving toward Option A, but acknowledges Option B could serve as a step toward Option A the following year. The board cannot bind future boards to implement Option A.

**3. Formally adopting the FY27 superintendent's budget as the board's proposal to submit to City Council.** This proposal must go to City Council for a workshop on April 7 and a vote scheduled for May 5.

## Open Questions

- **Will Option A or Option B be selected?** The board had not voted as of the March 23 meeting; another workshop was scheduled for March 30.
- **How will school boundaries be redrawn?** Under either option, redistricting decisions that affect which students attend which school have not been finalized. The district said transportation modeling cannot be completed until the board selects an option.
- **What is the plan for the projected FY26 deficit?** The district acknowledged it may close the current school year in deficit. If so, it would by default draw on city funds and repay in a future year, likely through a tax increase. No formal plan has been adopted.
- **How and when will a minimum fund balance policy be established?** The finance director explicitly said this is needed to prevent a repeat of the current situation, but no policy exists and nothing in the FY27 budget seeds a reserve.
- **What will LD 2666 (state school funding formula legislation) mean for South Portland?** Nine provisions of the statute would change how state education aid is calculated. As of March 23, only two of the nine provisions had been modeled. The district noted South Portland could receive more or less state funding; the outcome is unknown.
- **Will two pending community school expansion grants total $100,000 be received?** These are the only outstanding grant applications identified at the March 23 meeting; their outcomes are unknown.
- **How will required "meet and consult" sessions with four bargaining units conclude?** The district must consult with all four employee unions on changes to working conditions before the school year begins. What changes those discussions might produce—and at what cost—is not yet known.
- **What will happen with the DEI director position?** The transition to a "strategist" role was described as still under review as of March 23.
- **Will the board request more than 6% in tax revenue from the City Council?** The finance director recommended against requesting more than 6% because any amount above that would need to be sustained in every future year. The board had not voted on this question.
- **What is the Skillen boiler situation?** The district flagged potential new debt in FY28 depending on the outcome of a boiler assessment. No decision has been made.
