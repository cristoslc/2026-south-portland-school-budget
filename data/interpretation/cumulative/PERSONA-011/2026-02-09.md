# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

Five days after the budget forum laid out the $7.2M gap and the elementary reconfiguration proposal, this meeting moved the pieces forward in ways my group chats are going to have strong feelings about. The board unanimously voted to request a formal reconfiguration proposal for the March 2 workshop. That's not a study, not a maybe -- that's "bring us the plan." Chair DeAngelis said she's "totally in support" and thinks "this is good for kids," adding that parents have more anxiety about change than children do. That last part is going to land badly in my parent networks because it reads as dismissive of legitimate concerns about upending elementary school logistics.

The finance news got worse again. All three budget scenarios now show the district ending this year in deficit -- not just next year, THIS year. The new wrinkle is a $1.1 million nutrition fund shortfall that the school fund has to absorb. So on top of the spending freeze, on top of the 78 positions being cut for next year, the current year books are underwater too. Every time I think I've reported the bottom, they find a new hole.

Here's the bombshell that caught me off guard: Board Member Feller said he'd now be "more comfortable with something exceeding 6% than I was before" because cutting $7 million "in a way that's not draconic" isn't realistic. The 6% tax ceiling that the board promised -- the number I've been reporting as the constraint driving all these cuts -- may not hold. If the ceiling moves, everything changes: the size of the cuts, the reconfiguration timeline, the political dynamic. My group chats have been planning around 6%. If that changes, I need to be the first to explain why.

The teachers union president asked for "a more precise target tax percent" instead of the vague 6% ceiling, and you could hear the anxiety in her voice about staff losing their jobs. There are 41 people eligible for early retirement, and Feller calculated that if half took enhanced buyouts at $50K savings per person, that's over a million dollars in relief. That feels like the obvious move and I'm surprised it isn't already happening.

The one bright spot: community school expansion passed unanimously. The high school will get the wraparound services (mental health, family resources) that the middle school has been piloting. Also, student board members got approved to participate in superintendent interviews, which is a nice gesture even if it doesn't change outcomes.
