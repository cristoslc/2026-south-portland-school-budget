# Cumulative Summary: Ben (PERSONA-010)

### Current Understanding

Ben has tracked the South Portland school budget crisis across seventeen meetings from inauguration night through mid-March. The district faces an ~$8M structural gap — grown from an initial $1.9M mid-year deficit disclosure in December — driven by three compounding failures: elementary enrollment fell 23% (from 1,401 to 1,080 students) while staffing grew by 82 positions; state education funding covers only ~20% of actual costs against a 55% statutory obligation; and a revolving finance leadership door (six directors in five years, three interim) made institutional knowledge impossible to sustain, producing a repeat audit finding of "material weakness," 34 corrective journal entries, and $400K in mismanaged grants. A roll-forward budget would require an 18.95% tax increase; the board's self-imposed 6% ceiling — whose public origin Ben cannot trace to any documented vote — leaves a gap no currently viable solution closes. The administration's top reconfiguration recommendation (closing Skillin Elementary) was dead on arrival, opposed by at least four board members on equity grounds. The teachers union refuses to endorse plans eliminating colleagues. The 6% ceiling is cracking politically, while city council guidance of 3-4% pushes the effective gap still wider. A hidden $1.1M nutrition fund deficit and a fully depleted school fund balance ($739K, functionally zero) leave no cushion if any projection breaks wrong.

The crisis runs parallel to a city-level fiscal reckoning: Mahoney consolidation priced at an unanticipated $194M the architect cannot vouch for; city department requests already totaling a 15% levy increase before schools are added; and TIF mechanics that yield negligible net fiscal benefit to schools for decades. Ben has identified that no elected body has presented the combined school-and-city tax impact on the average South Portland homeowner in a single number — a gap he considers the missing lede of referendum season. September 2026 implementation of any reconfiguration is increasingly unrealistic after nine meetings produced no voted decisions on the core structural choices.
