# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

This meeting happened before Lila knew anything was wrong. She didn't know yet about the spending freeze, or the $1.9 million shortfall, or that in a few weeks a board member would formally request a plan to close her school. If she had watched this meeting in early December, she might have come away feeling safe — a girl her own age reading a poem about South Portland being a wonderful place for kids, people who traveled from far away joining the community, a school board chair telling warm stories about remembering students for years and running into them at the grocery store.

But knowing everything that came after, certain moments land differently. Mayor Tipton's story about the families in 1942 — given only nine days to pack up and leave their homes because the government needed the land for the shipyard — feels like a story about Lila now. "Can you imagine being given just nine days for that?" she asks. Lila can imagine it in a way she couldn't have at the start of fourth grade. It's a history lesson about her own city, but it's also a map of exactly how it feels when adults tell you that the place you belong doesn't get to be yours anymore. Nine days, or nine months — the feeling is the same.

The school board meeting at the end of the evening brought in the new chair, someone telling warm stories about never forgetting her students. Those stories sounded like a promise: even when schools change, teachers carry their kids with them. That promise felt good to hear. But Lila knows now what kind of language the chair uses when she talks about kids facing change — words like "flexible," assurances that disruption is "good for kids" — and the warm stories feel more complicated in retrospect.

Jules's poem — about South Portland's fourteen square miles as a community of care where neighbors look out for each other — was beautiful. It just describes a version of the city that Lila has spent three months watching come under pressure.

This meeting is the before. The last official record before the crisis became real, before the room got scary, before Lila started watching her parents come home from meetings tense and unwilling to share what happened. It doesn't explain anything that followed. But it holds the shape of what Lila thought her world was.
