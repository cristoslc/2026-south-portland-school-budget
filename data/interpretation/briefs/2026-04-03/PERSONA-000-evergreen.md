## Budget Snapshot

South Portland Schools is proposing a **$75,850,000 budget for FY27** (the school year starting July 1, 2026). That covers grades Pre-K through 12 and adult education.

If the budget passes as proposed, the local tax contribution would rise by **6.0%**. For a home assessed at $514,000 — roughly the city average — that works out to about **$257 more per year** in property taxes.

To reach that number, the district has already cut roughly **70 positions** — about 12% of the total workforce — through retirements, resignations, and planned eliminations. The cuts fall across teaching staff, educational technicians, and support roles.

Two structural decisions have been made by the board:

- **Kaler Elementary will close** at the end of the 2025–26 school year (board vote: 5-1, March 30).
- **Elementary schools will be reorganized under "Option A"**: one set of schools will serve Pre-K and grades K–1, and another will serve grades 2–4 (board vote: 4-2, March 30).

**The budget itself has not been adopted.** As of April 2, the board voted unanimously to present the budget to the City Council and gather guidance before taking a final vote. That is where the process stands today.

## What Changed

**Between March 23 and April 2, the pace of decisions accelerated.**

At the **March 23 budget workshop**, the superintendent presented the full proposed budget for the first time. No votes were taken. The board reviewed two options for elementary reconfiguration and heard the criteria used to evaluate the Kaler closure.

At the **March 30 regular meeting**, the board took three significant votes:
1. Closed Kaler Elementary (5-1; Member Dean opposed).
2. Chose Option A for elementary reconfiguration (4-2; Members Feller and Richardson opposed).
3. Declined to pass the full budget (5-2; only Members Smith and Risch voted in favor).

At the **April 2 special budget meeting**, the board did not adopt the budget. Instead, it voted unanimously to meet with the City Council before proceeding. The board also removed a proposed change to early-release days from the agenda at the start of that meeting — that item was tabled without action.

Also at the April 2 meeting, two pieces of **potential new state funding** were raised:
- The SSPA president mentioned the district may be eligible for up to **$300,000** tied to homeless and economically disadvantaged student counts — money not previously included in the budget.
- Board Member Richardson shared information suggesting the state's EPS formula may produce up to **$750,000 more** than currently projected.

Neither figure is confirmed. Both are being investigated.

## Major Decisions Ahead

**1. Will the board revise the budget before voting?**
The board has not yet adopted a budget. Before it does, it is waiting to hear from the City Council and to learn whether the possible new state funds ($300k and/or $750k) are real. If either amount is confirmed, the board could reduce the local tax ask — or restore some of the cut positions.

**2. What will the City Council say?**
The council is scheduled to receive the budget on **April 7** and hold a workshop on **April 14**. The council does not adopt the school budget, but it must vote to include it on the property tax warrant. If the council signals concerns, the board could face pressure to revise. The council vote is scheduled for **May 5**.

**3. Will the board build a reserve fund?**
The current budget carries a zero fund balance. The standard recommendation is to maintain a reserve equal to 9–12% of operating costs. The board has discussed this gap but has not committed to building a reserve in FY27.

**4. What happens to displaced staff and students?**
Collective bargaining and meet-and-consult obligations with three employee associations (SPTA, SSPA, SEA) are ongoing. Final staffing assignments following the Kaler closure and reconfiguration have not been announced. Families do not yet know which schools their children will attend under Option A.

**5. Public referendum — June 9.**
Maine law requires the school budget to go to a public vote. That date is June 9. The board must pass a budget, and the council must vote to place it on the warrant, in time to meet required 30-day public notice before that date. If either step is delayed, the timeline tightens significantly.

## Open Questions

- **How much new state money will actually arrive?** The $300k and $750k figures were raised at the April 2 meeting but are unconfirmed. The district has not published revised revenue projections.
- **Which positions, if any, will be restored?** If new state funds come through, no decision has been announced about whether cuts would be reversed or what criteria would be used.
- **Where will Kaler students and staff go?** The board approved the closure and reconfiguration model, but building-specific assignments have not been finalized.
- **What is the reconfiguration implementation plan?** Option A was approved but the detailed plan for which existing school buildings will serve which grade bands has not been publicly released.
- **Will the fund balance remain at zero?** No commitment to begin building a reserve has been made.
- **How will collective bargaining affect final costs?** Negotiations with the three employee associations are continuing. Outcomes could affect salary and benefit lines in the final budget.
- **What is the board's timeline for a final vote?** No special meeting date has been announced as of April 2. The council presentation is April 7, but the board's next action date is unconfirmed.
