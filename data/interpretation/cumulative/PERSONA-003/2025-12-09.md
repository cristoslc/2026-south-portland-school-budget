# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

This meeting is almost two hours long. Schools get two minutes of it.

I'm watching it now from the other side of three months of budget crisis, and that two-minute ratio is the most clarifying thing about December 9. DeAngelis walks to the microphone during public comment, announces that budget season will begin this month, says to watch the school district website, and sits back down. No councilor asks what budget season will involve. Nobody says: how big is the gap, how many positions are on the table, what's going to change? The $7.2 million shortfall and 78 positions were already forming in the district's internal deliberations by this date -- but from this chamber, you'd never know anything was coming. The council spent the rest of the evening on property assessment methodology and a zoning dispute over whether a building in Mill Creek needs its parking on the surface or inside a structure. I have now watched nine months of school board meetings where those numbers have consumed every conversation, and here they are, unborn, slipping past a council that didn't even think to ask.

What actually stopped me, though, is Zenya and Carly. Two parents paid over $40 for a babysitter just so they could both come and speak. Zenya explained that watching virtually without being able to comment doesn't feel like real participation -- that she wanted to actually be there. I watched this on my phone during Mia's nap and felt completely seen and completely terrified at the same time. Four days earlier, on December 4, the council rejected Zoom comments because of trolling concerns. I understood the argument. But seeing parents count out the cost of childcare just to claim two minutes of airtime makes the Zoom decision feel less like a policy choice and more like a structural declaration about whose schedule civic life accommodates. If Zenya and Carly -- organized, clearly paying attention, living near a councilor -- are still facing $40 cover charges to participate, then "watch the school website" is an instruction for people who don't have toddlers.

The 208-unit housing development gets approved 7-0. The developer says it will help balance the budget and benefit education. No one asks how. No enrollment analysis, no school capacity question, nothing. This is the third consecutive city meeting where new housing and school contraction are moving in opposite directions and nobody in the room is checking whether they're on a collision course. The thread I've been watching since December 1 keeps getting more evidence and no engagement.

The 61% figure -- schools are 61% of the property tax bill -- gets offered by DeAngelis and absorbed without follow-up. She'll say it again in January, at the first school board meeting where the budget crisis is fully public. Nobody will follow up then either.
