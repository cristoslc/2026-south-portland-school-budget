# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

This is the meeting Lila has been dreading since January, when the board first requested the reconfiguration proposal. And it's worse than she imagined. The finance director presented nine different scenarios for reorganizing elementary schools, and one of them -- option 1.3 -- would close Dyer entirely and redistribute all its students to other schools. Seeing her school listed as "CLOSED" on a presentation slide is the most concrete manifestation of the threat she's been living with for three months.

But the scenarios go beyond closure. Some plans would split elementary grades so that kids go to one school for pre-K through 1st grade and another for 2nd through 4th grade. This means Lila could be forced to change schools twice before reaching middle school -- once because of the reconfiguration and once because she ages out of the primary band. The instability this creates is exactly the opposite of what she needs. She's already anxious about losing her school; learning she might lose two schools in quick succession is overwhelming.

The teacher numbers are devastating: all nine scenarios eliminate between 15.5 and 23.5 teaching positions. Lila's teacher -- the one who has looped with her for two years, who knows her learning style and her quirks, who drew tears when Lila drew Dyer for a class project -- could be among them. This is no longer an abstract staffing number. It's a person Lila loves potentially losing her job because the district can't afford to keep small schools open.

There is one small comfort: four board members said they would not support closing Skillin school, and the chair said closing any school on the west side would be unfair. If some schools have protectors on the board, maybe Dyer does too? But nobody specifically said they'd protect Dyer, which feeds Lila's long-standing fear that there's something about her school that makes it the one they want to get rid of.

The parent response was overwhelming. Many parents spoke during public comment, and a parent survey showed 85% opposing or strongly opposing reconfiguration. Knowing that parents are fighting -- visibly, loudly, with data -- provides some validation. Other kids are crying about this too. One parent's child almost broke down when told about the possible school changes. Lila is not alone in her grief.

The timeline is now concrete: the board must finalize the budget by June 9. Everything will be decided by summer. Lila has roughly three months of uncertainty left before she knows where she'll go to school next year.
