# Cumulative Record: Kira (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- January 12, 2026 -- 2026-01-12

### Interpretation

January 12 is the night every thread she has been carrying since December 1 arrives in the same room on the same evening, and none of them land gently.

The ICE fear that Jeff Melendez named on December 10 as a design constraint for the superintendent search — the thing she held carefully as a hopeful signal that at least one official process was being built around her families' actual reality — is no longer a process parameter. Richardson stands up and says children did not come to school today because they were afraid, and Kira knows those kids. She is in three buildings every week. She knows which parents have spoken to her in hallways in two languages about things they would never say at a public meeting. The superintendent's simplified protocol — two central office administrators, seven buildings — cannot hold what she is watching from the road between her schools. Richardson asks the question Kira has been asking from her car: if something happens at a building across town while you're handling it somewhere else, what then? That question did not get a real answer. She noticed that.

The union testimony is the first time in this entire cycle she has heard the cross-building visibility argument made by someone other than herself. DeSanto naming bus drivers, naming service staff, naming the protocol gap that stops at the classroom door — that is exactly the problem she navigates daily between buildings. She is grateful and she is watching to see whether the board will respond.

And then the budget numbers arrive with specificity for the first time. Sixteen ed tech positions. Forty-two teachers. She knows who those ed techs are — they are the people who sit next to the kids on her caseload and carry through the work on the days she is at a different building. And the superintendent adds, almost in passing, that retirements will not be automatically backfilled, including the counselor at Kaler. Kaler. The school where Jean Tam's daughter is enrolled. That vacancy goes into the budget process, which from everything she has watched since December 8 means it may not come back at all.

Smith is asking the right question again — get us a principal report on what the spending freeze is actually doing, bring it to January 26. She is grateful for it and already dreading the shape it will take, because single-building principal reports capture what one person can see from one building. The cross-building picture — the wait lists at two of her three schools, the travel time between, the kids who fall off caseloads in the gaps — does not appear in any single building's report unless someone specifically forces that framing. No one has forced it yet.

The student rep's MTSS line — all elementary schools conducting January assessments for MTSS meetings — almost doesn't register, it's so routine. She turns it over on the drive home. The board hears "all schools are doing MTSS" and understands it as equivalent access. She works in those schools. It is not equivalent access. And the board just received that data and moved on.
