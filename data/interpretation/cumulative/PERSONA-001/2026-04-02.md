# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

April 2 is the first meeting in four months where the board heard parents and actually moved — and Maria left more frightened than she arrived.

The half-day early release days getting pulled at the start, unanimously, before anyone had to fight for it — that was real. Maria has been cataloging the gap between participation and response since December 4, when the council formally acknowledged the participation infrastructure was broken and kept moving. Tonight the board removed an item because parents pushed back. She took thirty seconds to feel it. Then the numbers went on the screen.

Thirteen elementary teachers. That is the number she keeps returning to on the drive home. Not 78. Not 42. Thirteen — the figure that maps to her daughter's building, her daughter's hallway, the teachers her daughter knows by name. She has been calculating this specific number since March 2, when nine reconfiguration options appeared and she started doing her own building-level math. Now she has it, and it doesn't help.

What she cannot stop replaying is Jenna Goldstein Walsh's statement read aloud at the microphone — the behavior specialist who works directly with nearly 60 elementary students across four schools, running formal behavior plans for kids who are struggling but not yet in special education. Eliminated. The administration's answer is that BCBAs and instructional strategists will absorb the work. Maria has been tracking the interlocking support infrastructure since December 17, when she first saw the 16 ed techs on the list and understood these roles are not interchangeable. The behavioral strategist cut crystallized at March 23 into "none full time." Tonight it has a name and sixty children attached to it, and an FLS teacher at Skillin describing ending her school day in a physical restraint because her backup was already gone.

The $300,000 from the state — unions driving to Augusta on their own time, making it happen mid-meeting — is the one thing she is putting in the parent group chat tonight. People need something. She needs something. But the board didn't vote on the budget. Again. Monday, maybe. The April 7 deadline is five days away, and Maria still cannot tell her daughter which school she will walk into in September.
