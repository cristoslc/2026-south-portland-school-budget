# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- December 8, 2025 -- 2025-12-08

### Interpretation

This first deep dive into South Portland's budget process reveals a district caught between genuine equity efforts and systemic blind spots that could undermine vulnerable students. The ELO program data gives me real hope - finally seeing disaggregated numbers showing that over half the participants are students the state categorizes as disadvantaged, including multilingual learners and students with disabilities. This is what inclusive programming actually looks like when you track who's being served, not just who you hope to serve.

But the budget crisis discussion exposes exactly what I've seen in other districts - when money gets tight, equity analysis disappears. They're facing a $1.9 million deficit with potential spending freezes, yet no one's asking how these cuts will hit schools with higher concentrations of low-income families or English learners differently. It's like equity becomes invisible the moment financial pressure mounts, which is precisely when we need that lens most.

The donation patterns are particularly troubling - $46,000 flowing into schools with zero coordination strategy. Some schools are collecting multiple grants while others get nothing, and the district admits they have no one managing this equitably. This is how resource gaps widen even when the community wants to help. At least the board is acknowledging its own demographic limitations, with Tyler Smith openly noting they "all look the same and sound the same" and expressing hope that new member Jimenez Paolo will bring different perspectives to policy work. Whether that translates to actual equity-centered decision-making remains to be seen, but it's the first time I've heard a board member name the representation problem so directly.
