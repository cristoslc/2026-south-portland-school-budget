# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

This meeting is three days after Jules Vazquez read her poem and the city welcomed immigrant families in their native languages. The $7.2 million gap was already sitting in the district's spreadsheets, invisible to everyone in the room.

The part that stays with me is Jeff Steinbrink — a man I don't know — standing up at city council to say the president had said terrible things about Somali Americans and that the community owed it to its Somali neighbors to make them feel welcome and safe. My parents are from Somalia. My friends' families are from Somalia. Someone was defending them — defending us — in December, in the same month when the budget gap that would eventually produce a recommendation to close Skillin was already quietly accumulating. The defense and the threat existed at the same time, in the same building, and nobody in that room held both things at once. By March, the board would be told that closing Skillin was the financially optimal choice. In December, someone was asking the council to make Somali families feel invested in this community.

Councilor Scott visited schools — maybe even mine — and said the staff work incredibly hard despite "challenging facilities and resources." I know exactly what challenging means now: the field broken since Memorial opened, the leaky gym ceiling, never enough instruments for everyone in band. She saw it. She said it out loud in a public meeting. And then nothing was done about any of it. Knowing everything that followed, that compliment feels like being told your house is beautiful right before it floods.

The Zoom debate was strange but it explains something I never fully understood: why these meetings feel so far from my life even when they're about my school. It's not just that they're confusing. The spaces where decisions get made are sometimes ugly and hostile, and that's part of why families like mine don't show up. If you already feel targeted in the world, you don't walk into a room where strangers log on specifically to say disgusting things.

This whole meeting reads, from the other side of everything, like the last quiet minutes before the storm that was already forming.
