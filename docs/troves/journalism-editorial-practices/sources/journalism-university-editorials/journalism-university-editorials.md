# A Comprehensive Guide to Crafting Impactful Editorials

*Source: Journalism University — https://journalism.university/writing-and-editing-for-print-media/guide-crafting-impactful-editorials/*

## Overview: What Is an Editorial?

An editorial represents "the official voice of the publication, weighing in on an important issue." Key distinctions:

| Format | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Editorial** | Unsigned; expresses the collective institutional stance of the editorial board |
| **Op-ed** | Signed guest piece; represents the author's personal view |
| **Column** | Recurring signed piece; reflects a specific journalist's ongoing personal commentary |

Unlike news reports (objective), editorials openly advocate for a position while maintaining ethical standards.

## Five Essential Steps

### Step 1: Select a Timely Topic

The foundation requires identifying a **"news peg"** — a current event directly relevant to readers. This involves:

- Scanning headlines for stories affecting your community
- Asking "Why does this matter?" to identify significance
- Finding topics with genuine debate and competing perspectives
- Testing whether the topic is timely, relevant, debatable, and defensible

Strong editorial topics are those with at least two valid, competing sides. If everyone agrees, there's no need for an editorial.

### Step 2: Develop Your Angle (Find Your Thesis)

Avoid fence-sitting. Your approach must present a **clear stance** rather than exploring all sides equally:

- Take a definitive position backed by reasoning
- Move beyond topic explanation to reasoned argument
- Establish what you believe should happen

An editorial must have a single, sharp, crystal-clear point. Before you write a single word, state your main argument in one simple sentence.

### Step 3: Conduct Essential Research

- **Gather evidence**: Collect facts, statistics, and expert perspectives supporting your position
- **Understand opposition**: Research counterarguments to strengthen your rebuttal
- **Verify accuracy**: Ensure all claims withstand scrutiny
- Use credible sources: official documents, government data, court records carry the most weight; expert analysis from academics adds authority

### Step 4: Structure Arguments for Impact

Classic editorial structure:

1. **Introduction** — builds reader interest; includes a hook
2. **Argument** — presents your opinion with supporting reasons
3. **Evidence** — supports argument with facts, research, or anecdotes
4. **Counterargument** — addresses the opposing perspective fairly
5. **Refutation** — explains why the counterargument is not accurate or relevant
6. **Call to action** — what should happen next

Many writers put their second-strongest point first and their strongest point last, so the piece builds momentum.

### Step 5: Execute Final Checks

- Adhere to publication deadlines
- Ensure conciseness (approximately 400 words; short paragraphs of 2–3 sentences)
- Verify all claims for accuracy

## Purpose of Editorial Writing

Editorials should "engage readers and contribute to public discourse," transforming timely issues into meaningful community conversation. An editorial is more than just an opinion — it's an exercise in public service that can shape public opinion, influence decision-makers, and spark real change.

## Ethical Standards

- Like other journalism, editorials use quotes, facts, and logic — content is still covered by ethical principles such as fairness and avoiding libel
- Opinions expressed on the editorial page should stay there — they should not affect news coverage
- Distinguish clearly between fact and opinion; phrases like "the data shows" signal fact; "this suggests" or "the board should" signal opinion
