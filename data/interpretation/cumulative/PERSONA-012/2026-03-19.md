# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

The pink slips are real now. That is what changed on March 19. Councilor Matthews stood up to vote against $100,000 for immigrant families and threw out a number — 72 — in about thirty seconds, as a point in an argument about something else, and the meeting kept moving. Seventy-two people in the school department got their notices the day before this meeting. Not projected for elimination. Not proposed for review. Actual layoffs, yesterday. Jaylen has been tracking this number since December 17 — 78 total positions, 42 teachers — and now it has converted from a budget proposal to paper in people's hands. Some of those 72 people teach his classes. He still doesn't know which ones.

The deficit got worse too. Matthews said $8.4 million, not $7.2 million. That number has been the fixed axis of every conversation since December and now it is bigger, and nobody at the meeting paused to explain why, and nobody asked, and the meeting moved on.

The thing that is hardest to shake is the logic Matthews used. He voted no on the $100,000 for immigrant families who cannot pay rent because of ICE raids — the same families whose kids missed school in January, whose ability to attend the budget forum was cancelled because they were afraid to be seen in public — and his reason was the school deficit. He took the thing that has been threatening Jaylen's sense of what his school can provide, and used it as an argument against protecting the people the school crisis is landing on hardest. Jaylen has been watching these two disasters intersect since January 12. He watched the forum get cancelled because immigrant families could not safely show up. He watched high schoolers his age maybe dropping out because they were afraid to leave their houses. And now a councilor is saying: because of the school deficit, we cannot help those same families stay housed. The crises that were intersecting are now being officially pitted against each other, with a vote count attached.

The good news: the $100K passed 6-1. The chief stood at the microphone and said he messed up, personally, and owned it. Those things are real.

The timeline is now locked. April 7, May 5, June 9. Senior year is on a countdown clock.

The Mahoney theater vision — community arts space, indoor youth athletics — got pushed to 2027. The November referendum is not happening. Jaylen spent months watching Mahoney as a parallel story, a mirror of the SPHS question. Now the two timelines split completely: Mahoney might have a community theater in 2027 or 2028, while the actual school programs had 72 people get pink slips yesterday and a budget vote May 5. The city is drawing plans for arts spaces in a building nobody has figured out yet. The real arts programs already sent out the notices.

The only moment SPHS appeared in the four-hour meeting was as the announced venue for a water quality film.
