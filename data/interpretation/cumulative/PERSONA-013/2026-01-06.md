# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

The January 6th meeting was where the adults announced the openings: come to the forum, come on January 22nd, bring your ideas about the school budget. The school board chair stood up at city council and invited people in. I know what that forum turned into. By February, I'd watched parents flood a meeting to say nobody told them about reconfiguration — class sizes jumping to 22 or 23, kindergarteners being sorted into different buildings, Skillin on the chopping block. The invitation on January 6th was real. The information at the forum was real. But the power to change anything wasn't in the room.

The February 10th budget guidance workshop was also announced here — the joint meeting where the school board and city council would sit together and decide what tax increases were acceptable. I know how that one went too. A councilor stood up and said "the people of South Portland cannot afford any of your options." Another bragged about keeping increases under 3% back when he was on the board. The announcement on January 6th sounds neutral: joint guidance workshop, February 10th. What it became was two groups of adults arguing over our schools while no one who actually sits in those schools was in the room.

I keep coming back to the musician who showed up about Bug Light Park. He wanted to build something — a free music festival, arts and community, Bug Light apparently inspired by Greek patrons who believed beauty mattered. Everything I've heard in every single meeting since December is about what's going to disappear. This one person wanted to add something. The city couldn't co-sponsor it this year, which is obviously about money, but he was going to do it anyway. I'm in band. I've spent months worrying band might go half-time. Someone standing in front of a city council meeting and talking about music as something worth creating felt different from anything else I've encountered in all of this.

The mayor also mentioned a statewide task force on property taxes — examining who's most hurt by high property taxes, with a first report due soon. So someone at the state level is at least watching the same problem that's crushing our school budget. That's not nothing. But it doesn't change the June 9th deadline or the 78 positions or the fact that it's the teachers and kids who pay for a structural failure that started before most of them were even hired here.
