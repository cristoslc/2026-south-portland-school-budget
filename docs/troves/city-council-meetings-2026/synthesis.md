## 1. School Budget Crisis (Primary Theme)

The school budget dominates council discussion from January through March. The situation is characterized by structural deficits driven by declining enrollment, post-COVID staffing growth, and increasing student needs.

### Key Facts

- **Enrollment:** Down from ~3,050 (2017) to 2,748 (Feb 2026) — over 300 fewer students (>10% decline).
- **Staffing:** 82 positions added since 2017, even as enrollment dropped. 80% of the school budget is personnel.
- **Budget gap:** Status-quo spending would produce a ~19% tax increase (school share only). The school board asked staff to model a 6% option (Option B) and a 9.8% option (Option C).
- **Option B (6% school tax increase):** Even with $4M in savings from three initiatives (elementary reconfiguration, middle/high school schedule changes, operational efficiencies), leaves a ~$3.5M unmet gap.
- **Option C (9.8% school tax increase):** Same $4M in savings leaves a ~$1.1M unmet gap.
- **School fund balance:** Essentially zero. The school side had $739K unassigned; $850K was already budgeted for FY26. A potential deficit in FY26 may need to be temporarily covered by city-side fund balance.

### School Cost Drivers Identified

- 10% health insurance increase
- Union wage settlements (negotiated in good faith, but budget impact is large)
- Mandated pre-K expansion for special education ages 3–5 (CDS transition)
- Increasing complexity of student needs (special education, multilingual learners, McKinney-Vento youth)
- COVID-era funding (ESSER, ARPA) created positions that must now be locally funded
- Finance department instability: six finance directors in five years; new director now stabilizing

### Proposed School Structural Changes

1. **Elementary reconfiguration:** Three schools become intermediate (grades 2–4), two become primary (pre-K–1). Estimated savings: $2M. Controversial — concerns about neighborhood school identity, equity, and community impact.
2. **Middle school schedule consolidation:** Merge staggered start times (currently 7:55 and 8:45). Potential savings from reduced duplicated staff/admin, but requires a $25K DOT traffic study and 6–12 month permitting. Unlikely to impact FY27.
3. **Operational efficiencies across all areas:** Shift changes, creative scheduling. Estimated savings: $1M each for items 2 and 3.

### Community Voices on School Budget

- Multiple parents and teachers spoke at the Feb 10 workshop, expressing concern over staff cuts and elementary reconfiguration.
- Teachers' union president stressed that salary negotiations were conducted in good faith and the budget gap is not their fault.
- Several residents pushed for administrative cuts before classroom cuts. One noted the first 25 names in salary records are six figures.
- Taxpayers on fixed incomes expressed inability to absorb further increases; one cited a 2.8% Social Security COLA.
- A resident noted South Portland spends ~$20M more than state EPS recommendations (though the comparison is imprecise due to local share formulas).

## 2. FY25 Financial Audit (Feb 3 Meeting)

The independent audit for FY25 was presented by Tim Gill (Runyon Kersteen Ouellette).

- **Opinion:** Unmodified (clean) — financial statements fairly presented.
- **Material weakness:** School department reconciliations — repeat finding. 34 audit journal entries (high), many school-related. ~$400K in grant funds (including Title I) had to be moved to the general fund due to ineligibility or overspending.
- **Significant deficiencies:** Transportation cash receipts (now moot — bus service transferred to Metro), city payroll procedures, school budget non-compliance (legally adopted budget exceeded — repeat finding).
- **Other findings:** School journal entry review procedures, purchasing card transaction approvals (inconsistent across departments), school payroll procedures.
- **Fund balance:** Increased $5.8M over prior year. Excess of ~$8.5M for city side, driven by auto excise tax ($1.7M over budget), parks/rec fees, and wage savings from staff vacancies.
- **City fund balance policy:** 9–12% of subsequent year's expenditure budget. If school budget increases significantly, the required reserve amount also increases.
- **No waste, fraud, or abuse** was identified by auditors.

## 3. Texas Instruments TIF Extension (Feb 3 Workshop)

Texas Instruments, operating a 200mm semiconductor fabrication plant near the Jetport since 1997, requested extension of their 30-year TIF district for an additional 20 years under new state law (LD 1739, 2023).

- **Current TIF:** Captures 50% of incremental value; generates ~$1.2M/year in TIF revenue. TI receives a credit enhancement agreement (CEA) payment that varies based on their capital investment.
- **Proposed extension:** Under new law, 75% of TIF revenues must support transit-oriented development and/or affordable housing. CEA would be limited to 25% of 50% capture = 12.5% of property taxes.
- **Staff proposal:** Combine TI TIF with expiring Growth Area TIF and Transit TIF into a new larger Mall Area TOD District. This would create a broad geographic area for eligible spending throughout the city's transit corridors.
- **Tax shift benefit:** TIF shelters value from state equalized valuation, which benefits state education subsidy, municipal revenue sharing, and county tax allocation.
- **BETE receipts:** TI generates ~$1.1M/year in Business Equipment Tax Exemption reimbursements from the state, on top of property taxes.
- **TI employment:** Part of a 34,000-employee global company; Maine fab focuses on automotive semiconductor wafers.

## 6. Immigration Enforcement and Community Impact

A significant thread across multiple meetings (Feb 3, Feb 17, Mar 5):

- ICE enforcement activity in South Portland created fear among immigrant communities.
- Multiple residents testified about families unable to go to work, children afraid to attend school, and resulting economic disruption.
- **Feb 3:** Council passed a community safety resolution; City manager investigated eviction moratorium legality.
- **Feb 17:** Business microloan/grant program investigated via GP-COG survey; staff recommended not moving forward at that time.
- **Mar 5 workshop/Mar 10 agenda:** Temporary Emergency Rental Assistance program proposed. Staff recommended $150K from undesignated fund balance to nonprofits (Project Home, Cumberland County Homeless Prevention Program). Average distribution ~$2,500/household.
- Intersection with budget: Rental assistance would come from fund balance; GA program costs noted as city pays 30%, state reimburses 70%.

## 9. Council-School Board Relationship

The Feb 10 joint meeting reveals tension in the council-school board relationship:

- School Board Chair DeAngelos pressed council for a specific numeric target, calling "do the best you can" insufficient.
- Council members bristled at the suggestion they were shirking responsibility.
- The structural challenge: School department is legally a city department whose budget council must approve, but council has no administrative oversight of school operations (city manager has no authority over superintendent).
- City Finance Director noted the "murky" responsibility boundary, while emphasizing that taxpayers see one combined tax bill.
- Multiple councilors expressed difficulty in giving guidance without full budget details.
- School board member Holman called out the regressive nature of property tax funding: "Where is the creative thinking?"
