# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

The Title I finding is the one she cannot put down. She has been fighting for intervention slots at three buildings for years — tracking the wait lists, writing the letters, being the only person in every room who has seen all the buildings at once — and the independent auditor just told a city council meeting that approximately $400,000 in grant funds, including Title I money specifically designated for educationally disadvantaged students, had to be swept into the general fund because it was overspent or ineligible. A repeat finding. The most severe category. And not one person in that room drew a line between those swept dollars and the forty-two teachers and sixteen ed techs currently on the elimination list.

Joe Prince's explanation of the three overlapping fiscal calendars is honest and she knows it is structurally accurate — she has seen the calendar problem from inside buildings where federal grant timelines and contract years and city fiscal years all pull in different directions. She credits the new finance director. But this is the second year of the same finding. "It's complicated" has to become "we fixed it" at some point, and the students whose services were supposed to be funded by that Title I money are still waiting.

The immigration testimony snaps her to full attention. Families with lights off at night. Missing work. Falling behind on February rent. She has been in those buildings for the past two weeks knowing which kids aren't there and now the council is hearing it in their own public record. The resolution and the moratorium request to the governor is more than she got on January 13, when Montgomery named the exact same mechanism with clinical authority and the council moved on inside three minutes. This is not enough. But it is on the record, it is actionable, and she will point to it.

DeAngelos telling the council directly — "you must accept responsibility for the school as a department of the city" — is the chair going on offense, and it is the first time in the entire cycle she has heard it. She has been watching the board navigate between the school side and the city side while the council received a passive pass. Tonight that changed. Whether the February 10 joint workshop is shaped by this confrontation or by the city manager's "add or not" framing from January 6 is still to be determined. But she heard it. She is holding the chair to it.

The forum is in eighteen hours. She has her data. She has been waiting since January 6 to say what she knows out loud in a room that might do something with it.
