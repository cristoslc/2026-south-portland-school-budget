# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

Looking back at February 3 from March, this meeting lands differently than it did when I sat through it. I came in expecting procedural business, and I got something more useful: an auditor standing up and saying the school budget was over-expended "by quite a bit" for the second year in a row, non-compliant with the legally adopted budget, and they moved $400,000 in grant funds to cover expenses those grants weren't supposed to pay for. Not once. Twice. Back to back years.

That word "non-compliant" stuck with me. The board has been telling us for months that this is a crisis born of enrollment declines and state funding failures — structural problems beyond anyone's control. But when your own auditor says you violated your legally adopted budget two years running and shuffled restricted grant money to plug the holes, that's not structural, that's operational. And when the assistant superintendent explains it by citing six people in the finance director's chair over five years, three of them interim, that's not an excuse — that's a confession. No continuity, no institutional memory. No one who knows where the money actually went.

The city fund balance growing $5.8 million was the one thing that gave me any comfort. Over-collected on auto excise taxes, saved money from staff vacancies. The city managed its money and banked the difference. The school board did the opposite for years. Sitting in the same building a week later at the joint workshop, watching the school board ask for 6-10% tax increases, I kept thinking about that $5.8 million. Same taxpayers, same economy, two organizations — and only one of them runs its books.

The Texas Instruments TIF surprised me. I came in with my guard up. After watching the Ocean Street developer walk away with half the new tax revenue for thirty years, I assumed every TIF deal in this city was a gift to someone with a lobbyist. The TI math is actually different: extending it nets the city $400,000 more per year than letting it expire. I don't love giving any company a tax break on principle. But if the numbers are right, this is a TIF that works in our favor, not against us. First one I've seen all winter. I'm watching it — but I'm not counting it until the ink is dry and the council has actually voted.
