# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

Maria has been tracking this budget crisis since December 1, when she sat through an inauguration ceremony where nobody mentioned that 78 positions were already on the line. She has attended forums, workshops, and regular meetings across two governing bodies, built a parent advocacy network through text chains and Facebook posts, and calculated combined household tax burdens in her head while councilors discussed apartment buildings and civic campuses. Tonight, a city councilor finally says the number out loud — not at a school board meeting, not in a budget workshop, but as a throwaway argument against a rental assistance vote. Seventy-two pink slips. Yesterday. And then six other councilors vote yes on Project HOME and move to the next agenda item.

Maria is not against the rental assistance — she has been watching the Project HOME fund drain toward zero since February 17, and she knows those families are real. What she cannot absorb is the way Councilor Matthews invoked the schools: the $8.4M deficit, the 72 people, the possibility of closing schools, deployed as a rhetorical cudgel and then abandoned. Nobody at that table asked what it means. Which teachers. Which schools. Which kids lose their ed tech. The number landed and dissolved, the way every piece of substantive information about the school budget has dissolved in a city council chamber all winter.

The 72 figure is also new in a way that shakes her. She knew 78 positions were proposed for elimination. But pink slips sent — that is no longer a proposal. That is the machinery in motion. The March 2 workshop gave her nine reconfiguration options and a room full of parents who share her fear. Tonight tells her the process has moved past options into notifications. The teachers her daughter knows by name are holding letters.

What she takes home that is actionable: the dates. April 7 public hearing. April 14 school budget workshop. May 5 council vote. June 9 referendum. She has six weeks, and April 14 is the meeting that matters — the one where she can still ask questions before the math is finalized. She is putting those dates in every parent channel she runs tonight, because she has learned across four months of watching this unfold that the window for meaningful input is always shorter than it looks.

The sewer rate increase — 22% annually for three years — sits on top of all of it. She did this math in January with the Mahoney numbers and ran it again tonight. Her household is absorbing simultaneous shocks from every direction, and nobody in either governing body is adding those lines together the way she is, standing at her kitchen counter after midnight.
