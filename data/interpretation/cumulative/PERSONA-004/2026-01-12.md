# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- January 12, 2026 -- 2026-01-12

### Interpretation

A month into the budget crisis and the picture keeps getting worse. The 78 positions are still on the table -- 42 teachers, 16 ed techs, the whole devastating package. But now there's a new wrinkle: the board unanimously voted to explore elementary school reconfiguration, and it feels like they've already decided. They'll study closing a building that could save millions, but at a pace that lets them keep cutting teachers in the meantime. Meanwhile, retirements and resignations aren't being backfilled -- that's administrative code for "we'll shrink the workforce by attrition and call it natural."

Sarah Gay's testimony was the highlight. She told them straight: we don't want more surveys, we want to see your actual proposals so we can tell you what they mean in the classroom. That's exactly right. When they say "eliminate 2.5 FTE in high school English," I'm the one who knows that means 30 more kids per section and no creative writing elective. Tyler Smith asking about the spending freeze impact was good too -- at least one board member understands that these aren't just spreadsheet exercises.

The current-year fiscal picture is alarming. Ninety-two percent of funds expended or encumbered at the halfway mark means we're tracking toward a deficit right now, not just next year. The spending freeze I've been living under for over a month isn't just a precaution -- it's a sign that this year's budget is already underwater. Between the non-backfilling of retirements, the continuing freeze, and the looming cuts, morale in the building is at its lowest point in my career. Half my colleagues are updating their resumes and looking at other districts. The board seems to understand the numbers but not what those numbers mean for the humans who show up every day to teach.
