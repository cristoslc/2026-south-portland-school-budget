# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

Reading December 9 from March 2026, this meeting is where South Portland's housing equity commitments stopped being aspirational and became a documented failure of will. The One 70 Ocean TIF vote is the thing I keep returning to. The city manager said it on the record: TIF funds can be used for income-restricted affordable housing, and they have been used that way before. The mechanism was not missing. The developer had just asked the council to approve 208 units after originally proposing 124 — a 67% density increase, as Councilor West noted — with parking relocated offsite. That was the leverage moment. That was when conditions could have been attached. The council voted unanimously yes. Nothing. Thirty years of 50% property tax rebates on an $80–90 million project, and not a single unit reserved for anyone who cannot pay $1,800 a month for a one-bedroom, which is what Zillow says the market asks right now. Carly Williams stood up and named that number directly and asked what "affordable market rate" actually means. The developer had a prepared answer: "lowercase a" affordable — studios, efficient layouts, workforce housing for police officers and restaurant workers. No income limits. Just smaller rooms. The council accepted that answer without a word.

Rosemary DeAngelo was at this meeting, weeks before her inauguration as board chair. She raised two things: the clerk reads too fast for non-English speakers to follow — a concern she had already raised before — and the rent control cap of 10% for landlords with 12+ units is too high. Neither got a council response. The clerk speed problem is a language access failure happening at every single public meeting, and it has been named repeatedly, and it persists. That pattern — documented, repeated, unaddressed — is the same one I watched play out with the in-person meeting barriers, where residents described $40 childcare costs just to attend and speak, and the city manager acknowledged the problem and committed to nothing.

What the December 9 meeting establishes, retroactively, is the starting condition for everything that followed. The council will not use structural tools to produce affordable housing when the market doesn't require it. The participation barriers were locked in place. And the budget season was announced — 61% of the property tax bill, 78 positions at risk — with no equity frame and no one in the room asking which students would absorb the consequences. The machine was already running.
