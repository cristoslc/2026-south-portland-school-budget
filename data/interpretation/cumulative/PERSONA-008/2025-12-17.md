# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: Workshop -- December 17, 2025 -- 2025-12-17

### Interpretation

This workshop hammered home the raw numbers behind the crisis I've been dreading, and they're even worse than I imagined. Forty-two teachers. That's not a "trim" -- that's a gutting. When I hear that the district added 82 staff positions while losing 300 students, I can't decide whether to be furious at the incompetence that created this mess or terrified about what the "fix" is going to look like for my kids. Both, probably.

The enrollment collapse at the elementary level -- down 23% in four years -- is the number that keeps me up at night. You don't lose a quarter of your elementary students and keep every building running at full capacity. The math doesn't work, and administrators know it. Even though nobody said "school closure" tonight, the data they presented is exactly the kind of ammunition that gets used to justify shutting down a neighborhood school. I can practically hear the PowerPoint slide: "With only X students at Building Y, it's more efficient to consolidate." Efficient for whom? Not for my seven-year-old who'd lose her teacher, her friends, and her walk to school.

What really infuriates me is that this was avoidable. They spent like enrollment would keep growing forever, and now my children are supposed to absorb the consequences. The per-pupil spending being highest among comparable districts tells me there's fat in this budget that doesn't involve closing schools or stuffing 30 kids into a classroom. But I don't trust this board to find it -- not after watching them spend $30,000 on a search firm that excludes parents from candidate selection. They've shown me who they listen to, and it's not us.

I'm shifting from watching and worrying to actively preparing. If they're going to come for our schools, we need to be organized before they announce it, not after.
