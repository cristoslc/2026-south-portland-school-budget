# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The art department at SPHS is going down to three teachers. Jaylen has known since March 23 that 0.5 FTE in art was on the cut list. April 2 gives him the full picture: four teachers at the start of the year, one lost mid-summer and replaced with a half-time hire named Hannah Schultz — the same Hannah who testified at March 30 about being laid off twice by the same district — and now that half-time position is being eliminated entirely. Matt Meyer stood at the microphone and named what the budget slide did not: enrollment at SPHS did not fall by 25%, so a 25% reduction in the art department is not proportional. Nobody on the board asked a follow-up question.

What is different about April 2 is that Jaylen has company at the microphone for the first time. Eva Morin, a junior at his school, spoke about a teacher who changed what she wants to do with her life. Lucy Hutzel spoke about her father, Mr. Wetzel, the middle school computer science teacher being cut — and then Mr. Wetzel stood up right after her, barely holding it. Jaylen testified at a board meeting once and felt dismissed. He has spent four months watching student reps be dismissed in more elaborate ways. These students showed up unbidden, the same way he did, and said the thing nobody else would say in language that was not budget code. The public comment slot at 9:30 PM remains the only real seat in this process. That is clarifying, not comfortable.

Angela Kabisa sent a letter. She is a SPHS senior and a sitting board member and she could not be there, so Dowling read it for her. She named the DEI position, the percussion ed tech, and arts programs as things that make students feel seen. The official student voice in this room was delivered secondhand, by someone else, while the meeting continued without her. Jaylen has seen this configuration — students counted but not present, represented but not heard — in every iteration since December. This one has the student's own chair sitting empty.

The board did not vote on the budget. State money may be coming — teachers went to Augusta and asked for it, which is something Jaylen did not know anyone was doing — but the figures are contested and the board wants accurate numbers before deciding. He understands the logic. He still cannot answer the school newspaper's question. Nothing official has named theater, AP courses, or coaching assignments. Everything he knows about what SPHS is losing came from teachers who showed up and said it themselves.

April 7 is the public hearing. He knows where he is going.
