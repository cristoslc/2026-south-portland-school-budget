# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

Looking back at this now, from three and a half months into the worst budget season South Portland schools has seen in memory, the December 1 inaugural feels like the last quiet moment before everything broke open. I watched the swearing-in ceremonies on my phone like I watch every school board meeting, and I remember the low-grade frustration of sitting through an hour of speeches with no substance. At that point I knew the district had financial problems building — I follow this stuff closely enough to know the signs — but the spending freeze hadn't happened yet, the $1.9 million deficit hadn't been laid out on the table yet, nothing had been quantified. I was watching for signal. I got ceremony.

What strikes me now, knowing everything that came after, is that the seeds of the people I'd spend the next three months watching were visible in those speeches. The new school board chair — DeAngelis, who I'd come to know as the person who stood up for Skillin families "west of the Dairy Queen," who pushed back hardest on the reconfiguration options that would hurt immigrant families — gave her inaugural speech entirely about welcoming immigrants and what public education should stand for. At the time I noted it as a nice sentiment that sidestepped reality. Now I read it as a preview of how she'd act when the real decisions came. The values she articulated on December 1 were the same ones she put into action when the stakes were highest.

Mayor Tipton's housing focus barely registered with me that night — I'm tracking school budget, not development projects. But three months later, watching the city council squeeze the school board's tax increase down to 4-6% at the joint workshop, I understand that those priorities compete for the same taxpayer dollars. Her silence on the school budget in December wasn't dismissive; it was just that the crisis hadn't fully surfaced yet. Still, the back-to-back inauguration of both bodies on the same night was unusual, and I spent the drive home wondering if that scheduling signaled coordination — or just convenience.

The no-public-comment structure of an inaugural is completely normal. But that's a fact that reads differently now. This was the one moment when new leaders were taking the stage and parents had exactly zero opportunity to put them on record before the freeze hit, before the cuts were announced, before everything got politically locked. I'll note it and move on.
