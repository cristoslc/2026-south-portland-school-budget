### Reactions

Honestly, this meeting was a perfect example of how equity gets lip service while the real decisions happen without any equity lens whatsoever. The immigration stuff was actually heartening - seeing the board unanimously support the city's resolution and hearing DeAngelis talk about driving people to work at 6 AM showed real community solidarity. And the adult ed response was exactly right - moving to WhatsApp, getting Chromebooks out, centering what families actually need.

But then we get to the big budget decisions and it's like equity just vanishes from the conversation. They unanimously approved this elementary reconfiguration thing with zero - and I mean ZERO - discussion of how it affects kids differently based on income, race, language, disability status, nothing. DeAngelis is out there talking about kids being "flexible" while parents are "anxious," but what about transportation equity? What about families who can't drive their kids across town? What about the fact that the proposed cuts hit ed techs and support staff way harder than administrators?

The community school expansion could be great, but again, no conversation about which schools get priority, which communities have been most underserved. Sarah Sergey from the union was the only person who called out that these changes have "imbalanced impacts" and that students are losing "safe adults." Thank god someone was naming what everyone else was dancing around - that when you cut 78 positions, it's not some neutral math problem, it's about which kids lose supports and which communities get hit hardest.
