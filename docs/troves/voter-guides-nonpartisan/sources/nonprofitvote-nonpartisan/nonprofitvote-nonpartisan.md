# Nonpartisan Voter Guides: What Nonprofits Need to Know

*Source: Nonprofit VOTE — https://www.nonprofitvote.org*

## Overview

Nonprofits and civic organizations play a vital role in voter education. Producing and distributing voter guides is a permissible and valuable civic activity when done correctly. The key is maintaining true nonpartisanship throughout.

## Staying Nonpartisan: Core Principles

The main principle for being nonpartisan is to conduct voter engagement and education **in the context of your educational and civic mission** — not in a way intended to support or oppose a specific candidate.

### Permissible Activities
- Distributing nonpartisan voter guides showing all candidates' responses to identical questions
- Hosting nonpartisan candidate forums
- Providing voter registration assistance
- Sharing information about polling locations and voting procedures
- Publishing "who's on your ballot" information

### What to Avoid
- Comparing candidates favorably or unfavorably to your organization's positions
- Selectively emphasizing certain candidate responses
- Leaving out candidates from your guide
- Providing commentary on candidate responses
- Allowing your organization's issue positions to shape question wording

## Creating a Nonpartisan Candidate Guide

### Question Development
- Questions should be open-ended and issue-based
- They should cover a range of issues relevant to the office
- All candidates for the same office must receive identical questions
- Questions should not reflect your organization's positions or advocacy priorities

### Candidate Outreach
- Reach out to all candidates simultaneously
- Use identical communication methods and timing for all candidates
- Send follow-up reminders equally
- State clearly what will happen if a candidate does not respond (typically: note that candidate did not respond)

### Publication
- Publish all responses without editing (except truncation for length)
- Use identical formatting for all candidate responses
- Include a clear nonpartisanship disclaimer
- Do not rate, rank, or editorialize about responses

## Distribution Best Practices

- Distribute widely and neutrally — the same materials to all communities
- Post online in accessible formats
- Partner with community organizations for distribution
- Target people who may not otherwise see election information
- Include clear identification of your organization and the disclaimer about nonpartisanship

## The Civic Education Role

Voter guides serve a critical civic education function:
- They provide information to voters who might otherwise be uninformed
- They help people make choices aligned with their own values
- They increase participation in democracy
- They are especially valuable for down-ballot races that get less media coverage

## Nonpartisan vs. Non-advocacy

Being nonpartisan in voter education does not mean your organization must abandon its mission or refrain from advocacy on other matters. It simply means that in the context of candidate elections:
- Your voter education work does not take sides
- Your materials help voters understand candidates' positions, not evaluate them
- Your organization's role is to inform, not to persuade toward a particular candidate
