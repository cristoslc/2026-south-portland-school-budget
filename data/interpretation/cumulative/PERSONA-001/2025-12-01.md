# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025

### Interpretation

Looking back at December 1 from March 2026 — with nine reconfiguration options on the table, 78 positions marked for elimination, and three more workshops ahead — this inauguration ceremony carries the weight of a prologue she didn't know she was watching.

At the time, Maria sat through two hours of ceremony waiting for someone to mention that the schools were already in trouble. Budget pressures were known inside the district. And yet: not a word. Housing development, the Mahoney Center, vibrant neighborhoods — Mayor Tipton's vision for South Portland has no room in it for the 42 teachers whose jobs are now on the line. That silence doesn't feel accidental anymore. It previewed everything that followed when the council's budget guidance came in at 3-6%, mostly 4-5%, undercutting even the school board's own floor.

Rosemary DeAngelis's inaugural speech is the part that haunts Maria most. The new chair described an immigrant student whose family took him in so he wouldn't have to switch schools senior year. She talked about connection and belonging. Immigrant community members spoke in Farsi, Arabic, Somali — Jean Tam from Kaler School was there, a parent whose daughter attends one of the same elementary schools now threatened by reconfiguration. Maria believed it. She still believes DeAngelis meant it. But this is the same chair who called reconfiguration "a fabulous idea" before any formal proposal existed, dismissed neighborhood schools as something the city "doesn't really have anymore," and chose a superintendent search process explicitly designed to keep community voices out. The equity language of December 1 and the governance choices of December through March belong to the same person, and Maria cannot reconcile them.

The four new board members — Feller, Richardson, Rich, Smith — were blank slates that night. She needed to know where they stood. She knows now. Feller is the one who at least raised the possibility of going above 6% on taxes. The others have mostly voted in lockstep on reconfiguration despite near-unanimous parent opposition and teacher union pushback.

December 1 was the last quiet moment before the crisis broke open. The people about to make consequential decisions were on a stage, taking oaths, saying nothing about it.
