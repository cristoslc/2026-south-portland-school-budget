# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The board punted again, but this time Dana does not care, because the twelve minutes she pulled from five hours are the strongest she has logged since Richardson broke down on March 30.

The Wetzel sequence is the season's best piece of accidental television. A high school girl walks to the mic to defend her father — the middle school computer science teacher being cut — and then the father follows her, visibly undone, and says he is going back to school tomorrow and still does not know what to tell his students. Dana has been looking for the teacher-on-camera moment since December 17, when she first clocked 42 teachers facing elimination and started calculating who she could get in front of a lens. Blair Bacon answered that thread on March 23. Wetzel supersedes Bacon — not because his credentials are more compelling, but because his daughter walked up first, and you cannot produce that sequence. It arrived.

The $300,000 state funding announcement is the variable that wrecked the board's evening and handed Dana a second segment. A union rep received a text *during public comment* and announced it from the podium mid-meeting. The board chair confirmed a legislator texted her a different number. Dana has been tracking the state funding gap — 20% actual versus 55% statutory — as an assembled fact she has not yet fully aired since she first logged it in December. She identified the need for a legislator on camera back in January. That thread has not closed. Tonight it cracked open from a different angle: Augusta is apparently moving, but nobody in the room knows by how much, and the board declined to vote on a budget while the number is live. The Monday meeting is now her next deployment decision.

Brian Green reading five board members' term expiration dates aloud, followed by Holman tearing up at the podium and comparing it to bullying — that exchange is the entire season arc compressed into ninety seconds. Angry community, defensive board, no resolution. Dana has been documenting the community apathy pattern since December 4 when Pride told the barbershop story. Green is the opposite of apathy. He showed up with a spreadsheet of political consequences. Both failure modes she identified in December — apathy and structural exclusion — have now been joined by a third: organized electoral rage. That is new, and it is airable.

The behavioral specialist testimony — 60 students, 40 behavior plans, no replacement — and the bus driver injury count — six injuries, three shoulder surgeries, three in physical therapy — are the institutional-cost-of-cuts material she files for the longer piece. The human cost of the RIF is no longer abstract.
