# Cumulative Summary: Lila (PERSONA-014)

### Current Understanding

As of March 23, 2026, Lila understands the South Portland school budget crisis as three months of escalating institutional failure. The $1.9M current-year shortfall first revealed in December — alongside a spending freeze that has prevented teachers from buying classroom supplies all year — has grown to an $8.4M structural deficit. The district's savings are depleted. State funding covers only 20% of what it should. Elementary enrollment has fallen by 300 students in four years. On March 18, 72 confirmed layoff notices went out; the formal budget proposes eliminating 78 total positions including 42 teachers.

The threat to Dyer has shifted but not resolved. Nine reconfiguration scenarios narrowed to two options (A and B); the administration recommends closing Kayler, not Dyer. The closure Lila has feared since December is not the one being recommended. But under Option A, Dyer is redesignated as a primary school for pre-K through 1st grade. Lila, in 4th grade, would transfer to an intermediate school — Brown or Skillen — while younger children inherit the building she knows. Her Dyer building survives. Her school, as she has known it, does not.

No vote has been taken. The board meets again March 30. The city has formally scheduled a public hearing April 7, a council vote May 5, and a community referendum June 9. The city council — which committed $87M for police and fire buildings in a November referendum — has never said Dyer's name in any of the more than a dozen meetings Lila has tracked. Board Chair DeAngelis, who called reconfiguration "good for kids" and drove the nine-scenario process, opened the March 23 meeting already in tears. Two 8th graders stood at the microphone and said true things. No vote was taken, and Lila still doesn't know whether her looping teacher is among the 72.
