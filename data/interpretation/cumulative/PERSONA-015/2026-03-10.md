# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

She has been watching the participation barrier eat its own remedies since February 17, when the city manager explained that families were too afraid to call the city to file a moratorium complaint. Tonight she gets the final piece of that structure: Chris Pupke confirms that if you stayed home out of fear — if you were employed, rent-paying, and doing exactly what a reasonable person does when federal agents are in the neighborhood — the state says that's not just cause. You're not eligible for General Assistance. The families who navigated the surge correctly, by her professional and human judgment, are the ones the safety net is designed to miss.

She has been carrying this thread since January 20, when the forum was cancelled and she named the shift from logistical barrier to active safety crisis. She watched the moratorium fail 4-2 on February 17. She watched the city manager explain that even a passed moratorium would have been functionally inaccessible for the same families it was designed to protect. And now the one municipal program that doesn't require a council vote has a state eligibility rule that excludes the people she knows from her buildings. The barrier has been complete for weeks. March 10 just made it official.

West's framing — four days, fewer than five arrests — is the number she has been dreading since January when she flagged the enrollment/staffing narrative as weaponizable. Arrest statistics are the wrong unit of measure for what happened in her buildings, and she knew the moment she heard it that this framing will travel. It will show up in conversations between now and the next council meeting as the reason the amount doesn't need to be larger. She has been in three buildings for two months watching what four days of operations does to a cohort of kids who are already on MTSS wait lists and who she barely has enough time at each building to reach. Those weeks don't show up in arrest counts.

What pulls her back is Scott, who did the connection she has been waiting someone in elected authority to do since January: eighty families is eighty students. Enrollment is already down. Per-pupil funding follows the child. Keeping those families here is not charity — it is cheaper than the downstream cost of losing those students from a district that is already cutting positions to close a $7.2 million gap. Scott said the whole thing, in the council chamber, on the record. And then the council deferred the number to the next meeting. No money committed. No check cut. The family she knows at one of her buildings who is already weeks behind on rent does not have a next meeting.
