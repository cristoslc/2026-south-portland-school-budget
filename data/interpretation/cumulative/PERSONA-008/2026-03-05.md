# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Three days after the March 2nd workshop where she watched nine scenarios for dismantling South Portland's elementary schools get formally laid on the table, Rachel is at city council watching them spend three hours arguing about where to put a fire station. The cognitive dissonance is almost physical. On Monday she heard the administration recommend closing Kaler or Dyer or Brown or Small — her children's schools, named out loud, with savings figures attached. On Thursday she's watching councilors debate Broadway traffic patterns and whether $87 million is the right number for public safety buildings. She stays anyway, because she's learned that the thing she comes to watch is never the thing that actually matters.

Councilor Matthews doesn't disappoint. In the middle of a discussion that has nothing to do with schools, he drops the number she's been bracing for since December: "more like eight, $8 million." She has been calculating against $7.2 million since the first school board meeting she attended. He says the real number is larger, in the casual way of someone for whom it is already common knowledge, and then moves on. No one follows up. No one asks which schools. She files it.

The $87 million public safety bond going to November is the number that reorganizes everything fiscal. She has been tracking the Mahoney project since January 13th, when the finance director told the council that the $194 million price tag had no meaningful grant offsets and would land on the property tax bill. Now the police station is settled — the Walgreens site, separate from Mahoney — and the fire station rebuild is confirmed at its current Broadway location, unanimous vote. The $87 million heading to November covers police and fire. Mahoney city hall gets postponed to March 19th. The monster she's been tracking has been split into pieces, and the pieces that have been resolved aren't going away — they're going onto the same tax bills.

The speaker who says "housing segregation continues to drive school segregation" lands differently than Rachel expected. She came to this meeting as the person fighting to protect her neighborhood school. The speaker is saying the neighborhood school structure itself may be doing work she hasn't fully accounted for. She doesn't revise her position. She files it alongside everything else she can't fully hold right now, and she watches the council postpone Mahoney to March 19th without resolving a single thing that needed resolving.
