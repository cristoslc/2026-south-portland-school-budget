# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026 -- 2026-02-09

### Interpretation

This meeting split cleanly into two halves for me -- one that showed what equity commitment looks like in practice, and one that showed how quickly it evaporates when structural decisions are on the table. The immigration response was genuinely heartening. The board unanimously endorsed the city council's resolution, Richardson gave emotional testimony about children afraid to come to school, and Chair DeAngelis mentioned personally driving community members to work at 6 AM. The adult education pivot to WhatsApp-based remote instruction with 100 Chromebooks was exactly the kind of responsive, community-centered adaptation that equity demands -- meeting families where they are, with the tools they actually use.

Then we got to the elementary reconfiguration and community school expansion votes, and equity vanished from the conversation. The board unanimously approved requesting a formal reconfiguration proposal for March 2 with zero discussion of how it would affect students differently based on income, race, language, or disability status. DeAngelis pushed hard for this major structural change while describing kids as "flexible" and parents as "anxious," as if the logistics of getting working families across town to different schools is just anxiety to be managed rather than a material barrier to educational access.

The community school expansion could be transformative -- wraparound services addressing root causes of inequity is exactly what I advocate for. But again, no discussion of which schools get resources first or which communities have been most underserved. The Pre-K lottery's 60% equity allocation for families with special education needs or economic disadvantage is a real model, as is their effort to mirror the district's 20% multilingual population. But the partnership seats at Chickadee do not follow the same equity requirements, creating a two-tier access system.

The teachers' union president was the only voice naming what everyone else danced around: these changes have "imbalanced impacts" and students are losing "safe adults and communities they've built relationships with." When you cut 78 positions -- with 16 ed techs and 14 facilities/food/transport workers bearing a disproportionate share while only 2 administrators are affected -- that is not neutral math. It is a statement about whose labor and whose students matter least.
