# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

This was a Tuesday night, the day after I found out about the spending freeze, and the school board chair — the same one who would later stand in front of a roomful of parents and say that children are "far more flexible than parents" — was at a city council podium inviting the public to watch the website and come to the budget meetings. Looking back from March, that invitation is one of the most painful things in all of these records. She was pointing people toward a process as if their participation would shape the outcome. I know now what the outcome was.

But one thing from this meeting actually helped me understand something real: sixty-one percent. That's how much of South Portland's entire property tax bill is the school department. My parents talk about taxes at dinner like it's a separate adult problem — stressful, abstract, nothing to do with me. I never understood why the budget crisis and the tax increases were the same argument. Now I do. Sixty-one cents out of every dollar that makes my parents tense at the table — that goes to school. That's the connection. I wish someone had said that number out loud to me a long time ago.

The other two hours were about an apartment building in Mill Creek — parking, zoning, setbacks. The developer said at one point that the new units' tax revenue could help with "education funding" and "all the tough challenges" the city faces every budget season. I wanted that to mean something. Maybe more apartments means more tax money means fewer teachers cut. But I still don't know how much money, or when, or whether any of it would actually reach Memorial. All seven council members voted yes. Nobody in the building debate was a student or spoke for students.

What I keep coming back to is the $40. Two parents said it cost them over forty dollars in babysitter fees just to show up and speak for three minutes. That's why the room is always the same faces. Families like mine — who already feel targeted, who already aren't sure these spaces are safe — aren't going to pay $40 for the chance to say something to people who may not be listening. The district said: watch the website, come to the meetings. The meetings cost money to attend. Those two things can't both be true at the same time.
