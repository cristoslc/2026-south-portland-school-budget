# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

This meeting belongs to a period when Rachel is still assembling the puzzle rather than fighting the assembled picture — and it's significant precisely because of the pieces that quietly click into place. She came to the December 9th city council meeting because she'd heard DeAngelos would be there, and she wasn't wrong to go. What she got was the first public confirmation that budget season is launching right now, delivered by the board chair herself, with the unmistakable subtext that nobody has actually told parents what they're about to face.

The superintendent announcement is the detail that will haunt her later. DeAngelos announces that three recruiting firms are presenting the very next evening — a fact Rachel didn't know before walking in. The district is heading into the most consequential budget cycle in a generation, and the person who will navigate it is an interim. George Entwistle, whose name she will come to know well, is a placeholder. All those decisions about 78 positions, about nine scenarios, about which schools survive — they will be shaped by a superintendent who wasn't hired, doesn't have a permanent mandate, and will be gone before the dust settles. That should bother more people in that room than it apparently does.

But the moment that lodges deepest is the city manager's offhand comment about "the old Hamlin school site" as a potential housing parcel — something mentioned as if it were already common knowledge, already discussed at prior forums Rachel apparently wasn't invited to. She knows this pattern from everything ahead: the administration asks "what buildings do we have?" and the educational question of what children need gets answered second, or not at all. A school becomes a real estate asset in a single subordinate clause. She files it away.

The tax burden context matters too. Schools consuming 61% of property taxes, homeowner values rising while commercial values fall — she understands now why the city is primed to accept painful school cuts. The pressure is systemic, not just a bad year. And the two parents pleading for virtual comment access because childcare costs $40 a meeting have just articulated, precisely, the problem Rachel herself will face as meetings multiply through February and March.
