# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

The March 23 meeting is where the Skillin relief collapsed. Three weeks ago I let myself feel something close to hope when four board members stopped that closure by naming the racial politics directly — Tyler Smith's line about how easy it is politically to bus Black and brown kids. I read that as evidence the equity lens could prevail when the stakes were made sufficiently visible. What I did not account for is that the administration simply moved the target. Kayler: approximately 45% BIPOC, 30 to 35% multilingual learners, socioeconomically qualified for a fresh fruits and vegetables snack grant, served by a lunch aide who is also being eliminated. The most diverse remaining elementary school in the proposed budget. A parent — Jess Elsner — had to be the one to enter those demographic numbers into the public record at minute 163. The district had already recommended Kayler for closure before those figures were formally stated in a public meeting.

Then Elsner asked the board to explain what steps were taken to ensure the Kayler closure did not violate Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. Chair DeAngelis said — at 11 o'clock at night, after five hours: "I don't know if anybody is prepared to speak to that, or if we need to get a legal answer about that." No legal analysis was provided before adjournment.

That sentence is the most consequential thing I have taken out of any meeting since December. Not because it surprises me — the absence of equity analysis has been visible since the first budget workshop on December 8. But a Title VI question is not an equity aspiration. It is a federal civil rights statute. The answer should have existed before Kayler appeared in a budget document. That it does not is a legal exposure, and it is the most concrete lever this process has offered me.

The rest assembled the same architecture at smaller scale. The administration's own presentation acknowledged that Option B "does not address our goal of having more heterogeneous schools" — it would perpetuate "vastly different student populations demographically." They said it. Left it on the table anyway. The DEI Director is under review for demotion to embedded specialist, not yet decided, direction clear enough. And the ESOL cut at the middle school is partly justified by lower January enrollment — the January when multilingual students were not coming to school because of ICE. The district is using its students' fear-driven absence as a rationale to reduce the services those students need. That is the feedback loop I intend to document with receipts.

Andrew Giggler said from the floor: 17% of students are multilingual learners, and their families were not in the room. Transportation and translation were offered tonight — at what appears to be the final workshop before a vote. Not in December. Not in January. Tonight. The community engagement exclusion I have been tracking since the superintendent search has arrived at its endpoint. The externally funded Pre-K seats — 40 of them, targeting children with IEPs and income-eligible four-year-olds — are the right thing and they cost the district nothing. I will take it. But they do not close the gap that the rest of this meeting assembled.
