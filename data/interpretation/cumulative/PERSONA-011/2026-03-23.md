# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Five hours. No vote. That's the headline I'm delivering tonight, and it's the worst kind — not a decision people need to act on, not a number that changes the picture, just an adjournment at 11:15 PM with three action items still untouched. The board heard two-plus hours of public comment and then Chair DeAngelis said she wasn't going to push them into a vote at that hour. I understand it. My group chats are not going to understand it. March 30, 6pm — that's what I'm posting, and I'm posting it first.

Here's what I CAN give people from tonight, and some of it is genuinely the clearest picture I've had since this started in December.

Kayler. That's the school. Not Dyer — Kayler. The recommendation changed in recent weeks, the slides weren't posted until the Friday before the meeting, and the families who showed up tonight found out alongside the rest of us in real time. The district zeroed out Kayler's entire FY27 budget line in the budget book. I've had "which school closes" open since March 2nd, when the board took Skillin off the table on equity grounds and the surviving options narrowed to something I couldn't see yet. The answer is Kayler. I'm waiting on the vote to make it final.

The dollar number I've been waiting for since December: $257 more per year for a home assessed at South Portland's average of $514,000. That's the 6% increase translated into something real. I've been reporting the percentage and the structural gap for three months, but this is the first time anyone put a household number on it. Slide 8. I screenshotted it and sent it before the meeting was half over.

The 78 positions are confirmed — 42 teachers, 32 of them on a recall list with no guaranteed placement. Pink slips went out March 18th; I caught that from Matthews at the March 19 council meeting and flagged it, and tonight the workshop laid out the full scope. Something that deserves more air time than it got: every single lunch aide is gone. All seven positions, every school. That's a tangible daily change for every elementary family starting in September and it barely came up compared to the percussion ed tech, who is apparently on the chopping block for a second year running.

The coldest moment of the night was the finance director — who is, I have to correct myself, actually the seventh in six years, not the sixth in five as I reported after the February 3rd audit — telling the board that this budget "resets our financial path but does not solve our core problems." She walked through why FY28 will be harder: labor costs automatically outrun 6%, utilities are up 13–14%, and the athletic field bond adds $300,000 in new debt service. We're cutting 78 people and the structural math still doesn't close. I said it in December. She just put a FY28 timestamp on it.

There's also a legal question hanging over the March 30 vote that I need to track: Kayler is approximately 45% BIPOC and 30–35% multilingual learners, and board members raised whether closing it triggers Title VI civil rights concerns. DeAngelis said she wants a real legal opinion before the vote, not a quick answer. I want that answer too, and I'll be looking for it Monday.

March 30. I'll be watching.
