# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

This meeting delivered more live drama than Dana expected from a regular session. Three major developments rewrite the storylines she has been tracking since December.

First, the 6% tax ceiling she flagged as a constraint in the December 17 workshop is cracking. Board Member Feller publicly broke ranks, saying he would support a tax increase "exceeding 6%" rather than make "draconian" cuts. His hedging -- "I don't want a double digit increase... but like, it's probably gonna be larger than previously contemplated" -- is the kind of quote that signals a political position shifting in real time on camera. This is news: the board's self-imposed fiscal constraint is unraveling before they have even finalized cuts.

Second, the teachers union came out swinging. President Sergay directly contradicted any suggestion that educators support the cuts, stating "We are not going to endorse plans that result in our colleagues losing their jobs." This is the counter-narrative Dana has needed -- someone willing to call out the board's framing on the record. The union's demand for a specific tax increase number from the city council rather than vague "guidance" resonates with the broader uncertainty theme Dana has been tracking.

Third, and most alarming: the current fiscal year is also heading for deficit. Finance Director Chen revealed that all FY26 budget scenarios now project red ink, with the nutrition fund alone facing a $1.1 million shortfall. Dana has been covering the $7.2M FY27 gap as the crisis, but learning that this year's budget is also underwater means the district is bleeding money on two fronts simultaneously. This reframes the story from "painful cuts next year" to "ongoing fiscal emergency."

The immigration resolution and the board's unanimous endorsement of the city council's position provides a softer human-interest angle if needed, but for Dana the budget storylines are stronger. The elementary reconfiguration study got its green light despite Feller acknowledging "most folks who have reached out to me have not been in favor," which reinforces the board-versus-community tension she has been tracking since December.
