# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

The reconfiguration just went from a concept being discussed to a plan the board unanimously approved for formal development. Chair DeAngelis called it "fabulous" and "good for kids," and they voted to have a full proposal ready for the March 2 budget workshop. That enthusiasm from the chair, honestly, makes me more nervous, not less. If the people driving this are this excited, it means it's really happening. My daughter could enter a school system that looks completely different from what exists today -- grade-band primaries for pre-K through first, then a switch to a different building for second through fourth. That's a lot of transitions for a little kid.

The budget picture got even grimmer. The finance director said all scenarios now lead to deficit, not just the worst case. That's a meaningful shift from what I heard before, when there seemed to be at least some paths that might work. Now it's not "if" there are painful cuts but "how painful." The $7.2M gap and 78 positions being cut -- numbers I've heard before -- land differently when you hear the board debating whether to go above their own 6% tax ceiling because even that level of increase requires "draconian" cuts. One board member was willing to go higher; another said they already committed to 6%. They can't even agree among themselves.

Two things gave me a small measure of comfort. First, the pre-K program details got more specific: 64 seats in district, 16 at Chickadee, CDS transition bringing special ed services for 4-year-olds in July 2026, and the lottery system means demand outpaces supply -- which signals that families actually want this. Second, enrollment ticked up by 12 students since January. That's tiny, but after months of hearing about 23% declines, any upward movement matters psychologically.

The community school model expanding from middle to high school, with potential state grants of $100,000 and a new Education Foundation, also registered as a positive. It suggests the district isn't only cutting -- there are people trying to build alternative funding and programming structures. But it's hard to celebrate growth initiatives when the board is simultaneously debating whether cutting 78 jobs is even enough.
