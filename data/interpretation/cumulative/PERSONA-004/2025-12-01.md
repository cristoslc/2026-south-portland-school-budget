# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

Looking back at December 1 now, after everything that's happened, the inauguration is almost unbearable to watch. All that ceremony and hope while the budget crisis was coiling underneath — nobody mentioned it. Not one word about the $7.2 million hole, the 78 positions about to be discussed, the 42 teachers whose careers were already in jeopardy. These people were literally being sworn in to make those votes, and they got poems and speeches about community connections instead of any honest accounting of what was waiting for them.

Rosemary's background was supposed to be encouraging. A teacher herself, decades in the community, all those connections to former students she's maintained. When she talked about the multilingual families and the kids who needed the most support — that's exactly why I got into this work. But I know now how it played out: she became the board's loudest voice for reconfiguration, called it "fabulous," dismissed parents who showed up to testify as people who underestimate how flexible their own children are. The teacher background didn't translate into protecting teachers. It became credential-washing for policies that gutted the workforce.

Four new board members sworn in simultaneously — half the board changing at the worst possible time. Feller turned out to be more honest than most: at least he admitted the 6% ceiling might not hold and floated the early retirement buyout idea that was the only creative alternative anyone put forward. Smith at least asked the right questions about what the spending freeze actually meant in classrooms. But Richardson gave us "PTAs can fill the gaps," and that told me everything I needed to know about how that vote would view teacher resources.

The mayor talking about housing and climate and music festivals while the school district was heading off a cliff. I understand they're separate governance structures. But it's the same tax base, the same families, the same community. The total silence about the education funding crisis on the night we inaugurated the people who would have to navigate it — that silence wasn't an oversight. It was a preview of the pattern that kept repeating for the next three months.

### Emotional Register

Marcus looks back at December 1 with a retroactive bitterness he couldn't have felt at the time: the same teacher-turned-chair who spoke so movingly about her former students would go on to champion the policies that will sever those kinds of relationships for the next generation of South Portland educators. The inauguration now reads less like a beginning than the last moment of institutional innocence before the collapse.
