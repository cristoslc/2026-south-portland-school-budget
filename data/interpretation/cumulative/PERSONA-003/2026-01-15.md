# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Goal Setting Workshop -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

The January 15 city council meeting is, in one sense, the emptiest thing I've encountered in three months of following this budget crisis. The agenda says "Annual Goal-Setting Session." There's an attachment I can't find. No outcomes are listed, no action items, no record of what was discussed or decided. From the outside, I have no way to know whether the council talked about schools at all, or whether they set goals that have any relationship to the $7.2 million gap and 78 positions that the school board has been bleeding over in public since December.

I already know all the numbers the fiscal summary cites -- the 42 teachers, the 23% enrollment collapse, the depleted fund balance, the state paying 20% of a cost it's supposed to cover at 55%. I know them because I've been watching the school board excavate them in painful detail since December 8. The city council didn't tell me any of this. The school board did.

What makes January 15 significant isn't its content -- it's its timing and its silence. Two days after the Mahoney workshop, where I learned about a potential triple tax stack that nobody was calculating as a combined number. One week before the January 22 citizen forum, when families would finally be formally invited to engage with the school budget. The city council is setting its annual goals in the exact middle of the most consequential stretch of education policy in years, and the public record is a one-line agenda item.

The January 13 Mahoney workshop already confirmed for me that the city's capital planning universe and the school district's crisis universe don't communicate. January 15 extends that finding into something even more foundational: even the city's basic annual planning process -- the moment when elected officials decide what they care about for the coming year -- is a closed room. Whatever goals were set here, they haven't resolved anything for families like mine. I know that now from the other side of it. The opaque agenda from this day is just another entry in a ledger that was already full: city governance is not where you go to understand what is happening to your child's school.
