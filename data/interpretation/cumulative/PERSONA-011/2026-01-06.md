# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

I'm watching a city council meeting five weeks after the spending freeze hit and the $1.9 million deficit got laid on the table, and the school stuff is buried in the last twenty minutes. That's city council for you — the items that are eating my group chats alive show up as line items between cemetery donation funds and a Bug Light festival proposal. But I'm here, phone in hand, because that's what I do.

The two dates I walked out of this meeting with: January 22nd and February 10th. Chair DeAngelis announced the public budget forum — 6-8pm, location still TBD, which is going to frustrate people who want to put it in their calendars tonight. But it exists, which is more than we had yesterday. Since December 8th I've been tracking a crisis with no formal public input channel attached to it. The spending freeze happened, the $7.2 million structural gap was being constructed in workshops, and parents had nowhere to officially weigh in. January 22nd is that moment, and I'm already pushing it in every group chat.

February 10th is the one that actually determines everything. The city manager named it plainly: that's when council tells the school board what tax increase they'll accept and whether this is a year to add positions or cut them. I've had an open question since December 1st about when the council would formally engage on school funding — when they'd stop being the housing and development body and become the people who control the school budget ceiling. February 10th is the answer. Everything the district has been building toward — the 78 positions, the reconfiguration options, the scenarios — gets constrained by whatever number comes out of that room. I don't know yet what they'll say, but I know now when they'll say it.

The state property tax task force is the kind of item I'd normally scroll past. But watching this city squeeze its school budget for months while the state covers 20% of costs against a 55% statutory obligation — anything at the state level that touches property tax relief is worth flagging. The first report is supposedly due this month. I'll believe it when I see it, but I'll be looking.

The heating oil donations were a small good thing in a season of bad news. 2,500 gallons from Global Partners plus over $1,100 in cash. I led my group chat summary with the forum date, ended with the heating assistance. People needed something warm to close on.
