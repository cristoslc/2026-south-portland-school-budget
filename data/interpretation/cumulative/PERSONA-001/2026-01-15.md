# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

January 15 is the meeting where Maria goes looking for the council's hand and finds it hidden. She's carrying everything she already knows by this point: 42 teachers proposed for elimination, 16 ed techs on the list, the fund balance gone, the state covering 20% of costs when it should cover 55%, elementary enrollment down 23% in four years. She learned all of that in December. What she doesn't know — what the city council is shaping right now — is the political ceiling that will determine how much of it comes true.

That's what makes the opacity of January 15 so corrosive. One agenda item. A "goal-setting workshop." An attachment that wasn't published anywhere she could find. She sat in that room, or tried to follow along, and got nothing she could read. From March 2026, she knows exactly what those goals translated into: guidance of 3-6% at the February 10 joint workshop, with most councilors at 4-5%, below the school board's own requested floor. The ceiling that would force deeper cuts than any scenario the board had modeled was quietly being fixed in a session she couldn't access. The participation infrastructure failures she first traced in December 4 — Zoom blocked, remote parents locked out, in-person attendance expensive and inconvenient — extend now to the council's own internal deliberations.

The ed tech piece keeps catching in her throat. She knew the number since December 17 — 16 positions — but January is when she starts pressing on what it actually means in a room with children. Ed techs are not interchangeable with teachers. They're the adults who sit beside kids with IEPs, kids who need the extra minute, kids whose parents fought the school system for a support plan that depends entirely on another human body being present. Eliminating 16 of them is not the same as increasing class sizes by two. It removes the scaffolding that keeps specific, identifiable children from falling through. And the fund balance being gone means whatever gets cut in FY27 stays cut — no phase-in, no restoration next year if revenue surprises on the upside.

What January 15 adds to Maria's accumulating picture is not new information. It's the shape of a decision being made without her. The council was setting the political terms for everything that would follow — terms that would constrain the board, guarantee deeper cuts than the 6% floor the board had set for itself, and leave no room for the tax increase Maria would choose in a heartbeat over losing those teachers. And she couldn't see any of it.
