# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

One day after learning about the spending freeze on laminating paper and the 78-position cut proposal, Jaylen stays up watching the city council meeting because everyone's been saying the schools will finally come up. They do — once, for two minutes, during public comment, not on the actual agenda. School board member DeAngelis gets three minutes at the microphone to say two things: budget season starts this month, and tonight there's a superintendent search workshop at the SPHS lecture hall. The public can come. Three minutes to speak, anyone. Then she leaves, and the council thanks her and moves on to an apartment building in Mill Creek.

The 61% figure is the number that should stop the room. DeAngelis says schools represent 61% of South Portland property taxes — the single biggest thing the city spends money on — and she says she raises this every time she stands at that microphone. Not one councilor asks a follow-up question. The budget crisis that will reshape Jaylen's senior year is treated as familiar furniture, not a question. It lands the way the mayor's "incredible investment in our children" did on December 1: acknowledged and immediately set aside.

Then a real estate developer gets more meaningful airtime. Casey Prentice is pitching a 170-unit housing development and specifically cites "education funding and all the tough challenges you face every budget season" as reasons the council should approve his project. A guy trying to build apartments knows the school budget is in crisis and names it in a business pitch, and still manages more substantive engagement with the district's finances than the entire council produced in response to DeAngelis. The $7.2M gap and 42 teacher cuts — unmentioned all night — are apparently common knowledge in the real estate community. Just not a council agenda item.

The invitation is the one live wire. DeAngelis told anyone watching to show up tonight at SPHS, watch the website, get involved. The superintendent search is happening in the lecture hall at his school and he can walk in and say something. He testified at a board meeting once and felt dismissed, but at least he was in the room. This meeting is the clearest possible demonstration of what Jaylen has been learning since December 8: the school budget is the city's biggest line item, and it is handled somewhere else, by someone else, in a process that continues without him unless he physically inserts himself into it.
