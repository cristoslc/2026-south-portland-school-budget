### Reactions

This was a master class in how budget "neutrality" perpetuates inequity. George gets up there with his fancy charts showing enrollment down and staff up, but conveniently leaves out that the students we're serving today need three times more support than they did in 2017. No breakdown of how many are English learners who need specialized instruction, how many have disabilities requiring intensive services, how many are experiencing homelessness or trauma. Just raw numbers designed to make it look like we're overstaffed when we're actually understaffed for the population we serve.

And then - this is rich - they propose cutting 78 positions, mostly teachers and ed techs who work directly with kids, while the teachers' union president has been waiting SIX WEEKS for basic data about central office staffing. Six weeks! They can produce elaborate PowerPoints with three budget scenarios but can't tell us how many administrators we have compared to other districts? That tells you everything about priorities right there.

The only moment that gave me hope was when Jess Walker actually named what's happening at Skillings - 50% students of color and new Mainers - versus Small School at 27%. Finally someone said out loud that we have apartheid elementary schools. If this reconfiguration can break up that segregation, maybe there's a silver lining. But I'm not holding my breath that equity will actually drive the decisions when push comes to shove.
