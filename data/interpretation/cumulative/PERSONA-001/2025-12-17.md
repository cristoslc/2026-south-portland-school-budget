# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Workshop -- December 17, 2025

### Interpretation

This workshop gives Maria the granular data she has been craving since the crisis broke open on December 8th, but the numbers only deepen her alarm. The 23% elementary enrollment decline -- from 1,401 to 1,080 in just four years -- is the statistical backbone behind the 42 teacher cuts she already knew about, and seeing the per-pupil spending figure of $26,651 (highest among comparable districts) creates a painful cognitive dissonance: how can the district be spending more per student than Scarborough or Cape Elizabeth while simultaneously gutting classrooms?

State funding is the structural villain here. Learning that the state covers only 20% of actual costs -- against a statutory promise of 55% -- reframes the crisis for Maria. This is not purely a local management failure; the state has systematically underfunded its obligations, and local property taxpayers have been absorbing the difference. But that context does not make the 78 proposed position cuts any less real. The 16 educational technicians slated for elimination hit especially close to home -- Maria knows families whose children depend on one-on-one reading support from ed techs, and she can already imagine those children being left behind.

The fund balance being fully depleted is the detail that keeps Maria awake at night. In the December 8 meeting she learned about the current-year deficit and spending freeze; the December 10 meeting revealed the board spending $30,000 it didn't have on a superintendent search. Now she understands there is literally no financial cushion remaining. If health insurance comes in above the 12% projection, if enrollment drops further, if any unexpected cost materializes, there is nothing to absorb the shock. The district is operating without a safety net while proposing to eliminate 12% of its workforce.

Maria leaves this workshop more informed but also more frustrated. She now has the data to make the case in her parent networks, and she is already texting other parents: we need bodies at every meeting from here on out. The board's 6% tax increase ceiling, which she first learned about at the February budget forum, now feels arbitrary -- she would rather pay more in taxes than watch her daughter's class swell to 28 students with no aide.
