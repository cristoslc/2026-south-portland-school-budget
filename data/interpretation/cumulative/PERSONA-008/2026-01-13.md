# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

This meeting doesn't have anything to do with elementary schools directly — and that's exactly why it matters so much. Rachel came in already primed from a week that included the January 6th council meeting where DeAngelos announced the budget forum, and the January 12th board meeting where the formal reconfiguration motion passed. She has been building a picture of compounding pressures on the same pool of South Portland taxpayers, and tonight that picture snapped into focus with a number: $194 million.

The Mahoney City Center project — converting a former school building into city hall, plus a new police station, plus eventually a new fire station — is being proposed at a price tag the finance director confirmed cannot be meaningfully offset by grants, tax credits, naming rights, or any other creative financing. It goes on the property tax bill. For the average homeowner in South Portland, that's over $1,100 a year added in the first year of the bond. That's on top of whatever the school budget needs. On top of the sewer upgrades someone mentioned at the podium. On top of the revaluation that sent people to this same podium in tears a couple of years ago.

What Rachel can't shake is the symbolic weight of the building at the center of this. The former Mahoney School — a place where Councilor Matthews's mother graduated, where his kids went to middle school — is being preserved and repurposed for city employees. It gets "a second chapter." Meanwhile, the district serving kids right now is cutting 42 teachers and considering forcing every elementary student in the city to change schools between first and second grade. The community will invest hundreds of millions to save a building that used to be a school, but cannot find a path to fully funding the schools that are open today. She knows the situations aren't directly fungible. She also knows they come out of the same household budgets, and nobody at any of these meetings is talking about them together.

The council doing something Rachel has rarely seen — pumping the brakes, calling $194M a non-starter, sending the architects back for cheaper options — is genuine relief. It's the first time she has watched an elected body respond to fiscal reality before committing. But the $748,000 already spent, the $400,000 more committed just to rework the design, and the looming November ballot deadline remind her that institutional momentum is hard to stop even when officials say the right things. The school board said the right things about Skillin too.

Rachel leaves genuinely relieved that the council had the sense to say no to $194 million but unsettled in a deeper way — the systemic picture is now complete enough that she can see the ceiling, and it's lower than she wanted it to be.
