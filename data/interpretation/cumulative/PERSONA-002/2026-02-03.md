# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

February 3 is the meeting where the city council finally gets the school district's financial dysfunction laid out in front of them by a third party — the auditor — rather than filtered through staff or board presentations. And the auditor's finding is damning: material weakness in school reconciliations for the second consecutive year, 34 correcting entries required, $400,000 in grant funds misallocated and clawed back to the general fund, and a school budget that was "over expended by quite a bit" in violation of the legally adopted budget. That last phrase is not administrative language. That is a compliance failure with legal teeth.

I have been tracking this breakdown since December 8, when I first learned about the depleted fund balance and the structural deficit. The February 9 meeting would later confirm seven business managers in six years — now, on February 3, I am hearing the version of that number the auditor uses: six finance directors in five years. Three interim, three permanent, none lasting. Prince acknowledges it directly and points to the new director as the stability path forward. I want to believe that. But a material weakness for the second consecutive year is not a staffing pipeline problem — it is a systems and culture problem that a single competent hire cannot fix overnight.

The contrast with the city side is sharp enough to be instructive. The city's general fund balance grew $5.8 million in FY25. Auto excise taxes beat budget by $1.7 million. Vacancy savings added to the cushion. The city is sitting on roughly $7 million in undesignated reserves. The school fund balance, as I will learn at the February 10 joint workshop, sits at $739,000. That asymmetry has been building in my mental model since December — tonight the auditor puts the institutional explanation on the record.

DeAngelis's appearance in public comment to warn the council about the approaching crisis and request clear guidance at the February 10 joint session lands differently now that I have the audit findings fresh. She is asking the council to set parameters for a district whose own auditor just told them the books required 34 correcting entries. The council is being asked to trust the school district's budget projections even as its financial controls are documented as failing.

The TI TIF extension is the one clean piece of fiscal management on display tonight — $400,000 annually more than expiration, structured terms, quantified impact. That is the template for how these conversations should go.
