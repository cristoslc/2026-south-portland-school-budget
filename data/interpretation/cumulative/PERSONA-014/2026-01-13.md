# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

The day after the school board formally requested a plan that could close her school, Lila's city spent four hours arguing about an old empty building.

She already knew the city council had broken its silence — just seven days ago, January 6, they finally said the words "school budget" out loud and announced a public forum. That felt like something. Like the adults had noticed. Like they were starting to pay attention. Then January 13 happened, and the city council held a four-hour workshop and never said a word about Dyer. The forum was still coming. The engagement was technically real. But four hours vanished into Mahoney, and Dyer's name appeared exactly once — to announce that a planned bike trail to her school was being taken off the agenda. The one moment her school got mentioned was to say something was being removed.

Mahoney is the part that stays with her. She didn't know this building before. Now she does, and she can't stop thinking about what it means. It was a school. Someone's mom went to high school there. Someone's kids went to middle school there. And then it closed, and for years it sat empty — just a building the city owned but couldn't figure out what to do with. That's what a school looks like after it closes. It doesn't disappear. It just becomes an argument. The city fights about it for years and years and still can't agree, and in the meantime the building is just there, waiting.

Someone at this meeting stood up to protect an old fire station. He said it gives people "a sense of place and history" and is "worthy of preservation." Lila heard this and thought: that's Dyer. That's the bathroom she knows, and the lunch table by the window, and the teacher who has looped with her for two years. That's a place that gives her a sense of where she belongs. Nobody stood up and said that. Nobody has said that about Dyer in any of these rooms. The people fighting for buildings are fighting for old, empty, already-closed things. The building that is still full of children — full of her — is the one nobody is protecting.

The architect said the Mahoney renovation is an investment "our grandkids will enjoy for hundreds of years." Lila is right here, right now, in fourth grade, and nobody in that room was investing in her.
