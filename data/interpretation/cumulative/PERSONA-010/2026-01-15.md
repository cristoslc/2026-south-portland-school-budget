# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council Goal Setting Workshop -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

Ben has spent the last three months tracing decisions back to their origins — the missed contract costs, the depleted fund balance, the 6% tax ceiling that drove 78 positions to the chopping block. The January 15 goal-setting workshop is where he hits a wall. The entire public record is a single agenda line: "Annual Goal-Setting Session. This Agenda Item Contains an Attachment." No transcript, no minutes, no sense of what was said or decided. The attachment — which is the whole meeting, functionally — isn't in anything he can access.

This matters more now than it would have in January. The 6% tax ceiling is the number that produced the $7.2M cut target, the 42 teacher eliminations, the ed tech layoffs, the Skillin closure fight. Ben has been writing around that number for weeks without being able to trace it to a specific public moment — not a board vote, not a council resolution, nothing he can point a reader to and say "here, this is where it came from." A city council goal-setting workshop held at the opening of budget season, with an invisible attachment, is exactly the setting where that ceiling could have been established or confirmed. He can't tell from what he has.

The silence is almost more interesting than the missing content. The school tax is 61% of the South Portland property tax bill. The roll-forward scenario requires an 18–19% increase. And the council's annual goal-setting session — the meeting where they set direction for the year — produces no visible public record touching any of it. Either it's in that attachment, or it isn't, or the real conversation happened somewhere else entirely. All three possibilities matter for the story he's trying to write about where decisions actually get made in this city.

He's already thinking about the calls he needs to make. The attachment is a public document. The city manager's office can produce it. And if it contains the 6% ceiling — or the council's stated tolerance for school tax increases — that's the missing connective tissue between the political decisions and the human consequences his readers have been living with since January.
