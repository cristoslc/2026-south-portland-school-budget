# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

The January 6 meeting is the December 9 pattern running on a second loop. DeAngelis shows up during public comment — three weeks after Jaylen learned about 78 positions and a $7.2M gap — to announce a citizen forum on January 22nd. "Potential cost saving measures." Adult language for: we're going to cut things, and you can come watch us discuss it. The council thanks her. Moves on. The rest of the night is cemetery ordinances and donation procedures.

But the city manager's announcement is the one that lands differently now that Jaylen has watched February 10 play out. The budget guidance workshop is coming, and the framing is already there: "whether it's a year to be looking to add new positions or not, adding new programs or not." He already knows the answer. The 4-6% ceiling that constrained the school board's options wasn't a decision made in February — it was forming in January, in that question, in that framing. Jaylen heard it as a neutral process announcement at the time. Now he hears it as the restrictive guidance arriving before the school board could even make its case.

The music festival proposal is the part that stings. Mark Emmi walks up to the microphone with an idea about a free arts event at Bug Light Park, and the council is genuinely excited. Discussion, encouragement, staff follow-up — this is what arts support looks like at city hall. Meanwhile, DeAngelis gets three minutes to announce the schools are about to make major cuts, and the council thanks her and pivots to cemetery rules. The institution that will enthusiastically discuss community arts programming is the same institution imposing the budget ceiling that puts theater at SPHS at risk. Nobody in that room is connecting these two facts.

The January 22 forum turns out to be February 4 — a room full of adults discussing the budget in elementary enrollment numbers and object codes, Angela and Alex sitting silently with no speaking role, questions redirected to a Google form. The citizen invitation meant: come watch us talk. Jaylen could have walked through that door and had nothing change. He knows that now.

What January 6 adds to his map is this: the city's austerity posture was already in place before the school board presented its case, and the formal budget process is structured so that the council's constraints land on students without requiring the council to look at any of them directly.
