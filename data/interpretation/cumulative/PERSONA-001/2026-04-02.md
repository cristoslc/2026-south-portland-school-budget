# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The meeting opens with the thing Maria did not expect: the board pulls the early release days before the first agenda item is settled. She has been texting about it all week. They heard the working-parent argument, and they changed course. For ten minutes, she thinks maybe the pattern breaks here.

It doesn't. But the ten minutes matter to her more than she expected, because they are the first concrete evidence since December that public pressure produced a visible reversal. Not a forum absorbed without response, not input acknowledged and set aside — an actual change. She is filing that away for April 14.

Then the behavioral strategist's statement gets read aloud, and Maria realizes she has been understating the problem in her own parent networks. She knew the position was on the cut list — she has been tracking the support staff reductions since December 17, when she first saw the 16 ed techs, and she understood the interlocking logic of it better after March 23. But the specifics land differently when they have a number: nearly 60 students, more than 40 with formal behavior plans that this one person designed and manages. The statement names what Maria has been circling for months without the right phrase: eliminating the role doesn't eliminate the needs. It removes the system built to respond to them. That is the middle layer disappearing — and it disappears in the same September that hundreds of Kayler students arrive at unfamiliar buildings with larger classes and fewer adults who know them.

Feller's question about attendance boundaries produces the answer Maria has been dreading: the district will not say where children will go to school until after the listening sessions, which begin next week, with September four months out. She cannot tell families in her parent network where their kids will be assigned. She does not know if her own daughter will walk to school or ride a bus across the city. The district says boundary decisions will be shaped by community input on walkability and family priorities — and Maria wants to believe that. But she has been to the forums. She has seen what "shaped by" means in practice, and she is going to those listening sessions anyway, not because she is certain it will matter, but because she is not staying home to find out.

The $300,000 in new state funding is real and she is sharing it tomorrow morning in every parent channel she runs. It is the first concrete number she has had to fight with rather than against. The board deferred the budget vote to get a firmer figure, which she thinks is right, even though it means more uncertainty before April 7. She does not know yet how much of it will go to positions. She is watching that carefully.
