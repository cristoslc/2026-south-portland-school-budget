# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

I've walked past the library. I've studied there after school. And I had no idea that 27 people walk through the door every single hour it's open — the library director had to install a door counter to prove it, because apparently nobody believed him about how much it gets used. He also had to prove that the building has asbestos under the carpet, electrical problems bad enough that they can't add parking lot lights without tearing out the whole system, and locks that break and cost over $300 to replace. He brought actual numbers. He documented everything. And then the council voted to cut the library from the Mahoney renovation anyway.

That's the same pattern I've been watching happen to schools for months. The Memorial field was broken for four years before anyone fixed it. The gym ceiling leaks. We never have enough instruments. Nobody at any city council meeting has ever shown up with a door counter to prove students use those classrooms. The library director did everything right — he quantified it, he brought the evidence — and it still didn't change what happened in that room.

The fire chief's testimony was genuinely scary. Firefighters sleeping above the truck bays, breathing contaminated air, no sprinkler system. I'm not saying they don't deserve a safe building. But police and fire are being offered an $87 million bond while the library gets cut from Mahoney and schools face 78 position eliminations. Someone decided which institutions get building investment and which ones get studied again and then set aside. I know now, from March 19, that Councilor Matthews voted NO on $100,000 for immigrant families because of the school's $8.4 million deficit. And here on March 5 she was already naming the worry out loud: voters can't afford the $87 million bond on top of school tax increases. She's watching the same competition from every angle. But the people paying for all of it are the same people my parents are.

The thing I keep returning to is the fire station aesthetic debate — people talking about how beautiful it looks when you drive over the bridge, whether it should go on a historic list. Firefighters are breathing contaminated air upstairs and the conversation is about how it looks from a distance. That gap between what something looks like from outside and what it's like to actually be inside it — that's been the whole problem all year.
