# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

Dana has been tracking the immigration story since December 1 — DeAngelo's multilingual welcome, Steinbrink's anti-Somali speech on December 4, children missing school in January, the canceled forum, the Red Bank car wash footage. On February 3 she watched the council bury an immigration resolution under four hours of corporate TIF business and called it a punt. February 17 is the meeting where the punt lands and nobody catches it.

The eviction moratorium failing 4–2 is the news peg. But Dana already knows the news peg is not the story. The story is the eighteen minutes before it — Cassie Moon describing an immigrant mother who dove to the floor of her car, crying, and had to be driven home on back roads at midnight to a different address because she was afraid to return to her own house. That is not policy. That is a neighbor filling in for a government that could not. Then Pedro Vasquez, chair of the South Portland Human Rights Commission, closes his testimony by telling the council he is carrying his birth certificate because his name and his skin make him a target. He said it calmly. Dana has her two clips.

She has been watching the council's pattern harden since February 3: symbolic action without financial commitment. The four no-votes were not indifferent — three of them said they would support direct rent assistance through General Assistance or a donation to Project Home. But there was no money in the room. No timeline. Just a promise shaped like an alternative. Meanwhile Carly Williams — the same Carly Williams who paid $40 in babysitting fees in December and showed up with specific staff contacts to help the city adopt Zoom technology, then never got a follow-up — is back at the podium telling the council that Project Home has ten days of emergency housing funds left. She keeps arriving with the most precise, data-backed testimony the council hears. The council keeps offering the moral equivalent of "good idea, someone should do that."

The Mahoney workshop running until 11:30 PM with six options between $57M and $105M and no decision attached is a reminder that the other crisis Dana has been tracking since December 1 — Tipton's "game changer" that the architect couldn't stand behind — is still unresolved, just at a lower price. She sat through five hours to get there. Nothing usable for tonight.

The eviction moratorium failure gives Dana the accountability angle she needs without requiring her to portray the no-votes as villains. The story is the gap: neighbor-to-neighbor mutual aid, a depleting nonprofit fund, and a council that voted no and offered an unfunded promise. The clock Dana has been assembling since January — ICE fears, canceled forums, immigrant families afraid to leave their apartments — just got a ten-day countdown attached to it.
