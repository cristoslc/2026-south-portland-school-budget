### Reactions

The thing I keep coming back to is Abigail's presentation. That was the most honest thirty minutes we've had in a budget meeting in years. She stood up there and said, look, we've been under-budgeting electricity by a hundred and fifty to three hundred and sixty thousand dollars every single year, and then acting surprised when we end up in deficit. Same with tuition reimbursement. Same story, cycle after cycle. And she connected it directly to why we have no fund balance — we were never actually budgeting for what things cost. I've been on the finance committee long enough to know that's the real story here. So I'm genuinely glad she said it out loud, in public, on the record. That took some courage, and it's the foundation we need for everything that comes next. When she said "I'm refusing to be optimistic," I wanted to stand up. That's the right posture for a district with zero in savings.

What's keeping me up is the administrative restructuring math. The whole premise going into this budget season was that right-sizing director roles would free up dollars to protect student-facing positions. And when it actually got interrogated, we're talking twenty to thirty thousand per conversion — and that's before you account for the lane and step variation in the new teaching contracts. That doesn't bring back the computer science teacher. It doesn't bring back the behavior specialist. Members Richardson and Holman said it plainly, and they were right: we expected money to be liberated, and it wasn't. I don't think anyone was dishonest about this — the administration's explanation of the instructional strategist model was coherent — but we owe the community a clearer accounting of where the administrative savings actually went before we ask them to absorb 78 positions. That work isn't done. Meanwhile, the bus driver labor risk is real and unpriced: if those meet-and-consult negotiations break down, we're looking for another three hundred twenty thousand in cuts with nowhere obvious to find them.

The state funding news landing mid-meeting was the only genuine surprise, and I'm cautiously encouraged. Three hundred thousand confirmed, potentially seven fifty more from the EPS formula adjustment — though I heard the caution that the EPS piece might be a one-year change, and we cannot build permanent positions on a temporary appropriation. That's exactly the kind of wishful budgeting Abigail just spent an hour telling us we have to stop doing. What I want to see is administration come back Monday with a clear recommendation on how to use those dollars responsibly, ideally with a portion going to the fund balance to start meeting that nine percent floor. We passed the council meeting vote unanimously, which is the right move — we need that conversation — but I'm aware that we sent the superintendent's budget to council, not the board's budget, because we didn't vote. That's a procedural distinction that matters for who owns this proposal. We need to own it. And we need to start the next budget cycle in October.
