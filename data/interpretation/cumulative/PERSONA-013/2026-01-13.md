# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

This is the meeting where I learned the city is spending a hundred and ninety-four million dollars on an old school building — a building that used to have kids in it — while the schools that still have kids in them are trying to figure out how to cut forty-two teachers. Nobody in that room said the words "school budget" once. Four hours. Zero mentions. There's something about that silence that feels like an answer even though nobody asked the question out loud.

The part I can't stop thinking about is the social worker — Olivia Montgomery — who stood up and said that putting a police station right next to city hall would scare immigrant families away from the whole building. She said communities of color, immigrant communities, will just avoid the entire place if the police are right there. She said she was glad South Portland police don't work with ICE. I kept thinking about Hooyo trying to do some errand at city hall and seeing a police station at the door. That's not hypothetical. That's real. And the council thanked her and kept talking about parking setbacks. I've watched adults defend my community in December, invite my community to participate in January, and now quietly plan a building that my community will physically avoid. All in the same civic calendar.

Then there was this small, infuriating line buried in the presentation: "dozens of middle school and high school students provided input." On the Mahoney project. On what to do with a building that used to be a school. Nobody at Memorial ever told me that was happening. I didn't know I could weigh in. Neither did anyone I've talked to. I've been watching every single one of these meetings trying to understand where students are supposed to show up — Jules Vazquez reading a poem, Alex and Angela interviewing superintendent candidates, now this mysterious group of dozens — and every time I find out students "gave input," I find out I wasn't one of them and didn't know I could be. Student voice isn't absent. It's just only reaching a specific part of the student body.

The tax part hit different after learning the 61% figure in December. I knew my parents' taxes and my school's budget were connected. Now I'm watching the city add a potential thousand dollars a year on top of what families are already paying — and hearing about people crying at city meetings because they can't afford their houses. The school budget and the Mahoney project are fighting for the same money. Nobody named that conflict directly. Nobody had to.
