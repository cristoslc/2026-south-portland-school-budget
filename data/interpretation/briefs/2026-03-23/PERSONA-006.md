## Since Last Meeting

No new inter-meeting evidence has emerged since the March 19 meeting.

That silence is itself notable. Tom arrives at this meeting with the full weight of March 19 unprocessed by any new public information — and no one has yet produced what he most needs: a single document that adds all four cost pressures together and tells him what they mean for his tax bill.

## Open Questions

Carrying into March 23 in Tom's voice:

- **What is my actual total bill?** School taxes, sewer, police/fire bond in November, Mahoney in 2027 — nobody has put those four numbers in one place and told me what a home assessed at the South Portland average will owe, year by year, through 2029. I want that number. Who has it?

- **Did the deficit go from $7.2M to $8.4M because of the health insurance revision, or is there more we haven't been told about?** The $1.2M gap needs an itemized explanation. What specifically changed?

- **The 82 positions the board added while enrollment fell — they promised the council a breakdown at the February 10 workshop and it still hasn't appeared. Where is it?**

- **Does the $8.4M gap mean more than 78 jobs have to go? The layoff notices covered 72 positions. Are there more cuts coming, or does the gap close some other way?**

- **Can the 6% ceiling actually hold now?** A board member was already signaling intent to blow past it in February. With the deficit higher than the original estimates, what keeps the school board inside any ceiling?

- **That 6–1 reserve drawdown on March 19 — what was the one dissenting vote, and why? That's the only fiscal voice I trust right now.**

- **The Suncor redevelopment: $4 million a year in potential tax revenue sounds good. What is the actual timeline? Is this a five-year story or a twenty-year story?**

- **LD 1829 mandates four-unit density on virtually every residential lot with no planning board review. Has anyone in this process modeled what that does to South Portland's assessed values and the residential share of the tax burden over the next decade?**

- **The 170 Ocean Street TIF returns 50% of new tax revenue to the developer for 30 years. Are there other deals like that in the city's portfolio that I don't know about?**

- **Two consecutive years of budget non-compliance is an auditor finding. $400,000 in misapplied grant funds. What are the actual legal consequences? Is anyone accountable?**

## Watch For

- **Whether the school board presents a budget number above 6%.** If they do, note whether any councilor makes a motion or takes a procedural step — or whether objection stays verbal. Verbal objection without a motion has been the pattern all winter. Tom should be prepared to name that pattern in public comment if it repeats.

- **Whether the 82-position breakdown finally appears.** If it does, Tom should be prepared to immediately ask whether it includes position classifications, hire dates, and whether any of the added positions were funded by one-time sources like ESSER that have now expired.

- **The language used around the $8.4M figure.** If board members or administrators describe the increase from $7.2M to $8.4M as routine or expected, that deserves a direct challenge — the public was never told the $7.2M figure was provisional.

- **Who the one dissenting vote was on the March 19 reserve drawdown, and whether that person speaks at this meeting.** That's the fiscal voice Tom should be tracking most carefully.

- **Any mention of the nutrition fund deficit ($715K in FY25, $1.1M projected FY26).** This fund has been invisible in most public presentations. If it isn't addressed in the FY27 budget proposal, Tom should ask where it appears.

- **Whether any administrator or board member uses the phrase "within guidance" without specifying the actual percentage increase.** That's a signal the number is uncomfortable and being softened before disclosure.

- **Signs of a deal being brokered behind closed doors.** If board members who previously signaled exceeding 6% are suddenly quiet or aligned with the council, it may mean a number was negotiated informally. Tom should ask what the agreed figure is and when it was decided.

- **Any new capital cost items introduced.** The pattern this winter has been new obligations appearing mid-meeting without prior public notice (sewer rates, nutrition fund deficit, Mahoney revisions). If something new is introduced tonight, Tom should note it, ask for written documentation, and resist any pressure to treat it as fully understood before the next meeting.

- **How the city manager frames the combined fiscal picture.** The city manager has occasionally been the most honest voice in the room. If they speak tonight about total city cost pressures, Tom should listen closely for whether they explicitly connect the school increase to sewer and capital obligations — or treat each bucket as isolated.
