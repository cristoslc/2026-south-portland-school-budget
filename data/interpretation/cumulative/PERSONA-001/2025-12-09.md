# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025

### Interpretation

The December 9 city council meeting sits in a strange temporal pocket — the day after Maria learned about the $1.9M current-year deficit and the classroom supply freeze, and the day before the board voted to hire a superintendent search firm explicitly designed to minimize community involvement. From March 2026, it reads like a hinge between two worlds.

At the city council, Rosemary DeAngelis stood up during public comment and extended what sounded like a genuine invitation. Budget season starts this month. Watch the school website. Come participate. There will be many opportunities. The two women who paid over $40 in babysitter fees just to make their three minutes of public comment were there that same night — a warning Maria could read plainly: participation is expensive, and the system does nothing to lower the cost. December 4 was when the council formally acknowledged that problem and kept moving. December 9 is proof that nothing changed.

What DeAngelis said on December 9 and what the board did the very next night are impossible to reconcile. On the 9th, she is at the city council inviting the public into the process. On the 10th, the board votes 5-2 for a superintendent search firm specifically because it keeps candidate screening with the board, not community committees. The same person, twenty-four hours apart.

The housing development discussion is a different kind of frustration. The 170 Ocean developer promised the project would help with "education funding and all the tough challenges you face every budget season." Maria wanted to believe it. But Councilor West named the TIF deal: the city has already committed to returning 50% of this project's property taxes to the developer for thirty years. The city manager's explanation — something about state subsidy formulas washing out the difference anyway — lands without comfort. The math is approximate, not precise. Development that was supposed to ease the tax burden is promising a benefit that might arrive in three decades, while 78 positions are being eliminated now.

What haunts her most, looking back: the council chamber was full of people talking about an apartment building, and not one of them was there about the schools. DeAngelis made her announcement and walked out. Budget season had started. Nobody moved.
