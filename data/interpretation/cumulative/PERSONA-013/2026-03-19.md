# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

The meeting opened with Savannah Johnson — Miss South Portland, SMCC student, a young woman not much older than me who talked about feeling invisible after COVID and then finding a place where she belonged. I've been watching these meetings since December and this is only the second time I've seen someone close to my age stand at a microphone and be genuinely celebrated for it. Jules Vazquez read a poem in December. Savannah got a proclamation in March. Those two moments are the only ones across all these months where young women were the center of the room instead of an afterthought. I notice that.

Then the room got heavy. Pedro Vasquez said his family carried their documents everywhere and held conversations about what they would do if someone got detained. We have had those conversations too. I don't know how to explain what it feels like to hear a stranger describe your family's private fear out loud in a public meeting. Chelsea Bien said she felt dread reading the police chief's texts. These weren't abstract policy concerns — they were the specific, physical fear that has been living in my neighborhood since January.

And then the police chief did something I didn't expect: he walked up to the microphone and apologized. Actually stood there and said it. He took the blame, said it belonged with him and not his officers, and one woman said she came specifically hoping he would speak. I've watched months of adults failing to say the right thing to the right people at the right time. This was different. I don't know if it changes anything. But he said it out loud.

The council voted 6 to 1 to give $100,000 to Project Home to help families who couldn't pay rent. That's the first concrete money I've seen directed at the actual human consequences of the ICE surge — not a resolution, not a statement, actual money. The one no vote came from Councilor Matthews, who said 72 people got pink slips the day before and the school has an $8 million problem. She wasn't wrong about the school. But I keep sitting with the fact that she turned the families' need into a competition with teachers' jobs. The schools and the immigrant families have been in the same crisis since January. Someone finally named that they are competing for the same limited resource — and chose the school, without asking any students what we wanted her to choose for us.

Councilor West said his own grandchildren came home scared during the ICE surge. Kids who, he said, had nothing to fear. If fear moved through the whole city — through children with every kind of background — and nobody at a four-hour meeting thought to ask what that felt like inside South Portland schools, that absence is its own answer about whose experience gets named.
