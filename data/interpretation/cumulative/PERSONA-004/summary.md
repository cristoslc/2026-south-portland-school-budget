# Cumulative Summary: Marcus (PERSONA-004)

### Current Understanding

As of March 23, Marcus understands the South Portland school district to be functionally insolvent and past the deliberation phase. The structural gap — now stated at $8.4M, grown from the $7.2M floor first identified in December — has already produced 72 RIF notices, 32 of which place certified teachers on a recall list with no guaranteed return. Finance Director Ketchem's confirmation that labor costs structurally outpace the 6% tax ceiling annually means the trap does not close after this cycle: FY28 begins at the same deficit regardless of what is cut now.

The crisis has three interlocking causes: state underfunding (20% actual coverage versus 55% statutory obligation), a staffing-enrollment mismatch built from documented service needs that is now being weaponized to scapegoat teachers, and a political tax ceiling that was always fiscally insufficient. The district's financial infrastructure was formally found materially weak — repeat audit findings, six finance directors in five years, legally non-compliant overspending — meaning the projections justifying the cuts were built on compromised ground.

The city council moved across the year from passive bystander to active fiscal competitor: TIF deals discounting development revenue for 30 years, an $87M November public safety bond referendum, and 22%-annual sewer rate increases arriving before the June 9 school referendum all draw from the same finite pool of voter goodwill. ICE enforcement has suppressed both civic participation and classroom attendance. Community organizing produced one board retreat — Skillin closure rejected — but the percussion ed tech position restored by advocacy last year has been reproposed identically, establishing that wins are not durable.

The April 7 public hearing and June 9 referendum are the remaining intervention points. Thirty-two colleagues wait in recall limbo. The board adjourned March 23 without voting on anything.
