## Key Findings

### The Budget Gap

The district faces a **$7.2 million structural gap** for FY27. If all current staff, programs, and contractual obligations are carried forward with inflation (including a projected 12% health insurance increase), taxpayers would face an **18.95% increase** in the school portion of their property tax bill. The board set an initial ceiling of **6%**, requiring roughly $7.2M in cuts or offsets. By the February 9 meeting, at least one board member (Feller) publicly acknowledged the 6% target may be insufficient to avoid "draconian" cuts.

### Root Causes

- **Declining enrollment**: South Portland is at its lowest K-12 enrollment in a decade (~2,736 students as of January 2026), part of a statewide trend. Enrollment peaked around 2022-2023 with the influx of asylum-seeking families, many of whom have since moved.
- **Staffing-enrollment divergence**: While enrollment declined, staffing levels continued to rise due to increasingly complex student needs and program expansion. This mismatch is the core financial problem.
- **Depleted fund balance**: Prior-year use of the savings account to offset tax increases means there is no cushion remaining.
- **Rising costs**: Health insurance (budgeted at 12% increase), contractual salary increases from recent labor agreements, and general inflation compound the gap.

### Proposed Solutions

Three categories of "big ideas" emerged from the administration:

1. **Elementary school reconfiguration** (~$1.5-2.2M savings): Reorganize the five elementary schools from geographic K-4 schools into grade-band campuses (e.g., pre-K-1 schools and 2-4 schools), potentially closing one building. Nine specific options (1.1-1.8 plus alternatives) were presented at the March 2 workshop. This is the largest single savings lever.

2. **Grades 5-12 staffing efficiencies** (~$1M savings): Increase class sizes, review teaching loads and course offerings, and reduce positions at the middle school and high school.

3. **Operational efficiencies** (~$1M savings): Shift changes, vendor contract renegotiation, supply reductions, and central office restructuring.

Even all three together leave a **$3M+ residual gap** that must be addressed through line-by-line budget review, revenue enhancement, or a higher tax increase.

A **non-reconfiguration path** was also modeled at ~$2.2M in savings, achieved through across-the-board class size increases and staff reductions at all schools -- but the superintendent explicitly stated this approach "kicks the problem down the road."

## Evolution of Discussion Over Time

### January 12 (Regular Meeting)
The budget was framed early: the chair announced it would be "another difficult budget year" and laid out an aggressive calendar of forums and workshops. The meeting focused largely on non-budget items (student reports, athletics, community belonging). The budget was positioned but not yet central. The superintendent search was announced alongside budget planning.

### February 4 (Budget Forum)
The first dedicated public engagement on the budget. Dr. Johanna Prince presented the financial picture with clarity: the 18.95% status-quo increase, the $7.2M gap, and the three "big ideas." This was the first time the community heard reconfiguration as a serious proposal. Questions focused on class sizes, transportation costs, and health insurance assumptions. The tone was informational and collaborative.

### February 9 (Regular Meeting)
The forum themes were reported back. The mood shifted noticeably: ICE enforcement activity had created fear among immigrant families, with children missing school. Multiple public speakers (teachers' union, parents, ed tech union) made emotional statements. The board unanimously endorsed the city council's immigration resolution. On the budget, the board formally requested a reconfiguration proposal for March 2. Member Feller publicly broke from the 6% ceiling. The teachers' union president asked for meaningful engagement beyond surveys and urged a specific tax target from the city council.

### March 2 (Budget Workshop I)
The most substantive meeting (3.5 hours). The administration presented nine detailed reconfiguration options with building-by-building analysis. Each elementary principal presented their school's data. The educational case for grade-band schools was made (better teacher collaboration, curriculum consistency). Public comment was extensive and emotional -- parents concerned about disruption to young children, lack of direct parent surveys, transportation burden, and the pace of decision-making. Chair DeAngelis made a pointed argument against closing Skillin school due to its service to the most vulnerable immigrant families. The board directed the administration to refine models for subsequent workshops.

## Community Concerns Raised

- **Impact on young children**: Parents worried about the disruption of moving children between schools, especially for pre-K and K students
- **Transportation and logistics**: Questions about bus routes, multiple drop-off/pickup locations for families with children in different grade bands, and costs
- **Equity and diversity**: Concern that reconfiguration decisions not disproportionately impact vulnerable populations; specific attention to Skillin school's role serving immigrant families
- **Speed of decision-making**: Parents felt they were not adequately consulted or surveyed before proposals were developed; the September 2026 implementation timeline felt rushed
- **Immigration enforcement**: Children missing school due to fear of ICE activity; demand for clearer district protocols and public statements of support
- **Revenue alternatives**: Community members asked whether the district is pursuing state aid increases, grants, an education foundation, or lobbying for policy changes, rather than relying solely on cuts
- **Staff impact**: Teachers' union expressed concern about RIF notification process (problems the prior year), bargaining requirements for any changes, and mental health impacts on staff and students
- **Communication gap**: Both board members and community noted that many residents are unaware of the budget crisis; better storytelling about the $7.2M gap was urged

## Board Member Positions and Debates

- **Chair DeAngelis**: Strongly favors reconfiguration as good for children educationally; argued against closing Skillin specifically due to equity concerns for immigrant families; pushed the city council for a specific tax-increase target
- **Member Feller**: Supported studying reconfiguration but "not in favor of it today"; broke from the 6% ceiling, saying a higher tax increase may be necessary; advocated early retirement buyouts; emphasized the need for community-led decisions
- **Member Richardson**: Called for better communication of the budget story to the public; urged accelerated staff training on immigration enforcement; asked for data on why school closure was ruled out; supported the reconfiguration study
- **Member Holman**: Served as finance committee chair; reported the current-year deficit trajectory; asked detailed questions about costs, portables, and implementation; noted the environmental condition of Kaler school
- **Member Smith**: Generally supportive of administration proposals; provided practical input on operational matters (e.g., the athletic field situation)
- **Superintendent Entwistle (interim)**: Expressed "high confidence" in the reconfiguration path and explicitly said alternatives would kick the problem down the road; supported the community school strategy expansion

## Decisions Made or Deferred

### Decisions Made
- Approved becoming a community school district (unanimous, Feb 9)
- Endorsed city council immigration resolution (unanimous, Feb 9)
- Requested formal reconfiguration proposal for March 2 workshop (unanimous, Feb 9)
- Approved student representatives participating in superintendent search interviews (unanimous, Feb 9)
- Joined CDS cohort three for pre-K special education services starting July 2026

### Decisions Deferred
- Selection of a specific reconfiguration option (deferred to subsequent workshops in March)
- Final tax-increase percentage (awaiting city council guidance)
- Reduction-in-force specifics (notification plan to be shared at March 9 regular meeting)
- Middle school bell-time consolidation (deferred to fall 2027 due to permitting/DOT requirements)
- Early retirement buyout feasibility (requires consultation with Maine State Retirement System, noted as "pretty limited")
- Headstart partnership details (to be brought back in March for board consideration)
