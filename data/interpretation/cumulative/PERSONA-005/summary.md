# Cumulative Summary: Priya (PERSONA-005)

### Current Understanding

Priya understands South Portland's school budget crisis as a multi-layered structural failure that cannot be attributed to state underfunding alone. The district's $8.4 million deficit has produced 78 position eliminations — 72 pink slips now issued — including 42 teachers, 16 educational technicians who directly supported students with IEPs, and 14 food service and transportation workers. Confirmed demographic data reveals de facto segregation: Skillin serves 60% BIPOC, 54% free/reduced lunch, and 33% multilingual learners, while the district's whitest elementary serves under 20% BIPOC and 6% multilingual learners. The administration's recommendation to close Skillin as an "equity intervention" was identified as equity-washing and rejected by four board members. State underfunding (20% actual vs. 55% statutory obligation) is compounded by years of internal mismanagement of equity-designated federal grants — a repeat material weakness audit finding — and a TIF portfolio that actively suppresses state education aid through value sheltering.

Across twenty-one meetings, Priya has documented four distinct council failure modes on equity: erasure (Montgomery's police co-location testimony absent from deliberation), inversion (moratorium framing of landlords and tenants as symmetrical parties), weaponization (Matthews deploying school cuts to defeat immigrant aid), and equity-as-termination (bike share rejection using equity research to close the program entirely). A TIF and public-asset pattern has produced three instances of affordable housing foreclosed without enforceable conditions. Community participation has been structurally suppressed by compounding barriers: in-person-only meeting requirements locked in December, active ICE enforcement in Red Bank, and a state GA ruling excluding fear-based income loss from eligibility — reducing the most affected residents to proxy testimony as their only available mechanism. The $100,000 immigrant rental assistance passed 6-1 on March 19 — real money through a trusted intermediary — against an $87 million public safety bond approved unanimously. The gap between South Portland's equity language and its structural decisions is now precisely documented.
