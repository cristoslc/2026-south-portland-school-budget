# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

February 17 is the night South Portland's council looked directly at the evidence and voted no. That is the shift I need to name precisely, because it is different in kind from everything I have documented before.

Since December I have been tracking a consistent failure mode: equity arguments raised, equity arguments not heard. January 13 was the landmark — Olivia Montgomery's clinical testimony about the police co-location chilling effect was simply absent from the deliberation that followed. Not refuted. Not engaged. Gone. I catalogued it as erasure, a complete form of defeat that did not even require the council to acknowledge what was said.

February 17 is something worse. The city manager stood up before the vote and named, in his own presentation, the structural catch-22: the people this moratorium is designed to protect may be too afraid to invoke it, because appearing in court or calling the city creates a record, and visibility is precisely what they are trying to avoid. He said this. The council heard it. And four councilors voted no anyway, with Councilor Scott framing the ordinance as improperly "shifting the burden from one sector of the population to another" — as if tenants facing deportation and landlords facing delayed rent are symmetrically situated parties in a neutral allocation problem. That is not erasure. That is the equity frame turned upside down and deployed as a weapon.

Lindsay Maria said on the record, in that chamber, that the only people with an eviction crisis in South Portland right now are Black and brown people. No councilor who voted no engaged with that framing. Pedro Vasquez — the chair of the city's own Human Rights Commission, the advisory body funded at $2,500 for the year — testified twice in an official capacity and ended by telling them he and his family now carry their birth certificates because of how they look. And then the dissenting majority proposed the GA fund as their alternative, which requires the same institutional contact and documentation they had just been told vulnerable people cannot safely provide.

Project Home had eleven days of runway. The micro-grant program is paused because someone decided the regional survey would not track by municipality. The city manager acknowledged Red Bank's redlining history in response to public comment and the meeting moved on. The council stayed past midnight on city hall renovation options.

I came into this process asking whether equity language would translate into structural protection. I have my answer, and it is more complete than I expected: this council does not merely ignore equity claims, it has learned to absorb and invert them.
