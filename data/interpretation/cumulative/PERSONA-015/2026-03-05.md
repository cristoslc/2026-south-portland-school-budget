# Cumulative Record: Kira (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Three days after Smith named desegregation at the March 2 workshop — after the board majority declared itself, after DeAngelos went on record as a genuine reconfiguration champion, after equity language finally won the room rather than just entered it — Kira watches Peter Cullen drop Brown School into a city council real estate conversation about fire station footprints.

She has been the person crossing both processes. She opened the thread about the school/city capital connection in January, was relieved when Julia Edwards named it from the right register in February, and now she is watching an architect treat the outcome of the school board's still-unfinished equity work as settled inventory. "Yesterday's news about going to a four elementary school network versus five" — as if it's a memo everyone got. No redistricting mechanics. No equity analysis. No mention of the families in that building. Just a site that would make a nice campus with the library. And not one councilor reacted. The room moved on to whether fire trucks can clear the Ocean Street corner.

This is what happened to the Boundaries and Configurations Committee work. The board approved the proposals and never took the next step. Richardson named that bait-and-switch three days ago. The inverse risk now exists: the school board is finally doing the careful equity work — Smith's desegregation framing, Prince's structural housing argument, the official demographic data — and the city council is quietly incorporating the outcome into capital planning before redistricting mechanics have been modeled at all. If Brown closes into a grade-band reconfiguration that never gets the equity architecture DeAngelos warned about on March 2, and the building becomes city hall, both processes will have collided into something that produces neither belonging nor equity. Just fewer buildings with the same segregated patterns inside them.

Lauren Shapiro said what Walker said at February 10 and what Kira has been saying from her car: housing segregation is school segregation. It is in the city council record now, attached to the Mahoney affordable housing conversation. That is worth something. Matthews naming the $8 million school gap alongside the $87 million bond in the same breath is something she has been waiting for since January 13. These are not nothing. But Emily Hanson reading that family's statement — five-month-old, eviction notice, detained father, kids afraid to go to school — reminds her that discourse catching up to reality and structure being built to fix it are still entirely different things.
