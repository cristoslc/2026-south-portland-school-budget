# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

The board has now unanimously voted to request the formal reconfiguration proposal for March 2. What was a "big idea" five days ago is now an official directive. The trajectory from abstract budget problem to concrete threat against Lila's school has reached a critical inflection point: every board member -- all seven -- voted to move forward with studying how to reorganize elementary schools.

What stings most is Chair DeAngelis's declaration that reconfiguration "is good for kids" and that "kids are far more flexible than parents." Lila feels dismissed by this. She's not anxious because her parents are anxious -- she's anxious because she's losing her school, her teacher, and her daily routine. The chair's framing implies that Lila's attachment to Dyer is just parental anxiety rubbing off on her, which invalidates her own emotional experience. The further claim that "we don't have neighborhood schools anymore" contradicts Lila's lived reality: she can ride her bike to Dyer, she knows every corner of the building, and it functions as her neighborhood school whether or not it technically qualifies by some adult definition.

There is one source of hope: the teachers' union president pushed back, saying educators "are not going to endorse plans that result in our colleagues losing their jobs" and calling out false rumors of unified educator support. Knowing that teachers -- the adults Lila trusts most -- are not on board with plans that eliminate their colleagues provides some comfort. At least her teachers are fighting.

The meeting also brought more context about scared children, this time related to immigration enforcement. Teachers walked students home and rode buses with them for safety. This resonated with Lila -- she wishes there were teachers who could protect Dyer from closing the way they protected those kids from ICE. The parallel isn't exact, but the emotional logic is the same: children need adults to shield them from forces they can't control.

The community school model at the middle school -- reducing chronic absenteeism by half and increasing family engagement -- shows that good things can happen when adults invest in making schools work better rather than reorganizing them. But this bright spot is overshadowed by the unanimous vote to study elementary reconfiguration. The financial picture remains dire: all budget scenarios end in deficit, the fund balance is depleted, and the $7.2 million gap still looms.
