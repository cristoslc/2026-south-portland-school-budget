## Since Last Meeting

The March 23 board workshop delivered the news Tom had been skeptical about since February: the 6% ceiling held. The finance director put a concrete number on it — approximately $257/year on a median South Portland home. That's the first hard, school-specific cost figure of the entire winter, and it reverses the "political theater" read Tom was carrying after a board member publicly advocated blowing past the cap just five days after the February public forum.

But the same official who confirmed the FY27 number also stated explicitly that FY28 is heading for another structural crisis — auto-escalating labor costs, 13-14% utility increases, and new athletic field bond debt service all arriving at once. That's not a speculative risk or a critic's complaint. That's the district's own finance director saying the underlying problem hasn't been fixed, only delayed by one year.

The March 23 Google Slides deck confirms how the 6% was reached: 78 position cuts and an elementary school closure. What's still missing from the public record: which school was ultimately selected for closure. Skillin was vetoed at the March 2 workshop on equity grounds, but no confirmed replacement has been announced in the evidence. The savings difference between Skillin and whatever actually closes is real taxpayer money — and Tom is entitled to know the gap.

Also still missing, six weeks after the February 10 joint workshop: the breakdown of the 82 positions that were added before the crisis, which the board promised the city council. That was a precondition for trusting the cut numbers. Its absence is no longer an oversight.
