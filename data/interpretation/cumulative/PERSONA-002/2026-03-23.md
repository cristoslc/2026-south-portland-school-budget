# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Ketchem is the first person at a district podium to give him a structural account rather than a damage report. She names the causal chain plainly: enrollment fell by 300 students over four years, staffing didn't adjust, COVID funds propped up headcount and then expired, the district drew from reserves instead of cutting positions, and there was no minimum fund balance threshold to trigger an alarm. As the seventh finance director in six years, she is also explaining her own institutional preconditions. He has been assembling this narrative from scattered audit findings and enrollment tables since December 8. Hearing it confirmed from inside the finance office — without euphemism — is the first time the district's self-understanding matches the external data.

But this meeting also confirms his worst structural concern about the year after next. Ketchem is explicit: labor costs grow faster than 6% annually even with no staffing changes, because it is baked into the contracts. Add the athletic field bond moving from interest-only to full principal and interest — roughly $330K in new debt service in FY28 — plus utilities running 13–14% annually, and a potential Skillen boiler bond still unresolved, and the district is already pointed back at the same arithmetic wall it just spent the year climbing over. She says it herself: FY27 wipes out the balance, but that doesn't solve the core problem.

The fund balance answer is the one he takes home. Feller asks directly: what is the plan to seed reserves? There is no plan. "This year is too dire." Richardson clarifies the only identified mechanism is future taxpayer asks or a windfall. David has tracked the zero-reserve condition since December 8. What he now understands is that it is not a temporary posture but the planned state of affairs through at least FY27 — no cushion for anything unexpected.

Five hours of public comment contain more signal than he expected. The occupational therapy cost-shifting argument — cut two OTs now, pay more for contracted out-of-district placements later — is exactly the downstream fiscal logic the board should be modeling. Most of what follows is repetition. The meeting ends at 11:15 PM without a vote. The grant tracking acknowledgment — no formal system, ad hoc — connects directly back to February 3's audit finding of $400K in misallocated federal funds. The structural gap that produced that finding has not been closed.
