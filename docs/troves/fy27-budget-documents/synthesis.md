## 1. Budget Shortfall and Fiscal Pressure

The central fiscal reality driving the FY27 budget process is a structural gap between costs and revenue. A roll-forward budget (same staffing, same services, new contract rates, projected health insurance increases) would produce an operating budget of approximately $82.9M--$84M, representing a 13--15% increase over FY26 and requiring an 18--21% increase in the school tax levy [005, 007, 009, 011].

**Key figures:**
- FY26 total operating budget: $73.4M [005, 011]
- FY27 roll-forward ("no change") estimate: $82.9M--$84.0M [007, 009, 011]
- FY27 proposed (Superintendent's budget): $75.6M--$75.85M, a 3.33% overall increase [011]
- Required reductions to bridge the gap: **$8.4 million** [011]
- Board-directed tax increase ceiling: approximately 6% [007, 009]

The fund balance (savings) has been nearly exhausted, declining from $5.9M in FY23 to an estimated $739k entering FY26, with no fund balance available for FY27 [005, 011]. The Leadership Team has explicitly recommended that any savings from lower-than-projected costs (e.g., health insurance coming in at 11% instead of 12%) should rebuild fund balance rather than restore spending [011].

**Budget increase drivers** include $3M in contractual wage increases, $1.2M in projected health insurance increases (12%), $639k in urgent capital expenses, $488k in underbudgeted electricity costs, and $99k for the first athletic complex debt service payment [011].


## 2. Staffing Reductions

The proposed FY27 budget eliminates **78 positions** across all bargaining units, a 12% reduction in total staff, from 657 FTE in FY26 to 579 FTE in FY27 [011].

| Group | FY26 | FY27 | Cut | % |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Teachers (SPTA) | 354 | 312 | 42 | 12% |
| Ed Techs & Clerks (SPESPA) | 155 | 139 | 16 | 10% |
| Building Administrators (SPAA) | 13 | 11 | 2 | 15% |
| Facilities/Food/Transport (SEA) | 100 | 86 | 14 | 14% |
| Non-Bargaining | 35 | 31 | 4 | 11% |

Of the 78 reductions, approximately 20 can be absorbed through existing vacancies, retirements, or resignations, leaving about 58 positions where staff will be directly impacted (9% "people impact") [011].

The staff-to-student ratio has already tightened from 5.26:1 in 2017 to 4.14:1 in December 2025, reflecting that staffing grew even as enrollment declined [005].

**Contract wage pressures** are significant: SPTA base raises are CPI + 1.5% (4.2%), but actual salary increases including step and lane changes run 6.3%. SPESPA actual increases reach 8.4% [011]. Director-level positions grew from 10.8 FTE in FY21 to 14 FTE in FY25 before being trimmed to 13 in FY26 [011].


## 3. Elementary School Reconfiguration

The most extensively debated proposal is the reconfiguration of South Portland's five elementary schools. This is analyzed across nine distinct models in the evaluation matrix [012] and discussed at length in workshop presentations [005, 007, 010, 011].

### Evolution of the proposal

- **December 2025** [005]: Four broad "ideas for exploration" introduced, including elementary grade-level reconfiguration with an early learning campus, SPMS schedule review, high school programming changes, and operational efficiencies.
- **February 2026** [007, 009]: January analysis refined to: (a) create two primary schools (PreK-1) and three intermediate schools (Grade 2-4); (b) review SPMS/SPHS schedules; (c) explore shift changes. Estimated savings: $2M + $1M + $1M.
- **March 2, 2026** [010, 012]: Nine models formally presented with a matrix scoring system. Leadership ranked their top three: Option 1.7 (close Skillin, reconfigure to primary/intermediate), Option 1.3 (close Kaler or Dyer, reconfigure), Option 1.8 (close Skillin, keep others PreK-4).
- **March 9, 2026** [011]: Proposal pivoted to a refinement of Option 1.4 -- **closure of Dyer Elementary School**, redistricting those students, after hearing community feedback against closing Skillin.

### Model comparison (5-year savings, from matrix) [012]

| Option | Description | 5-Year Savings | Matrix Score |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1.7 | Close Skillin, reconfigure | -$19.6M | 46 (highest) |
| 1.3 | Close Kaler/Dyer, reconfigure | -$16.4M | 40 |
| 1.8 | Close Skillin, others PreK-4 | -$18.8M | 38 |
| 1.5 | Close Brown, reconfigure | -$15.8M | 37 |
| 1.6 | Close Small, reconfigure | -$15.8M | 37 |
| 1.4 | Close Kaler/Dyer, redistrict | -$12.8M | 20 |
| 1.1 | Reconfigure all 5, no closure | -$10.5M | 23 |
| 1.2 | No closure, reductions across | tbd | 9 (lowest) |

The matrix evaluated options on instructional coherence, grade-level collaboration, special education coordination, family disruption, equity (multilingual %, racial demographics, change impact), and fiscal sustainability [012].

**Elementary enrollment context:** Total elementary enrollment declined from 1,401 in 2021-22 to 1,080 in 2025-26 -- a 23% drop [010]. Skillin is the largest school (310 students) while Small is the smallest (190) [010].

### Key facilities considerations
- Skillin has the most expensive capital needs (HVAC is primary concern), with $739k in FY27 capital project savings if closed [012]
- Dyer and Kaler face roof repairs: Dyer at $596k ($535k for roof), Kaler at $365k ($302k for roof) in the coming five years [011]
- Vestibule projects already scheduled for Dyer and Skillin in summer 2026 [010]


## 4. Revenue and State Funding

State funding covers only about 20% of actual district costs, despite Maine's stated goal of funding 55% of education statewide [011]. The Essential Programs and Services (EPS) model calculates South Portland's "should cost" at $48.3M, while actual costs are $75.6M. State share for FY27 is 22.86% of the model cost, yielding $15.5M [011].

Non-tax revenue is projected at $16.3M for FY27, down slightly from $17.2M in FY26 (partly because FY26 included $850k in fund balance drawdown that is no longer available) [011].

A hearing on LD 2226 (An Act to Amend the Essential Programs and Services School Funding Formula) was noted as in progress, acknowledging the formula is "known to be a problematic formula" [011].

**Per-pupil cost comparison** [005]: South Portland at $26,651 per pupil is the highest among area comparisons (vs. Gorham at $20,305, Portland at $25,891, Yarmouth at $25,451).


## 5. Demographic and Enrollment Trends

Enrollment has declined from 3,085 in 2015-16 to 2,744 in 2025-26, a decrease of 11% [005]. As of February 2026, enrollment was 2,748 (+12 students since January) [008].

The student body has become significantly more diverse:
- Multilingual learners: 1% (2015-16) to 17% (2025-26); 26% of students have a non-English primary/home language; 40+ languages spoken [005, 010]
- Students of color: 18% to 38%
- Students in special education: 18% to 23%
- McKinney-Vento eligible: 1% to 10% [005]

Economically disadvantaged students have fluctuated between 33% and 43% [005].

The CDS (Child Development Services) transition mandated by state law will add responsibility for 80-90 four-year-olds in FY27 (September 2026), with three-year-olds following in FY28 [010].


## 6. Community School Strategy

The Board voted (or considered voting) on February 9, 2026 to become a Community School district [008]. The SPMS Community School model has produced measurable results:

- Chronic absenteeism dropped from 22.2% (22-23) to 10.5% (25-26, as of 12/18), compared to 24.1% statewide [008]
- 22% increase in parent-teacher conference participation [008]
- 996 student visits to the School-Based Health Center; 300 students enrolled [008]
- 7,000 pounds of food and 6,000 snacks distributed through the Locker Project [008]
- Academic scores trending upward in both math and reading [008]

The estimated local financial commitment for FY27 is $100,000 or less, given grant potential [008]. A South Portland Education Foundation (501c3) is being organized to support the strategy through philanthropy [008]. The research basis cited includes a Learning Policy Institute (2025) California study and a figure of $1 invested = $7.11 in net benefits [008].


## 7. Community Engagement and Feedback

Budget forums held February 4-5, 2026 drew approximately 100 participants across two nights [008]. Language interpretation was provided in French, Portuguese, and Spanish [007].

**Points of agreement among participants** [008]:
- Students should be the first priority
- Shared commitment to equity and diversity
- Long-term financial sustainability is important
- Appreciation for the dialog and transparency
- Goal of protecting what makes SP schools strong

**Points of disagreement** (inferred from the shift between March 2 and March 9 proposals):
- The leadership team's top-ranked recommendation (close Skillin, Option 1.7) was not well received by the community, leading to a pivot to close Dyer instead (Option 1.4 variant) [011]
- Tension between reconfiguration models (primary/intermediate split) vs. keeping neighborhood schools as PreK-4 [010, 011]
- Whether to prioritize long-term capital savings (favoring Skillin closure) vs. community attachment to specific schools

**Data request process** established: community members can submit budget questions via Google form, though anonymous requests will not be processed [007].

## 9. Fiscal Controls and Audit Findings

The FY25 audit revealed five findings [008]:
1. **Material weakness** in the school's financial reconciliation procedures
2. **Significant deficiency** with allowable federal costs
3. Budgetary non-compliance
4. Payroll procedures comment
5. Journal approvals comment

Two prior issues from FY24 were cleared. Leadership is addressing findings through team stability, policy/procedure updates, training, and monitoring/reconciliations [008].

The FY26 mid-year finance reports show deteriorating projections: by January 2026 (58% of FY elapsed), all scenarios projected a budget shortfall ranging from $622k to $1.7M [008]. The Finance Committee noted that nutrition funds and new contractual provisions were contributing factors.

The district is also transitioning to a private Paid Family Medical Leave (PFML) plan, joining the City of South Portland, saving approximately $145k in the four-month opt-out period and $21.8k annually at 0.95% vs. 1.0% [008].


## 10. Gaps and Open Questions

The following significant gaps remain in the evidence pool:

1. **Detailed budget book**: The line-item budget detail has not yet been presented (scheduled for March 16-23 workshops). The cost center summary in [011] is high-level.

2. **Impact analysis on students**: While staffing reductions are quantified, the direct impact on class sizes, program offerings, and student services under the proposed 579-FTE model is not detailed.

3. **Transportation cost modeling**: The elementary reconfiguration will require revised bus routes. A transportation consultant is referenced as a cost [010] but no routing analysis has been shared.

4. **Community School expansion costs**: The $100k local commitment estimate [008] is acknowledged as dependent on grant success. No contingency for grant shortfall is discussed.

5. **Special education compliance risk**: With 78 staff reductions and growing special education obligations (23% of students, CDS transition for 80-90 new preschoolers), the adequacy of remaining SPED staffing is not explicitly addressed.

6. **Long-term enrollment projections**: Historical trends are documented but no forward projection beyond the current year has been shared. The viability of any reconfiguration model depends on where enrollment stabilizes.

7. **City Council perspective**: The February 10 joint discussion [009] is sparsely documented. Council feedback on the school budget's impact on the combined tax rate is not captured.

8. **Comparison to Option 1.2 ("no closure")**: This option scored lowest on the matrix (9/46) but was never fully costed. "More analysis needed" is noted [012]. The counterfactual of deeper across-the-board cuts without school closure is undefined.

9. **Capital planning policy**: The leadership team references wanting to "collaborate with the Board to adopt a policy with regards to funding capital investments" [010], but no policy draft exists in this evidence pool.

10. **Adult Education**: The adult education local contribution increases from $75k (FY25) to $275k (FY26) to $250k (FY27) [011] with minimal explanation of what this funds or why it increased.
