# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Three months after sitting through the January 13 workshop where an architect said "I'm not confident, Dickie" on a $194 million estimate, and two and a half weeks after watching five hours of February 17 deliberations end at 11:30 PM with six options between $57 million and $105 million and no council majority, Dana finally has a number and a date. Eighty-seven million dollars. November 2026. That is not nothing — that is the story she has been waiting to produce since the Mahoney chaos began.

The Walgreens announcement is the cleanest news the city has handed her all season. A specific site, a specific name, a phrase she can put on a lower-third: "geographic center of South Portland." She can get b-roll at Cash Corner tomorrow. More importantly, reviewing this now with the knowledge of everything that followed, she can see that the Walgreens purchase quietly solved a problem that was never named as a problem: Olivia Montgomery's January argument that putting the police station adjacent to Mahoney would drive immigrant families away from city services. The police station went to Cash Corner. Nobody said Montgomery's name. The council just acted around the concern and moved on. That is how South Portland resolves things — not with a formal response, but with a site plan.

The fire station fight is where the meeting actually lives for Dana, and West is doing something she has not seen from a councilor since December: showing up with data. Response times failing 40 percent of the eastern city. Flood maps for a 75-year facility. A $1.78 million temporary fix against a $31.5 million new build. That is a stat that stops channel surfers, and it opens the question she is now carrying into the November referendum story — did the city just vote to spend $87 million on a fire station in the wrong place?

The rental assistance testimony is harder to separate from what she covered on March 19. The mother and daughter on March 5 are part of the pressure campaign she can now trace through to the binding vote. That testimony is b-roll for the story she already aired.

Matthews is the through-line she keeps finding. He warned on March 5 that voters would not stomach the bond "given all the other financial pressures" — and then two weeks later he cast the lone no vote on $100,000 for Project Home by naming the school deficit and the sewer rates out loud. He has been saying the same thing every meeting. The combinatorial tax burden story she has been assembling since January just found its most consistent narrator.
