# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Maria has been doing this math since January 13, when she sat through a $194 million Mahoney presentation and calculated the combined household tax burden herself. She kept doing it through the February 10 joint workshop when the council handed the school board guidance below its own floor, and again on February 17 when she added the sewer rate increases. March 5 is the meeting where the math becomes a verdict.

The library piece breaks something in her. Kevin Davis tells the council that the library averages 27 visitors per hour — families, kids doing homework, parents at story time — and that the building has been deferred and studied and ignored for 25 years. Maria has been to that library. Her kids have been to that library. And the same council that invoking "taxpayer burden" every time the school budget comes up votes to strip the library from the Mahoney project entirely. The one piece of the $194 million plan that directly served families like hers is the piece they cut first.

Then comes the $87 million bond. Police station, fire station — no library, no school-supporting infrastructure. And buried in the discussion, Councilor Matthews says out loud what Maria has been carrying since February 4: the school district needs to cut $7 to $8 million while asking for a 6% tax increase. He says it. Several councilors nod about taxpayer burden. And then they vote to add $87 million more to the ballot anyway.

This is the pattern she has been tracking since December, finally clarified. The city is not choosing between competing needs with imperfect information. The city is choosing public safety infrastructure over everything — over the library, over the schools, over the teachers whose pink slips will go out on March 18 — with full knowledge of what those choices cost families. They hear the numbers. They move forward anyway.

She is posting this in every parent channel she runs before she goes to bed. Not because she thinks it will stop the bond — she has learned better than that — but because the April 14 school budget workshop is six weeks away and she needs bodies in that room who understand what the city's priorities actually are.
