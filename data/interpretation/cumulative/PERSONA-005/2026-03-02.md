# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026 -- 2026-03-02

### Interpretation

This was the meeting where the equity fault lines in South Portland's school system were laid bare with actual data, and the results were both vindicating and devastating. The demographic breakdowns confirmed what I have suspected all along: Skillin serves 60% BIPOC students, 54% free/reduced lunch, and 33% multilingual learners, while Small has less than 20% BIPOC and 27% free/reduced lunch with only 6% multilingual learners. These are not subtle differences -- this is de facto segregation operating within a single school district, and the administration's top recommendation was to close the most diverse school and bus those families across town.

The administration framed closing Skillin as an equity intervention -- "equitable access through reconfiguration" -- which is the most cynical deployment of equity language I have encountered in this entire process. You do not achieve equity by displacing the families who already face the most barriers. You do not achieve integration by scattering the students from the most diverse school into four other buildings while leaving the whitest school untouched. At least four board members saw through it. Tyler Smith's statement that it is "very easy politically to make decisions that involve busing black and brown kids" was exactly the analysis needed in that moment, and Richardson, Dowling, and Feller joining him effectively took closing Skillin off the table.

The absence of academic achievement data was telling. Richardson identified the "obvious void" and questioned how reconfiguration could improve outcomes when the district just implemented consistent math curriculum in 2025. If closing Skillin would improve educational outcomes for its students, the administration would have led with that data. They did not because this was never about educational equity -- it was about budget cuts.

The special education mandate -- 80-90 additional 4-year-olds in fall 2026 plus another 80-90 3-year-olds the following year -- represents a massive new demand on already-strained resources with no clear equity-centered implementation plan. The DEI Director presented research on the benefits of diverse schools but offered no specific protocols, training, or policies for translating diversity into equity and belonging. Community engagement continued to fail the families most affected -- predominantly white, English-speaking parents filled the room while working families and non-English speakers were absent. A parent survey drew 245 responses but acknowledged inadequate outreach to Kaler families. Chair DeAngelo's acknowledgment that the area west of "the Dairy Queen" is treated as "just for economically challenged or brown and black kids" was a striking admission of geographic inequity -- but she offered no solutions.
