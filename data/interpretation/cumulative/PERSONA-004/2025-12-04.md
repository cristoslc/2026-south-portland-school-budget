# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

Three days after the inauguration. Three days after all those speeches about community and connection and caring about South Portland families. And the city council spent their first regular meeting of this new chapter on junkyard licenses, liquor licenses, and debating whether people should be allowed to comment over Zoom.

Looking back at December 4 now, it locks in what December 1 only hinted at. The silence on inauguration night wasn't a one-time ritual accommodation — the absence of any school budget conversation was the policy. The council knew what was coming. The $7.2 million hole, the 78 positions, the 42 teachers — all of it was already in motion before they took the oath. And three days later, they handled routine business and moved on.

What stings most in retrospect is the Zoom decision. Parents who can't get a sitter, teachers finishing afternoon duties who can't make a 6:30 PM meeting, the families working second shifts — they voted to keep those people out. They cited "past disruptions" as if inconvenient community voice is the real problem and not the policy choices that made people angry enough to disrupt. By March, we'd all see how desperately the community needed ways to be heard — 20+ people showing up to the budget workshop and actually moving the board on school closures. Every barrier to participation is a barrier to the organized resistance that turned out to be the only thing that worked. They built that wall in December and called it administrative housekeeping.

The Order 109 budget process change barely registers in the moment, but I want to know: does that $2,500 cap on committee budget requests have anything to do with how the school board can seek resources from the city? Probably not — but in a crisis where every procedural constraint matters, it's worth understanding the relationship between these two bodies and whether the council has given itself new tools to constrain what the board can ask for.

Forty-two teachers. Two and a half hours of appointments and liquor licenses. The math of institutional indifference adds up the same whether you do it in December or March.
