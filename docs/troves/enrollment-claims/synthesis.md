## Claim Patterns

### The Decline Narrative

The administration's enrollment narrative rests on three pillars:
1. Total enrollment declined 11% (3,085 → 2,744) over a decade (EC-001)
2. Elementary enrollment collapsed 23% in four years (1,401 → 1,080) (EC-005)
3. Staffing grew while enrollment fell, creating a structural mismatch (EC-014, EC-015)

This narrative is internally consistent and supported by the historical data points cited. However, it treats the decline as a single trend when it is actually composed of multiple dynamics:
- **Overall K-12 decline** (11% over 10 years — moderate)
- **Elementary-specific collapse** (23% in 4 years — severe)
- **Asylum-seeker arrival and departure** (EC-004 — described as "brief increase" but actually a significant demographic event)
- **Multilingual learner growth** (1% → 17% — massive, ongoing, policy-driven)

The administration presents these as a single "declining enrollment" story. An independent analysis should decompose them.

### The Missing Forward Look

The most significant pattern is what's *absent*:
- Only one forward projection exists: "40 fewer students next year" (EC-007) — one year out, no methodology, no grade-level breakdown
- No multi-year enrollment projection has been published (EC-020, EC-022)
- No modeling of housing development impact (EC-021)
- No modeling of ICE enforcement impact on enrollment (EC-023)
- No modeling of CDS pre-K mandate impact on total enrollment trajectory (EC-017)

The board is making a permanent infrastructure decision based on a backward-looking trend with a single-year forward guess.

### Contradictions and Tensions

- **ESOL reduction justification** (EC-013): The March 23 presentation claims ESOL student numbers are declining and language ability is higher — used to justify ESOL staff cuts. But the 1%→17% multilingual learner growth is cited elsewhere as a driver of staffing needs. These claims are in tension.
- **"Brief increase" framing** (EC-004, EC-016): The asylum-seeker enrollment spike is framed as an interruption of a decline, resulting in "additional hires" that are now excess. But the multilingual learner percentage continued growing even after the enrollment peak — suggesting the complexity driver is ongoing even if total headcount dropped.
- **Superintendent's "kicking it down the road" framing** (EC-024): Presented as evidence-based conclusion but is actually a judgment call without published projections to support it.
