# Synthesis: Authoring Voter Guides and Nonpartisan Public Information

## Key Findings

### What a Voter Guide Is

A voter guide is a document that helps voters understand what and who is on their ballot. Sources distinguish between:

- **Official voter guides**: Published by government entities (Secretary of State, Dept. of Elections); legally required in many states; authoritative but often dense
- **Unofficial voter guides**: Published by nonprofits, civic organizations, newspapers; can be more accessible, plain-language, and community-specific

Both types share the same standard of nonpartisanship, but unofficial guides by civic organizations have additional obligations to protect their institutional reputation for impartiality.

### The Nonpartisanship Imperative

All sources treat nonpartisanship not as a nice-to-have but as the foundational requirement:

- The public's trust in a voter guide depends entirely on its perceived impartiality
- Any deviation from true nonpartisanship — even subtle question bias or selective outreach — can permanently damage credibility
- Nonpartisanship must be embedded in **every step** of the process: question development, candidate outreach, publication, and distribution

The defining test: **does every candidate receive exactly the same questions, outreach, opportunity, and treatment?** If not, the guide is not truly nonpartisan.

### Question Development Standards

Sources converge on clear standards for candidate questionnaires:

**Must be:**
- Open-ended (not yes/no — yes/no questions have a more partisan appearance)
- Uniform across all candidates for the same office
- Related directly to the office being sought
- Reflective of broad electorate interests, not just the publishing organization's positions

**Must not be:**
- Asking candidates to pledge support for causes
- Incorporating the organization's own policy positions
- Leading or biased in wording
- Originated from candidate or party suggestions

### Publication and Formatting Standards

- Publish all responses verbatim, without editing (except truncation for length)
- Consistent formatting across all candidate responses — same font, same structure
- Clearly state if a candidate did not respond
- Do not publish if only one candidate responds (per LWVMN guidance)
- Include clear nonpartisanship disclaimer on every page/section

### Plain Language and Accessibility (especially for ballot measures)

The Center for Civic Design's research shows that plain language is necessary but not sufficient:
- Even plain language can fail if concepts are unfamiliar or context is missing
- The guiding question: **"What does a voter need to know in order to make a choice?"**
- Context matters: explain the problem a measure addresses before describing the measure
- Test with real voters before publication
- Use illustrations, diagrams, and clear headings
- One topic per page; civic information in the front; ballot specifics in the second half

### Distribution

- Distribute as widely as practicable with clear disclaimers
- Offer multiple formats (PDF, web, print)
- Consider multilingual versions for diverse communities
- Partner with libraries, schools, and community organizations
- Target people who may not otherwise see election information

## Points of Agreement

- **Identical treatment of all candidates**: Every source treats this as non-negotiable. The same questions, the same outreach, the same format, the same follow-up effort.
- **No evaluation of responses**: The guide's job is to present, not assess. Comments, ratings, or comparisons introduce bias.
- **Plain language matters**: Voter comprehension is meaningfully improved by accessible writing. Dense legalese disenfranchises voters.
- **Disclaimer required**: Every source specifies that nonpartisan guides must explicitly disclaim any support for or opposition to candidates or parties.
- **Wide distribution**: Making the guide accessible is as important as making it accurate.

## Points of Disagreement / Nuance

- **Whether to publish incomplete guides**: LWVMN explicitly says do not publish if only one candidate responds. Other sources do not address this — some organizations publish the guide anyway, noting which candidates declined to participate. The LWVMN approach is more protective of nonpartisanship.
- **Question topics**: How many topics, and which ones, is a judgment call that varies by office and community context. Sources agree on the criteria (relevant to the office, reflecting broad interests) but not on any specific list.
- **Plain language vs. legal accuracy**: The Center for Civic Design notes that plain language rewrites of ballot measures must be reviewed for legal accuracy (the EVG uses the California Legislative Analyst's Office). Organizations without access to such review must balance simplicity with precision.

## Gaps

- **Voter guide for budget referenda**: All examples focus on candidate races. Local budget votes (like a school budget referendum) have different dynamics — no candidates, issues focused on fiscal policy — and require adapted approaches.
- **Follow-up and accountability**: Guides explain what candidates say they will do; no source addresses how to help voters track whether elected officials followed through in subsequent cycles.
- **Digital interactivity**: Sources largely describe static documents. Interactive tools (vote calculators, candidate comparison tools) are not addressed in the plain-language and design guidance.

## Application to This Project

For a South Portland school budget voter guide:
- **Not a candidate guide** — a school budget vote involves no candidates, so questionnaire-based formats don't apply directly
- **Ballot measure format applies**: Explain what a "yes" or "no" vote would do, what the fiscal impact is, and what problems the budget addresses
- **Nonpartisan language**: Avoid advocating for or against; focus on accurate, accessible description of what voters are deciding
- **Plain language design**: Follow Center for Civic Design principles — context first, then the measure, then fiscal impact; test language with community members for comprehension
- **Disclaimer required**: Even if the publishing organization supports the budget, the voter guide itself must present information without endorsement
- **Multilingual and accessible**: Consider the full range of South Portland's community
