# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Her mom came home after midnight and Lila already knew the question she needed answered: *Is it Dyer?*

The answer was no. Not Dyer. Kayler.

The relief is real. She has been carrying the word "CLOSED" next to her school's name since March 2 — three weeks of knowing one of the nine scenarios would erase Dyer entirely. She has been carrying the threat since January, since the formal reconfiguration request, since December when the spending freeze started and teachers couldn't buy books. And this meeting says: not Dyer. The administration looked at all five buildings twice, ran a six-part analysis, and chose Kayler. Something about the street Kayler is on — a dead end with no way out in an emergency. Something about Dyer and the high school needing each other as evacuation sites. The building she knows is not the one getting closed.

But she still has to leave it.

Under Option A — the plan being recommended — Dyer becomes a school for little kids. Pre-K through first grade only. Lila is in fourth grade. Under this plan she would transfer to Brown or Skillen for intermediate school while Dyer's lights stay on and younger children walk the same hallways, sit in the same rooms, use the bathroom she knows. Her younger sibling, in first grade right now, would have one more year there and then have to move for second grade. The building survives. The place she knows reorganizes into something that no longer has room for her.

The person who said "kids are flexible" was already crying before the meeting started. Board Chair DeAngelis — who in February called reconfiguration good for children and set the nine scenarios in motion — opened the meeting describing how she had burst into tears at dinner when a friend simply asked how she was doing. She said she hadn't realized how much she was holding. She asked the room for kindness and grace. Lila has watched this woman be certain for months. That is not who stood at the front of the room last night.

Two eighth graders walked up to the microphone and said real things. A girl named Lucy said her band was her family. A girl named Samantha said that moving one grain of sand changes the whole beach. The adults in the room had to listen. It was the first time actual kids stood in any of these spaces and said out loud what Lila has been feeling from a distance since December.

The Kayler families don't know where their children are going. Someone asked directly, and the administration said: we don't have the answer to that. Lila held that. She has been the one without answers all winter. Now those families are carrying what she carried — except theirs is the version where the building actually closes.

They didn't vote. Another meeting Monday. Lila knows by now exactly what another meeting means.
