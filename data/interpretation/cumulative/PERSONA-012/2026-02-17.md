# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

February 17 is the night the two crises Jaylen has been tracking separately — the school budget and the ICE enforcement — collapse into each other inside one council chamber. He already knew kids were missing school. January 12, the board discussed "protocols and training." January 20, the citizen budget forum was postponed because families were afraid to be seen in public. But February 17 puts ages on it. A 10-year-old asking "why are they doing this to us? My parents work and pay taxes and do everything right." That is not a board discussion item. That is a kid Jaylen might know, asking the exact question he has been asking — in different language, about different decisions — since December 8. Someone else says high schoolers might drop out entirely because they are afraid to leave their houses. South Portland high schoolers. His school.

Then the council votes 4-2 to reject the eviction moratorium. He has been building the map of this institution's priorities since December 1: Jules Vazquez's poem applauded and forgotten, DeAngelis's 61% statistic thanked and moved past, the TI deal getting three hours while the immigration resolution waits until 9:25 PM on February 3. But the 4-2 vote is different. It is not agenda ordering. It is a voting record. After an hour of testimony about children missing school and a fund running out of money in ten days, four councilors explain their no-votes in careful policy language — "shifting the burden," "a sledgehammer, not a scalpel," call Senator Collins — without once mentioning the children. The institutional logic he has been documenting all year is now attached to names and a roll call.

Walker voting yes and naming the school budget, the library, and Mahoney as a single compound disinvestment is the first time any councilor has said out loud what Jaylen has been mapping for three months. She has been doing this consistently — the Human Rights Commission in December, the compound crisis tonight — and she keeps being in the minority. Naming the problem correctly is not the same as having the votes.

The Mahoney theater surviving into A1 is real good news and he takes it seriously, even arriving at midnight after everything else. The city manager's revelation that the school district formally relinquished Mahoney in 2022–23 — that no one ever formally asked for it back as a school campus — is a small, sharp illustration of a pattern that runs through this entire year: the city and the district make consequential decisions about overlapping futures without talking to each other. He has known that dynamic since December 1. Now there is a date on it.
