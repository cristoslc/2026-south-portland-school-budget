# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

This is the meeting where the abstraction finally became a decision, and the decision is: Mia will switch school buildings between first and second grade.

I've watched reconfiguration move through every stage of bureaucratic softening since January -- concept, nine scenarios, administration recommendation, pending vote -- and tonight it stopped being pending. Option A passed 4-2. Kaler closed 6-1. The school map I've been studying for months is already being redrawn, and my daughter isn't even in the system yet.

The thing that hit me hardest wasn't the votes themselves. It was Richardson. She's apparently the only board member who currently has elementary-age kids in this district -- the only person at that table with real-time skin in the game -- and she said the projected kindergarten class sizes "terrify" her. Not "concern" her. Terrify. I've spent months wondering whether I'm catastrophizing, whether a parent who's more plugged in would read this more calmly. Richardson is plugged in. She voted for Option B and she's still scared of what her kids are walking into. That word, from that chair, at that table, does something I can't undo.

Then a parent at the microphone described having a child entering pre-K and said "who knows where" -- that's the answer for incoming families in March 2026. The model is decided. A school is closing. Buildings are being reshuffled. And families like mine cannot determine which building their child will land in.

I've been rebuilding the pre-K thread as my reason for staying since January. First it was the expansion seats, then the state mandates and physical space protections, then the Early Childhood Strategist position getting a mention tonight. That position is something real -- someone is thinking about the pre-K to kindergarten pipeline -- but it got two minutes of airtime in a five-hour meeting, and the budget that would fund it didn't pass. The board voted to close a school and restructure all five elementary buildings and then failed 5-2 to pass the actual money. Another meeting Thursday. The structural decisions are locked in; the fiscal framework they run on is not.

Three months ago I was calculating whether to move to Scarborough. Now I'm watching a board vote decisions that will shape Mia's first four years of school, and nobody can tell her family which door she'll walk through in the fall.
