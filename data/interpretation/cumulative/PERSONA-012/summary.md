Over four months of sustained attention, Jaylen has built a detailed map of how the crisis was produced: state underfunding (Augusta pays roughly 20% of school costs against a 55% formula it never contests), declining elementary enrollment, rising health insurance costs, athletic complex debt service, and documented internal financial mismanagement — 34 audit journal entries, illegal budget overspending, $400K moved from grants, six finance directors in five years — are all co-equal drivers. The governance architecture involves three layers: the district (generating the cuts), the city council (setting the revenue ceiling via 4-6% guidance that school board chair DeAngelis explicitly solicited, not a unilateral imposition), and Augusta (whose funding failure local government accepts as fixed context rather than contests). Students have been structurally excluded from every consequential decision point across all three layers, though the February 9 vote granting student reps access to superintendent search executive sessions is a genuine exception to that pattern.

The school budget crisis and the immigration enforcement crisis, which Jaylen initially tracked as parallel concerns, have now been formally weaponized against each other: Councilor Matthews used the school deficit as an official argument against rental assistance for immigrant families — the same families whose fear of ICE operations caused the January budget forum cancellation — making their exclusion from the budget process and their exposure to eviction the same compound harm.

Theater, music, robotics, and fine arts at SPHS were confirmed as actively thriving as of March 9. The threat to those programs has not been resolved — the $3M+ residual gap requires line-by-line cuts across all cost centers — but the most recent school-level evidence is cautiously encouraging. The Mahoney bond referendum, which Jaylen cannot vote in regardless, has been deferred to 2027. Senior year is on a locked countdown.
