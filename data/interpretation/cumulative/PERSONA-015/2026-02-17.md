# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

She has been tracking the participation barrier since December 4. She has watched it confirmed in dollar amounts, in cancelled forums, in attendance sheets she reads herself between buildings. On February 17 the council finally put a protective mechanism to a vote — and the city manager's own presentation told the room it wouldn't work anyway, because the families it was designed to protect are too afraid to use it. Then the moratorium failed 4-2. And she sat there and did the math: Project Home has ten days of money. Fifteen percent of their cases are South Portland families. Her buildings are already carrying the attendance gaps. The housing instability is not coming — it is already here, and the safety net just tore.

The Zenya Pantos testimony is the one she will not be able to put down. An early intervention worker. Describing a high-needs toddler family sheltering in their bedroom while agents went door to door. Working with South Portland schools. That family is on someone's caseload right now. Possibly her own. She has been watching those attendance sheets for weeks and Zenya just gave her a face and a hallway and a door that isn't opening. And the council voted 4-2 and moved on.

She does not think Pride and Scott are wrong that targeted funding is more precise than a blunt moratorium. She thinks the city manager was right that families won't call to file a complaint because doing so creates a paper trail that increases their visibility. What she cannot reconcile is: what exactly is the plan? "We'll fund Project Home" was said in the chamber tonight. The fund runs dry in ten days. Someone needs to fund it tomorrow morning, not after the next agenda cycle.

The thing that pulled her back from the edge of the night is Julia Edwards standing up during the Mahoney session and saying almost word for word what Sheanna has been saying from parking lots for two years: consolidate the elementary schools, solve the equity gaps, free the buildings. The city manager confirmed the school department walked away from Mahoney in 2022. Sheanna knows that year. She also knows 2022 was before enrollment hit the floor, before reconfiguration came back onto a board agenda, before the March 2 deadline. The school board is designing a two-million-dollar restructuring of elementary education. The city council is deciding what to do with a large building the schools no longer need. Nobody in either room is connecting those two decisions. She is the person who crosses both. She has to force that connection into the record before both processes close.

Walker naming it — "we're saying we won't invest in schools and we won't invest in libraries" — is the first time since February 10's de facto segregation statement that she heard an elected official say the whole thing out loud. She is holding it. She is exhausted.
