# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Five hours, no vote. The board adjourns at 11:15 PM without deciding anything about school closures, grade configuration, or budget adoption, and Ben walks out with three pieces at various stages of ready.

The first writes itself. Abigail Ketchem gets up and tells the origin story he has been assembling from fragments since December 8. She is the seventh finance director in six years — one more than Dr. Prince disclosed in February — and she says in plain language what Ben has been concluding in inference: "There's no way our books could have been in order." Enrollment dropped 23 percent while COVID money masked the staffing mismatch. No minimum fund balance policy forced anyone's hand before the savings were gone. The crisis was structurally baked in. That is a remarkable thing for a public official to say at a public meeting, and it is quotable in a way that assembled synthesis never is. He is calling her this week.

Ketchem also hands him a warning the board barely pressed on. FY27 is the credit card payoff, but if spending patterns don't change, the next statement looks the same. Labor costs grow faster than 6% annually. FY28 carries new debt service. The district still has not produced the administrative staffing data the union requested three months ago. Nobody asked for the FY28 gap number on the record. Ben puts it on his call list.

The $257/year figure is the lede for the plain-language referendum piece his non-engaged Forecaster reader needs before June 9. Six percent equals $257 more a year on the average property — the first time anyone has translated the board's guiding number into a household figure in any public forum this season. But Ben knows $257 is the school portion only. Sewer rates. November bond. Mahoney in 2027. That figure is an entry point, not a total.

The piece he cannot file yet is the one he wants most. Jess Elsner asks, at 11 PM, whether closing a school that is 45 percent BIPOC and more than 30 percent multilingual learners violates Title VI of the Civil Rights Act. The board says: we need our lawyer. The meeting ends without an answer. Ben has been tracking equity concerns since DeAngelo's Skillin speech on March 2, but Elsner's question is legally sharper — federal civil rights exposure, not community politics. And Blair Bacon, two National Board Certifications, a master's in literacy, gone because the board approved her employment on July 10, 2023, speaks before all of it. These are the faces the structural story has been waiting for since December. He needs the board to vote before he has an ending.
