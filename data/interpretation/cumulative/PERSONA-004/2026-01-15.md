# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop (Goal Setting) — January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

By January 15, the pattern is so established I could write it myself: the city conducts its institutional business, the school crisis gets a passing mention or none at all, and the people who will live with the consequences are somewhere else. December 1 was inauguration speeches. December 4 was junkyard licenses. December 9 was five minutes of public comment. January 6 was a procedural announcement. January 13 was four hours on a $194 million building. And January 15 is annual goal-setting — the one meeting of the year where the council actually decides what it cares about — and the entire content is a single agenda line that says "see attachment," and the attachment isn't public.

I needed to know what the council decided to prioritize. That's the whole point of a goal-setting session — it tells you what a body thinks actually matters. And I got nothing. Whatever happened in that room, whatever goals got written on whatever whiteboard, they aren't in anything I can read. The school district was collapsing in real time. The fiscal briefing they had in front of them at that very session named 42 teachers and 78 total positions on the chopping block. It described a state that owes 55 cents on the dollar and delivers 20. It put the $7.2 million structural gap right there in the materials. And the council looked at all of that and produced an unreleased attachment.

What I can't stop thinking about is the framing that briefing carries. The 82 positions added while enrollment dropped 300 students — I've known that number since December, but now it's been handed to city councilors as the background context for setting their annual priorities. That's the narrative that's going to show up at every budget hearing this spring. Not "Augusta owes us $200 million it hasn't paid." Not "the mental health crisis required counselors." Just 82 positions and 300 fewer students, standing there as an accusation. The briefing was distributed to the council at the exact moment they were deciding what story to tell about this city. And by March, that framing is everywhere — in testimony, in local coverage, in the justifications board members repeat when they're challenged. I watched it happen and I couldn't find the moment where the counter-narrative got equal footing.

Looking back from here, the January 15 goal-setting session is the cleanest example of what the whole year has been: the formal institutional occasion that should have generated a public commitment didn't, and the absence of that commitment is its own kind of answer.
