# Cumulative Summary: Meg (PERSONA-011)

### Current Understanding

As of March 9, 2026, Meg understands the South Portland school budget crisis in full structural depth. The district faces a $7.2 million gap requiring elimination of 78 positions — including 42 teachers — under every budget scenario, regardless of how high the tax rate goes. That gap has three compounding causes: a staffing-enrollment mismatch (300+ students lost since 2017, 23% at the elementary level, while the district added 82 positions); chronic state underfunding (the state covers ~20% of actual costs against a 55% statutory obligation); and years of internal management breakdown (six finance directors in five years, $400K in mismanaged grants, two consecutive years of exceeding the legal budget limit). The city council set a 4–6% tax increase ceiling at the February 10 joint session. No scenario saves the 78 positions; the tax rate only determines the size of the taxpayer bill on top of the cuts.

Elementary reconfiguration remains the central unresolved structural question. Nine specific options were presented at the March 2 workshop. The superintendent's preferred option (closing Skillin) was rejected by at least four board members, with Chair DeAngelis framing Skillin's protection as an equity obligation to immigrant families. Whatever option survives will save less than the rejected plan, meaning the arithmetic has to close somewhere else. A final recommendation is expected at the March 23 workshop, now operating under a tightened public comment policy the board adopted on second reading at the March 9 meeting — poor timing given that 85% of parents in a recent survey reported feeling inadequately informed.

The property tax picture carries two structural complications beyond the school budget: TIF revenue diversion deals (170 Ocean Street and Texas Instruments) that redirect tax increment revenue away from the general fund for decades, and a Mahoney City Center capital bond still ranging $57–104M with no formal vote, potentially landing in the same tax cycle as the school increase.
