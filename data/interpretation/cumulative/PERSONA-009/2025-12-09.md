# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

The meeting Dana would have filed under "housing vote, skip it" turns out to be December 9 — the day after the $1.9 million deficit went public, and the day before the superintendent search workshop she covered at South Portland High. DeAngelo did not walk into the city council chamber that Tuesday to talk about a building permit. He came to remind seven elected officials, twenty-four hours after his district announced a mid-year spending freeze, that schools eat 61% of their constituents' property tax bills. Dana first logged that statistic in January, from the school board's own January 12 meeting. It was being deployed at the council table in December.

This reframes the intergovernmental conflict she has been covering since February. She has been treating the February 10 blowup — DeAngelo calling the council's guidance "not sufficient, not adequate, not appropriate, and not acceptable" — as the moment the school board engaged the city council as an adversary. But DeAngelo was already positioning with the council on December 9, the morning after the crisis became public. His cross-governmental strategy was not a reaction to February's impasse; it was the opening move. She has been watching the second act.

The $40 babysitter story adds texture to the access-and-apathy thread she first identified on December 4. Pride's barbershop anecdote was about a constituent who knows but doesn't show up. Zenia Pantos and Carly Williams are different: they showed up, they paid real money to be in the room, and Carly had done the actual research — staff names at Portland and the state legislature, ready to consult. The mayor said "we might have to be a little brave." Zero follow-through. The community access failure is not just apathy; it has always had a structural barrier dimension too, and that distinction matters for how Dana tells this story.

West is a character she has not yet developed. He put "I think we've been misled" on the record — the TIF deal locked in a 50% tax rebate for 30 years based on 124 units with onsite parking; the developer delivers 208 units with parking in the strip mall next door and the deal holds. Then West votes yes and jokes about the Christmas lights. He speaks dissent and votes consensus. Dana files him as a source who tells her what is actually wrong and then does nothing about it — which, in her experience, is the most useful kind of council member to know.
