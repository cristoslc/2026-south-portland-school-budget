# Editorials: How to Write Opinion Journalism

*Source: Jerz's Literacy Weblog, Seton Hill University — https://jerz.setonhill.edu/writing/journalism1/writing-an-editorial-journalism-tips/*

## What Distinguishes an Editorial

An editorial represents "the official collective position of the editorial board" and aims to inform, persuade, and entertain through opinion-driven journalism. Unlike straight news reporting, editorials openly defend a perspective while maintaining ethical standards around fairness and accuracy.

## Core Requirements

### Structure and Length

Successful editorials are concise — approximately **400 words** with short, journalism-style paragraphs of **two to three sentences**. Begin with your main argument rather than lengthy introductions. Do not bury the lede.

### Substance Over Slogans

The difference between a strong editorial and a rant is **evidence**. Simply repeating talking points doesn't constitute genuine opinion. A developed position is grounded in research and a genuine understanding of competing viewpoints.

## Research and Sourcing

Investigate the actual reasons behind the issue you're addressing.

- **Name specific sources** — avoid vague references like "some people say" or "research shows"
- Interview actual stakeholders: professors, community leaders, organization heads
- One example: a student transformed a complaint about course scheduling into an informed piece by interviewing faculty about budget constraints and enrollment realities

## Persuasive Approach

- Rather than attacking opponents, **acknowledge their legitimate concerns** and explain why your position remains superior
- Misrepresenting opposing views through exaggeration or unflattering labels undermines credibility with informed readers
- Position yourself as persuading reasonable people who recognize merit in multiple perspectives

## Solution-Focused Writing

Move beyond listing complaints. **Propose constructive alternatives** backed by investigation. Discovering the rationale for a problem allows you to suggest practical solutions rather than simply venting frustration.

## Distinguishing Fact from Opinion

Readers need to know when you're presenting verified information versus your interpretation:

- "The data shows..." → signals fact
- "This suggests..." or "The board should..." → signals opinion
- Mixing the two without clear signals undermines your credibility

## Key Advice

- An editorial must be **decisive** — acknowledge the other side, but only to effectively argue against them
- "Substance over slogans" — gut reactions ("Topic X is bad!") are not positions; developed positions emerge from research and understanding competing viewpoints
- The best editorials address something **timely and specific** — writing about a solved problem has zero impact
