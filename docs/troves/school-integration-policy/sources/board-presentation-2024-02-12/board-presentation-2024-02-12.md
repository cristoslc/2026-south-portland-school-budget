# Steering Committee Presentation to the Board — February 12, 2024

**Elementary Boundaries and Configurations Steering Committee**

## A Rising Tide Lifts All Boats

This presentation outlines the BCSC's policy recommendations to the School Board.

We found significant socioeconomic and racial inequities between schools. To address this, we recommend the board implement short and long-term solutions. This will benefit our community by helping close the achievement gap and better prepare our students for the real world as well as work towards a future of welcoming and compassionate citizens.

After months of community feedback, a common narrative emerged. Initially those living in closer proximity to their schools feared for their children's experience, and were resistant to change. However, as they grasped the impact of inequities on students and educators, as well as the benefits to their children, a shift occurred. The necessity for change became apparent.

## Major Findings

### Finding 1: Demographic Disparities

The school district's elementary schools differ in substantial and significant ways in terms of the number of students:

- Who are students of color
- Who are multilingual learners
- Who are meeting learning expectations
- Who are economically disadvantaged
- Who are affected by mobility
- Who live in close proximity to their schools

As a result, our students can have significantly different educational experiences in our schools.

### Finding 2: Benefits of Integration

Students in homogeneous schools miss out on learning from diverse peers. As students grow up, the US will become a more diverse nation. The school district must prepare all students for this future.

Examples of the benefits of integrated schools:

- More positive later racial attitudes
- Increased likelihood of greater tolerance and better intergroup relations
- Higher levels of social cohesion
- Improved learning outcomes

(Sources: Institute for Race & Justice, Century Foundation)

Currently, there are significant differences in opportunities to learn from diverse peers in our elementary schools.

### Finding 3: Economic Integration

It is best practice that economically disadvantaged students learn alongside peers who are not economically disadvantaged.

- Students in integrated schools have higher average test scores.
- Students in integrated schools are more likely to enroll in college.
- Students in integrated schools are less likely to drop out.
- Integrated schools help to reduce racial achievement gaps.

(Source: Century Foundation)

Currently, two of our schools have substantially more economically disadvantaged students than the other three.

## Specific Findings and Proposed Actions

### Proximity and Attendance Zones

**Finding:** The Steering Committee is aware that families place value on living near their child's elementary school. Many parents love having a neighborhood school. Families of color are more likely to live far from their assigned school, leading to more students of color taking buses. The district's attendance zones, lack of affordable housing, and geographic locations of schools contribute to this issue.

**Proposed Action:** The district should conduct an analysis of attendance zones and make changes so that:

1. As many families as possible can live within one mile of their assigned elementary school
2. There is greater equity among families who live in close proximity to their schools.

This action should be coupled with viable grade-level configuration, as attendance zone changes alone will not fully address the issues of the major findings. That analysis should be conducted by outside expert(s) with experience in this work. The analysis should include the impact of any potential changes. The expert should provide the Board with multiple options by January 30, 2025.

This will have impact in the short term, while other long-term solutions are explored.

### Brickhill Assignment

**Finding:** Brickhill families live more than 4.5 miles from Brown School. This is the neighborhood located the furthest distance from its assigned school, and a significant majority of students living there are students of color.

**Proposed Action:** District administration should immediately initiate the process of reviewing the assignment of Brickhill students to Brown School. That review should begin with securing language-accessible input on this topic from Brickhill families. If Brickhill families are rezoned, current Brown School students should be able to complete their elementary education at Brown if their families so choose.

This will have impact in the short term, while other long-term solutions are explored.

### Triennial Zone Review

**Finding:** The school district should not have waited 15 years for a public discussion of its elementary school attendance zones and their impact on the demographic composition of those schools.

**Proposed Action:** The School Board should approve a new policy that would require that the Board review and approve attendance zones every three years. This approval must occur at least five months prior to the start of that school year. This cycle would begin in 2024. The policy should also require that administration report by November 30 of every school year about the impact of attendance zones on the demographic composition of elementary schools.

This can be enacted now to have long lasting impact in the district.

### Economically-Disadvantaged Students

**Finding:** Two schools in the district have substantially more economically-disadvantaged students than other schools. Economic disadvantage is substantially related to learning outcomes for students.

**Proposed Action:** The Board should ensure that schools with disproportionate numbers of economically-disadvantaged students have significantly lower teacher-to-student ratios than other schools.

This can be enacted now to have long term impact on students.

## Best Practices and Long Term Policy Solutions

Three proposals during the Steering Committee process have been identified as best practices to explore:

- Viable grade-level configuration changes
- Magnet programming
- Controlled choice

**Proposed Actions:**

- The Board should identify a consultant to complete an impact study of potential grade-level configuration changes on our schools (including needed resources, traffic implications, etc.)
- District administration should secure family input and review highly-successful magnet programs in other school districts.
- The Board should identify a consultant to complete a proposal of a controlled choice model (including needed resources, traffic implications, etc.).
- The findings from the research above should be presented to the Board by January 30, 2025.

These are the most viable long term solutions that address our needs as a community.

## Summary of Proposed Actions

### Short Term

- Analyze current attendance zones and enact policy to review every 3 years
- Review Brickhill assignment
- Increase teacher to student ratios in high needs schools
- Expand Pre-K
- Create district-wide PTA
- Continue community engagement efforts to recruit diverse members for strategic planning

### Long Term

- Impact study to explore new configuration and attendance zones
- Explore magnet programming
- Impact study to explore controlled choice model
