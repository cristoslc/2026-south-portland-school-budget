# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

I have been waiting since January 13 for the Pearl Street rate impact numbers — the council promised them by end of January, and they arrived six weeks late, but they arrived. CDM Smith did the work properly: $38.9M pump station replacement, $12.8M sludge dewatering, $51.68M in revenue bonds total, 22% annual rate increases for FY27, FY28, and FY29 on top of a 4% operating baseline. Monthly residential bills roughly double over three years. That is a painful number, but the regional comparison actually holds — South Portland currently has the lowest rate in Greater Portland, and after three years of 22% increases it lands near the middle of the pack because Westbrook and Gorham and everyone else faces the same aging infrastructure cycle. I can accept hard news when it comes with methodology attached. This presentation gave me methodology.

What I cannot accept is what happened around it. In a single evening, the council processed $51.7M in sewer revenue bonds, discussed a Mahoney renovation that — even stripped of the library, the police station, and with the third floor left vacant — still runs $70 to $76 million, and approved a purchase of the Walgreens site for a future police station that will go to a November 2026 referendum at a construction cost still being estimated. Three major capital commitments. No integrated slide showing what all of this looks like on one household's bill simultaneously. I flagged this thread explicitly in February. It is still open.

The Walgreens deal is the cleanest item on the agenda — $2.525M funded with asset forfeiture funds and TIF money already earmarked, no property tax increase, unanimous vote. That is the template. Everything else tonight was messier.

The Mahoney number has moved again. January 13 it was $193M, architect not confident. February 17 it was $57 to $104M depending on scope. Tonight the stripped-down version — the one nobody particularly wants — lands at $70 to $76M, while a new standalone city hall would cost $38 to $45M and yield more usable square footage per dollar. The referendum is pushed to November 2027, which at least removes it from direct collision with the June 9 school budget vote. That is sequencing progress, not fiscal progress.

The moment that stays with me is Councilor Matthews voting no on the $100K Project HOME appropriation and citing school layoffs and an "$8.4 million" deficit. Nobody else engaged. Six to one. The $8.4M figure does not match what I have been tracking — the council's own February 10 guidance implied $8 to $9M, but the source figures David has worked from ran $7.2M before guidance tightened. I need to understand where Matthews got $8.4M. But his underlying point is exactly the question I have been building a spreadsheet to answer since February 17: all of these fiscal buckets hit the same property tax base, and this city has never produced a single document showing the cumulative household impact. Matthews said it in ninety seconds and got outvoted six to one. The conversation the city needs to have lasted less time than a parking discussion.

The school budget calendar is now locked in: April 7 public hearing, April 14 Budget Workshop #1 with schools listed first, April 28 Workshop #2, May 5 council vote, June 9 referendum. That schedule gives me something concrete to plan against. The next six weeks are when this year's decisions actually get made.
