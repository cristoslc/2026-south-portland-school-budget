# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

This was the meeting where reconfiguration stopped being abstract and became nine specific options on a spreadsheet. The administration presented a full matrix of scenarios, and I can finally see what's actually on the table. Three of the nine options involve closing individual elementary schools -- Small, Brown, Dyer, or Kaler depending on the scenario. The administration's top-ranked plan, Option 1.7, would create separate pre-K-1 schools and 2-4 schools, meaning my daughter would switch buildings after first grade. Several options require purchasing or renting portable classrooms because existing buildings can't handle the consolidated enrollment -- portables cost $270K to buy or $100K to rent per unit. None of this sounds like a district investing in the future.

But then something genuinely encouraging happened: board members started pushing back on the worst options. Four members explicitly said they would not support closing Skillin school, with one citing historical inequity -- that closure would force immigrant families to bus across town while wealthier families keep their neighborhood schools. That's exactly the kind of equity lens I want to see applied to decisions that affect my daughter's generation. The board isn't rubber-stamping the administration's preferred option, and the equity argument is gaining real traction.

The pre-K numbers got more concrete and more encouraging. The state is requiring the district to serve 80-90 four-year-olds starting September 2026, with three-year-olds added the following year. Current capacity is 64 seats, expanding to 128. This is real growth in early childhood services on a timeline that directly overlaps with my family's needs. Combined with what I learned about Head Start partnerships and the physical space protections, pre-K remains the strongest positive thread in this entire saga.

What's deeply frustrating is the timeline opacity. When a board member asked when families would know which schools their children would attend under redistricting, administrators said they need board guidance first and couldn't commit to providing details before the budget vote. So they're asking the community to support a budget that includes reconfiguration without telling families which school their kids will actually go to. For a parent planning two years ahead, that's maddening. Parents at public comment described transportation nightmares and loss of community connections -- these are exactly my fears, voiced by people already living in the system.
