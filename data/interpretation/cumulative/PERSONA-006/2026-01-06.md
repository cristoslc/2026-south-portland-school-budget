# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

Looking back at January 6th from where I'm sitting now, this was the first city council meeting after the school board revealed its full deficit picture -- and you'd barely know the building next door was on fire. The council worked through cemetery donation funds and committee announcements while $7.2 million in cuts were being mapped out two blocks away.

That said, this meeting gave me two things I don't usually get from city hall: a moment of honesty and a flicker of genuine hope. The city manager was right when he said asking councilors to name an acceptable tax increase is "a very unfair question" without showing them the books. That's exactly backwards, and I'm glad someone with a title said it out loud. And the January 22nd citizen forum on school cost-saving measures -- the school board chair actually asking residents for ideas instead of just presenting a bill and waiting for applause -- felt like a different posture than anything I'd seen from them in December.

But I have to read both through what I now know. The February 10 guidance workshop that was announced here is the one that eventually produced the 3-6% council pushback -- the one real moment of fiscal discipline I got all winter. I was glad it happened. What the city manager was also doing on January 6th, though, was laying out exactly why that number would be hard to hold: waste management contracts locked in higher, union wages up, Eco Maine costs climbing. Every pressure he named that night showed up as a line item in the joint workshop deck five weeks later.

The Mahoney Center keeps nagging at me. The city manager practically winked when he said the "dollar tag" had been in the press and the full recommendation was coming the following week. That's the mayor's signature capital project -- the one she was promoting at her inauguration while the school board was already burning through its fund balance -- and it's being treated as a completely separate conversation from the school budget crisis. Nobody in that room connected those dots. Every dollar committed to a new city center is a dollar that isn't relieving pressure on property taxes that are already at the breaking point.

The state property tax relief task force is the one genuinely new thing that caught my attention. Augusta admitting that property taxes are a real burden on seniors and fixed-income households is more than I expected. I'll believe it means something when I see it on my actual tax bill.
