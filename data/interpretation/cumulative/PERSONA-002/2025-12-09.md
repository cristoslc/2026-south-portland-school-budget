# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

This is the first city council session David attends already knowing the district is in crisis. He processed the December 8 shock — the $1.9M deficit, the depleted fund balance, 78 positions on the chopping block — and then watched the council spend two hours on property assessments and a zoning vote while the school budget got 90 seconds from a board member standing in public comment. Not a councilor. A board member. In public comment. With no figures.

The assessor's presentation is actually solid data. The taxable base grew $125 million this year, and the mill rate came in at 1364, below projections. That is the good news. But the assessor's own numbers show residential values climbing while commercial values drop — a multi-year burden shift toward homeowners that nobody on the council connects to the school budget math. Schools are 61% of property taxes. If the residential base keeps carrying more of the load while the district needs $7+ million in cuts to stay anywhere near a reasonable tax increase, those two facts compound each other in ways that hit every homeowner twice.

The One 70 Ocean debate produced the meeting's most interesting moment. West flagged that the original TIFF gave the developer 50% of property taxes back for 30 years — structured when the project was 124 units with on-site structured parking and no zoning changes needed. It is now 208 units, surface parking, and a zoning amendment. West said plainly: "I think we've been misled." The city manager's response — that municipalities lose roughly half of new property value to state aid clawbacks anyway, making the credit roughly cost-neutral — is the kind of argument that needs a spreadsheet, not a rule of thumb. David is going to model this himself before he accepts it. A 30-year 50% developer credit is not a detail.

But what stays is the pattern. December 1: ceremonial silence one week before the crisis. December 4: routine governance four days before. December 9: the crisis is already public, and it gets 90 seconds. The silence no longer looks like pre-disclosure caution — it looks structural. The school board and city council are operating in separate fiscal universes even when the deficit is on the table and the meter is running.
