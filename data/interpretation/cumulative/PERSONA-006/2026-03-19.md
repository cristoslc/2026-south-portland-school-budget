# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

Everything I've been tracking since December came into focus in one meeting last night. I didn't like the picture.

The sewer bill is the thing that's going to blindside people. I've known since January that the Pearl Street pump station was coming and that it would land on sewer rates without a referendum. Now it has a rate schedule. Twenty-two percent annual increases, three years in a row, on top of the regular operating bump. Your bill goes from about $56 a month to somewhere around $90 before this is over. The finance director showed a comparison chart -- "you'll still be cheaper than Cape Elizabeth" -- as if that's a comfort to someone budgeting on a fixed income. I've been sitting with this question since January. Now I have the answer. I don't enjoy having it.

Then, same meeting, six of seven councilors vote to pull $100,000 out of the city's rainy-day fund for rental assistance. The one no vote was Councilor Matthews, who said what I was thinking: 72 school employees got pink slip notices the day before this meeting. And the school deficit -- which we've been calling $7.2 million all winter -- is now $8.4 million. The health insurance finalization and whatever else has piled on has made it worse. Six of them voted yes anyway. That's the same reserve fund the city manager pointed to in February as evidence of sound financial management. Now it's being drawn down while the district is issuing layoff notices. Nobody asked me. Nobody asked any of us.

Mahoney at least moved off the November calendar, which is a small mercy. They stripped out the library and the police station and the number still came in at $70-76 million. They want a 2027 referendum instead. Meanwhile, there's a police and fire bond heading to voters this November.

Here's what I kept waiting for someone to do last night, and no one did: add it up. School property tax increase. Sewer bill doubling over three years. Police and fire bond this fall. City center bond the year after. Not one piece at a time -- the total. On my house. What does all of this actually cost me, combined, over the next three years? Nobody at that meeting had that number, or nobody wanted to say it out loud. Until someone does, I don't fully trust any single piece of it.
