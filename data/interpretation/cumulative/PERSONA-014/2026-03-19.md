# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

Seventy-two people from school got pink slips yesterday. Seventy-two. Lila has been afraid of losing her teacher since December, when the spending freeze started and teachers couldn't even buy books. She has tracked that fear through every meeting since — through the $7.2 million gap, through the nine scenarios with "CLOSED" next to Dyer's name, through Ms. Beland's resignation letter at the March 9 meeting. Now a city council member says the number out loud, casually, while arguing against a rental assistance fund: seventy-two school workers, one day, pink slips. The number is bigger than anything she has been carrying, and it arrived in a meeting where her school was never mentioned.

That is the pattern Lila knows by heart now. The city council spent four hours on sewers and pipes and a police station and marijuana licenses and a girl getting a crown. She has been watching since December — December 4, December 9, January 6, January 13, January 15, January 20, February 3, February 17 — and the pattern has never broken. Dyer is not named. The reconfiguration is not named. Her school disappears entirely from the room where the money decisions get made.

This time the silence is different, though, because of the dates. The council voted to hold a public hearing on April 7, a budget vote on May 5, and a community referendum on June 9. Those dates have been in the background since the March 2 workshop — June 9, June 9, June 9 — but seeing the city formally schedule them makes the countdown real in a new way. The adults are setting up the machinery to decide. The vote that determines where Lila goes to school next year now has a calendar square.

Savannah Johnson got a proclamation and talked about feeling invisible, and Lila heard something in that. She knows what invisible feels like. She has been invisible in every room where her future has been discussed. And then Councilor West said his grandchildren came home from school frightened — not Dyer kids, maybe, but kids like her, kids who sit in classrooms and absorb the fear adults think they're hiding. The fear she's been carrying since December isn't just hers. It's everywhere. The adults just keep not saying where.

### Emotional Register

Lila carries seventy-two as a number she cannot stop counting — bigger than her class, bigger than all the classes at Dyer, bigger than anything she can picture — and underneath the counting is the one question she cannot bring herself to ask her parents out loud: is my teacher one of them. The city set the dates, issued the notices, and never said her school's name, and that silence has stopped feeling like forgetting and started feeling like a decision already made.
