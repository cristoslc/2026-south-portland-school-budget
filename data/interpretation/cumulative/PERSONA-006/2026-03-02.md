# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

Three and a half hours in a packed room and I came out more frustrated than I went in. The chair opened with the same numbers I already know -- 19% status-quo increase, 300 fewer students, 82 more staff, fund balance gone. At least she's being honest about the scale of the problem. But then the real action started: nine different elementary reconfiguration options, each with different savings, and the board killed the one that saves the most money before the ink was dry.

Option 1.7 -- closing Skillin school -- would have saved $19.6 million over five years. Four board members publicly said they wouldn't vote for it, citing equity concerns because it's the school with the most immigrant students. I understand the sensitivity, but nobody offered an alternative that makes up the difference. The cost of that political decision shows up directly on my tax bill. They'll pick a less efficient option and the gap gets wider. The non-closure path scored lowest on their own evaluation matrix and wasn't even fully costed. So they're eliminating the best financial option for political reasons and the backup plan isn't even finished.

What really got me was the public comment. Twenty-plus speakers, almost all parents, all wanting to keep things exactly the way they are -- small classes, neighborhood schools, specialist teachers. Not one of them addressed how to pay for it. Not one offered to accept a higher tax increase. They want the district to find $7.2 million somewhere that doesn't affect their kids. That's not a plan, that's a wish. The chair had to remind people that board members make $1,000 a year doing this -- they're not the enemy. But somebody has to be honest about trade-offs, and right now the only people talking about trade-offs are the people writing the checks.
