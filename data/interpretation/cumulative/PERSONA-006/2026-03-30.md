# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

Five months of meetings, and the board can't pass its own budget. Five to two. The superintendent's own proposal, the one they've been building toward since December, and a majority of the board votes it down at eleven o'clock at night. Tom took the $257 figure home from March 23 as something finally concrete — a number he could write down, carry to his neighbors, bring to the June referendum. Tonight that number is in question again. Some of the board members who voted no are already talking about going back to the city council to ask for more money. The chair was clear about what that means: a loan from the city's reserve fund — the same one Tom watched get drawn down on back-to-back weeks in March — or a higher tax ceiling on the June ballot. Tom is a direct voter on that referendum. He was told one number. He may be voting on a different one.

He's been waiting since December for anyone inside this district to put the per-pupil comparison figures on the record with real specificity. Feller finally did it tonight: Gorham at $20,000, Windham at $21,600, Brunswick at $24,000, Portland — twice the size, higher-needs students — still below South Portland's $26,651. Tom has been saying something was wrong with this spending level for months. Now he has the receipts. What he still doesn't have is anyone explaining how South Portland got to the top of that list, or who approved eighty-two new hires while three hundred students walked out the door.

Kahler is the one clean outcome. Six to one, the superintendent files a closing report. It's roughly $400,000 on an $8.4 million problem, but it's a real decision that actually happened, which puts it in rare company this winter. He'll note it. He won't be relieved by it.

The pattern Tom has been tracking since December now has its clearest expression. A board that spent five months discussing a crisis couldn't vote yes on resolving it. The September warning signs, the December bombshell, the February forum, the March workshops — all of it leads to a 5-2 no at eleven at night, and a follow-up meeting scheduled for Thursday. The people who most loudly demanded fiscal restraint all winter are now the ones pushing to ask the city for more. That's not an irony. That's the whole winter in one vote.
