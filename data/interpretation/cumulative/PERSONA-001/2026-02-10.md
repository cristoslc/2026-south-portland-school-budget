# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

This joint workshop is the meeting where Maria sees the full political machinery surrounding the budget crisis, and it is worse than she feared. The day after watching the school board unanimously advance reconfiguration despite teacher and parent opposition, she now watches city councilors wrestle with the same impossible math -- and most of them land below the school board's requested 6% floor. Guidance ranging from 3% to 6%, with most councilors at 4-5%, means the cuts will be even deeper than the already devastating scenarios the board has been modeling. Maria came in alarmed about reconfiguration; she leaves understanding that the political dynamics between city council and school board may force even harsher outcomes.

The reconfiguration proposal has gained specificity overnight. The interim superintendent now frames it as two primary schools (pre-K-1) and three intermediate schools (grades 2-4), saving an estimated $2 million. Dr. Prince confirmed that class sizes would increase under this plan -- current averages of 20 students in K-2 and 24 in grades 3-4 would grow as grade levels consolidate and teachers are eliminated. This is no longer an abstract scenario; it is the administration's preferred path, presented to the city council as the solution.

Chair DeAngelis's claim that "we don't have neighborhood schools anymore" lands as a direct insult to Maria's experience. Maria walks her daughter to school, knows the teachers, runs the PTA network from that building. The chair is telling her that the community she has built around her child's school is an illusion -- or at least, not worth preserving. Meanwhile, the teachers' union president reminded everyone that staff negotiated in good faith and did not cause this crisis. Maria appreciates the solidarity but it reinforces her sense that the people closest to children -- parents and teachers -- have the least influence over decisions that reshape their lives.

The revelation that 82 positions were added while enrollment dropped by 300 students crystallizes a question Maria has been circling since the December 17 workshop: where is the $26,651 per-pupil spending actually going? Central office staffing is the obvious suspect, but nobody on the board or council is pressing that question. Instead, the conversation focuses on cutting classroom positions and restructuring the schools children attend.
