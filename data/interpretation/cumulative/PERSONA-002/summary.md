# Cumulative Summary: David (PERSONA-002)

### Current Understanding

David understands South Portland's school budget crisis as a structural collapse with documented institutional roots. Elementary enrollment fell 23% (1,401 to 1,080 students) while the district added 82 staff positions. Per-pupil costs of $26,651 are reportedly the highest among comparable districts. State funding covers only ~20% of actual costs against a 55% formula assumption, forcing 61% of the total property tax burden onto local residential ratepayers — a burden compounded by a multi-year shift away from commercial valuations.

The district's financial controls have failed persistently: six finance directors in five years, a second consecutive audit material weakness, 34 correcting entries required for FY25, $400K in federal grant funds clawed back to the general fund, and a $1.1M nutrition fund deficit that went untracked for years. The operative budget gap for FY27 is now $8–9M, tightened from $7.2M by the city council's February 10 joint guidance of a 3–6% tax increase. The school fund balance sits at only $739K — functionally depleted — while the city holds $8.5M in undesignated reserves. FY26 itself projects between a $170K surplus and a $1M deficit depending on spending controls, with health insurance actuals not arriving until March 25.

Elementary reconfiguration (~$2M savings) is the primary identified mechanism, but four board members oppose closing Skillin, the no-reconfiguration scenario remains unmodeled, and transportation costs and Pre-K mandate implications are uncosted. A remaining $3M+ gap is unresolved entering three scheduled workshops.

Compounding everything: the city faces its own capital obligations — Mahoney renovation ($57–104M with no council-approved ceiling), existing city bonds ($89.1M), and Pearl Street revenue bonds (~$50M) — all loading onto the same property tax base. The combined fiscal math has never appeared in a single staff document. David's structural conclusion, confirmed across 18 meetings, is that the city and school board operate in institutionally isolated fiscal universes connected only by a calendar-bound joint workshop mechanism that does not flex for crises already in motion.
