### Reactions

I walked away from this meeting with such mixed feelings. On one hand, you had Police Chief Ahern giving probably the clearest statement I've ever heard from local law enforcement about their duty to protect all residents, including from federal agents who break the law. That felt significant. But then you had this whole discussion about new business loan programs and zoning changes that sounded great for existing business owners but had zero language about equity or supporting businesses owned by people of color or immigrants.

The housing workshop was fascinating but also frustrating as hell. Milan's talking about allowing duplexes and triplexes throughout the city, which could genuinely help with affordability, but when I asked myself "who's going to benefit from these changes?" I realized nobody had done that analysis. Are we going to see more housing that working families can afford, or are we just going to see more investment properties for people who already have capital? The fact that they haven't mapped this against demographic data or considered anti-displacement measures tells me everything I need to know about how seriously they're taking equity.

And honestly, the whole meeting was overshadowed by the fact that ICE is literally terrorizing families in our community to the point where people can't participate in their kids' school budget process. Pedro Vasquez calling in from his daughter's basketball game to talk about people carrying their birth certificates around - that hit me hard. When Mayor Tipton talked about being "aghast" at the end, I appreciated the sentiment, but where are the concrete policy responses? Where's the rapid response fund for affected families? Where are the know-your-rights workshops in multiple languages? Recognition without action just feels performative at this point.
