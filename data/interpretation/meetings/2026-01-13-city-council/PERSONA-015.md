### Reactions

So I stayed up and watched the whole Mahoney meeting Tuesday — four hours, I was falling asleep at the end — and the thing I can't shake is that this is the school building we closed. Mahoney Middle School. We closed it because we don't have enough kids anymore. Councilor Matthews stood up and said his mom went to school there, his kids went to school there, he went to school there. And I sat there thinking: we gave up that building because enrollment collapsed, and now the city wants to spend $200 million turning it into a city center. While we're cutting 42 teachers and trying to serve 1,080 kids across five elementary schools. I understand the city has real needs — I know the fire station is a 1950s fire station and the police station has standing water in the basement. But nobody in that room connected those two facts out loud. The same decisions that emptied Mahoney are what's breaking the school budget right now.

The moment that actually made me sit up straight was a social worker named Olivia Montgomery. She stood up during public comment and said, clearly, that placing a police station right next to city hall would stop immigrant families from accessing city services — that the fear of police presence creates real barriers, that it's not theoretical, that people just won't show up. She even talked about voting. And she's right. I work with those families at three different schools. I've seen what happened after the ICE raids — it takes years to rebuild enough trust that families will walk into any building that looks like a government office. And then the council spent two more hours talking about whether to include the library in the bond, and literally not one councilor brought her point back up. Councilor Scott said she was against the police station location "for many, many reasons" and then didn't say what they were. That's the moment when our families disappear from the policy conversation. Every time.

The thing that's going to keep me up at night is the stacking. The school athletic field bond already passed. The Pearl Street pump station is coming — city manager said probably under $50 million — and that's a sewer rate increase, not even a ballot question, so families won't see it coming. Now potentially $194 million more for the city. And our school budget needs its own tax increase just to hold roughly steady. The finance director showed a $514,000 average home value for South Portland — this city bond alone would add over $1,100 a year to that household. In the best case. And the district's piece isn't in any of those numbers. These all land on the same people. The same immigrant families working two jobs. The same seniors on fixed income who were crying at the podium a couple years ago when assessments went up. My families. The council didn't make a single decision last night — they're sending it back to a committee for more scenarios, again. I hope whoever shows up on January 27 is willing to say what nobody said Tuesday: all of it is connected, and the most vulnerable people in this community are standing at the intersection of every single one of these choices.
