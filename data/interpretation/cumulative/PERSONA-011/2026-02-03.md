# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

The night before the rescheduled budget forum — the one that got pushed from January 22nd because families were too scared to leave their houses — I'm watching city council do their regular business. And then the auditor starts talking about the school department's finances, and I'm suddenly writing faster than I can keep up.

Six finance directors in five years. Three interim, three who lasted about a year each. Joe Prince said it like it was background context, but I'm sitting there thinking: every time I've reported on this crisis since December — the $1.9M deficit, the $7.2M gap, the 78 positions, the fact that the district somehow added 82 staff while losing 300 kids — I've been reporting on outcomes. Tonight I'm hearing about the mechanism. You cannot have stable financial management with six people running the books in five years. You cannot catch grant misuse before $400,000 has to be transferred back to the general fund. You cannot stop the budget from exceeding its legal limit two years running. Prince says the new hire is "amazing." He sounds like he means it. But we've apparently said that before. Thirty-four journal entries in a year — normal is under ten. I'm texting that number while he's still talking.

The city's own finances are in decent shape, which is the one thing I can hand to my neighborhood Facebook group without dread attached: auto excise taxes came in $1.8 million over budget, fund balance up $5.8 million. After required reserves and the $1-2 million already set aside for projected school overages, there's about $2 million sitting there for genuine emergencies. Schools keep eroding it. But it exists.

The Texas Instruments TIF discussion went long and technical, and I was tracking it on my phone the best I could. Basic deal: TI generates $1.2 million a year for the city and wants a 20-year extension, with staff now proposing to expand the district to include the mall area for transit-oriented development. I understood the TIF mechanism from December 9 — 170 Ocean Street showed me how a housing deal can divert property tax revenue for thirty years. But TI is a different scale, and it's the same pattern. One project I could explain as an isolated deal. Two projects in the same budget season is a strategy.

Chair DeAngelis used her school board liaison slot to tell the council they "must accept responsibility for the school as a department of the city" and provide "clear guidance during a very difficult budget year." Not a request. A statement of obligation. That was the night before the forum she'd been fighting for, and seven days before she'd be sitting across from those same council members at the joint session. I've been watching her since December 1. The values she named at the inaugural were real — she proved it every time the stakes got high. This was another one.

The immigration resolution passed unanimously, amended to ask Governor Mills for a 60-day statewide eviction moratorium. The same fear that canceled the January 22nd forum got a council response. Not enough. But a response.
