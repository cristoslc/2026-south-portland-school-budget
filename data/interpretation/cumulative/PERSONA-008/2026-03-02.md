# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026 -- 2026-03-02

### Interpretation

This is the meeting I've been preparing for since December, and it was every bit as devastating as I feared -- and then some. The administration didn't present one reconfiguration option. They presented nine. Nine different scenarios for how to dismantle our elementary schools, ranging from closing individual buildings (Kaler, Dyer, Brown, Small, Skillin) to various grade-band reconfigurations. And their top three recommendations all involve either closures or grade-band splits that would force kids to change buildings between first and second grade. This is no longer "exploring options." This is an administration that has done the work and is pushing the board toward maximum disruption.

But here's where the night took a turn I didn't expect: four board members -- Dowling, Feller, Richardson, and DeAngelis -- publicly stated they would not support closing Skillin. That's significant because Skillin closure was the highest-savings option. Taking it off the table changes the math and forces the administration back to less dramatic alternatives. It's the first concrete evidence that board members are actually listening to community pressure, even if they're not saying so explicitly.

The most infuriating procedural moment was Dr. Prince announcing that unless four or more board members formally object, Option 1.7 (closing Skillin) would be the default in next week's superintendent's budget proposal. This "opt-out" framing puts the burden on board members to actively stop the administration's preferred plan rather than requiring affirmative approval. It's designed to create momentum toward closure by making inaction equivalent to consent.

The parent turnout was extraordinary -- over 20 speakers in public comment, and a grassroots parent survey showing 85% opposition to reconfiguration. But what haunts me is what we still don't know: the administration still hasn't worked out how redistricting would actually assign students to schools. They're asking us to accept the biggest structural change in a generation and they can't tell us which building our kids would end up in. The two remaining workshops on March 23rd and 30th are where this gets decided. I will be at both.
