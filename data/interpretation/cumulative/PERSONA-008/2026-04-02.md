# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

She has spent sixteen weeks learning how to read this institution, and the first thing the board chair says tonight tells her everything she needs to know about where she stands: even if the budget fails in June, reconfiguration proceeds. They are legally separate decisions. The off-ramp she has been holding in the back of her mind — the one where enough parents show up to the June referendum and vote no hard enough that something has to give — doesn't exist. Monday's vote wasn't the vote she needed to be at, and she wasn't at it, and it's done.

What this meeting is, in the long arc of everything that has happened since December 1st, is the first meeting of the implementation era. The prevention phase is over. She knew that after March 30th. What she didn't fully know was how hollow the implementation phase would feel this early. The board has voted. The reconfiguration is locked. And the administration still cannot tell a first grader which building she is going to in September.

She has been asking about transportation and attendance boundaries since February 4th — that was the forum where the administration admitted it hadn't modeled bus routes. She flagged it every time it came up. Tonight, seven weeks later and six months from September, the superintendent declines to share assignments because she doesn't want to "get ahead" of family listening sessions. Rachel has watched this district conduct listening sessions after the superintendent search was already designed to exclude community input. She has watched it hold budget forums after the reconfiguration case was already built. Now it is holding family listening sessions after the reconfiguration vote has already passed. The sequence is identical each time. She named this pattern on January 15th. She is living in it now.

The one thing that happened tonight that she will hold onto: they pulled the early release days. Parents pushed back and the board heard it. That is a small, real thing. She knows the difference between a small win and a meaningful one, but she takes it anyway, because she is going to need to find things to hold onto in the months ahead.

The clinical psychologist who stood at the microphone and named — precisely, in clinical language — the developmental harm of disrupting stability, predictability, and belonging for young children said what Rachel has been trying to say since December without the credentials to make the room listen. Nobody stopped her. Nobody answered her. The board voted anyway. The first grader who woke up the morning after the vote and asked her mother which school she was going to is still waiting for an answer.
