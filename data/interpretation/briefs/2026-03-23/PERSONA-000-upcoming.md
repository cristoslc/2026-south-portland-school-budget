## What This Meeting Is For

Tonight is structured as a **workshop followed by a special meeting** — the first of two such back-to-back sessions scheduled in the coming weeks (the second is March 30).

The workshop portion is a working session where board members can ask questions, review numbers, and discuss options with school administration. These sessions are not typical vote-heavy meetings; they are where the board gets into the details before decisions are formalized.

The **special meeting** portion, held the same evening, is where formal votes can occur. It likely exists to give the board a vehicle to act on any items that need official approval before the next regular meeting.

The central subject is the **FY27 school budget** — what it will cost to run South Portland's schools next year, what must be cut, and what the tax impact on residents will be.

## Key Public Questions

**What will this cost me in property taxes?** The slide deck explicitly notes that the tax impact figures were still being worked out ("meeting with City Finance on Monday") as of when the slides were prepared on March 20. Expect the administration to present updated numbers tonight, but residents should ask whether those figures are final or still preliminary.

**Which elementary school is being recommended for closure?** The available document did not show the complete comparison of the two options. Tonight's presentation should name the schools and explain the rationale for the recommendation.

**What happens to students if a school closes?** A closure does not mean a building is sold or demolished immediately. The slides mention the possibility of a lease arrangement of up to four years, which suggests the district may want to keep its options open. What happens to the students and staff currently at that school is a practical question that should be addressed.

**What do the pink slips mean in practice?** In Maine public schools, layoff notices sent in March are required by statute but do not necessarily mean all 72 employees will lose their jobs permanently. Some positions may be recalled before the school year begins, depending on what the final budget looks like. The board should clarify how many of those notices are expected to result in actual separations.

**Is the $75.6 million number firm?** The budget process still has multiple steps ahead (see below). The number being discussed tonight is a proposal, not a final figure.

## What To Watch For

**The school closure vote or signal.** Whether the board takes a formal vote on a closure recommendation tonight, or simply gives direction to administration, this is the highest-stakes item. If a vote happens, it sets the course for the April 7 City Council presentation.

**Updated tax impact numbers.** If the administration brings revised figures from its Monday meeting with City Finance, those numbers will give residents the first concrete estimate of what this budget means for their tax bills.

**The board's appetite for the proposed cuts.** Board members may push back on specific reductions — to staffing, programs, or the closure plan — or they may signal broad agreement. Watch for any dissent or calls to revisit the 12% reduction figure.

**The road ahead.** Tonight's meeting is not the end of the process. The board holds another workshop on **March 30**. The budget goes to the City Council on **April 7**, with a council workshop on **April 14**, a council vote on **May 5**, and a **public referendum on June 9**. Residents who want to weigh in before the public vote have multiple opportunities — but the decisions shaped in these March workshops will be hard to reverse by the time June arrives.
