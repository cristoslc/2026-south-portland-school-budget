# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

Looking back at this December 1 inauguration, it reads now like the last quiet moment before the storm. I watched the new mayor take her oath, heard her big speech, and the warning signs were right there if I'd known to look for them.

The outgoing councilor Cohen said the quiet part out loud: without businesses anchoring the commercial tax base, property taxes would be going "through the roof even more than they already are." He was being diplomatic. What I know now -- and didn't know then -- is that the roof was already on fire. One week later, the school board would reveal a $1.9 million deficit and an emergency spending freeze. By December 17th, the real number was a $7.2 million structural gap requiring either an 18-19% tax increase or 78 job cuts. Cohen's warning wasn't a hypothetical. It was already happening.

And here was the new mayor, about to inherit all of that, talking about transforming the old school building into a "Mahoney city center," building more housing, modernizing public safety facilities. Ambitious stuff. What I didn't hear once -- not in the inauguration, not in the address -- was any mention of property tax limits, spending caps, or what any of this would cost homeowners already getting squeezed. She used the word "bold" like it was a selling point. To me, bold means expensive.

I'll give her this much: she acknowledged businesses matter because they strengthen the tax base. That's basic common sense and I'm glad she said it. But in the same speech she's describing "generational investments" without any acknowledgment that homeowners on fixed incomes are already at the breaking point. The problem isn't that she wants good things for South Portland. The problem is that she walked in the door with an expansive vision and no fiscal guardrails, while the school budget crisis was already building in the background.

When the city council pushed back on the school board's asks in February, I called it cautiously optimistic. Looking at this December 1 record now, that pushback was a pleasant deviation from the baseline, not a reliable pattern. The leadership came in spending-forward. Any fiscal restraint they showed later was against the grain of their own stated priorities. I shouldn't assume it holds.
