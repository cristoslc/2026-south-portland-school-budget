# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

She almost missed it. Four hours of meeting, most of it rental assistance and e-bikes and Metro bus schedules, and the thing that matters most arrives in the last ten minutes during a workshop schedule review that barely anyone is still paying attention to. "Future Use of City Hall, Hamlin, & Main Library." Six words in a list. Labeled "on hold." No explanation. No timeline. No one asking what that means.

She has been tracking Hamlin since December 9th. That was the night she first heard it — a school-associated building described as a housing parcel at "prior forums" she hadn't been invited to, mentioned as if it were already common knowledge. She wrote it down then. She has been waiting to see where it surfaced again. Now here it is, on an official workshop schedule alongside city hall and the library, with the city manager confirming in the same breath that design consultants are already retained and presenting on public facilities at *next week's* meeting. And the mayor saying she wants public facilities on the agenda *every single meeting* going forward. That is not the language of preliminary exploration. That is the language of a project already in motion.

She runs the sequence in her head the way she has learned to run it across fifteen weeks of meetings: consultants retained, workshop item scheduled, regular agenda presence demanded by the mayor. She has watched this pattern produce nine reconfiguration scenarios, 72 pink slips, and a councilor using the phrase "closing schools" as casual vocabulary. Hamlin is in the machinery now. The question is what it costs to get it out.

The rest of the night's school content confirms what she already knows but adds one thing she didn't have before: Councilor Scott said out loud, clearly, at the podium, that the approximately 80 immigrant families potentially displaced by ICE enforcement represent "eighty students who may not be in that school system next year" — and that enrollment loss is a bigger financial burden than the $100,000 rental assistance program he was defending. Someone in an official position has finally named the connection she identified in February: the enrollment-decline number driving the reconfiguration case is being produced, in part, by the same federal enforcement activity that is making it unsafe for parents to attend the forums where they're supposed to respond to that case. Nobody followed up on that. Nobody connected it back to the nine scenarios. But it is now on the record, and she has the timestamp.

DeAngelis's clarification that there was no ICE activity inside any South Portland school is something Rachel registers as important and real. "Near the driveway" is still close enough to terrify a child into not going to class, but she trusts DeAngelis on the specifics here — and the specific matters if parents are making decisions about attendance based on fear that is running ahead of the facts on the ground.
