# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

Ben is watching this meeting from the other side of March 19, which means he already knows the ending: the $100K passes 6-1, Matthews is the lone no, and she cites school pink slips. What March 10 gives him is the origin of that vote — the deliberative record of a council trying to figure out where it stands between two legitimate crises landing on the same property tax bill.

The three letters Margot Kralik reads into the record are what he's been missing since January. Not statistics. Not community members speaking in general terms about fear. A man who fell in a freezing cell because the detention shoes didn't fit, lost his job, can't pay rent, faces eviction. A mother keeping her children home because she heard ICE was near the school driveway. A woman who drained the rent account for a lawyer when her husband was taken on his way to work. Ben has had the structural story for months; now he has the faces.

But the GA revelation is the one that stops him. He'd been tracking the city's fund balance against the school's depleted balance since February, knowing the city held $8.5M while the school had $739K. What he did not know was that the state of Maine told South Portland that missing work out of fear does not count as "just cause" for General Assistance eligibility. Families who stayed home — who did exactly what every public safety message told them to do — cannot access the emergency program built for precisely this kind of income shock. The city manager confirmed it on the record. This is not a city failure Ben can put on the council; this is a state-level policy choice, hidden in an administrative ruling, that compounds every other failure he has been covering since December.

Councilor Scott hands him the connective sentence he's been assembling since February 17. Eighty families at risk equals roughly eighty students the system could lose. At $26,651 per pupil, the enrollment math produces a school revenue loss far larger than $100K. Ben has been carrying this argument as inference for six weeks. Scott puts it in a council chamber. Now Ben can quote it rather than assert it.

The bike share vote is the detail that rounds the night out. The council declines $100K in state funds requiring only a $20K match, then spends two more hours debating whether to commit $100K from its own depleted reserves. Neither decision is wrong on its face. Together they're the picture of a city that has run out of margin for anything — including opportunities.
