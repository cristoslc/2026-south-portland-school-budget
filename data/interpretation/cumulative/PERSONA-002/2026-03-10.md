# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

The Metro update is the exception that proves the rule I have been documenting since December. They come in with 201K rides in 2025, an on-time performance trajectory toward their stated 90% goal, a clear cost-allocation formula tied to route mileage — South Portland hosts roughly 28% of the Scarborough pilot route, so roughly 28% of any local assessment flows here — and a two-year pilot structure where continuation requires both data and council approval. That is how you present to a governing body. That is the quarterly accountability loop West was pushing for back in December, actually functioning. I have a number I can put in a spreadsheet and a decision rule I can evaluate. The contrast with every budget presentation I sat through between December and February is not subtle.

The rental assistance discussion is harder. The GA director confirmed something real: state instruction explicitly excludes immigration enforcement fear from "just cause" under GA work requirements, so residents who temporarily lost wage income may be ineligible for standard assistance through no fault of the program's design. That is not a talking point — it is a documented structural gap, and it explains the fund rationale more clearly than anything said at the February 17 meeting when the moratorium failed without an alternative. But the $168K figure that entered the room as the established need estimate left it as something the city manager himself couldn't verify — he didn't know mid-discussion whether it covered families already assisted or families still waiting. The reimbursement-basis structure through Project HOME provides accountability the raw figure didn't, and landing near $100K is defensible. But a data-driven process does not start with an ambiguous denominator.

Councilor West's bike share research is the second clean decision of the evening: Boise, Baltimore, and Oakland charge operators $70K–$100K annually plus per-ride fees. South Portland was being asked to pay $3,000 per bicycle. That is a density problem expressed as a cost structure, not a policy disagreement, and the council read it correctly.

What I keep returning to is the backdrop. Three budget workshops start April 14. The school structural gap has not closed. Seventy-eight positions are still on the table. This meeting ran three and a half hours without touching any of it. I understand that transit operations, rental relief, and bicycle policy are all legitimate agenda items. But I have watched this council process $100M+ in capital obligations as disconnected line items, adjourn without budget ceilings, and now spend a full evening on policy questions while the fiscal reckoning is five weeks out. The discipline I need to see in April does not yet have evidence behind it.
