# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

I've been watching city council meetings for fifteen weeks waiting for a moment when anyone in that chamber asks what South Portland will look like for families arriving in a few years. March 5 is not that meeting. It's the opposite.

The library item is the one that hit me hardest, and I didn't expect it to. I hadn't really been tracking the library as part of this crisis. But Kevin the library director stood up and described a building with asbestos flooring and failing electrical systems that 27 people walk through every single hour it's open -- 15,000 program participants a year, a lot of them young families with kids like Mia. And he'd been waiting since 2008 for a renovation. Then the council removed the library from the Mahoney project entirely, with no alternative plan, no timeline, nothing. The pre-K spaces and early childhood programming that were part of the library renovation -- designed for exactly the age my daughter is right now -- just got shelved with no replacement. I've watched the school district's pre-K expansion become one of the most durable positive threads in this entire saga because it has state mandates and physical space requirements behind it. The library's equivalent programming has none of that protection, and the council just demonstrated it.

This is where the Mahoney thread I've been following since December 1 finally lands for me personally. Mayor Tipton's inauguration vision of a vibrant city center, the four-hour January workshop, the January 27 revised scenarios -- all of that led here: a police and fire facility complex that might get built, a library that might wait another decade, and a council debating it all against the backdrop of a school budget that someone mentioned needs $7-8 million cut while also generating a 6% tax increase. The numbers are all competing for the same pool of money, and the family-serving infrastructure keeps losing to the infrastructure families never directly interact with.

I already identified the triple tax stack in January -- school increase, Mahoney bond, sewer rates. March 5 adds a fourth layer I hadn't counted: the deferred cost of library infrastructure that won't go away just because it got removed from the project scope. Somebody is going to pay for that asbestos flooring eventually. The only question is when, and who's still here when the bill arrives.

Nobody in that room was asking what South Portland looks like for families entering the system in two or three years. They were managing the current problem. That has been true of every city council meeting I've watched, and March 5 confirms it isn't changing.
