# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- March 9, 2026 -- 2026-03-09

### Interpretation

After last week's marathon budget workshop, this regular meeting felt almost routine -- and that itself is telling. We're in the calm between storms, with three more budget workshops scheduled for March and the city council workshop on April 14th. The intensive deliberation calendar is actually one of the things I'm most proud of right now. No one will be able to say we rushed the $7.2M in cuts. We're creating the procedural space for these decisions to be made carefully, even if the outcomes will still be painful.

The policy work was a genuine bright spot. BEDH -- the public participation policy -- finally passed its second reading after months of tweaking. Given what we saw at Budget Workshop I, where public comment ran for three hours, having clearer ground rules for managing these sessions is going to be essential. The temperature standards policy clearing first read was more routine but still represents the kind of governance housekeeping that keeps the system functioning even when all the attention is on the budget.

What caught my eye first in the superintendent's report was the special education resignations. Abigail Mingle from Brown's functional life skills program, effective this Friday, and Sherry Beland from Pre-K. We're hemorrhaging experienced special ed staff at exactly the moment we're taking on 80-90 more Pre-K special needs students next fall under the expanded mandate. I keep running the numbers: we're cutting 78 positions district-wide but still have to serve every mandated student. The tension between budget cuts and mandated service obligations is going to be the next crisis if we're not careful.

The enrollment bump of 6 students to 2,754 is worth noting -- it continues a modest upward trend from February -- but it's a drop in the bucket compared to the structural deficit. Every student matters for per-pupil funding, but +6 doesn't move the fiscal needle when you're staring at a $7.2M gap. The field trip approvals for All-State music and robotics were small but symbolic: we're still supporting academic and extracurricular excellence even as we prepare to cut deeply. That continuity matters for morale and for the validation narrative.
