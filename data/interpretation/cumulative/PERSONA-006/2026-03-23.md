# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Five months in and I finally got what I asked for at the very first meeting I attended back in December: a finance person who would just say what happened and why. Seventh one in six years, she told the board that herself, and she was blunt about what that revolving door produced: no way the books could have been in order, no way anyone could have better planned. But more importantly, she named the mechanism. COVID came, enrollment jumped, they hired. Federal relief money flowed in and masked the structural mismatch. When both reversed — enrollment down 300 kids, federal money gone — nothing in the district's policy structure forced a correction. No minimum fund balance threshold. No floor that would have triggered a staffing reckoning before the savings account was empty. She said it plainly: if that policy had existed, "it would've been far less painful than where we are today." That's the whole winter in one sentence. Not just individual failures, but a policy void that made the crisis inevitable once the COVID money ran out.

The $257 figure is what I put on my refrigerator. That's what 6% actually costs me on a home like mine. I was braced for worse. But here's what she also put on the table, and this is the part that keeps me up: by contract, labor costs go up more than 6% per year automatically — step and lane adjustments, every year, for everyone who stays. Personnel is essentially the whole budget. She said "mathematically impossible not to have a problem" year after year at 6%. The city council handed this board a 6% ceiling in February. The labor contracts already break that ceiling for FY28 before FY27 is even voted on. She called this year a credit card payoff. I believe her. But paying off the card doesn't change the spending habits, and next year the athletic field bond starts costing $300,000 in debt service. The Skillen boiler is a wildcard. Utilities are running up 13–14% per year. And if there's a bad winter, the answer from the dais was: borrow from the city's fund balance, pay it back by raising taxes the following year. That's the plan. No cushion, no margin, no floor.

And then nearly midnight — the support staff union president. Every lunch aide cut, minimum wage, ten hours a week. Administrator count barely moved. She said it out loud: "our schools can function with fewer administrators." I've been thinking that since December. This is the first time someone inside the district said it to the board's face. The board nodded. Then they adjourned. Five hours. No vote.
