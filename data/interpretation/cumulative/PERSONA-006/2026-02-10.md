# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: Joint Budget Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

This was the meeting I was waiting for -- the city council getting to weigh in on what the school board can actually spend. Three and a half hours, but at least most of the councilors seem to understand that regular folks can't afford these crazy increases. The school people came in with their same deck -- 19% status quo increase, 300 fewer students, 82 more staff -- and presented three options ranging from 6% to nearly 10%. Most councilors told them that's too much and pushed guidance into the 3-6% range. That's the first time someone with actual budget authority has stood up for taxpayers.

The most telling moment was when councilors asked the school administration to explain what those 82 new positions are and why they weren't adjusted sooner. The answer? Vague hand-waving about "complexity of students" and a promise to provide data later. They can't account for their own hiring and they want us to trust them with a bigger budget? A taxpayer in the audience nailed it when he pointed out that his property assessment went up $27,000 -- mine went up about the same. We're getting hammered from both sides: higher assessments AND higher rates.

The city budget has its own pressures too -- health insurance up 10%, solid waste costs rising, water rates up 6.8%, and they need to permanently fund two positions that were on ARPA money. It's not just schools; the whole tax bill is climbing faster than my income. But at least the council pushed back instead of rubber-stamping the school's ask. The fund balance is down to $739,000 with $850,000 budgeted this year, which means it could go negative and require a city bailout. Nobody said that out loud but the math is right there.
