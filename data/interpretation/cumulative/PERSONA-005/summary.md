# Cumulative Summary: Priya (PERSONA-005)

### Current Understanding

South Portland's 2026 budget crisis is a multi-layered structural failure operating at the intersection of state underfunding, local political choices, and internal management breakdown. The district faces a $7.2 million structural gap requiring elimination of 78 positions — 42 teachers, 16 education technicians, 14 facilities and food service workers, and only 2 administrators — against a backdrop where Maine covers just 20% of actual education costs versus its 55% statutory obligation. The district's own TIF portfolio compounds this by sheltering assessed value from state funding formulas, suppressing the aid it would otherwise receive. Internal management instability — six finance directors in five years — produced a repeat material weakness finding and $400,000 in mismanaged grants designated specifically for English learners and students with disabilities.

The demographic reality is now documented in hard numbers: Skillin Elementary serves 60% BIPOC students, 54% free/reduced lunch, and 33% multilingual learners; Small School serves fewer than 20% BIPOC. The administration recommended closing Skillin under equity language — the most egregious equity-washing of the entire season. Four board members rejected it by naming the racial politics directly, marking the first instance in this process where equity analysis prevailed in a structural decision. The community school model has been approved district-wide and immigration protection resolutions are on the books. But non-biased hiring training has been postponed twice, the Human Rights Commission operates on $2,500, and the eviction moratorium failed 4-2 — with the dissenting majority not merely ignoring equity arguments but actively inverting them. Every equity-framed proposal from either governing body now requires active scrutiny of who bears implementation costs before Priya extends any credence to it.
