# Cumulative Summary: Jess (PERSONA-003)

### Current Understanding

As of March 19, Jess understands the South Portland school budget crisis as having moved from deliberation into execution. The deficit has grown to $8.4 million — up from the $7.2 million that defined every prior conversation since December — and 72 layoff notices were delivered on March 18, before the April 7 public hearing, before the May 5 vote, before the June 9 referendum. The process she has been tracking for fifteen weeks was running alongside a faster, quieter administrative one she couldn't see.

The reconfiguration plan — nine specific scenarios, with grade-band schools (pre-K through first grade in two buildings, grades two through four in three buildings) as the administration's preferred direction — has moved from concept to formal development. Buildings are not closing because pre-K and special education mandates require the space. State law requires the district to serve 80–90 four-year-olds by September 2026, expanding capacity from 64 to 128 seats. That mandate-driven pre-K expansion is the most structurally durable positive thread across all nineteen meetings and remains Jess's primary anchor for staying in South Portland.

The financial management picture is darker than enrollment numbers alone explain: six finance directors in five years, recurring audit failures, and two consecutive years exceeding the legally adopted budget compound the structural crisis caused by state underfunding and enrollment decline. The 82 positions added since 2017 were mandate-driven, not discretionary — meaning proposed cuts reach into legally required services. The city-school governance divide operates at every level with no body presenting the triple tax stack (school budget increase, Mahoney bond, sewer rates now confirmed at 22% annually for three years) as a combined household number. Civic participation is structurally inaccessible through cost, scheduling, and — for some community members — fear of federal enforcement. Jess has decided to stay in South Portland and evaluate, but redistricting details remain unavailable and she is acutely uncertain whether showing up at April hearings can change anything already in motion.
