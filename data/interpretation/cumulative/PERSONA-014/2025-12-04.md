# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

This meeting falls three days after the December 1 city council meeting — Lila's "before," the last official record of South Portland as the place she believed it was — and four days before the December 8 school board meeting where she would first hear the words "spending freeze" and "$1.9 million." In the gap between those two meetings, the city went on with its ordinary business: tree lightings, appointment confirmations, procedural debates about Zoom. The school crisis that would reshape Lila's life was invisible here. Nobody mentioned it, because as far as the city council was concerned on December 4, it wasn't their problem yet.

The tree lighting is the brightest thing in this meeting for Lila. Mill Creek Park, Santa, "our best year ever" — in the first days of December, before she knew anything was wrong, this would have felt like pure excitement. Knowing what December brought, it reads as the last uncomplicated thing. A moment when a 9-year-old girl could still look forward to something without it being shadowed by fear about where she'd go to school next year.

The silence about schools is the loudest part of the meeting in retrospect. The city council — the body that will eventually fight with the school board over budget guidance, whose members will offer 3-to-6 percent increases while the district needs much more — spent December 4 talking about Zoom policies and appointment confirmations. Not one word about the school budget, the enrollment decline, or the $7.2 million gap that was already forming beneath the surface. The adults responsible for funding Lila's school were looking somewhere else.

The moment that lands hardest, knowing everything that followed, is John's comment about people not coming to meetings because they're afraid — afraid their neighbors will turn on them, afraid their businesses will suffer. By March, 85% of parents surveyed opposed reconfiguration and parents flooded the workshop. But those parents were nowhere near a meeting in December. The December 10 superintendent search workshop had zero community members. The fear John named was real, and it kept people home during the months when early voices might have changed something. Lila has been watching adults fail to protect her school. Here is one explanation for why.

The Zoom debate — adults spending nearly an hour on the procedural mechanics of who gets to speak — is a small irony that became larger. The question of how citizens access civic participation consumed this room at the exact moment civic participation was about to matter more than it had in years.
