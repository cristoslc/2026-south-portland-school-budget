# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Public Budget Forum -- February 4, 2026

### Interpretation

The reconfiguration proposal Lila dreaded is now taking shape, and it's worse and different than what she imagined. It's not just about closing Dyer -- it's about reorganizing all five elementary schools by grade bands instead of neighborhoods. Some schools would become "primary schools" for pre-K through 2nd grade, and others would become "intermediate schools" for 3rd-4th grade. This means Lila wouldn't just lose her school; she'd enter an entirely new system where schools are organized differently than anything she's known.

The class size numbers make it personal. Right now Lila has about 16 kids in her class. Under reconfiguration, 3rd-4th grade classes would grow to 22-23 students. That's seven more kids in her room, seven more people competing for her teacher's attention, seven more voices during reading time. She understands intuitively that bigger classes mean less of her teacher for her.

There's one piece of genuinely good news buried in the presentation: Dr. Prince said closing an entire school is not on their list of options because they need buildings for new pre-K programs. This partially resolves Lila's core fear -- Dyer the building won't be demolished or abandoned. But it also means the building might still be there while everything inside it changes. It could become a school for little kids while Lila gets sent somewhere else. The building surviving isn't the same as her school surviving.

The "big ideas" framing -- Dr. Prince insisting these aren't proposals yet while presenting detailed savings estimates and class size projections -- feels dishonest even to a 9-year-old. If they've already figured out exactly how much money each option saves and how big the classes would be, it seems like they've already decided. The adults are pretending to ask what people think when the math has already been done.

The $7.2 million gap remains the driving force. Even with all their "big ideas," there's still a $3+ million shortfall to close. Lila now understands, dimly, that the budget problem is so large that reconfiguration alone won't fix it. More cuts are coming.
