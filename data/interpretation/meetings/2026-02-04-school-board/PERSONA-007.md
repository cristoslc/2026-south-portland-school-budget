### Reactions

Well, that was both better and worse than I expected. Better because the process feels solid - Johanna presented the numbers clearly, the three big ideas are reasonable starting points, and the community engagement format actually worked. People were asking the right technical questions about class sizes and busing costs, which tells me they're taking this seriously.

But God, that health insurance jump from 8% to 12% without any board discussion really bothers me. I get that Abigail is hearing signals from other districts, but that's a $400K+ swing in our budget assumptions and we should have had that conversation in December or January. Now we're locked into a higher number right as we're making final cuts. And losing the middle school consolidation option because of DOT timelines? That's the kind of external constraint that keeps me up at night - we can work as hard as we want but we can't control the state bureaucracy.

The elephant in the room is that even with all three big ideas, we're still looking at $3M+ in cuts we haven't identified yet. Everyone seemed to gloss over that green slice of the pie, but that's where the real pain is going to come from. Central office, supply budgets, contracts - those are all politically harder sells than reconfiguration. And Joe's point about community composition was spot-on. This room was full of educators and involved parents, but where are the taxpayers who don't have kids in school? They're the ones who could sink this at validation if they feel blindsided by the process.
