# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Five hours of presentation data and public comment, and the board went home without voting on any of it. The council deadline is April 7. The next scheduled meeting is March 30. Maria got home before midnight, put the dates in every parent channel she runs, and could not stop thinking about one slide.

The behavioral strategist cut is the one that won't let her sleep. One position — the only one in the district whose full-time job is helping regular education teachers manage student behavior — proposed for elimination, with the parenthetical confirming exactly what that means: "none full time" remaining. She has been tracking the support infrastructure since December 17, when she first saw the 16 ed techs on the elimination list and understood that these are not interchangeable with teachers. Ed techs, MTSS specialists, behavioral strategist — each one does something specific that cannot be absorbed by asking teachers to stretch further. And the board is doing all three simultaneously: eliminating the behavioral strategist, cutting four MTSS positions (two math, two literacy), and confirming that class sizes will increase in both Option A and Option B as Kayler's students redistribute across four remaining buildings. These are not parallel cuts. They are interlocking — the disruption the reconfiguration creates removes the people who exist to manage exactly that disruption.

Kayler's closure recommendation gives the crisis a specific address. Maria has been calculating which option touches her child's school since March 2; now she has a partial answer. The rationale is at least legible — dead-end street, limited egress, smaller displaced population than Dyer. But what Option A versus Option B means for her daughter's specific school, in specific terms, is still unknowable, and the district has said plainly it will not model transportation routes until after the board votes. The multi-grade family problem is the detail she cannot stop calculating. Option A puts a kindergartner and a third grader at different buildings with conflicting arrival times. A woman named Samantha described her family facing three different elementary schools and a daycare with the same arrival time. Maria knows families exactly like that.

She has been here before — every forum announced as a genuine opportunity, every deadline compressed until meaningful response was impossible. She is not waiting anymore. The vote is six days away and the window is closing.
