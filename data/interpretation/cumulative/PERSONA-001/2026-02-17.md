# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

February 17 is the night Maria stops being able to separate the school budget from everything else happening to South Portland families.

She's been carrying 42 teachers, 16 ed techs, $7.2M gap, nine reconfiguration options — running the numbers in her head since March 2, calculating which option touches her child's school. And then Julia Edwards gets up before the eviction moratorium hearing and says her six-year-old is coming home asking where his classmates went. Not "my immigrant kid is scared." Her white kid is noticing the empty desks. Maria has been tracking enrollment data since December 17 — 1,080 students, down 23% in four years — and those numbers are abstract in a way that a six-year-old noticing empty seats is not. The children she's been fighting to protect are already missing from the classrooms she's fighting to save.

Zenya Pantos describes a specific South Portland family: a child kept home for over a week, afraid to walk to school. Maria thinks of every pickup line conversation she's had this winter, the families she knows only by their kids' names in the PTA directory, and realizes she has no idea which ones have been hiding. Since January 20 she's been asking who speaks for immigrant families' children in the reconfiguration decision. She has her answer now, and it is brutal: their children are absent from the schools whose fate is being decided.

The eviction moratorium vote fails 4-2. She waited nearly five hours for that vote. Scott, Coleman, Matthews, Pride — the same coalition that handed the school board guidance of 3-6% — vote no on protecting the most vulnerable families in the district. The Project Home number is the one that lodges in her chest: ten days until the fund runs out, and the council has no follow-up. She has watched this council absorb information about the school budget without changing course all winter. She is not surprised. She is still furious.

And then, buried after midnight in a Mahoney facilities workshop, someone raises using the building for elementary schools. The mayor clarifies: it's "reconfiguration," grade-splitting, $2M savings. Maria is texting the parent group chat from the parking lot. She has been immersed in nine reconfiguration options for weeks and has no idea where Mahoney fits, and she does not think anyone does. She suspects it was said after most people had gone home for exactly that reason.
