# Cumulative Summary: Tom (PERSONA-006)

### Current Understanding

Tom is a South Portland homeowner on or near a fixed income who has tracked the budget crisis from the district's first public deficit reveal in December 2025 through the March 23 budget workshop. His current picture: the school district carries an $8.4M structural deficit — up from an initial $1.9M reveal — rooted in COVID-era over-hiring masked by federal relief funds, a 23% elementary enrollment drop (1,401 to 1,080 students) during which staffing grew by 82 positions, and the complete absence of a minimum fund balance threshold policy that would have triggered corrective action. The state covers only 20% of costs against a 55% statutory obligation; per-pupil spending of $26,651 is the highest among comparable districts. The 6% tax increase ceiling (~$257/year on the average home) may hold for FY27 — the most restrained budget in five years — but is structurally broken for FY28 because labor contracts automatically increase costs beyond 6% annually.

Three simultaneous capital obligations compound the school pressure: an $87M police/fire bond targeting a November 2026 referendum; a Mahoney City Center bond deferred to 2027 (estimated $70–76M stripped down, with no binding council cost ceiling committed); and Pearl Street sewer upgrades structured as revenue bonds driving ~22%/year sewer rate increases for three consecutive years, requiring no voter approval. No public body has produced a combined per-household annual cost across all four pressures.

The city council has been an inconsistent fiscal backstop — genuine 3-6% guidance at the February joint workshop and a 4-2 eviction moratorium defeat stand against repeated failures to set binding capital cost ceilings and two consecutive weeks of reserve fund drawdowns while 72 school employees carried layoff notices. The 170 Ocean Street TIF returns 50% of new property taxes to the developer until approximately 2055. Tom sees the dysfunction as systemic across both the school district and the municipal apparatus: the city's own admission of no capital planning until five years ago produced simultaneous infrastructure failures now queuing for bond financing alongside the school crisis.
