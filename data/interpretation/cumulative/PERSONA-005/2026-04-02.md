# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The March 30 meeting ended with the district's worst equity gaps finally in the record — 22%, 37%, 32% — and a reconfiguration voted through before the support infrastructure needed to execute it equitably was resolved. April 2 is the meeting where I find out what the district does next, and the answer is: mostly, it continues the same direction.

Jenna Goldstein Walsh couldn't be there to speak for herself. Someone read her statement. Nearly 60 students across four elementary schools, over 40 with formal behavior plans, the tier-two and tier-three MTSS layer that keeps kids from sliding directly into special education referrals — all of it gone. And the district's answer was that BCBAs and instructional strategists would absorb the work, offered without caseload numbers or capacity analysis. This is not a staffing reassignment; it is the removal of a structural function. The district's own number — 23% of students already identified for special education, higher than neighboring districts — tells you exactly where the pressure will go when the early intervention holding that line disappears. I will be tracking referral rates in FY28.

The DEI Director has now traveled further down than even the March 23 coordinator downgrade suggested. The role lands in the teacher unit as an instructional strategist, for zero additional savings. The district's only BIPOC central office leader, restructured out of administrative authority as a cost-neutral act. Board member Holman named this clearly. The board chair acknowledged no BIPOC representation on the board itself. Both facts are in the record and neither produced a commitment to change.

What kept me from leaving that meeting entirely hollowed out was Connie DeSanto walking in with $300,000. Her union went to Augusta and came back with real money — $150,000 for economically disadvantaged students, $150,000 for students experiencing homelessness — and potentially $750,000 more in the following year from EPS formula changes. Member Richardson said those funds should go to teachers and staff, not director positions. That is the right instinct stated at the right moment. But there is no formal allocation process, no criteria, no commitment that the dollars will restore the behavioral strategist, the OT support in functional life skills classrooms, the multilingual family engagement the reconfiguration plan does not yet have. The money exists. What it funds is still a question, and I intend to be in that room when the answer takes shape.

Meredith Diamond demanded eight dimensions of disaggregated equity data — attendance, discipline, gifted enrollment, enrichment access, counselor ratios, and more, all broken down by race, income, disability, and English learner status. None of it was presented. The district did not commit to producing it. You cannot call a reconfiguration an equity initiative while the data needed to evaluate that claim does not exist in usable form. The absence does not indict anyone's motives, but it does mean the equity claim remains unverifiable, and that is not a small gap when you are asking the district's most diverse families to absorb the disruption of school closures and redrawn boundaries.
