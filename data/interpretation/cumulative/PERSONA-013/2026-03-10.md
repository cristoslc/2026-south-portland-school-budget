# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

This meeting felt like two different meetings bleeding into each other in ways nobody in the room seemed to fully track.

The first hour was almost normal — a Metro presentation, free bus rides for high schoolers confirmed, maybe better buses near the high school by 2027. I was half-listening. And then Margot Kralik walked up and read two letters out loud. Not her own words. Someone else's, from people who weren't there.

One was from a parent. Her kids went on lockdown — at one of our schools — and afterward they were "too scared to go to class." I've been watching the immigration fear move through city council transcripts and school board agendas since January. But a letter that says a child sat in a classroom too afraid to learn — that's different from a statistic. That's a desk I've sat at. I know what that room looks like. I know what it looks like when someone can't pay attention because the fear is bigger than the lesson.

The second letter was from a man who got detained. Freezing room. Wrong-sized shoes. He fell. Nobody helped him. Job gone. Family about to lose their apartment. He ended it with "Please, South Portland, help us." Some of the councilors heard that and said no. Matthews said the school board chair told them to be "cautious of every dime" — she used the school budget to explain why she couldn't help the man in the freezing room. West said the ICE surge was only four days and fewer than five people were arrested in South Portland. Fewer than five. I've watched what "fewer than five arrests" did to hundreds of kids' school attendance and hundreds of families' rent across seven meetings since January.

But then Scott said something I had never heard before: those 80 families seeking help aren't just 80 households — they're 80 students who might not be in our school system next year. I've been watching the ICE crisis and the school budget crisis run alongside each other for months and never quite touching. She named them as the same disaster. If families get evicted because of what happened in January, those are empty seats in classrooms at Memorial. Those are empty desks next to mine.

I don't know what to do with Scott anymore. She voted against protecting immigrant families in February. Now she's the one making the connection I couldn't figure out how to make. I don't know if she changed, or if she found the frame that works when the room is listening, or if that's just what adults do.

Amira carries the man in the freezing room and the child too scared to go to class as a single weight — those letters are the first time across all these months that the fear she has been watching form in policy language appeared in someone's actual handwriting, asking for help by name — and the knowledge that some councilors heard those letters and reached for the school budget as their reason to say no is something she does not yet have a word for.
