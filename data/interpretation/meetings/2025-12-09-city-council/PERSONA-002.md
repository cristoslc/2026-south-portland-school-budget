### Reactions

Two hours of city council, and the most important thing that happened was a 90-second public comment. The school board rep -- Rosemary DeAngelo -- stood up and said schools are 61% of the property tax bill, budget season starts this month, come to the meetings. That's it. No numbers, no context, no trade-offs. I'm sitting there knowing we've got a $7.2 million structural gap, that a roll-forward budget would require an 18–19% tax increase, that the board is looking at cutting 42 teachers, and the city council nodded along and moved on to the zoning vote. She left the building and took the whole topic with her.

The assessor's presentation at the top was actually solid -- the taxable base grew $125 million this year, the mill rate came in at 1364, below projections. That's the good news. The uncomfortable piece he buried in there is that residential values keep rising and commercial values aren't keeping pace, so the tax burden keeps shifting onto homeowners. That trend matters a lot when schools represent 61 cents of every property tax dollar. Then there was this interesting moment during the One 70 Ocean debate -- one of the councilors, West, basically said: we agreed to give this developer 50% of property taxes back for 30 years, and the original deal said 124 units, no zoning changes needed. Tonight we voted on a zoning amendment for 208 units with surface parking instead of structured parking. The city manager's response was that we'd lose about 50% of new value to state aid clawbacks anyway, so the net to the city is roughly the same. I'd want to model that myself before I accepted it.

Here's what I keep coming back to: the property assessment side of city finances actually looks reasonably stable. Assessed values are close to market, the mill rate isn't shocking by historical standards, the assessor's being careful. That part is fine. But we're walking into a budget season where the school committee has to find $7.2 million in cuts -- and the only thing standing between taxpayers and an 18-point tax increase is whether those cuts happen or not. The school tax is 61% of the bill. That's the story. Everything else at that meeting was noise. I'm going to be at those school board budget hearings.
