# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

Jaylen watched three and a half hours of a budget workshop that was almost entirely about elementary schools -- buildings he has never attended, communities that do not overlap with his daily life. Five elementary principals presented enrollment data and demographics. Parents lined up at the microphone in tears about their kindergartners. Nine different reconfiguration scenarios were modeled. And through all of it, the high school was barely mentioned. The superintendent stated that middle school and high school workshops would come later in March, which means the information Jaylen needs to plan his senior year is still weeks away while decisions about elementary schools accelerate.

The math is becoming clearer and more alarming. Elementary reconfiguration saves $1.5-2.2M. Operational efficiencies and schedule changes add roughly $2M more. That still leaves a $3M+ gap that will be addressed through "line-by-line budget review across all cost centers." Those cost centers include the high school. The cuts Jaylen fears -- to AP classes, theater, coaching positions -- are buried in that unnamed residual gap. Nobody has disclosed them because they have not been decided yet, but they are inevitable.

The athletic complex's first debt payment of $99,000 now sits in the budget alongside the teacher cuts. The district invested in sports facilities and is now paying for them while reducing the teaching staff. For Jaylen, the irony extends to his displaced graduation: the construction that blocks the stadium ceremony is the same facility whose debt service competes with teacher salaries.

Chair DeAngelis's statement that 80% of constituents do not have children in the schools reframes the political dynamics. The budget is not just a school board decision -- it is a referendum question that will be decided mostly by people who do not experience the consequences. Jaylen has no vote, and the majority of people who do vote have no direct stake in whether his AP Lit class exists. This is a new dimension of powerlessness: not just being excluded from the process as a student, but understanding that even the democratic mechanism (the referendum) is dominated by non-parents.

The one continuity of hope is the student representative role in the superintendent search, confirmed again as proceeding. Jaylen is considering trying to participate, recognizing that "somebody should be in the room asking what all this means for actual students."
