# General Budget Briefing
## Current Budget Picture

## Budget Snapshot

South Portland Schools is in active budget workshops for fiscal year 2027 (FY27), which covers the school year beginning in fall 2026. The superintendent's proposed budget stands at **$75,600,000**, up from $73,131,556 in FY26. The increase comes primarily from rising costs in special education ($15.0M to $16.4M), facilities ($6.2M to $7.3M), and transportation ($2.8M to $3.3M) — areas where spending is largely driven by enrollment needs and contractual obligations.

To bring the budget to a level the district considers financially sustainable, the superintendent is proposing significant cuts alongside those cost increases. The core elements of the proposal are:

- **A 12% reduction in staff** across the district
- **Closure of one elementary school** for the 2026-27 school year
- **Reductions at the middle school and high school level**
- **Non-personnel reductions of approximately $703,000**, covering building supplies, software, hardware, and other operating costs
- **Additional reductions of approximately $808,000**, including a reduced nutrition fund transfer, removal of part-time positions, and deferred obligations

As of March 18, 2026 — the day before the March 19 City Council meeting — 72 school department employees had received layoff notices. This was reported by Councilor Matthews at that meeting. The budget is being workshopped jointly by the school board and, later, the full city council, with a public vote scheduled for June 9.

At the March 19 City Council meeting, Councilor Matthews characterized the school department as facing an **$8.4 million deficit**. That figure was stated in council discussion but does not appear as a defined line item in the March 23 budget workshop slides. The precise definition of that gap — whether it represents the difference between uncut budget requests and available revenue, or something else — is not specified in the available source documents.

## What Changed

The March 23, 2026 board workshop represents the clearest consolidated view of the superintendent's proposal to date. Several things are new or clarified compared to earlier discussions:

**Elementary closure is now a formal recommendation.** The superintendent's budget includes a specific recommendation to close one elementary school. Two options are being presented to the board at the March 23 workshop. Notably, the proposal does *not* recommend immediately decommissioning the closed building. Instead, the district would consider leasing the vacant building for up to four years before any permanent decision, potentially generating some revenue during that period.

**The cost of not closing a school is now quantified.** If the board does not proceed with an elementary closure, the slides state that an additional 12 to 16 positions across the district would need to be eliminated to produce a balanced budget.

**PreK structure is settled for FY27.** The proposal maintains four locally funded pre-K classrooms, expands special-purpose pre-K using state CDS funding, and adds a Head Start partnership classroom — all without additional local dollars.

**The tax impact is not yet final.** Slide 5 of the March 23 presentation notes that administration was meeting with the city's Finance Department on the Monday following the workshop, with finalized tax impact numbers to be posted that day. The tax impact number was not included in the slides available for this summary.

## Major Decisions Ahead

**Which elementary school to close.** The board is reviewing two specific options at the March 23 workshop and is expected to move toward a decision. This is the largest single structural choice in the proposal.

**Whether to proceed with the full 12% staff reduction.** The layoff notices issued on March 18 set a process in motion, but the final personnel decisions depend on what the board approves in the coming weeks.

**City Council vote on the school budget, May 5.** The city council holds a formal vote on the school budget. Budget tensions with city-side spending (including a roughly $51.7 million wastewater infrastructure bond expected in FY27) will likely shape that discussion.

**Public vote on the school budget, June 9.** Residents vote directly on the school budget. This is the final approval step.

**Long-term use of any closed school building.** The district recommends against immediate decommissioning, but the board has not yet made a formal decision about what a lease arrangement would look like or how long it would last.

- What revenue, if any, could the district realistically generate by leasing a closed school building, and on what timeline?
- Will the school board approve the full reduction package at the March 23 or March 30 workshops, or is additional negotiation expected before the April 7 presentation to the city council?
