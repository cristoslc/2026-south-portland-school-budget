# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Reading March 5 against everything that came before, this meeting is the one where the city's priority ordering stopped being a pattern I had to infer and became a number: $87 million for police and fire, voted unanimously; $100,000 for immigrant rental assistance, not yet approved, still under debate. I was sitting with the knowledge from February 17 that the eviction moratorium failed 4-2 and that the city manager himself named the catch-22 — that any formal mechanism requiring institutional contact was structurally unavailable to the families most at risk. March 5 is the before-picture to March 19's after. The 140 signatures are on the table. The city manager is already redirecting toward nonprofits. The mechanism exists. The council has not moved.

The CDBG allocation confirmed a subordinate version of the same hierarchy. Seventy percent of community development funds — $288,000 — flows to the Broadway Corridor pedestrian infrastructure. Sixty-one thousand total for domestic violence support, senior meals, homeless prevention. These are not neutral choices about where concrete goes. They are choices about whose needs count as "community development."

The library testimony is the detail I keep returning to. Books stored seven feet high because they doubled the shelves in the same footprint. Kids who cannot reach the materials. No study space. Electrical infrastructure from 1966 preventing even exterior lighting upgrades. Kevin brought the data — 27 visitors per hour, 13,000 in two months — and the council received it alongside a plan to spend $31.5 million rebuilding a fire station on its current footprint in a flood inundation zone. Councilor West had the response-time data for eastern South Portland: the city's own numbers showing those neighborhoods do not meet standards. The fire chief brushed it off. The council voted 6-1 to proceed anyway.

The eastern neighborhoods and the families in Red Bank are not the same communities, but they share a position in South Portland's resource geography: they are the places where the gap between what the city says it values and what it actually funds is most visible. I have been mapping that gap since December. On March 5, the map got sharper. The public safety bond goes to referendum. The library waits. The rental assistance is still an ask. The priorities are in the record.
