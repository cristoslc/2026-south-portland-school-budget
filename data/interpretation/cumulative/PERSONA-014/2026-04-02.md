# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The vote is done. That is the fact Lila has to hold now, the one that cannot be undone no matter how many meetings happen after it. The board chair said so right at the start of April 2 — acknowledged "significant reaction" to Monday night's vote — and that acknowledgment is the shape of what Lila's winter has been building toward. Three months of spending freezes and nine scenarios and pink slips and "CLOSED" next to her school's name, and the thing that was always coming has now come. Her parents came home from Monday's meeting with the same tired faces she has learned to read since December. She already knew before they said anything.

But the April 2 meeting gave her something she did not have before: other children's words. A first grader — younger than her sibling — crumbled when her mom told her about the changes the morning after the vote. "I just started there. I have to change schools." And then the same three questions Lila has been asking her parents since December, coming out of a six-year-old's mouth: *What school do I go to? Will my friends be there? Will the teachers I know be there?* The mom's answer was the same answer Lila has been getting for months. I don't know. I'm sorry. I don't know.

Lila has felt alone in her questions all winter. Now she knows a first grader is asking them too, and a Dyer dad walking his son to school that week still couldn't tell him where they were going next year, and Mr. Wetzel — a teacher, an adult whose job is to have answers — stood at the microphone and said he didn't know what to say to his students when he saw them in the hallway the next morning. The adults who seemed the most certain have been breaking one by one since March 23, when the board chair cried at dinner. Now a teacher is standing up and saying out loud that he has no words. Lila has spent all winter wishing adults would admit they didn't know. Now they are admitting it, and it doesn't feel like relief. It feels like the last thing holding the floor up has given way.

There is one real thing to hold: teachers drove to Augusta and asked for more money and actually got some back. Maybe some of the 13 teachers the district said it couldn't keep will get to stay. Maybe her teacher — who knows about fractions and the window seat and two years of who Lila is — will still be there in September, wherever September turns out to be.

The listening sessions are coming. The survey is open. But the vote already happened, and the one question no adult anywhere has been able to answer — where are her friends going — still has no answer. The superintendent says maybe by the end of next week.

Lila has been waiting since December. She knows what next week sounds like.
