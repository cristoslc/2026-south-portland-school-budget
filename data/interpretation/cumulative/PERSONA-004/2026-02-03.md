# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

The audit presentation was the piece I'd been missing. Every time I'd sat in a school board meeting listening to finance projections shift — $1.3 million deficit, now $1.9 million, now all scenarios end in the red — I'd chalked it up to bad leadership or bad luck. But February 3 is the night the city auditor walked into the city council chamber and put a name to the chaos: material weakness, repeat finding, 34 audit journal entries, $400,000 in grant funds misallocated. This isn't bad luck. This is a school finance operation that has been breaking down for years, and the city council is only now hearing it said plainly in public.

Six finance directors in five years. Three of them interim. Joe Prince saying it out loud is the first honest administrative accounting I've heard since December, and I almost don't know how to process it. The budgets we've been living inside, the spending freeze, the projections that keep moving — all of it was being managed by a revolving door of finance leadership with no institutional continuity. Of course we exceeded the legally adopted budget. Of course there are 34 entries the auditor had to clean up. The district has been operating without functional financial infrastructure while simultaneously making billion-dollar decisions about whether to close buildings and eliminate 42 teaching positions.

The legally non-compliant budget overspending is the part that should alarm everyone. Breaking state law isn't a procedural footnote — it's a documented pattern of the district not knowing, in real time, where it stands. And the council is hearing this one day before the February 4 budget forum where the administration is supposed to present its plan to close the $7.2 million gap. The same administration whose finance function required 34 corrective journal entries wants credit for its fiscal analysis.

DeAngelis standing up and telling the council they have to take responsibility and provide guidance is right on the substance — 80% salaries means every dollar of cut comes out of a person. But watching her demand accountability from the council the night before a budget forum she's already called "fabulous" in private sits wrong. She's performing fiscal seriousness before an audience that just watched the auditor document her district's repeat accounting failures.

The new finance director may be real improvement. But one competent hire doesn't undo five years of institutional damage. The numbers the board will be voting on this spring were built on that wreckage.
