# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

March 10 is the meeting that did the scene-setting work that March 19 gets the credit for. Reviewing it now, with the 6-1 vote already in her back pocket, Dana can see that the strongest footage from the entire rental assistance arc came out of this room, not the room where the vote happened.

Two community advocates stood at the podium and read written statements from residents who couldn't be there. One described being held in a freezing detention room, falling because ICE issued him shoes that were too big, losing his job, receiving an eviction notice. "Please, South Portland, help us." That is not a quote that needs framing. That is the lede she did not air because nothing was voted on that night, and that is the editorial call she is still second-guessing.

The police chief's "less than five" figure is the detail that sharpens every human-interest story she has been telling since January into something quantifiable. She has spent eight weeks covering canceled forums, midnight back-road drives, a father who stopped going to work, a Human Rights Commission chair carrying his birth certificate. The official arrest count for South Portland city limits is fewer than five people. The city manager says 80 households are on Project HOME's emergency list, each needing roughly $2,100. That ratio — five detentions, eighty households displaced, nearly $170,000 in cascading financial need — is the immigration story's analytical spine, and she did not have the numbers to write it until this meeting.

Councilor Scott handed her the synthesis she has been approaching for months without getting from an elected official. Eighty immigrant families housed equals eighty students enrolled equals per-pupil revenue the district desperately needs. The school budget story and the immigration story have been parallel threads since December — Dana has been watching them run alongside each other, touching at DeAngelo's equity arguments and the canceled January forum. Scott put them in the same sentence for the first time. Dana is calculating whether she can use it in a school segment without the connection feeling forced.

Matthews is doing what Matthews does. No vote on anything school-related tonight, but he names the deficit anyway — school department consuming 62% of the total budget, city "financially in big trouble." She knows now he said essentially the same thing nine days later when he cast the lone no vote on the appropriation itself. March 10 was the rehearsal. He has been running the same line since January 13, and she now has him on tape saying it in three different meetings about three different agenda items. That is no longer a quote — that is a character.

Leah Day goes in the file. Passionate, specific, eleven workers, credible fear of national vendor displacement. The bike share pilot is dead for now, but the frame — local small business versus city infrastructure spending — is going to come back.
