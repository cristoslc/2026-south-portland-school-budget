# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

Watching the city council and school board sit in the same room was a different experience entirely. The school board has been talking to itself for two months; now I'm seeing how the broader political reality constrains what they can do. Most councilors landed between 4% and 6% for tax increases, with one explicitly supporting 6%+ and another saying taxpayers "cannot afford any of your options." So the money question isn't just about the school board's willingness to cut -- it's about whether the council will even let them have the revenue they need. That makes the cuts more certain, not less.

The reconfiguration plan got more specific here. Five elementary schools would become three intermediate schools (grades 2-4) and two primary schools (pre-K-1), saving about $2 million. Chair DeAngelis said something that hit me hard: "we don't have neighborhood schools anymore" because enrollment patterns already mean most kids are bused. If that's true, then my mental image of walking my daughter to the neighborhood school was already outdated before any of this started. That reframes the whole debate -- they're not destroying something that currently works; they're formalizing a reality that already exists. I'm not sure if that makes me feel better or worse.

Pre-K came up again in a way that strengthened my earlier assessment. They're exploring partnerships with Head Start and other providers to expand access without local tax cost, and the special education mandate for 3-5 year olds is a hard requirement. This is becoming one of the most durable threads in the entire budget discussion -- pre-K expansion has state mandates, physical space needs, and partnership funding behind it. It's the one area where I feel genuinely confident things will be better, not worse, when my daughter is ready.

The staffing numbers got important context: the 82 positions added since 2017 while enrollment dropped by 300 were largely driven by special education and multilingual learner needs. That undercuts the simple narrative that the district was recklessly hiring. The cost structure is being driven by mandated services, not optional bloat. Understanding this changes how I evaluate the proposed cuts -- they're not trimming fat; they're cutting into services that students are legally entitled to receive.

A union representative said teachers are "nervous about losing their jobs" and feel like "their family might be taken away." That's the human cost behind the spreadsheets. If the people who would be teaching my daughter are demoralized and fearful right now, what kind of learning environment will she walk into?
