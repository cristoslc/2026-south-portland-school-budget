# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

March 5 is the night $87 million stops being a vague obligation and becomes a number Tom has to carry home. He already knew the police and fire bond was heading to November — the Walgreens purchase had been announced, the direction was clear. What he didn't have was the scope: $47 million for a new police station, $40 million for a new fire station, $59.1 million in construction costs alone. Now he has it. And it lands in a room where a man at the microphone is describing his neighbor's foreclosure on a 984-square-foot house because property taxes hit five thousand dollars a year.

Councilor West is the only person doing the math Tom does at his kitchen table. She found a $1.78 million remediation path for the fire station — fix the specific deficiencies, use the vacated police building space after relocation, don't rebuild from scratch. The fire chief even confirmed current response times are within standards, so this isn't an emergency in disguise. The council heard it and moved past it. By the time Tom is sitting in the March 19 meeting watching the reserve fund get drawn down by six votes, he knows exactly when the cheaper option died: this room, this night, without a real fight.

The Facilities Director's testimony deserves more attention than it got. No capital planning. No preventive maintenance. Until five years ago. Tom has been watching the school board account for seven years of staffing growth while enrollment collapsed — now he learns the city ran its physical plant the same way. Assume nothing breaks, defer the maintenance, bond it when it's unfixable. Everything is coming due simultaneously because nobody paid the smaller bills when they were manageable. This isn't bad luck. It's a system that chose short-term comfort over fiscal reality and called it governance.

Kathy Chapman asked the only question that mattered: what does the $87 million actually do to my tax bill? Nobody answered her. Tom has been watching this pattern since December — each piece gets defended in isolation, and the total stays invisible. He's been carrying the sewer numbers since March 19, the school tax trajectory since February, the Mahoney deferral. What the March 5 meeting confirmed is that the unanswered question isn't an oversight. Nobody in that room wanted to say the number out loud.
