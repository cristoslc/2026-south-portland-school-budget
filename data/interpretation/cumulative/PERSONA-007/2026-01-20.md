# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

Seven days after watching the council fracture on the Mahoney bond and learning about the 50-cent capture rate on rising valuations, I'm at a city council meeting watching Rosemary stand up and announce that the January 22nd forum — the one she publicly confirmed at this same podium on January 6th — is being postponed. Families in Red Bank are afraid to leave their homes because of ICE enforcement at a car wash and a laundromat. The community budget forum I'd been counting on to launch our public case for the cuts is now two weeks out.

I've been tracking the immigration disruption since January 12th, when it first surfaced at the board meeting as an attendance and operations problem. I knew it was coming into our planning process. But postponing the forum is a different order of magnitude. We're not just losing some attendance at a public meeting — we're losing the first structured opportunity to explain the $7.2 million gap to the people who will vote on validation. Every day of delay is a day we're not building the case. And the community that's most afraid to show up is the same community whose children attend the schools we're consolidating. We cannot hold these proceedings without them and call it public engagement.

Then the housing density workshop. Planning Director Nevajda walks through LD 1829 — state mandates allowing up to four units on 5,000-square-foot lots, with staff modeling 2,300 additional units if conversion rates hit 10%. Not a word about school capacity. Not a word about impact fees. The council is streamlining approvals down to staff-level review for 2–4 unit developments, and nobody at that table is running the student generation numbers. I've been watching enrollment fall for four years — 23% at the elementary level — and building my budget case around right-sizing for a smaller district. If the state forces density and we start generating students again in two to three years, every closure decision I'm defending tonight becomes a liability. I cannot plan for contraction and expansion simultaneously on a zeroed-out fund balance.

The contrast with the business loan programs is the detail that will stick with me. The city approves a million dollars in revolving loan instruments while I'm preparing to cut 78 positions and explain to parents why we can't afford music. I don't begrudge economic development. But the city makes these fiscal choices in a room where no one is required to account for what they mean to the school side of the tax bill.
