# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

The vote happened. She knew it would. She cleared her calendar, she brought people, she stayed five hours, and at the end of it the board voted five to one to close Kaler and four to two to restructure every other elementary school in the city by September. Not Kaler families. All families. PreK through first grade goes to Dyer and Small. Second through fourth goes to Brown and Skillin. If you have a first grader and a third grader, they will be in different buildings starting in the fall. This is no longer a scenario on a PowerPoint slide. This is a decision with a vote count and a timestamp.

She has been carrying the question Ishmael Daniels asked tonight since February 4th — she just didn't know his name yet. That was the forum where she first learned the administration hadn't modeled transportation impacts. Tonight Ishmael stands at the microphone and asks the most reasonable possible question: when will my daughter know where she's going to school? Can we visit? Can we meet the teachers? And the answer is that they couldn't tell him before the votes because they needed to vote first — and now they've voted, and the answer is still "as soon as possible." His daughter has 97 days. Rachel has been watching this specific evasion drift across seven weeks and nine meetings and it has never once gotten more specific.

The thing she keeps returning to is Richardson. The only board member with elementary-aged children. The only one who lives a version of what Rachel lives every morning — the drop-off, the relationship with the teacher, the friend group, the routine. She voted no. On both things. She said the pace was reckless. She said staff morale was very low. She said community was being divided. That is not a dissent Rachel finds comforting. It is a confirmation, from inside the room where the decision was made, that the people with the most direct knowledge of what was at stake knew the timeline was wrong and voted yes anyway.

The budget failing 2-5 almost doesn't register. The structural decisions are locked. April 2nd is accounting. What Rachel is sitting with tonight, for the first time in sixteen weeks, is the particular stillness of someone who has run as hard as she could and arrived at the thing she was running to stop. She is not done. But the fight to prevent has ended, and she doesn't fully know yet what she is fighting for now.
