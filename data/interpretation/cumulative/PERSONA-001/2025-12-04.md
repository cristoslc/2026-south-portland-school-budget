# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025

### Interpretation

Looking at December 4 from the vantage point of March 2026, what strikes Maria most is that the city council was formally discussing participation failures while the school budget crisis was already forming in the background — and nobody connected the two.

The budget committee process discussion feels like bureaucratic housekeeping at the time. A $2,500 cap on routine committee requests, unlimited special project funding, new submission deadlines. But Maria knows now that the Human Rights Commission — which typically requests $10,000 — suddenly has to justify itself as a "special project" under the new rules. The commission that is supposed to advocate for exactly the immigrant families Chair DeAngelis invoked three days earlier at her inauguration is being cap-limited the same week that the superintendent search process is being quietly designed to exclude community voices. The pattern is there in December if you know where to look.

The Zoom comment discussion is the part that keeps echoing. The council acknowledges it would be good to allow remote participation — working parents, people with caregiving obligations, people afraid of retaliation — but their hands are tied because of First Amendment constraints. Past Zoom bombing incidents made remote participation legally and practically unmanageable. Maria understands the problem even as she resents the outcome: she will be the one rearranging her family's schedule to attend every six-thirty meeting between now and April, because that is the only way to participate. The parents who cannot do that will be the "silent majority" three councilors name but cannot reach.

That "silent majority" framing is the thing Maria is still turning over in March. One speaker says people don't participate because they're afraid — of neighbors, of employers, of being seen as difficult. Maria has watched this play out in her parent networks all winter: parents who text her privately that they're horrified by reconfiguration, who sign petitions but won't speak at board meetings, who whisper at pickup that they'd rather pay more in taxes than lose their child's school. Those parents are invisible to decision-makers. December 4 is where the city formally acknowledges the participation infrastructure is broken — and continues anyway. What follows — near-unanimous parent opposition that the board advances past without hesitation, a superintendent search explicitly designed to keep community voices out — isn't a failure to hear the warning. The warning was never acted on.
