# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

Reading December 4 from where I am now in March 2026, I can see the seeds of everything that followed embedded in what looked, at the time, like routine committee administration. The $2,500 cap on committee operating budgets sounds like fiscal hygiene — standardization, uniformity, prudent governance. But Councilor Walker saw it immediately: committees like Conservation and Economic Development have departmental staff and resources to draw on, while the Human Rights Commission has to function on its own. A $2,500 budget for the body charged with protecting the rights of the city's most marginalized residents — cut from a historical baseline of around $10,000 — is not neutral fiscal management. It is structural deprioritization dressed up as uniform policy. And the majority approved it anyway, comfortable calling it an "experimental year" with a promise to adjust. I have spent the last three months watching South Portland make that same promise about equity and then not go back.

Walker's pushback here matters more in retrospect than it could have in the moment. Two months later she would be the one to name Skillin's 50%-versus-27% demographic gap as de facto segregation in the joint council-board workshop. The through-line is consistent: Walker spots when "neutral" institutional decisions produce unequal outcomes and names them plainly. That she was outvoted in December is a foreshadowing of the governance pattern that has defined this entire budget season.

The Zoom rejection is the other thing that reads differently now. The council couldn't implement virtual public comment because of past "Zoom bombing" — I understand the technical argument. But by January, families were keeping children home out of fear of ICE enforcement. The structural decision to make in-person participation the only option was already locked in before that crisis arrived. By February, a budget forum was drawing only teachers, administrators, and English-speaking parents. These barriers accumulate. The decision that looked like a minor logistical call in December was a load-bearing wall in the community engagement failures that followed.

Jeff Steinbrink's condemnation of anti-Somali rhetoric from federal officials was the right thing to say, and it mattered that someone said it. But federal signals were already reaching South Portland homes in ways that would show up as absences in January. A council chamber statement of welcome, however sincere, is not the same as structural protection — a distinction this city, and this school district, have struggled to close all year.
