## Summary

27 transportation-related claims, questions, commitments, and non-answers were extracted from the FY27 budget evidence pools, persona briefings, and cumulative interpretation folds. Of these:

- **9 are claims** made by the administration or documented by analysis
- **3 are commitments** (consultant budget, Portland agreement, timeline promise)
- **10 are unanswered questions** from parents and analysis
- **3 are non-answers** (responses that acknowledged the question without providing substance)
- **2 are deferred** (information withheld pending process steps)

## The Chronological Pattern

The transportation evidence record tells a clear story of escalating questions and persistent non-answers, now extending into the post-decision era:

### February 4 — The Question Is Raised
A parent asks about busing costs at the first budget forum (TC-001). No answer is provided. The administration does, however, note that middle school bell time changes require a $25K DOT traffic study and 6-12 month permitting — establishing that they understand transport changes require analysis (TC-002).

### March 2 — The Question Returns, With Testimony
The busing question comes back with force. Parents describe 45-minute bus rides under the current system (TC-008), working families that "will be crushed" by split-building logistics (TC-006, TC-007), and the lack of transportation cost estimates (TC-009). The administration's survey data shows 90% of parents worried about busing complications and 57% concerned about before/after care disruption (TC-011).

The administration responds with: "we would need to kind of look at the whole picture" (TC-003) and "have budgeted for transportation consultancy" (TC-004). They also state, without supporting analysis: "we don't anticipate [route cost changes] being a significant cost" (TC-005).

### March 9 — The Gap Is Formally Documented
The evidence pool synthesis identifies transportation cost modeling as a critical gap: "A transportation consultant is referenced as a cost but no routing analysis has been shared" (TC-012).

### March 23-30 — Confirmation of No Analysis
The Director of Operations confirms on the record that "no modeling exists and none will be done before the board votes" (TC-014). The board is asked to choose between configurations with materially different transportation implications, without transportation analysis.

### April 2 — Post-Vote, Still No Plan
The reconfiguration vote has passed. The April 2 special budget meeting is the first meeting of the implementation era. And the implementation era begins with zero implementation artifacts:

- **No boundaries, no assignments, no routes**: Multiple sources confirm that no attendance boundary maps, bus ride length parameters, school assignment criteria, or sibling placement policies have been published (TC-020, TC-026). A parent states plainly: "none of the parents know where their children are gonna go next year" (TC-022). A Dyer parent who currently walks his son to school asks whether his child will be sent to Skillin across town — no answer given (TC-023).

- **Consultant confirmed, deliverables withheld**: Dr. Prince confirms the district is working with a transportation consultant (TC-019), updating the March 2 commitment (TC-004) and March 9 gap analysis (TC-012). But she declines to share any information, citing the need to avoid getting "ahead of" family listening sessions. The consultant has moved from "budgeted" to "engaged" — but still with no public scope, timeline, or deliverables.

- **Timeline of a timeline**: The superintendent commits to publishing a formal timeline for boundary decisions "by the end of next week" (~April 10) (TC-021). This is the first concrete date offered in the entire process — but it is a promise to announce when decisions will be made, not a decision itself. 97 days remain until September.

- **Driver attrition crisis**: Bus driver and union representative testimony reveals six injuries, three shoulder surgeries, three workers in physical therapy from non-driving duty changes. Drivers are actively seeking other jobs and the district is already down two positions (TC-024). This compounds the 14% SEA staffing cut (TC-016) and raises the question: is the district factoring driver attrition into its transportation plan? (TC-027). No answer from administration.

- **Portland contract stale since 2017**: The bus maintenance cross-billing agreement with Portland has not been renegotiated in nine years (TC-025). South Portland provides a mechanic and parts at 2017 rates. Director of Operations Nally confirmed renegotiation is underway but gave no completion date. This updates TC-017 with specifics on how stale the arrangement is.

## Key Contradictions

1. **Double standard on traffic studies**: The administration deferred middle school bell time changes because they require a $25K DOT traffic study (TC-002, TC-013). Elementary reconfiguration affects more students and more buildings, but no equivalent analysis was required or produced.

2. **"Not significant" without modeling**: The claim that route cost changes "are not anticipated to be significant" (TC-005) was made without having modeled the routes. This is an assumption presented as a conclusion.

3. **Cutting transport staff while expanding routes**: 14% SEA staffing reduction (TC-016) decided before any assessment of whether the reconfigured system needs more or fewer routes. Now compounded by active driver attrition (TC-024) — the district is losing drivers it hasn't yet cut.

4. **Survey results ignored**: Administration's own survey shows 90% of parents worried about busing (TC-011), yet no analysis was commissioned to address this concern.

5. **Post-decision engagement sequence**: Thirteen family listening sessions and a digital survey were launched after the reconfiguration vote, not before. Transportation and boundary information is withheld pending these sessions — the same consultation-after-decision pattern documented throughout the budget process.

## Unfunded Obligations Not Addressed

The following cost categories are absent from public discussion:

- **McKinney-Vento transport obligations**: 10% of students are eligible. School closure triggers school-of-origin transport rights.
- **SPED door-to-door mandates**: 23% of students have IEPs, many with transport requirements. The superintendent offered no placements, grouping logic, or timeline for special education continuity through reconfiguration.
- **Before/after care gap costs**: Split-building families face mismatched schedules with no district plan.
- **Portland bus agreement renegotiation**: Cross-billing arrangement running at 2017 rates (TC-025). Renegotiation underway, but no completion date or impact assessment on route restructuring costs.
- **Driver replacement and retention costs**: With two positions already vacant and active attrition (TC-024), the district faces recruitment costs on top of route expansion needs.
