# Cumulative Summary: David (PERSONA-002)

### Current Understanding

David understands the district's FY27 fiscal situation as a genuine structural crisis with documented systemic causes, now entering its most consequential governance week. Finance Director Ketchem has formally established the root causes: enrollment fell 23% while staffing grew by 82 positions, COVID funds masked the misalignment, no minimum fund balance threshold existed as a guardrail, and six years of finance leadership turnover prevented sustained oversight. The FY27 proposed budget meets the 6% local tax increase ceiling — approximately $257 per year for the average homeowner — but Ketchem's own analysis shows FY28 labor auto-escalation, 13–14% annual utility growth, and at least $300K in new debt service will push costs above that ceiling without additional structural action. The fund balance is fully exhausted; the city's comparable 9% of operating expenses benchmark implies a $6.75M recovery target with no rebuild plan yet adopted. Administrative reclassification savings are confirmed as minimal: director-to-strategist conversions yield $20–30K net, and the DEI coordinator swap produced zero confirmed savings. The FY27 budget failed a board vote 5–2 on March 30; a second vote was deferred April 2 pending unverified state aid figures of $300K and $750K, with an April 7 city council presentation locked on the calendar regardless of outcome. Kaler closure (6–1) and Option A reconfiguration (4–2) have been authorized. On the city side, David is tracking three simultaneous capital obligations — a public safety bond ($80–87M), sewer revenue bonds ($51.7M), and a Mahoney renovation range ($70–76M) — against a residential property tax base where schools already represent 61% of the total bill, with no integrated household impact analysis produced by either governing body.
