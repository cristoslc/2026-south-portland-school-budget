# Transport Configuration Comparison

**Spec:** SPEC-065 | **Epic:** EPIC-031 | **Initiative:** INITIATIVE-006
**Date:** 2026-03-31

---

## The Missing Analysis

The South Portland School Board has been asked to choose between elementary reconfiguration options with materially different transportation implications, without transportation analysis. The Director of Operations confirmed on the record that "no modeling exists and none will be done before the board votes" (TC-014). A transportation consultant has been budgeted ($60-125K) but not hired, with no scope or timeline defined.

This document assembles the independent analysis that should have existed before the vote. It compares three configurations across five transport metrics, using publicly available data and documented methodology. Every figure is an estimate — disclosed as such — and every assumption is transparent and correctable with district data.

## What These Numbers Mean

### Option A (Administration Recommendation)

The administration's preferred option has the **highest transportation impact** across every metric:

- **139-169 families** (18-24% of all elementary families) would have children in two different buildings every day. This is not a one-time disruption — it's a structural feature of the grade-band model that persists as long as the configuration exists.
- The total fiscal exposure of **$1.05M-$1.79M annually** could offset **48-119% of the claimed savings**. At the high end, the transportation costs alone exceed the projected savings from closure.
- If a 4th bus tier is required (likely under grade-band routing), 118-144 families face new before/after care costs of $3,400-$5,600 per family per year. This is a transferred cost — it doesn't appear in the district budget but is borne by working families.

### Option B (Board Alternative)

Option B has the **lowest transportation impact** by a wide margin:

- **Zero families** face grade-band splits. Every child attends the same K-4 building as their siblings.
- The 3-tier bus system continues unchanged. Wider catchments add route time but don't change the tier structure.
- Total fiscal exposure is **$30K-$80K** — the small McKinney-Vento obligation from Kaler closure, which is unavoidable under any closure scenario.
- Transportation does not meaningfully erode the claimed savings.

### Variant C (Citizen Alternative)

Variant C falls between the other two:

- **123-150 split families** (fewer than Option A because the grade boundary is higher)
- Total fiscal exposure of **$743K-$1.40M** — lower than Option A but still substantial
- The single grades 3-4 building creates city-wide routes, increasing pressure toward a 4th tier
- **34-93% of claimed savings** potentially offset by transportation costs

## Critical Findings Beyond the Numbers

### 1. The Care Capacity Paradox

After-school care is **FULL at 4 of 5 elementary schools**. The only school with openings is Kaler — the one being closed. Closing Kaler removes the only care capacity headroom while simultaneously creating new demand from split-building families. Under Option A or Variant C, 42-144 new families need care at schools that already have waitlists.

### 2. The Staffing Contradiction

The SEA bargaining unit faces a 14% cut (100 → 86 FTE) — the steepest of any unit. This was decided before any route modeling existed. While the aggregate numbers suggest adequacy for regular routes, the cut was set without knowing how many routes the reconfigured system needs. This is a staffing decision made in the absence of the data needed to make it.

### 3. The Traffic Study Double Standard

The administration deferred middle school bell time consolidation because it requires a $25K DOT traffic study and 6-12 months of permitting. The elementary reconfiguration affects more students, more buildings, and more routes — but no equivalent traffic study was required, conducted, or budgeted.

### 4. The "Not Significant" Claim

The administration stated that route cost changes "are not anticipated to be significant" (TC-005) without having modeled the routes. This independent analysis estimates route expansion costs alone at $570K-$855K annually for grade-band configurations — roughly 19-29% of the current $2.99M transport budget. Whether that is "significant" is a judgment call, but it is a judgment that should be made with data, not without it.

## What This Analysis Cannot Tell You

This comparison uses publicly available data and documented methodology. It is **order-of-magnitude analysis**, not route-level precision. Specifically:

1. **These are ranges, not precise figures.** The low and high estimates reflect uncertainty in assumptions. The actual numbers could fall outside these ranges.
2. **Route expansion costs use aggregate models.** Actual costs depend on specific route configurations, fleet utilization, contract terms, and driver availability — data only the district has.
3. **Care costs are estimates.** The number of families who would actually use paid care (vs. informal arrangements) is uncertain.
4. **SEA staffing adequacy is estimated.** The transport-specific share of SEA FTE is not disclosed in budget documents.
5. **This analysis does not recommend a configuration.** It surfaces the transportation data that should have been part of the decision. The decision itself belongs to the board and the community.

## Net Savings After Transportation

| Configuration | Claimed Savings | Transport Exposure | Net Savings Range |
|---------------|:---------------:|:------------------:|:-----------------:|
| **Option A** | $1.5M-$2.2M | $1.05M-$1.79M | **$210K-$1.15M** |
| **Option B** | $1.5M-$2.2M | $30K-$80K | **$1.42M-$2.17M** |
| **Variant C** | $1.5M-$2.2M | $743K-$1.40M | **$100K-$1.46M** |

Option A's net savings could be as low as **$210K** after transportation costs — 86-91% less than the headline figure. Option B preserves nearly the full claimed savings.

**Important caveat:** The "claimed savings" figure itself has not been independently verified in this analysis. These net savings numbers assume the $1.5-2.2M figure is accurate.

## Invitation to Improve

Every estimate in this comparison can be replaced with facts the district possesses:

- **Family-level enrollment data** → exact split-family counts (replacing sibling rate estimates)
- **McKinney-Vento roster** → exact MV-eligible student counts per school
- **SEA function-level staffing** → actual transport FTE (replacing proportional estimates)
- **Fleet size and route data** → actual route requirements and tier feasibility
- **Care waitlist numbers** → actual unmet demand (replacing binary FULL/Available status)
- **Route-level cost modeling** → actual route expansion costs (replacing aggregate model)

The methodology is documented and transparent. All analysis scripts are available in `pipeline/transport/`. Corrections, data contributions, and methodological challenges are welcome — the goal is the most accurate picture possible, not any particular conclusion.
