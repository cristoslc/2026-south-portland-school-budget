# Cumulative Record: Kira (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

The Project Home money passed and she should be sitting with relief, and she is sitting with relief, and she is also sitting with what Councilor Matthews did on the way to voting no. He dropped *72 pink slips* into the chamber — not as a demand that the council address both crises at once, not as a call to action, but as the reason he could not in good conscience vote yes. Those are the people she works alongside in three different buildings every day. The ed techs who carry through her caseload on the days she is somewhere else. The counselors. Possibly traveling specialists like herself. They went into the official record as a dissent rationale and then the vote moved to 6-1 and the meeting moved to Mahoney and nobody said: so what are we going to do about the 72?

She has been watching the council use numbers to compress harm since January — West's arrest count, the enrollment/staffing figure, the stacking capital demands that never appeared in the same sentence. Matthews's move is the same pattern applied inward, to her own institution, to her own colleagues. It works because the number is real. The 72 pink slips are real. She does not dispute the fiscal constraint. She disputes using the pink slips as a reason to vote no on $100,000 for families whose children are on MTSS wait lists because they were afraid to leave their homes.

The Project Home vote is real and she will take it. Pedro Vasquez said what she already knows from every attendance sheet she reads at three buildings — families withdrawing from school, from work, from public life — and now there is money moving toward approximately 50 households. The chief's apology is a reckoning she has been watching her multilingual families wait for. It is not enough and she knows trust doesn't repair at a podium. But it is in the record.

What she is carrying home is the calendar: April 7 public hearing, May 5 council vote, June 9 referendum. The clock is locked. And she still does not know which 72. She knows what a traveling specialist looks like on a cost-efficiency spreadsheet — inefficient, duplicative, hard to defend — and she knows what it means when that position goes: the kid on the wait list at her multilingual-dominant building stays on the wait list, because the person who would serve them is no longer in the system. April 14 is the budget workshop. School leads the agenda. She needs to be in that room with everything she has.
