# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

The joint workshop was a reality check on top of a reality check. Just one day after the school board meeting where Feller questioned the 6% ceiling, the city council essentially said even 6% is too high -- their guidance landed in a 3-6% range with most councilors gravitating toward 4-6%. They rejected both the 6% Option B and 9.8% Option C that the school board presented. That means the district is now looking at cuts closer to $8-9 million rather than the $3.5-7.2 million range they had been modeling. The math just got significantly harder.

The enrollment-staffing disconnect was presented again -- 300+ students lost, 82 positions added -- and this time the audience included councilors who control the purse strings. The per-pupil cost of $26,651 came up again through a public commenter who noted the district spends approximately $20 million more than the state's Essential Programs and Services recommendation. That thread I opened in December about verifying this figure remains unresolved -- staff still have not directly addressed the methodology.

A new and troubling transparency issue emerged: a teacher reported that six weeks after the union president requested historical central office staffing data for peer comparison, the information still has not been provided. When I am asking for data-driven decisions and the district cannot produce basic workforce analytics on a six-week timeline, that is a fundamental capacity problem.

The fund balance situation was further clarified: school fund balance sits at only $739K with $850K budgeted for FY26. Meanwhile the city has $8.5 million in excess fund balance. It is like one side of the house is on fire while the other is financially healthy. The city also faces its own pressures -- department requests totaling ~15% before cuts, 10% health insurance increases, rising utilities, ARPA-funded positions needing property tax replacement -- so there is no easy rescue path from the municipal side.

The elementary reconfiguration plan got more specific: 3 intermediate schools (grades 2-4) and 2 primary schools (PreK-1) saving approximately $2M. The middle school consolidation remains blocked by the DOT traffic study timeline, now estimated at $25K with 6-12 months for approval. These details confirm the feasibility constraints I noted earlier.
