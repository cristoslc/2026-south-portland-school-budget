# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

Olivia Montgomery walked up to that microphone and did exactly what an equity advocate is supposed to do: she named the mechanism of harm with professional precision. A clinical social worker and social work professor laying out, clearly, that placing a police station adjacent to city hall and the library will cause immigrant families, undocumented residents, anyone with reason to fear police contact, to stop accessing city services entirely — including voting. She was not asking for anything radical. She was asking for the buildings to be in different locations. And then the council deliberated for over an hour about rooftop gardens.

This is the same erasure pattern I have been tracking since December. On December 4, Walker named structural inequity in committee funding and was outvoted. On December 9, DeAngelo raised language access and rent control and was not acknowledged. On January 6, the budget engagement process was designed to generate political cover rather than surface community needs. Now a credentialed expert raises a direct, concrete equity argument about physical access to civic infrastructure — and it is structurally absent from the deliberation. Not disagreed with. Not refuted. Simply not heard.

The Mahoney housing story is the one that compounds everything I already know about this city. The South Portland Housing Authority had a real proposal for that site. The council chose facilities instead and offered a promissory note — vacating the other buildings will "open up" those sites someday — with no affordability covenant, no timeline, no legal commitment of any kind. I watched them make the same move in December with the TIF: the tool was available, the leverage moment arrived, and they chose the market-rate path without dissent. Now they have done it again with land. The December 9 TIF vote and the January 13 Mahoney vote are the same decision, expressed in different currencies.

And then, at 10:15 PM, they pushed the housing ordinance presentation — the actual policy work that could affect people's tenancy rights right now — to a future meeting because the facilities discussion ate the whole evening. That is not an accident. That is what structural deprioritization looks like when it is invisible enough that no one has to own it.
