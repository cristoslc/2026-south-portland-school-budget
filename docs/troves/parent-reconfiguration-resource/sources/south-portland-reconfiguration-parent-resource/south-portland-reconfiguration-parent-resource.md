## Purpose

South Portland School District is facing an 8 million dollar shortage for FY 2027. On 3/30/2026 The school board approved the closure of Kaler Elementary School and a full elementary school reconfiguration.

We are a group of parents who started working together because we were all very surprised a reconfiguration or closure was being proposed for THIS fall. We created a survey to try to get the word out there that this was happening, and to understand how others felt. This survey is now closed, it was presented to the school board at the 3/2 meeting.

Moving forward, the goal of this group is to support the facilitation of transparent, inclusive, responsible decision-making that prioritizes meaningful parent and community engagement as our district considers important decisions affecting all members of our schools communities.

This Document is a resource that will continue to be updated and changed as things progress. Please note the tabs to the left.

Parent Survey

The parent survey was a grassroots effort, with the intention of spreading awareness about the reconfiguration proposal, and to get a general sense of how some families were feeling. This survey was conducted by parent volunteers over a two-week period, including February break, and was shared primarily through word of mouth by English-speaking creators. As a result, participation was limited and does not fully represent the broader community. The district was asked and declined to share the survey broadly through their email platform.  We have asked the district to conduct an official survey using their communication and translation resources so that all families can participate.

Reconfiguration Approval Next Steps

Reconfiguration Approval Next Steps

This document outlines possible steps following the approval of reconfiguration, including ways to engage at the state level and optional email templates for those who would like to reach out. Email templates and contacts below.

We are not challenging the board's right to vote; we are challenging the district's operational readiness to open safely in September 2026. As of today, there is no attendance zone map, no transportation plan, and no labor agreement for the transition. This creates an environment of operational negligence that risks the district’s Basic School Approval and student safety.

When we talk about “operational negligence,” we’re not questioning intentions, we’re naming a gap in readiness.

A district of this size should have clear plans in place for transportation, staffing, facilities, and student placement before implementing a system-wide change.

Without those, there is a real risk that schools may not be able to operate smoothly or safely on day one.

Key Compliance Gaps and Citations

- Failure to Meet Basic School Approval Standards: Under Maine Rule Chapter 125, districts must maintain a Comprehensive Education Plan with specific operational protocols. A reconfiguration without a transition plan risks Provisional Approval status (Section 2.10). This means the state can intervene if the district cannot prove it will safely operate by opening day.[https://www.maine.gov/sos/sites/maine.gov.sos/files/content/assets/071c125.docx](https://www.maine.gov/sos/sites/maine.gov.sos/files/content/assets/071c125.docx)
- Transportation Safety Risk: The Maine School Transportation Safety Commission is currently heightening oversight on district protocols. Per Maine Title 20-A Section 5401, the district is legally obligated to provide safe transportation. Launching a new system without months of route modeling and safety vetting is an actionable negligence claim.[https://legislature.maine.gov/statutes/20-A/title20-Asec5401.html](https://legislature.maine.gov/statutes/20-A/title20-Asec5401.html)
- Contractual and Labor Hard Stop: Involuntary transfers (moving teachers to new buildings or grades) typically require formal notice and bargaining. Per Maine Labor Relations standards, if the district hasn't amended contracts to include summer moving work, they lack the legal labor force to execute the transition by September.[https://www.maine.gov/mlrb/](https://www.maine.gov/mlrb/)
- Facility Modification Oversight: The Maine DOE Office of School Facilities monitors whether buildings are educationally appropriate for the age groups they house. Changing a building from grades 3-5 to K-2 (or vice versa) often requires physical modifications like bathroom heights or playground safety that must be vetted by the state.[https://www.maine.gov/doe/schools/facilities](https://www.maine.gov/doe/schools/facilities)

- Commissioner's Office: Pender Makin (pender.makin@maine.gov). Request a Compliance Review of South Portland's transition viability.

# TEMPLATE 1: Email to Commissioner (Request for Compliance Review)

Who to send to: Pender Makin\
Email: pender.makin@maine.gov\
Subject: Request for Compliance Review – South Portland Reconfiguration

Hi Commissioner Makin,

I am a parent/community member in South Portland writing to request a review of the district’s readiness to implement a full elementary school reconfiguration for the 2026–2027 school year.

We understand that school boards have the authority to reorganize schools. However, this request is not about the decision itself—it is about whether the district can meet the operational and safety requirements necessary to open schools in September 2026.

Based on publicly available information, several key compliance areas appear unresolved:

- Basic School Approval (Chapter 125): The district has not presented a comprehensive transition or implementation plan, raising concerns about its ability to meet required standards.
- Transportation Safety (Title 20-A §5401): There is currently no publicly available transportation plan or route modeling for a district-wide reconfiguration.
- Facilities Readiness: It is unclear whether required assessments or approvals have been initiated to ensure buildings are appropriate for new grade configurations.
- Labor and Staffing Capacity: There is no clear indication that staffing or contractual requirements have been addressed for a transition of this scale.

At this time, there is no finalized attendance zone map, transportation plan, or implementation framework available to families.

We are not challenging the board’s authority to vote. Rather, we are asking whether the district has demonstrated sufficient operational readiness to meet state requirements and ensure student safety.

I respectfully request that the Maine Department of Education review the district’s transition planning and determine whether it meets the standards required for Basic School Approval.

Thank you for your time and for your commitment to safe and compliant school operations.

Current Questions

Logistical Questions

“The details are not the details, they make the design”

Before moving forward with a decision as significant as closing or reconfiguring a school, the district and/or board should ensure that the educational, financial, logistical, and community impacts are clearly understood and transparently addressed.

Assuming these things will just be taken into account seems irresponsible. Here are some questions we have thought of.

Outcomes & Equity

1.  How will children be placed in new schools? Will they stay with neighborhoods or other students on their street? Or will they be assigned by random lottery?
2.  What evidence exists that reconfiguration will improve academic outcomes?
3.  What safeguards are in place to prevent unintended increases in segregation?
4.  How will we know if equity has improved, what metrics are we using to measure it?
5.  How will resources be allocated differently (e.g. weighted student funding)?
6.  What measurable equity outcomes are expected within 1 year, 3 years, 5 years?
7.  How are decisions being made about which students will go to which school and is equity central to that?

Impact on Young Children

6.  What does research say about school transitions for elementary aged students?
7.  How will attachment, continuity, and social-emotional stability be supported?
8.  What transition plan exists for children with IEPs or specialized services?
9.  What does research say about attachment and relationships with school teachers and staff in very young children who have previously experienced trauma or instability at home, and how might an abrupt, significant change be particularly challenging?

Special Education and Related services

10. How will IEP continually be guaranteed during building transitions?
11. Will caseloads increase?
12. Are there adequate therapy spaces in each building?
13. How will students with special needs receive specific support during this big transition?

Transportation and aftercare

14. How many families who currently walk will now require bussing?
15. How will sibling splits be handled?
16. What will average bus ride times be for young children?
17. How will aftercare be handled-will it be available at every site?
18. How will this impact families who have already secured childcare for September?
19. How will traffic in the broader community be impacted by additional bussing routes and/or parents who need to drop off children at multiple schools if bus times are not feasible for work schedules?

Financial Transparency

19. What is the contingency plan if projected savings are not realized? Do we keep the reconfiguration?
20. What are the one-time transition costs (moving, staffing shifts, facilities changes)?
21. What new transportation costs will be incurred?
22. Could implementation expenses be reduced if a phasing structure was implemented instead?
23. Would 5+ year savings be the same if reconfiguration implementation was phased in over time?
24. Where does the district have debts related to the elementary schools?

Immigrant and Multilingual Family Support

24. How will communication be translated and distributed in gathering caregiver input during this process and communicating about future changes, if approved?
25. How will trust with immigrant families be preserved during the transition?
26. Has the district consulted directly with families most impacted?
27. Has the district studied the impacts of separating ESOL students from their siblings?

Staffing

27. How will teacher reassignment affect school culture?
28. How will staff morale and retention be supported after the trauma of last years’ budget cuts?
29. What professional development will be required?

Facilities

30. Are receiving buildings developmentally appropriate for pre-k students?
31. What is required by law to be present in pre-k classrooms (bathrooms?)?
32. Are bathrooms, playgrounds, and furniture age-appropriate?
33. What renovations are required, and at what cost?
34. Is building security being re-evaluated?
35. What is the cost of moving classroom furniture?

Governance

36. What would phasing implementation look like instead of a full roll out?
37. What are the risks of this happening during interim leadership?
38. Will the new superintendent have a background in working with staffing cuts and resulting morale issues?
39. What is the capacity for current staff and administrators to implement this project?
40. Has the work of implementing the new CDS project been considered when assessing staff capacity to implement this project?

Community

40. How will PTA structure shift?
41. How will families build relationships and engage if siblings are separated across buildings? (parent engagement is a resource)
42. How will hyper local community partnerships be maintained? (some of which we saw the impact of in the recent increase in ICE enforcement)

Closure Specific Questions

1.  What are the actual projected savings, and over what time frame?
2.   What new costs will be created (transportation, renovations, staffing changes)?
3.  What capital improvements will receiving schools need?
4.  What will happen to the closed building and what costs will it carry?
5.  Which of the buildings carry debt that needs to be paid off?
6.  What are birth rate trends in the community?
7.  What are the five- and ten-year enrollment projections?
8.  Are housing developments or demographic shifts likely to change enrollment?
9.  Could the district face overcrowding in a few years if enrollment rises?
10. What sunk costs have already been invested in the facilities?
11. How will student impact be minimized?

Research

Research

Transitions:

\- This paper illustrates that although the risk of both internalized and externalized psychological symptoms increases with school transitions, a reduction in negative psychological symptoms is seen with high quality student-teacher relationships that during the transition: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31191415/>

-This paper, while focused on primary to secondary school transition, shows that specific school-based interventions can increase coping efficacy with transitions.  However, they must be applied preemptively, illustrating that support needs to be implemented well before the transition occurs: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39450133/>

-In this study school changes predicted declines in academic performance and classroom participation with the key mechanism being disruption to teacher and peer relationships: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19037953/>

School belonging and mental health:

-This resource defines school connectedness comprises 3 elements: school attachment/belonging, teacher attachment, and peer attachment: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40619959/>

-A systematic review of 34 longitudinal studies found that higher levels of school connectedness significantly predicted lower depressive and anxiety symptoms in youth, with both intervention studies showing improvements in depression through enhanced school connectedness: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36424575/>

-The protective effect appears especially strong for students facing adversity - this study shows that school connectedness at younger ages buffered against poor mental health outcomes in teens. Quote: "feeling connected to school at younger ages may disrupt processes linking childhood adversity to adolescent mental health. Schools should foster students' feelings of connectedness to protect vulnerable individuals and benefit all pupils' mental health: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39506487/>

Transportation:

-This exploratory study from NYC schools found longer bus commute times linked to deleterious student outcomes, increased absenteeism, and potentially decreased test scores: <https://ies.ed.gov/use-work/awards/between-home-and-school-school-bus-and-student-outcomes>

This data isn't directly applicable to our exact situation, but still provides information:

-Longer bus commute times have been shown to increase depression and anxiety symptoms, and increase potential bullying behavior in Asia and Canada: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39642115/>, <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27376652/>

-Active transportation to school (walking/cycling) showed improved physical and mental health outcomes in 9 countries: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33255182/>

Siblings:

A bit of a stretch, but evidence that increases in sibling intimacy (which could potentially come from shared school experiences) was linked to increases in peer competence and decreases in depression symptoms: <https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17605528/>

- A snapshot of peer-reviewed research on elementary school transitions. Peer-reviewed research from prominent academic journals suggests that adding school transitions in the elementary years decreases academic performance and hurts working families and families with multiple children.

- [A 2017 study](https://direct.mit.edu/edfp/article/12/4/419/10279/Moving-Matters-The-Causal-Effect-of-Moving-Schools) in Education Finance and Policy found that school transitions in the elementary years are associated with declines in academic achievement, attendance and engagement, especially for economically disadvantaged kids.
- [A 2018 study](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6123573/) in Frontiers in Psychology found reduced self-esteem, increased anxiety and reduced sense of belonging in students who transition between schools more often.
- [A 2022 study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35087445/) in Frontiers in Psychology found that families with more school-aged children experience significantly greater stress from school-related logistics disruptions.
- [A 2024 study](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/08959048241271370) in Educational Policy found that the logistical burdens of rezoning fall unevenly, with lower-income families least able to absorb them.

Additionally,[ ](https://edworkingpapers.com/sites/default/files/ai22-530.pdf)[this paper from Annenberg at Brown](https://edworkingpapers.com/sites/default/files/ai22-530.pdf), which isn't peer-reviewed and where they studied a small town in Arkansas.

Another compelling study: <https://direct.mit.edu/edfp/article/12/4/419/10279/Moving-Matters-The-Causal-Effect-of-Moving-Schools>

"Thus, our estimates indicate that the type of mobility most commonly ignored in the literature (structural) has long-term negative consequences for both math and ELA performance, and articulated, nonstructural moves have significant positive consequences for ELA. These effects are meaningful in magnitude and the results are robust to a variety of alternative specifications, instruments, and samples. Importantly, they speak to the effects of mobility among some of the most vulnerable populations of students—those living in rental housing who are disproportionately poor and underperforming."

The elementary school map that we recommend is attached in the Appendix and would affect only 31 students and could be filtered over time."
