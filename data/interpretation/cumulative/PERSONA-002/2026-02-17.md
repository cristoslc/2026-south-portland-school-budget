# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

February 17 is the night the Mahoney no-ceiling pattern stops being a recoverable failure and becomes a confirmed governance feature. On January 13, the architect asked the council explicitly for a budget target. He didn't get one. The committee presumably convened on January 27. Still nothing. Now it's five weeks later, and the council is working from a range — $57 million to over $104 million — with police and fire facility costs not even on the same slide. The meeting ends at 11:30 PM with informal guidance that amounts to "keep deliberating." That is the same outcome as January 13, dressed in more specific option letters.

The number itself has shifted underneath the story. The $193 million figure I was tracking since Tipton's inaugural has been replaced by renovation scenarios that top out at $104 million — a meaningful change in the fiscal gravity of this project. But the absence of a ceiling is what matters most to me, and that hasn't changed at all. The council is being asked to advance toward a November referendum without telling the design team what they're willing to spend. That is not a negotiating posture. That is a decision-making failure.

What nobody in that room did — and I've looked at every piece of the packet — is put the combined numbers in the same sentence. City bonds outstanding: $89.1 million. Mahoney renovation: $57 to $104 million plus unknowns. School structural gap: $8 to $9 million. School tax share of total property burden: 61 cents on every dollar. Councilor Walker came the closest when she said the council might be becoming a community that doesn't invest in schools or libraries. She stopped before the next question, which is the one I'm building a spreadsheet to answer: what does the combined property tax burden look like when all of this lands at once?

The PFML switch to Symetra is the rare exception I need to note fairly. HR explained it cleanly: skip four months of pre-launch premiums, save $100,000 upfront, $15,000 annually ongoing, 29-month rate lock, employee benefits contractually identical. That is documented, explained, and sound. The three-month gap at the end of the rate guarantee — when you can't yet re-enter the state plan — is a minor exposure worth getting in writing from the broker, but it doesn't change the grade on the overall decision.

The moratorium failing 2-4 is probably the analytically defensible outcome, but for the wrong reason: the council had no eviction filing data, GA applications were actually down year-over-year, and the best population estimate was a rough proxy from Project Home. You cannot design a targeted ordinance without knowing whether you're addressing 50 households or 500. What the council doesn't have is an alternative plan, and that's what I'd push on next.
