# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

One week after the January 6 meeting where Scott announced a February 10 budget guidance workshop and DeAngelis invited parents to watch the school website, Maria sits through nearly four hours of city council discussing a $194 million building project. She is not there about schools. But she cannot stop thinking about them.

The Mahoney cost revelation hits her in a specific way. $194 million for a consolidated city center — police station, fire station, library expansion, municipal offices. Ellen Sanborn's math is straightforward and brutal: $2.26 added to the tax rate, $1,152 per year for the average home. Maria runs the number immediately. That is nearly $100 a month on top of the property tax increase the school budget will also require. She is already calculating what the school board's 6% ceiling costs her household; now she has to stack Mahoney on top of it. The city is asking families to absorb two simultaneous shocks, and nobody in this room seems to be talking to anybody in the school board's room.

The library piece is genuinely appealing — 17,820 square feet of new space, 37% of the total project. Her kids use the library. She would vote for a library expansion in almost any other context. But not at this price, not this year, not when 42 teacher positions are on the line and her daughter's school might be reconfigured out of existence by September. The city's vision for a gleaming consolidated civic campus feels like it belongs to a different South Portland than the one her family lives in.

Four councilors — West, Matthews, Pride, Coleman — say they cannot support sending $194 million to voters. Maria feels a flicker of recognition: at least some elected officials understand that families cannot absorb unlimited cost. But the council's direction to explore "bare bones" options opens a new anxiety. Scaled-back usually means the parts that serve kids and vulnerable families get cut first, while the police station and administrative consolidation survive intact.

And Olivia Montgomery's testimony about the police station location stopping immigrant families from accessing city services lands harder than Maria expects. She thinks immediately of Jean Tam at the December 1 inauguration, of the families DeAngelis invoked in her equity speech. The city is designing its civic center in a way that may wall off those families from the services inside it.

From March 2026, this meeting reads as the moment Maria understood that the school budget crisis and the city's capital ambitions were not separate conversations — they were competing claims on the same households, and nobody was integrating them.
