# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Three hours, and the one clean thing I could hand my group chats without caveats came in the first six minutes: the police station is going to the Walgreens corner on Broadway. City is under contract. The mayor said those words out loud, and I screenshotted it and sent it before the meeting was half over. After months of watching Mahoney estimates balloon to $194 million and then get hacked down through January and February workshops, something finally got decided. I'll take it.

Everything else was harder to deliver. The $87 million total bond — police and fire, library now formally cut — is where the Mahoney capital project finally landed. I've been watching that number try to find a floor since January 13, when the architect told the council he was "not confident" in $193 million. The February 17 range of $57M-$104M was still live. March 5 is where it closed: $87M for the two buildings, fire station staying at its current Broadway location despite the flooding and decontamination problems, 6-1 with West the lone no vote.

West. Same person who stood alone on school funding, alone on the eviction moratorium, alone on a handful of equity-adjacent decisions since December. Here she was, same pattern: did the homework, had the numbers, made the case for a cheaper path, got outvoted. I've been calling her the council's education-and-equity voice. That's still true, but it's not the whole picture. She's the person who pushes back on expensive status-quo solutions full stop — across school budgets, tenant protection, and now an $87 million capital bond. The arena changes. The character doesn't.

What lands hardest looking at this meeting from March 23, knowing what happened on March 19: speakers were already citing eight million dollars in school cuts at this council meeting. Not $7.2 million — the number I've been sourcing and repeating since the December 17 workshop. Eight million. Matthews would put $8.4 million on the record two weeks later while voting against Project Home. But the number was already shifting in public on March 5, at a meeting about police stations and fire trucks, while I was still holding the line at $7.2M. That's the part that stings.

The $150,000 rental assistance recommendation — city general fund, to nonprofits serving immigrant families — was the first concrete city mechanism I'd seen since Project Home's February 27 funding cliff came into view. It didn't pass in final form until March 19. But the will was there on March 5, which matters.
