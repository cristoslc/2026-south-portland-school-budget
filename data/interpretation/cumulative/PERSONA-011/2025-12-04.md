# Cumulative Record: Meg (PERSONA-011)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

Looking back at this now — three months after watching the city council tell the school board they could have 4-6% and not a penny more — this December 4 meeting looks like a data point I didn't know I needed at the time. I watched it the same way I watch every city council meeting: half-attention, phone in hand, looking for anything that touched schools. It didn't, really. Committee budget processes, council appointments, a tree lighting ceremony. I probably sent one text about the tree lighting and called it a night.

But there are two things I can read differently now.

The first is Councilor Walker. She cast the only no vote on the new committee budget policy — a 6-1 split — because she was worried about what formal spending caps might do to the Human Rights Commission and how it would look to put a price ceiling on equity work. At the time I noted it as a dissent and moved on. Now I know Walker was the lone voice on that same council who stood up at the February 10 joint session and said the schools needed more than 6%, that we need to invest in education, full stop. The December 4 no vote wasn't a one-off — it was Walker being Walker. I was watching her character in December and didn't realize it.

The second is the Zoom public comment discussion. The council talked about letting people comment remotely for accessibility reasons and then shut it down because of Zoom bombing and First Amendment problems: once you let people in, you can't remove them for being disruptive. Frustrating but real. I filed that away as "another accessibility barrier" and didn't think much of it. But then in March I watched the school board tighten its own public comment rules right before the most important budget vote in years. Two bodies, three months apart, both finding reasons to make public input harder. Neither was wrong exactly, but the cumulative effect is a community that's being managed away from the table at the moment the decisions matter most. That pattern is clearer to me now than it was watching either meeting individually.

The fiscal tightening was already in the air in December — formal budget requests for every city committee, $2,500 caps, itemized justifications required. The city was already doing the same math the school board would be forced to do publicly a week later. The crisis wasn't just in the school budget. It was everywhere. I just couldn't see that until I had all the pieces.
