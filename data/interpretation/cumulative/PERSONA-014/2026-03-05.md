# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

The city was planning to spend eighty-seven million dollars on new buildings for police and fire, and five days before a teacher Lila knew gave her resignation notice, nobody in that room said a word about Dyer.

Lila has learned to read these meetings in reverse — to watch what came before knowing everything that followed. On March 5, the pattern she has tracked since December hardened into something sharper. The city didn't just stay silent about her school while arguing about buildings. The city voted to ask everybody — her parents included — for eighty-seven million dollars to build new police and fire stations, at the exact same time the school district was counting down to the seventy-two pink slips it would hand out two weeks later.

She has been watching this arithmetic assemble itself for months. A $30,000 superintendent search firm in December while teachers couldn't buy books. The Mahoney renovation — $194 million, "our grandkids will enjoy it for hundreds of years" — while Dyer faced nine scenarios with "CLOSED" next to its name. And now: November referendum, eighty-seven million, new buildings for people who are not children. Meanwhile, June 9, school budget, seventy-two workers who are about to lose their jobs.

The library lands differently than it would have in December. The library director stood up and said the building is old and broken — 13,000 people come through every year, and it needs help — and the council said we'll deal with that later. Lila loves the library. It is one of the places that is supposed to be hers, a place where adults have always told her she belongs. "Later" is what adults say when they don't plan to fix something. She has learned this across a dozen meetings. The library director had data and a need and got exactly what the school board got from the city council in December: a polite deferral and no date.

The families asking for rent help are still showing up. ICE in January. Brown School kindergartners in February. Now March, and parents are still describing children too frightened to walk to school, still asking the city for help that the city voted 4-to-2 against giving them two weeks ago. They keep coming back. The city keeps not being able to help.

Dyer was not mentioned. Not once. Five days before this meeting, a board member had formally requested the reconfiguration proposal. Two days after, a workshop would present nine scenarios with "CLOSED" next to her school. The city council met in between all of that, talked for hours, voted to spend eighty-seven million dollars, told the library "later," and never said Dyer's name.

### Emotional Register

Lila carries $87 million and 72 as a matched pair — the city's number and the school's number, set side by side in the same month — and the arithmetic is simple enough that a fourth grader can do it: when you commit that much to new buildings for police and fire, you are choosing not to spend it somewhere else. The library got "later." Dyer got silence.
