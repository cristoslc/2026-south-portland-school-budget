# Cumulative Record: Priya (PERSONA-005)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

The $100,000 passing 6-1 is real money reaching real families, and I am not going to minimize it. Project Home knows how to move funds fast. Direct payments to landlords, no bureaucratic maze, approximately fifty households. After watching the city manager name the catch-22 in February — that any moratorium's effectiveness depended on vulnerable families making institutional contact they cannot safely make — and after watching the council vote it down anyway, an organization with actual community trust as the intermediary is the right mechanism. The vote matters. Something finally moved.

But Pedro Vasquez at that podium put a human face on the full cost of what the council chose not to do in February. He said he was speaking as a private citizen — the chair of the Human Rights Commission, funded at $2,500 for the year, speaking not in his official capacity but as a man whose family carried their documents and sat together to plan for detention. Two months after the January surge. He named it exactly right: neighbors still not going to work, still keeping children home, still withdrawing from civic life — not just a human rights emergency but an economic failure, a governance failure. The council nodded along and mostly did not wrestle with what structural withdrawal looks like: which services those families stopped accessing, what that absence means for schools, for public health, for the democratic legitimacy of every decision this body makes about their community.

Then Councilor Matthews' no vote delivered the most complete version of a rhetorical move I have been tracking since December. I have now documented two prior failure modes at this council: erasure, where equity arguments are raised and simply absent from deliberation; and inversion, where equity language is turned against equity claims. February 17 gave me inversion — landlords and tenants framed as symmetrically situated parties. March 19 gives me a third: weaponization, where one equity crisis is deployed to foreclose action on another. Seventy-two pink slips went out the day before. Matthews used them as a reason to vote no on immigrant rental assistance. The question he never asked — that no one in that chamber asked — is which families those 72 positions served. Forty-two teachers and sixteen ed techs do not vanish from neutral buildings. They disappear from the schools with the highest concentrations of multilingual learners and students with IEPs. The families Project Home is writing checks to and the families losing those school supports are not competing claimants. They are the same people.

Ahern's acknowledgment that he did not push back on the HSI agent's slur is a mode of public accountability I have not seen from any South Portland official in this entire process. I do not know what to do with it yet. It reveals a specific failure in the moment of contact with federal enforcement that no prior record documented. It also reveals a willingness to name that failure at a public podium. Both things are true, and I am holding them without resolving them.

The sewer rate math was the last thing running through my head when I left. Twenty-two percent per year for three consecutive years. I need the demographic map of which neighborhoods carry the oldest infrastructure. Because I already know the answer, and I want the receipts before anyone tries to tell me it is neutral.
