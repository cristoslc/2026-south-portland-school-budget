### Timeline of Understanding Shifts

1. **2025-12-01** — Looking back at the inauguration of four new board members and Chair DeAngelis, Marcus reads the ceremony's total silence about the $7.2 million gap as a preview of the pattern to come: the people being sworn in to make the votes would not acknowledge what they were being sworn in to face, and the teacher-turned-chair who spoke movingly about multilingual families would go on to champion the reconfiguration policies that severed those relationships.

2. **2025-12-04** — Three days after the inauguration, the council conducted a full regular meeting on junkyard licenses, liquor licenses, and administrative process with zero school budget discussion; the rejection of Zoom public comment built the first concrete barrier to community participation, and city council disengagement is confirmed as deliberate operating posture rather than a one-time ceremonial exception.

3. **2025-12-08** — An immediate non-personnel spending freeze and a $1.3–1.9M deficit from the very contract the district claimed was budgeted shatter Marcus's trust in administration's budget messaging, opening concrete anxiety about classroom supply shortfalls, substitute coverage gaps, and whether the ELO program can survive on a single staff member.

4. **2025-12-09** — The 170 Ocean TIF deal — a 50% property tax credit for 30 years — shifts Marcus's understanding of the city council from passive bystander to active fiscal architect: municipal decisions structurally constrain the tax base available to schools for a generation, and the council is not merely inattentive to the education crisis but actively shaping the revenue constraints that define it.

5. **2025-12-10** — The board's approval of $24,000 for a superintendent search firm that recommended excluding community and teacher voices reveals a governance failure deeper than financial mismanagement: administration will spend premium money to avoid educator input while preparing to cut 42 teachers — the crisis has broken institutional priorities, not just budgets.

6. **2025-12-17** — The full scale of proposed cuts becomes concrete: 42 teachers, 16 ed techs, only 2 administrators — a disproportionate burden on frontline educators for a staffing-enrollment misalignment driven by leadership decisions while enrollment dropped 300 students, compounded by a state covering only 20% of what its formula requires.

7. **2026-01-06** — The city manager's announcement of the February 10 joint budget guidance workshop signals to Marcus (in retrospect) that the council is actively shaping the school budget fight through procedural moves: the February 10 session is where the 6% ceiling originates, making this ostensibly quiet meeting a strategic pivot point, not a routine agenda item.

8. **2026-01-12** — With the board approving elementary reconfiguration study, retirements not being backfilled, and FY26 tracking at 92% of funds expended at the midyear mark, Marcus shifts from understanding the spending freeze as a temporary precaution to recognizing the current fiscal year is already in deficit — the freeze is triage, not caution.

9. **2026-01-13** — A four-hour city workshop on a $194 million building project establishes the city as a direct fiscal competitor for voter goodwill: the $2.26/$1,000 bond tax impact lands on the same property tax base as the school budget increase, public commenters are already describing themselves as financially exhausted, and the $900,000 rooftop garden line item will follow Marcus as a damaging image he cannot unknow.

10. **2026-01-15** — The council's annual goal-setting session — with the full crisis briefing distributed as background, including 42 teacher eliminations and the 20%-vs.-55% state funding failure — produces only an unreleased attachment; even the most consequential planning occasion of the year generates no visible public commitment to school funding, and the silence is now a completed answer rather than a gap.

11. **2026-01-20** — ICE raids in Red Bank cancel the January 22 budget forum; the families DeAngelis invoked during her inauguration speech are too frightened to attend public meetings, and Marcus's understanding of the threat to organized community voice shifts from institutional friction to active federal displacement of the most vulnerable families from civic participation entirely.

12. **2026-02-03** — The city auditor names what Marcus has been living inside: a repeat material weakness, 34 corrective journal entries, $400,000 in misallocated grant funds, six finance directors in five years; the budget projections used to justify eliminating 42 positions were built on a finance function formally documented as non-compliant for multiple consecutive years.

13. **2026-02-04** — The public budget forum reveals the administration is weaponizing the staffing-enrollment data: the 82 positions added were for documented ELL, special ed, and behavioral health needs the district itself approved, not administrative bloat, and a $3M+ gap beyond the "big ideas" means the administration is withholding even worse news than it has presented.

14. **2026-02-09** — All FY26 budget scenarios confirm deficit — already underwater, not just tracking toward it; Chair DeAngelis's "fabulous" remark on reconfiguration and dismissal of parent concerns signals the outcome is predetermined; but Feller's early retirement buyout idea and the union's impact bargaining rights emerge as the first real alternatives to direct layoffs.

15. **2026-02-10** — The joint session confirms the 82 added positions were mostly ed techs and teachers for complex student needs — meaning proposed cuts specifically undo capacity built for the most vulnerable students — while six weeks of stonewalling on the central office staffing breakdown has shifted Marcus's suspicion from incompetence to deliberate concealment.

16. **2026-02-17** — The February 10 joint session produced no durable attention shift — one week later, 42 teacher positions are invisible in five hours of council business — and the ICE crisis has moved from suppressing forum attendance to degrading classroom attendance itself: those empty seats are in Marcus's gradebook and his colleagues' rosters.

17. **2026-03-02** — The seniority revelation transforms Marcus's vulnerability map: bumping means any teacher anywhere in the district is potentially subject to elimination regardless of which buildings are reconfigured, superseding the question of which departments lose positions; the board's rejection of Skillin closure proves organized community resistance works, but the superintendent remains committed to reconfiguration.

18. **2026-03-05** — A second major capital referendum materializes: an $87 million public safety bond (November 2026) adds a third distinct fiscal ask to the same voter base already facing the June 9 school referendum and sewer rate increases, and the library director's testimony about 25 years of deferred needs sits in Marcus's mind as a mirror of exactly how public institutions die one reasonable deferral at a time.

19. **2026-03-09** — The crisis resolves into confirmed insolvency: the fund balance is fully depleted, the nutrition program carries a $1.1M debt threatening the city's credit rating, and the district must serve 80–90 new special education students in September with no new revenue — no longer a severe but potentially manageable crisis, but a controlled demolition of public education infrastructure.

20. **2026-03-10** — Teacher outreach to councilors is already happening — Matthews disclosed his phone was filled with direct texts from teachers and ed techs — but the political effect is inverted: educator distress is being absorbed into a general fiscal-caution argument rather than channeled into protective action for specific positions, and the April 14 budget workshop is confirmed as the operative arena.

21. **2026-03-19** — The deliberation phase ends: 72 RIF notices went out on March 18, Matthews called them the first wave, the gap is now stated at $8.4 million, the budget calendar is locked (April 7 public hearing, April 14 workshop, May 5 council vote, June 9 referendum), and sewer rate increases of ~22% annually compound the voter household burden before the referendum; the fight over proposals is now a fight to reverse actions already taken.

22. **2026-03-23** — Finance Director Ketchem states the structural truth that changes everything: labor costs grow faster than the 6% ceiling every contractual year, making this not a one-time emergency but the first iteration of a recurring machine; simultaneously, the 42 teacher RIF disaggregates into 32 on recall and 11 on involuntary transfer, Blair Bacon's challenge to seniority rules creates a potent public counter-narrative, and the percussion ed tech position last fought and won is back on the list.
