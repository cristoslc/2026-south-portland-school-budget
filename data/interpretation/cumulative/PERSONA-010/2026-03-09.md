# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- March 9, 2026

### Interpretation

One week after the marathon budget workshop, this regular meeting finds Ben surveying the full landscape of a budget crisis that has consumed the district since December. The numbers have not improved. The $8M gap persists, the fund balance remains depleted, 78 positions still face elimination, and enrollment continues its decline. What has changed is the political and community terrain: the administration's top reconfiguration recommendation is dead, parents are mobilized and angry, the union has drawn its line against endorsing colleague layoffs, and the board is splintering between those who want to push past 6% and those facing council pressure to stay at 3-4%.

The nutrition program deficit -- $1.1M that was not being tracked properly until February -- remains an unresolved drain that compounds every other problem. Ben notes that this is the third time a major cost has surfaced after the district told the public it had the full picture: first the missed contract costs in December, then the nutrition fund in February, and throughout it all the administrative position growth data the union has been requesting for three months without receiving. The pattern of incomplete disclosure is becoming as much a story as the budget gap itself.

For Ben, this meeting is a moment of synthesis. He has attended or watched nine meetings since December 8th. He has tracked the deficit grow from $1.9M to $7.2M to "closer to $8M." He has watched the board hire a $35,000 search firm while announcing a spending freeze, celebrate community donations while failing to organize an education foundation, invite public engagement while acknowledging they cannot manage the volume, and recommend closing a school while four board members immediately reject the recommendation. The central tension of his coverage is now clear: this is a district that knows what its data says but cannot act on it, because every data-driven solution runs into political, equity, or community resistance.

The piece Ben needs to write now is the one that connects all of these dots for readers who have been getting fragments. Not just the numbers -- readers can find those in the budget packet. The story is about institutional capacity: can a district that added 82 positions while losing 300 students, that missed contract costs and nutrition deficits, that cannot produce its own administrative staffing data, actually execute a complex reconfiguration in five months? Ben is increasingly skeptical, and his skepticism is grounded in nine meetings' worth of evidence.
