# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

The January 6 city council meeting is where the scaffold of everything that followed starts to become visible, even if Maria couldn't see it clearly at the time.

Scott's announcement of the February 10 budget guidance workshop — where council would tell the school board what tax increase level is "acceptable" — is the detail that lands hardest in hindsight. Maria didn't know on January 6th what guidance council would give. She knows now: 3-6%, with most councilors at 4-5%, below the school board's own requested floor, meaning the cuts would have to run deeper than even the board's worst-case scenarios. That workshop was scheduled here, in this room, and Maria was looking past it toward the January 22nd citizen forum instead.

The January 22nd forum is its own kind of retroactive ache. DeAngelis announced a school board citizen forum specifically on cost-saving measures — parents invited to weigh in before decisions are made. Maria was planning to go. She was going to bring neighbors. But from March 2026, after watching the board advance reconfiguration against near-unanimous parent opposition, after watching the chair call it "a fabulous idea" before any formal proposal existed, the January 22 forum looks like the opening gesture of a participation theater that would run all the way through the March workshops. The input gathered there didn't slow the machinery.

The state property tax relief task force is the kind of thing Maria wants to believe in — finally, state-level attention on the crushing local property tax burden. But she has learned to hold these promises lightly. A task force with a first report due in January and a final report at year's end is operating on a fundamentally different timeline than her daughter's classroom, where 42 teaching positions are on the line for September 2026.

What warms her, just a little, is the community response to ordinary need. Two thousand five hundred gallons of heating oil from Global Partners. Over a thousand dollars for heating assistance and the library. These are the same instincts she sees in her parent networks, the same impulse that fills PTA donation drives and coordinates school supply runs during the spending freeze. People stepping up when institutions falter. She wants that energy in the budget fight. She hopes it will be enough. So far, the institutions have not seemed particularly moved by it.
