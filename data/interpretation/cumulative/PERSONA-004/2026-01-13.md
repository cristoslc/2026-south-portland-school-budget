# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

I wasn't supposed to care about this meeting. A municipal facilities workshop — city hall renovation, not schools. But I pulled it up anyway, and by the time Ellen Sanborn put the tax math on the screen, I couldn't stop watching.

$194 million. That's the number for the Mahoney City Center proposal. $153 million for the stripped-down alternative. Both drawing from the same property tax base that's supposed to rescue 42 of my colleagues from the chopping block. I've been sitting in school board meetings since December watching administrators explain why there's simply no money — the structural gap is real, the math is brutal, the 6% ceiling is the political reality. And here's the city pricing out a building project that dwarfs the entire school budget crisis by an order of magnitude.

The tax impact Sanborn quantified — $2.26 per thousand, roughly $1,161 a year for a typical South Portland homeowner — doesn't exist in isolation. That's $1,161 of taxpayer tolerance that won't be available when the school board needs it. And the parents who stood at the microphone during public comment and described crying at their tax assessment notices? Those are my parents. The households I send newsletters home to. The voters SPEA needs in order to make the case that protecting 42 teaching positions is worth funding.

The rooftop garden is going to haunt me. I know it's not why the project costs $194 million. But $900,000 for a rooftop garden — that's seven or eight colleague salaries. That number is going to end up on social media next to the FTE elimination list, and someone is going to be very angry. I don't blame them.

What I keep coming back to is the pattern I first noticed in December: city government pursuing its own institutional agenda while the school district careens toward insolvency, treating the two tracks as if they don't share the same finite pool of voter goodwill. December 1 was inauguration speeches. December 4 was junkyard licenses. January 6 announced the February joint session. And January 13 is a four-hour workshop on a $194 million building — while the $7.2 million school gap, and the 78 humans attached to it, gets a single passing mention about a geothermal grant.

At least Councilor Matthews said out loud what teachers have been saying in hallways: this bond will not pass. Then the council advanced it anyway.
