# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

January 6th was a quiet agenda by any surface reading — a cemetery ordinance, rules cleanup, scheduling housekeeping. But watching it now from March, after everything that followed, I see three things this meeting actually contained.

The February 10th budget guidance workshop was formally locked in. Three weeks after December 17th's grim confirmation of the $7.2M structural deficit, Scott putting that date on the record was the first signal that the intergovernmental process would actually run on schedule. I'd been watching the council carefully since the December 9th city meeting, when I learned that rising local assessments automatically reduce our state subsidy. I needed council to engage seriously, not perform engagement. Knowing now what happened at February 10th — the 3-6% guidance range, the Prince warning that 3% means 50+ additional staff cuts, the fund balance reality finally quantified — I can mark January 6th as the moment the clock formally started for the hardest conversation we'd have.

The property tax relief task force was the other piece that caught my attention. Mayor Tipton reporting that the state legislature's task force was due out this month, with a final report by year's end, was the kind of news that feels hopeful in January and looks naive by March. I've testified at the statehouse about EPS formula problems. I understand why people want to believe Augusta is working on it. But a task force report doesn't arrive in time to reduce the 78 position cuts, and nothing from that process will change the math for our validation campaign. The state gap — 20% of actual costs versus the 55% formula target — has been my frustration for years. I noted the task force and moved on.

The executive session on police and fire union contracts was the part I sat with longest. Contract negotiations run on their own calendar, and I don't begrudge the city's employees reasonable wages. But every public safety settlement signals something to me about the fiscal environment the school board operates in. Personnel costs are 80% of our budget and the least controllable. Knowing the city was locking in new contract terms in January while we were still scrambling to close a $7.2M gap — that asymmetry is the chronic condition I've spent years trying to name.

DeAngelos announcing the January 22nd forum at a city council meeting, publicly and on record, is worth noting. It doesn't resolve my December concern about her communication posture on the budget crisis, but it's movement.
