# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Goal Setting Workshop -- January 15, 2026 -- 2026-01-15

### Interpretation

Two days after sitting through four hours of Mahoney deliberation and watching Montgomery's immigrant access testimony vanish from the council record in three minutes, Sheanna looks up the January 15 city council workshop and finds a single line: *Annual Goal-Setting Session. Contains an attachment.* That's it. No transcript. No discussion notes. No attachment text. Nothing to read on her laptop during a planning period. Nothing to argue with.

She has been tracking the participation barrier since December 4. The pattern she documented — credible voice names the structural problem in the official record, institutional silence follows — runs at the school board level and now at the city council level too. But January 15 produces something different and in some ways worse: the barrier is no longer about which families can make it to the room. It's about whether the room produces any public account of itself at all. She cannot be silenced in a meeting she can't see. She cannot follow up on a discussion that isn't recorded. She is an engaged, professional, cross-building advocate who reads every document she can get her hands on — and she is shut out by a one-line agenda item the same as everyone else.

What makes this particular opacity sting is what she knows is sitting underneath it. The 6% tax ceiling the school board is operating within — the constraint that turns a structural funding gap into 78 position eliminations — did not come from nowhere. It came from this body's political calculus. The goal-setting session on January 15 is the meeting where the council decides what the year looks like. Whether anyone in that room said *we are about to eliminate 42 teachers and 16 ed techs and that is a goals-year constraint* is entirely unknown from the available record. And the February 10 joint workshop, when the council officially tells the school board what is acceptable, is now 26 days away.

The enrollment and staffing numbers from the fiscal context are already worrying her in a specific way. Twenty-three percent enrollment decline, 82 positions added. That is the narrative that will be used to justify every cut on the list — it is accurate and it is weaponizable and it will land in every public conversation between now and the vote. She has been in three buildings watching what those 82 positions actually do. Some of them were added to close the very equity gaps she documents on her caseload. The number does not say that. Nobody who wasn't in those buildings will know to ask. And the January 15 record won't help her find out if anyone in that room knew to ask either.
