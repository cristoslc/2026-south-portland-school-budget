# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Workshop -- January 13, 2026 -- 2026-01-13

### Interpretation

The Mahoney price tag finally landed. I'd been watching this thing come toward me since December 1st, when the new mayor stood at her inauguration talking about transforming the old school building into a city center while the school budget was already imploding in the background. Then the city manager teased the "dollar tag" at the January 6th meeting. Now it's official: $194 million.

Except it's not really $194 million — that's the floor. When a councilor asked the architect straight up how confident he was in that number, the architect said — I'll remember this — "I am not confident, Dickie." Tariffs, structural unknowns, construction market volatility. Nobody can tell you what the ceiling is. And if you want to skip the whole Mahoney consolidation and just renovate the existing buildings in place? Still $153 to $154 million. There's no cheap path here.

What I keep coming back to is the geometry. The school district has a $7.2 million structural gap and still can't close $3 million of it. Stack the Mahoney bond on top: $1,160 more per year on the average home if they borrow it all at once. Then add the Pearl Street pump station — $50 million, no voter approval required, coming through sewer rates regardless of what anyone thinks about it. Ed Cobb stood up at the microphone and said his taxes started at $1,600 when he moved here in 1993 and are heading to $6,000. David Bertoni did the math: $194 million with thirty years of interest totals $300 million, roughly $10,000 of debt per household. These aren't slogans. These are calculations.

The council pushed back — most of them said they wouldn't take $194 million to a referendum — but here's what they didn't do: set a number. They sent the team back for "bare bones" alternatives and scope tiers without naming what this city can actually afford. The meter is still running. $780,000 already spent on design. Another $400-500,000 to reach June. Sunk costs if November fails. And the moment that stopped me cold: this is almost exactly how the TIF vote played out in December. Councilor West said he'd been misled on that deal. Then voted yes. Verbal skepticism, no binding action. I'm watching the same script.
