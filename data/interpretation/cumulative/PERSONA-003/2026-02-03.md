# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

The February 3 city council meeting is the day before the budget forum where Jess finally learns what reconfiguration means. From where she's sitting now, having lived through everything that followed, this meeting retroactively fills in a piece of the picture she was missing. She thought she understood the financial crisis: enrollment collapse, state underfunding, $7.2 million gap, 78 positions. What she didn't know was the institutional chaos underneath all of that.

Six finance directors in five years. Thirty-four journal entries when there should be under ten. Four hundred thousand dollars in grant funds that had to be moved because the district couldn't keep track of eligibility or was simply overspending them. A school budget that exceeded its legally adopted limit for the second year in a row — technically a violation of state law. The February 10 meeting will later tell her that the 82 positions added since 2017 were largely mandate-driven, not discretionary, which contextualizes the staffing numbers. But this audit information tells a different story alongside it: the financial management has been genuinely broken for years, not just structurally constrained. These aren't competing explanations. They're compounding ones. The district was navigating mandated costs it couldn't control while simultaneously failing to reconcile its own books. Jess has been inclined to read the budget crisis as something that happened to the district — enrollment fell, the state didn't pay its share — and she still believes that. But now she has to hold a second truth at the same time: the people running the finances weren't keeping up with any of it.

The city has $28.8 million in undesignated fund balance. After reserves and expected school overages of one to two million dollars, about two million is actually available. That number lands strangely. It confirms the city already expects the schools to run over budget, is accounting for it as a recurring expense, and is treating it as routine financial planning rather than an emergency. It's not nothing. But it's also not a cushion — it's a line item for a problem everyone has quietly accepted.

What Jess didn't expect was DeAngelis. The school board chair she has been watching make two-sentence announcements at city council meetings since December got up and told the council directly: taxpayers get one tax bill, one bottom line, and "you are not off the hook." That is not the careful, informational DeAngelis of December 1 and December 9. That is a confrontation, delivered in a room where the school budget has been addressed until now as a brief public comment item between other business. This meeting is the first time Jess has seen anyone force the connection from the school side, out loud, in the council chamber. Whether anything changed as a result, she already knows the answer from three more months of watching. But someone finally said it.
