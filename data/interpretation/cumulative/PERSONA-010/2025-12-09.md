# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

December 9 is the missing panel in Ben's establishing sequence — squeezed between the night the school board discovered its deficit and the night they chose a search firm, it was easy to overlook. Watching it now, from the other side of nine school board meetings and an $8M fiscal reckoning, it delivers three things he needed: confirmation of the structural tax burden shift Cohen warned about on December 1, a live demonstration of how the city's development narrative actually works, and the most important financial fine print he has found all season.

DeAngelo's appearance is what hits hardest in retrospect. She came to city council the day after the school board learned it had a $1.9 million deficit — and she invited the public to engage with budget season, mentioned the superintendent search workshop the next night at SPHS, and said nothing about the crisis that had surfaced at the previous evening's meeting. Ben cannot tell from the recording whether she was still processing it, was advised to keep it quiet while numbers were being verified, or was already operating in the institutional mode of compartmentalizing bad news. Whatever the reason, December 9 is the day the school board's crisis and the city council's civic optimism occupied the same calendar without touching each other.

The assessor's briefing confirms what Ben has been assembling since Cohen's farewell speech: residential values rose roughly 3% while commercial values fell 2.5% this year. Homeowners are bearing a growing share of a tax base the city keeps promising will be relieved by new development. Then comes the One 70 Ocean vote, and Ben gets the honest arithmetic behind that promise. A 30-year, 50%-rebate TIF deal — approved when the project was 124 units, now at 208 — means the city already bargained away half its fiscal gain before the building opens. The assistant city manager explains the rest of the erosion: every dollar of new assessed value triggers a reduction in state education subsidy, leaving a net benefit of roughly 50 cents on the dollar before the TIF rebate even applies. Councilor West says "we've been misled," then votes yes. The development narrative survives the math it cannot withstand.

For Ben's referendum piece, this is the connective tissue. The city has been promising tax relief through growth. One 70 Ocean is the flagship. The arithmetic says the school budget gains almost nothing from it for thirty years. Meanwhile, the 18.95% school tax increase is a 2026 question, not a 2055 question.
