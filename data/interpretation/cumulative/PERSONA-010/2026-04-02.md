# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

Ben came to April 2 expecting a budget vote. He has been watching boards decline to vote since March — the March 23 adjournment at 11:15 without a single decision, the March 30 fractured 2-5 failure on the fiscal package. Tonight the board declines again, and this time he files it differently: the delay is actually the news.

Partway through public comment, DeSanto walked up to the mic and read from her phone. The teachers and staff who drove to Augusta in March, who sat down with lawmakers and made the case for their district, had apparently produced results: a likely $300,000 in new state money, split between homeless students and economically disadvantaged students. Then, during deliberations, Richardson held up her phone. A text. Potentially $750,000 more from a state EPS formula change — unconfirmed, possibly one-time. The board is recalculating everything on the fly. The April 7 council presentation is five days away. Nobody votes. The budget goes to council as the superintendent's budget, unadopted, which is itself a sentence Ben needs to explain to his readers.

The finance director's chronic under-budgeting slide is what he had been waiting for since December. He had the structural story from March 23 — seven directors in six years, no fund balance policy, COVID inflation never walked back. Tonight he has the annual mechanism: electricity overspent four years running, tuition reimbursement overspent every year, the district budgeted optimistically and paid for the wishful thinking in job cuts. That is the machine. The best-practice fund balance is 9–12% of operating costs — roughly $6.8 million — and the district has zero. Feller called it "negligent" on the record. That sentence is in the lede.

The board's open frustration about the DEI and special education administrative title changes confirms what Ben assembled from March 30: the moves saved almost nothing. Holman said so plainly. But now the board members themselves are saying it, which transforms Ben's framing from inference to quotable accountability.

The behavior specialist statement is what stays with him. Nearly 60 students, 40-plus formal behavior plans, and a direct warning: eliminate the middle layer and those children either get nothing or land in special education, which costs significantly more. The district's special education identification rate is already 23%, above comparable districts. Ben has been tracking this crisis in budget terms since December 17. Tonight he hears for the first time that the cuts themselves might accelerate the cost problem by shifting children from prevention into intervention. The computer science teacher's daughter spoke on his behalf, and then he came up himself — still not knowing what to tell his students. Ben wants to talk to both of them before Friday.
