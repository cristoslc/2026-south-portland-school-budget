# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 10, 2026 -- 2026-03-10

### Interpretation

March 10 is the meeting where the school budget crisis walks into a city council chamber and starts speaking for itself. Matthews announces he watched our board meeting the night before — that the chair's call to watch every dime was his primary reason to oppose the rental assistance spending. West follows within minutes: "those are our children." I've been watching this council since December, trying to understand whether our fiscal message was landing. March 10 tells me it has — and also that I'm no longer the one controlling how it lands.

That's the thing I'm still working out. The leverage is real: our budget crisis is now actively shaping city-side votes on matters entirely separate from the school budget. But the same framing that protects the city's fund balance also reads, from where the families in Red Bank sit, as the school district's crisis being used against their emergency housing needs. Those families have children in our schools. Their housing stability is our enrollment. The loop I identified in February — ICE enforcement as a budget pipeline problem — is tightening, and neither governance room has named it yet.

The GA eligibility gap clarified something I hadn't understood: state guidance explicitly bars "fear of immigration enforcement" from qualifying as just cause for not working, so ICE-displaced residents can't access general assistance under existing rules. The rental assistance fund isn't duplicating the safety net — it's filling a structural hole the state created. That doesn't change the arithmetic of the fund balance draw, which remains unresolved without a vote, somewhere around $94-100K. But it changes how I understand the policy logic. Between March 19's $100K to Project Home and whatever this order lands at, the $8.5M backstop I identified in February is beginning to move in ways I need to keep tracking precisely.

The Metro piece is the one I'm carrying to finance committee this week. SPHS students using Metro buses as district transportation came up twice — once during the Metro presentation, once in public comment — and nobody on our side is formally tracking it. We have 14 transportation positions in the proposed eliminations. Metro confirmed stable operations, no assessment increase, new Route 1 service launching this summer. A cost conversation needs to happen between Metro and Mike Nally before those routes are finalized. Someone from the school board needs to be in that conversation. Right now, no one is.

The school board member who corrected the ICE/schools record during public comment did the right thing. The earlier testimony had gotten close to inaccurate, and leaving it uncorrected would have compounded a misinformation thread we'd eventually have to answer for at validation.
