# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 5, 2026 -- 2026-03-05

### Interpretation

Three days after watching four board members push back on school closures at the March 2 workshop — the first real crack in the administration's predetermined plan — I'm at another city council meeting watching the pattern complete itself one more time. DeAngelis stands up and tells the council what I've been living: $8 million in cuts, 78 positions, the fund balance gone. Real numbers, in that chamber, finally. And then the council turns to an $87 million public safety bond.

I want to be precise about what I'm feeling, because it's not simple. The police station is falling apart. The fire station apparently needs to be demolished, not renovated — 25 years of deferred maintenance past the point of saving. I understand that. But since December 1 I've been mapping a municipal government with a finite supply of taxpayer goodwill managing a queue of competing institutional needs. In January I identified the Mahoney/city hall project as the main competitor for that goodwill. Now there's a second referendum — a November public safety vote — coming after the June 9 school budget referendum, drawing from the same households that are already being warned about sewer rate increases.

The library director's testimony hit me like a mirror. Twenty-five years of deferred needs. Twenty-seven visitors every hour. A building that serves 15,000 people in programs every year, being told "not yet." That's the exact thing we say to teachers about class sizes, about supply budgets, about ed tech positions. Public institutions don't fail dramatically — they erode steadily while successive councils and boards find reasons to prioritize something else. And then one day you're facing a demolition bill that dwarfs what maintenance would have cost, and nobody in that room remembers making the decision to defer.

The cumulative arithmetic is what I can't stop running. School referendum in June. Sewer increases starting FY27. Public safety bond in November. On the same property tax base, in the same year, hitting the same homeowners who have been telling every public meeting since December that they're at their limit. The council has never put those numbers side by side in one document and asked what these households can actually absorb — and I don't think they're going to.

I came into March 5 with the cautious energy from the March 2 board pushback. I'm leaving it with the arithmetic of a city asking for everything and betting voters say yes to all of it.
