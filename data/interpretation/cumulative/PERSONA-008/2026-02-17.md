# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

Rachel has been tracking this crisis across two governing bodies for nearly three months. She has watched budget forums get postponed, audit findings reveal institutional mismanagement, board members declare reconfiguration "fabulous," and the board unanimously vote to formalize a proposal. She thought she understood the scope of what was coming. Then she sat through four hours of immigration testimony at a city council meeting until almost midnight — and heard the mayor of South Portland explain the reconfiguration plan at a Mahoney facilities workshop like it was already settled policy.

That's the thing that shakes her most about February 17th. She didn't hear about the grade-split from a worried parent in a Facebook group, or from a leaked presentation at a school board meeting. She heard it from Mayor Tipton, correcting a community member's vocabulary in a matter-of-fact tone: not "consolidation," *reconfiguration*. Pre-K through first in two buildings, second through fourth in three others. Every current elementary student switches buildings at second grade. Two million dollars saved. The mayor knows the shape of this plan and is comfortable explaining it casually, at 10:30 at night, at a meeting about city hall. That tells Rachel something important about how far along this is inside the institution — far enough that the mayor has already absorbed the talking points.

The community member who proposed putting all elementary students on a single Mahoney campus is, in isolation, just one person's idea. But nobody pushed back on it. The city manager's clarification that the school board hasn't formally requested Mahoney back is the one guardrail Rachel is holding onto — but a guardrail isn't the same as a wall. She named the pattern back in January: real decisions get shaped before the public record documents them. An idea floated at 10:30 PM that goes neither endorsed nor rejected is an idea that remains available.

The testimony about children being kept home from school because of ICE fear hits differently alongside everything else. The kids being kept from their classrooms right now are the same kids the district says it has too few of to sustain neighborhood schools. Every absence compounds the enrollment math being used to justify reconfiguration. The crisis is not cleanly separable from its own causes.

She leaves past midnight, texting parents, with the specific dread of someone who just heard the decision being described in the past tense.
