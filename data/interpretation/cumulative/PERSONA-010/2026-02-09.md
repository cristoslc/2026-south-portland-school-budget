# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- February 9, 2026

### Interpretation

Five days after the public budget forum, this regular meeting reveals that the fiscal picture is even worse than what the district told the public. Finance Director Abigail Chen reports that all three budget scenarios now end in deficit for FY26 -- the current year, not next year. The "rosier" Scenario A that previously showed a possible surplus has evaporated, largely because the nutrition fund's $1.1M projected deficit has finally been surfaced. Ben's instinct from December 8th -- that the district's financial reporting was unreliable -- is confirmed again. They were not tracking a million-dollar deficit in their own cafeteria program.

The political dynamics shift visibly at this meeting. Chair DeAngelis declares she is "totally in support" of elementary reconfiguration, dismissing the neighborhood schools concept by noting South Portland already eliminated six schools by 1999. This is not neutral facilitation -- it is the board chair putting her thumb on the scale, and Ben knows it changes how every other board member calculates their vote. On the other side, SPTA President Sarah Sergay goes on record that educators "are not going to endorse plans that result in our colleagues losing their jobs," directly contradicting any rumored staff support for the proposed changes. The battle lines are drawn.

Meanwhile, Member Feller publicly walks back the board's informal 6% tax increase agreement, saying he would now be "more comfortable with something exceeding 6%" rather than making "draconian cuts." Ben reads this as the political consensus cracking. If even one board member is wavering publicly, the real number being discussed privately is probably higher. This is the political story behind the budget story, and Ben knows his readers need to understand that the 6% ceiling they heard about at the budget forum may not hold.

The community engagement system is buckling under demand -- Dr. Prince reports 51 data requests through the new online Q&A since the forums, acknowledging "we may have reached the unmanageable point." Ben files this as evidence that the district invited engagement it was not staffed to handle. Feller's early retirement buyout proposal -- estimating that 20 of 41 eligible staff taking $50K buyouts could yield $1M in savings -- is the first creative solution Ben has heard that does not involve either school closures or across-the-board cuts.
