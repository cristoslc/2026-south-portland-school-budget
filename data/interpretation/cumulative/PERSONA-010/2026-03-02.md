# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop I -- March 2, 2026

### Interpretation

After months of data and debate, this three-and-a-half-hour budget workshop is where the abstract becomes specific and the political becomes personal. The administration presents nine reconfiguration options (1.1 through 1.8 plus alternatives) with building-level detail, and recommends Option 1.7 -- closing Skillin Elementary and reconfiguring remaining schools into grade bands -- as the top-ranked choice at 46 points. Dr. Prince and the leadership team are recommending the option that saves the most money. The board immediately signals it is politically dead.

The equity angle is what transforms this from a budget story into a community story for Ben's readers. Chair DeAngelo argues passionately that closing Skillin would force all families "west of the Dairy Queen" to bus their children while concentrating all schools and amenities "east of Cash Corner," calling this "abhorrent." Tyler Smith makes the same point about how politically easy it is to bus black and brown kids. At least four board members declare opposition to closing Skillin. The superintendent's top recommendation is dead on arrival, and the remaining viable options all save less money while concentrating disruption elsewhere.

The budget gap has grown again. Chair DeAngelo corrects the figure to "closer to $8 million" that must be cut to stay within the 6% tax increase guidance -- larger than the $7.2M figure used since February. Meanwhile, Richardson points out a glaring omission: no academic achievement data was presented to justify reconfiguration on educational grounds, despite the administration framing it as good for students. Ben sees this as the most damning detail: if you are going to disrupt 300 families' lives because their children will learn better in a different configuration, show the evidence. They did not.

The three hours of public comment reveal parents who feel blindsided. The district keeps claiming transparency, but transparency is not the same as communication -- most families had no idea their child's school might change until word of mouth reached them. The union president, Sarah Gay, presses on a different transparency gap: the district still has not provided historical data on administrative position growth three months after the union requested it. Ben sees a pattern: the district is transparent about the things it wants to talk about and opaque about the things it does not.

Last year's reduction-in-force notification -- described as "a disaster" involving impersonal emails -- remains unaddressed. When asked about a more humane approach, Dr. Prince promises "more of an update on that for next week" without specifics. The meeting ends after three and a half hours with no decisions, only promises of more workshops.
