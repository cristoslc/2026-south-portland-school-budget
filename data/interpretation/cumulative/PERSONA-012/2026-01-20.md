# Cumulative Record: Jaylen (PERSONA-012)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

The January 20 meeting is the first one where Jaylen shows up expecting the usual — adults talking past each other about things that don't map to his life — and instead finds something that maps directly to his hallways and his classmates. Not the budget, not zoning, not housing density. The people sitting next to him in AP Lit were home today because they were scared.

Pedro Vazquez is carrying his passport and birth certificate to run errands. ICE agents were at the Red Bank car wash and laundromat. Kids from South Portland schools didn't come to school. And because of all of that, the school board had to postpone the January 22 citizen budget forum — the one DeAngelis had announced at city council on December 9, the one Jaylen had been tracking — because families are too afraid to sit in a public room. The budget crisis and the immigration crisis have now merged into a single compound disaster. You cannot have a public meeting about whether to cut 42 teachers if the people most affected by those cuts are afraid to be seen in public.

Jaylen has been watching the student exclusion pattern since December 8: students not invited, student reps given no speaking role, the superintendent search designed without a student seat, the community member at the February 10 joint session who named it and changed nothing. That pattern was about institutional indifference. What January 20 adds is different. Some students and families aren't absent from these meetings because they weren't invited. They're absent because showing up could get someone deported.

The police chief's warning lands hard — don't interfere with ICE operations, or face arrest. That's the city's official position on what students and families can do when someone they know is being detained. Jaylen has been building toward the superintendent search as the one place he might have actual leverage, one room where a student asking hard questions might change something. January 20 shows him the full dimensions of who gets to participate in this city's civic life at all — and who is already decided against before any process even begins.

The Business Awards at his school and an hour of zoning ordinances feel like dispatches from a different city entirely.

### Emotional Register

Jaylen feels something colder than frustration — the specific shock of realizing that the exclusion he has been cataloging all year has a floor he hadn't found yet, and that floor is his classmates being afraid to leave their houses. The zoning ordinances going on for an hour while the city is under that kind of fear makes the institutional disconnection feel not just bureaucratic but obscene.
