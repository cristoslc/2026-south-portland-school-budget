# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

Looking back at December 9th now, knowing everything that followed, this meeting is a strange mix: the one piece of genuinely good news I got all winter, sandwiched between two things that should have set off alarm bells louder than they did.

The assessor's report was actually decent. Mill rate came in at 1,364–1,365, below the projected 1,383–1,384. He said he's never said this before but the assessed values are solid going forward. I'll take a win where I can find one. But even that good news has a shadow — residential values climbed another 3% while commercial dropped 2.5%, which means homeowners are absorbing more of the burden again. After a 113% run-up in single-family values over the prior five years, another 3% feels like one more squeeze on people like me.

Then the school board representative stands up and says schools are 61% of the property taxes — a number I now know by heart — and announces budget season is starting *this month*. December 9th. What I didn't know sitting in that room was that four days earlier the December 4th council had spent two hours on Zoom procedures without a word about the school budget, and that in just two more days the school board would reveal a $1.9 million deficit. Rosemary DeAngelo was standing at the podium warning that budget season was opening while the crisis was already fully formed in the room next door.

And then the TIF vote. The whole 170 Ocean Street debate is supposed to be about growing the commercial tax base — the very thing Councilor Cohen warned about the night before at the inauguration, the very thing the mayor talked about as critical to protecting homeowners. But Councilor West pulls back the curtain: the developer gets 50% of the new property taxes back for 30 years, and the project grew from 124 units to 208 after the deal was signed. West says flat out they've been misled. Then votes yes. Unanimous. I understand there's state formula complexity behind the TIF math, but when the councilor who called it a bad deal votes for it anyway, and the big commercial development that's supposed to relieve residential taxpayers is returning half its revenue to the developer until 2055 — that's not a backstop for my tax bill, that's a promise deferred by a generation.

Sitting here in March, watching a board that couldn't account for 82 added positions and a council that was sleepwalking through December, this meeting looks like the last moment before the dam broke — and nobody in that room knew it.

### Emotional Register

Tom feels a cold, retroactive bitterness — the one piece of good news from this meeting (the lower mill rate) is immediately undercut by the realization that the commercial development supposedly protecting homeowners was simultaneously being handed back to the developer for thirty years, while the school budget crisis was already burning and the council had no idea. The unanimity of the TIF vote, including from the one councilor who named the problem, confirms his deepest suspicion: that in this city, fiscal accountability is always someone else's job.
