# How Local Opinion Journalism Serves Civic Discourse and Democracy

*Source: American Press Institute — https://americanpressinstitute.org/how-local-opinion-journalism-serves-civic-discourse-and-democracy/*

## Overview

According to Kevin Loker's report from the American Press Institute, local opinion journalism performs three critical functions for democratic health: **modeling, equipping, and facilitating** healthy civic discourse.

## Three Key Functions

### 1. Modeling Healthy Civic Discourse

Local opinion sections demonstrate respectful engagement across differences. People lack examples that model healthy civic discourse, and social norms significantly influence behavior.

**Example: The Tennessean (Nashville)**
The paper established "Civility Tennessee," a set of guiding principles emphasizing civil discourse and diverse viewpoints. Their "Tennessee Voices" video podcast has conducted 400+ episodes featuring authentic conversations with community leaders — intentionally demonstrating productive dialogue on contentious local issues like school vouchers and political violence.

### 2. Equipping Residents for Civic Discourse

Opinion sections teach argumentation and persuasion skills necessary for democratic participation. Democracy requires "organizing, participation, awareness, and negotiation" beyond voting alone.

**Example: Kansas City Star**
Editorial staff actively **coach contributors from underrepresented communities** on writing craft and argumentation structure. This coaching helps writers develop transferable skills for community organizing and leadership while expanding whose voices appear in the opinion section.

Editors seek to retain the author's voice while making gentle suggestions about sentences and organization — coaching the writer improves the content for the reader. They pay special attention to people in underrepresented communities and different age groups, encouraging them to submit columns.

### 3. Facilitating Healthy Civic Discourse

Opinion sections provide structured forums for community problem-solving and deliberation, offering independence from government incentives.

**Example: The Coloradoan (Fort Collins)**
Through partnership with Colorado State University's Center for Public Deliberation, the paper created "Coloradoan Conversations," hosting online discussions on local issues. Staff synthesized conversations using deliberation expertise, analyzing arguments, highlighting key themes, and advancing dialogue — ultimately driving subscriptions while improving discourse quality.

## Broader Implications

These functions benefit both communities and media sustainability:

- Opinion journalism that models, equips, and facilitates discourse **increases reader engagement**
- It attracts partnerships with civic institutions
- It builds support for local news organizations facing economic pressures

## For Community Contributors

- Local opinion journalism provides a structured, moderated forum for community voices
- Contributors don't need professional journalism training — what matters is grounded argumentation and authentic community perspective
- Opinion editors who coach contributors help more voices reach more readers
- **Amplifying underrepresented voices** is an explicit value in healthy civic journalism

## Separation of News and Opinion

- Opinions expressed on the editorial page should stay there — they should **not affect news coverage**
- Individual reporters shouldn't slant their stories to reflect or rebut editorial opinions
- The editorial firewall is foundational to reader trust in both news and opinion sections
