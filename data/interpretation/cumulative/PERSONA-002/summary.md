# Cumulative Summary: David (PERSONA-002)

### Current Understanding

David is a data-driven analyst who has tracked South Portland's fiscal crisis from its pre-disclosure silence in December 2025 through the school board's second consecutive failure to ratify the FY27 budget on April 2, 2026.

The district's structural breakdown is now documented in primary sources. Finance Director Ketchem delivered a four-year actuals table confirming systematic under-budgeting across electricity and tuition reimbursement lines — the mechanical explanation for the December 8 deficit disclosure. The FY27 proposed budget meets the 6% tax ceiling, translating to roughly $257 per year for the average homeowner. That is a genuine one-year success. But the fund balance is fully exhausted, the 6% ceiling is already exceeded by labor contract auto-escalation in FY28, utilities are rising 13–14% annually, and the athletic turf bond adds at least $300K in new debt service next year. The board voted to close Kaler and adopt Option A reconfiguration, then failed to ratify the implementing budget in two consecutive sessions — most recently deferring on the strength of two unverified text messages about possible state funding.

The city side compounds the picture. South Portland carries $89.1M in outstanding bonds, a $51.7M sewer revenue obligation producing 22% annual rate increases for three years, and a Mahoney renovation project ranging $70–76M with no adopted scope or financing model, now deferred to a November 2027 referendum. A November 2026 public safety bond ($80–87M) is advancing toward referendum. No governing body has produced an integrated household taxpayer impact analysis combining all of these obligations with the school operating gap. The one councilor who named this connection — Matthews — was outvoted 6–1. David has been building that combined model in a private spreadsheet since February.
