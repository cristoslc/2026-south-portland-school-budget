# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- January 12, 2026

### Interpretation

The finance committee confirmed what the data has been telling us for months: at the halfway mark of the fiscal year, 92% of funds are already expended or encumbered, and the projection range runs from a $534K surplus to a $1.4M deficit, with the committee saying we are "likely to be in the red." Claire was direct about it -- no amount of accounting tricks changes the actual dollars. That kind of honesty is exactly what I have been asking for since December.

Board Chair DeAngelis framed this as "another difficult budget year" and is pushing hard for broader community participation at a public budget forum scheduled for January 22. She specifically called out non-parent taxpayers, noting that school taxes represent 61% of the total property tax bill. That framing matters -- it acknowledges this is not just a school parent issue but a community-wide fiscal question.

Two developments gave me genuine encouragement. First, Tyler Smith asked for data on how the spending freeze is actually affecting day-to-day operations in schools -- you cannot evaluate a policy tool without measuring its real impact, and that request shows the analytical rigor I hoped for from the new board. Second, the teachers union president asked for structured engagement rather than just surveys. She was clear that staff will not try to write the budget but want to review actual proposals and give professional feedback. That is exactly how stakeholder input should work: people contributing expertise within their domain rather than everyone opining on everything.

The superintendent confirmed that the seven staff resignations and retirements will not be automatically backfilled -- positions will be evaluated through the budget process. That is a sensible approach to natural attrition during a deficit, and it partially addresses my earlier thread about how the spending freeze would translate into operational decisions.
