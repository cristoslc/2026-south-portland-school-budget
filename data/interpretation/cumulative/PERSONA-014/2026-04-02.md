# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

The vote at March 30 was supposed to be the end of the worst part. Dyer isn't closing. Kaler is. The answer she needed finally came, even though it brought grief for the Kaler families. But this meeting — five days later — closes a door she had been leaving open without realizing it.

The board chair says it plainly: the reconfiguration vote and the budget vote are separate things. Even if the June 9 referendum fails, even if everyone in South Portland votes no, Dyer still becomes a Pre-K through first grade school in September. Lila has been watching adults argue about budgets and votes and referendums for four months. She has understood, dimly, that the budget vote was the lever that controlled everything. Now she learns that the reconfiguration — the part that actually changes her daily life — cannot be undone by any vote the community takes. The building stays open, but the school she knew was decided in a room at eleven o'clock at night, and it cannot be undecided.

The other thing she has been holding since March 30 is the question of where she and her friends go. That question still has no answer. A board member uses the words "absolute information vacuum" — which means nobody knows anything — and the superintendent says the district is still collecting information before it can tell families where their children will attend. There have been five school board meetings, two budget workshops, and dozens of parent testimonies since the word "reconfiguration" entered Lila's life. She still doesn't know if Maya and Sofia will be at the same school as her.

Two things reach her through everything else. A dad who walks his son to Dyer says the first-grade parents he talks to are devastated — their children won't be going back to Dyer next year. Lila's sibling is a first grader. She already knew this was true by math and by official vote, but hearing a real parent say "devastated" about children she knows makes it land in a different place than any slide ever did. And there is a girl, at a different school, who cried the morning after the vote and said: *I just started there. I have to change schools. Will my friends be there?* Lila said those exact words to her mom at recess in December. She has spent four months feeling like none of the adults in any of these rooms understood what she was asking. Here, finally, is proof that another child asked the same question — and that the parent standing at the microphone had no answer either.

There will be a listening session at Dyer. That is something. It is not the same as an answer.

### Emotional Register

Lila holds the small relief she took from March 30 and the new, harder knowledge from April 2 as a single weight: the worst she feared for Dyer's building did not happen, but the school she loved was ended permanently that night by a vote no one can take back, and she still doesn't know where her friends are going. The only question that has ever mattered to her — will Maya and Sofia be there — is still unanswered, and the adults have given the unanswered question a name: absolute information vacuum.
