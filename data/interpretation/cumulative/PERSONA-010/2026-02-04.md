# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Public Budget Forum -- February 4, 2026

### Interpretation

This is the meeting where the district finally puts the full picture in front of the public, and Ben can see both the honesty and the gaps. Dr. Prince presents the enrollment-versus-staffing chart that Ben has been assembling from fragments since December 17th -- enrollment at its lowest in a decade while staffing kept climbing -- and calls it a "math problem." Ben would call it a management problem that became a community crisis, but the chart itself is the story. It is the visual that will make readers understand everything.

The tax arithmetic is brutal and now fully exposed. A roll-forward budget requires an 18.95% tax increase. The board has capped the school portion at 6%. That leaves a $7.2M gap. The district's three "big ideas" -- elementary reconfiguration (~$2M), grades 5-12 efficiency review (~$1M), and operational changes (~$1M) -- get them to roughly $4M in savings. That leaves over $3M with no plan. Ben notes the structural dishonesty of presenting partial solutions to the public and asking for input as though the math is not already unforgiving.

The elementary reconfiguration details emerge for the first time: shifting from geographic boundaries to grade bands, with average class sizes moving from about 16 to 18-19 for K-2 and 22-23 for grades 2-4. The potential $2M savings is the single largest lever, but the implementation details are thin. Middle school bell-time consolidation, which could save $400-500K, has been pulled from the table because it requires DOT traffic studies that cannot be completed by September 2026 -- bureaucratic reality hitting budget fantasy, as Ben sees it.

The health insurance projection jumping from 8% to 12% since December is the kind of moving target that makes Ben question whether any of these numbers will hold. The district is budgeting against unknowns while the knowns keep getting worse. And the fund balance -- confirmed again as fully depleted -- means there is no cushion if any projection breaks the wrong way. Ben's story is now clear: the district is asking the community to weigh in on partial solutions to a problem they have not fully solved, under a timeline that does not accommodate the complexity of the choices.
