# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 3, 2026 -- 2026-02-03

### Interpretation

This meeting is the last city council meeting before the February 4 forum — the one where Lila's mom finally came home and explained grade bands and intermediate schools and class sizes growing to 22. On February 3, Lila doesn't know that yet. She just knows that all the grown-ups are still arguing about who is supposed to fix things while the problems keep multiplying.

The money problems have gotten worse in a new direction. It isn't just that there isn't enough money — the district apparently couldn't even track what money it had. Thirty-four corrections in the accounting. Four hundred thousand dollars moved around to fix mistakes. Lila has known since first grade that sloppy counting has consequences. The adults who run her school made 34 mistakes in their counting and had to move what sounds to her like an enormous sum just to fix the books. The new finance director is described as "amazing" and already fixing things. Lila has heard this before. They hired a $30,000 search firm and described them as the right choice, and the crisis is still here. They hired a new superintendent and set his salary at $165,000, and the nine scenarios with Dyer listed as "CLOSED" still got presented. New people don't change the structural problem. She knows this now in her bones, even if she can't say it in those words.

The school board chair's voice is the sharpest thing in this meeting. When she tells the city council it's going to be a "very difficult budget year" and asks them to "give clear guidance" — Lila hears the frustration underneath the formal words. Adults use "difficult" the way they use "reconfiguration": as a word for something worse than they're willing to say plainly. Lila won't know until February 10 how badly this relationship between the board and the city council has frayed. But she can already hear it starting to crack.

The part that makes her stomach hurt is Brown School. Five-year-olds scared. Families sitting in dark houses at night, not turning on the lights. In January she heard about kids in Red Bank staying home. Now it's an elementary school in her own city, and it's kindergartners. The city passed a resolution saying everyone should feel safe going to school. Lila has watched adults announce forums that got canceled, pass votes that didn't close the $7.2 million gap, and say warm words that changed nothing. A resolution is a piece of paper. The five-year-olds were still afraid.

The forum is tomorrow. After everything — the January 22nd postponement, the weeks of waiting, the immigration fear that kept families home — it's finally happening. Her mom is going. Lila doesn't know yet what she'll hear. She just knows that something is about to become real, and all the grown-ups in this room are still arguing about who is responsible for fixing what.
