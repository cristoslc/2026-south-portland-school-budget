# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

Looking back at December 1 now — after everything that's followed — this meeting was the last moment of normalcy before the storm. The governance was functioning exactly as it should: smooth leadership transitions, ceremonial speeches, procedural housekeeping. Elise Tipton gave a thoughtful inaugural address, and I was genuinely impressed by her focus on housing affordability. She understood the development pressures facing the city. Rosemary and Adrian were re-elected without drama, which I knew we'd need — a contested chair election going into budget season would have been a disaster.

But sitting here in March, knowing everything that followed, I keep coming back to DeAngelos's speech. She talked beautifully about immigrant families, about belonging, about the district's role in welcoming newcomers. I agree with all of it — those are real values, not just rhetoric. But the $7.2 million deficit was already forming in the shadows. The teacher contract had already been negotiated. And not one word about what was coming. Was she protecting the moment? Or did board leadership not yet grasp the scale of what the finance director would reveal one week later?

Linda Cohen's farewell warning is the line I keep replaying: without a healthy business community, "property taxes for homeowners would go through the roof even more than it already is." She knew. Fourteen months on the council, and she chose those words deliberately. She was the only person that night who said anything honest about where South Portland's fiscal trajectory was heading — and she was walking out the door.

The complete absence of any budget discussion is now its own piece of evidence. The mayor, the full council, and school board leadership were in one room — and the looming crisis was completely invisible. Seven days later I would be sitting in a board meeting learning we'd blown through our fund balance and were staring at a $1.9 million hole with no safety net. December 1 was the last night we pretended everything was fine.
