# Cumulative Record: Linda (PERSONA-007)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

This meeting has two things that define it for me: a small win I didn't let myself register fully enough in the moment, and a late-night Mahoney workshop that reversed the best piece of news I'd had since January.

Start with the PFML win. Rosemary walked to that podium and announced the school department is joining the Symetra plan — coordinated with the city, real cost savings, quietly executed. In the middle of a $7.2 million gap, any actual cost containment is worth marking. I wish we had more of those moments.

The Scott conflict-of-interest question was exactly the distraction I expected. Ed Cobb asked it correctly; the city attorney answered it correctly under Maine statute. Nothing compelled. But it's in the transcript now, and anyone who wants to cloud the validation result will find it. I'm not worried about the legal outcome. I'm worried about the bandwidth.

Then the Mahoney workshop at 10 PM. In January, when the council fractured on the full $194 million package and sent them back for scaled-down scenarios, I let myself believe the November collision was receding. That was premature. Craig Piper walked through six scenarios — $57 million to $104 million just for Mahoney, with police and fire adding another $57 million if addressed together — and by midnight the informal consensus was coalescing around A-one plus geothermal, with November 2026 as the bond target. The same November we're asking homeowners to absorb our school increase. Nobody totaled the combined number. I've been asking since January what that figure is, and it still hasn't been produced.

Then Julia Edwards at 11 PM floated using Mahoney as a consolidated elementary school campus. The mayor said she'd raised it before. The city manager confirmed the board had formally told the city it had no use for the building. That position was taken before we were staring at closing Kaler or Small. The board needs to decide whether it still stands — quietly, before someone asks us publicly.

The ICE testimony shifted something in my fiscal thinking. When Project Home is ten days from exhausting its emergency housing fund with 15% of requests from South Portland, those are families at displacement risk. Families who leave South Portland are students who leave our rolls. Lost enrollment hits our per-pupil state subsidy the following year. I've been treating immigration enforcement as a participation problem and a moral problem. It is also a budget mechanics problem, and neither governance room has said that out loud.
