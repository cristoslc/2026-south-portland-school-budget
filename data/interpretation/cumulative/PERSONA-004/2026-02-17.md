# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

One week after the joint session where city councilors finally sat in the same room as school board members and heard the scale of this crisis — 42 teachers, 78 positions, a fund balance that's gone — and the council spent five hours on February 17 without saying the number once. Not once. This is the meeting I keep returning to when I need to explain to colleagues why I stopped believing the joint session mattered.

But before I get to the silence, I have to get to Julia Edwards. She stood up during public comment and described what I have been watching from my classroom: kids who haven't left their apartments since the ICE surge started. A six-year-old asking a classmate's parent why certain kids still aren't in class. Families hiding in bedrooms while agents work the building. I know those kids. Those empty seats are in my gradebook. I've been tracking the ICE crisis as an organizing problem — which forums get cancelled, which parents can't show up to testify — but February 17 is the meeting where it stops being a civic participation issue and becomes an instructional one. The children aren't just staying away from budget meetings. They're staying away from school.

Then the eviction moratorium goes down 4-2, with only Walker and Tipton voting yes. Project Home had 655 contacts to its emergency fund since January 23rd. The fund was ten days from empty. The people who voted no don't have to find seats in September for the kids those families produce when the housing finally falls through. I do.

I'm flagging two things specifically for the union. First, the PFML Symetra plan: HR confirmed it reduces take-home pay by half a percent annually, and the school board rep confirmed the school department is joining the same plan. Nobody at that table mentioned collective bargaining implications. Half a percent doesn't sound like much until you're already watching 42 colleagues get cut and your own position is potentially in play through seniority bumping. Second, Councilor Scott: someone from the audience asked whether her spouse working for the school department means she should recuse from school budget votes. City attorney says no. She was one of the four votes against the moratorium. She will be one of the seven votes that approves or rejects the budget that determines whether my colleagues keep their jobs. I'm writing that down.

Near midnight, Julia Edwards floated the idea of consolidating everything into Mahoney. The city manager acknowledged the district hasn't formally requested it. That's the most consequential thing said all night about the school crisis, and it came from a community member at hour five, not from any councilor or administrator. These two bodies are still not talking to each other — formally — even as the fiscal collision accelerates.
