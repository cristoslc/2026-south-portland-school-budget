# Cumulative Summary: Priya (PERSONA-005)

### Current Understanding

Priya sees South Portland as a district with a profound credibility gap between equity rhetoric and equity action. Over nine meetings, she has tracked a consistent pattern: the district demonstrates genuine equity commitment on discrete, visible issues -- halal meal certification, immigration solidarity resolutions, adult education pivots to WhatsApp, community school expansion -- while systematically failing to apply an equity lens to the large structural decisions that actually determine resource allocation. No budget scenario has included disaggregated impact analysis by race, income, disability, or English learner status. No school-level per-pupil spending comparisons have been published. The elementary reconfiguration -- the single largest structural decision in this budget cycle -- proceeded through multiple meetings without basic equity homework on transportation impacts, class size equity, or demographic displacement.

The March 2 budget workshop was a turning point. Hard demographic data confirmed de facto segregation: Skillin serves 60% BIPOC students and 33% multilingual learners while Small serves less than 20% BIPOC and 6% multilingual learners. The administration's recommendation to close Skillin -- framed as an equity intervention -- represented the most egregious deployment of equity language Priya has encountered. Four board members rejected it explicitly, with Tyler Smith naming the racial politics directly. This was the first time the board demonstrated it could apply an equity lens to a structural decision when the stakes were made visible and the racial impact was named.

Priya now evaluates every equity-framed proposal through a lens of implementation burden: who bears the cost of the change, and are the families most affected centered in the process or merely invoked in the justification? Community engagement remains structurally exclusionary, with forums drawing predominantly white, English-speaking attendees while immigrant families face active barriers to public participation. The district's capacity for equity is real but selective, and the gap between aspiration and structural practice remains the central tension.
