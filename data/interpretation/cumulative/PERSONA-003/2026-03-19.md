# Cumulative Record: Jess (PERSONA-003)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- March 19, 2026 -- 2026-03-19

### Interpretation

I have been watching this budget crisis for fifteen weeks, and I thought I understood its shape. Proposals, scenarios, workshops, votes. A process with stages. What March 19 told me is that the execution is already happening, ahead of the process, while parents like me are still waiting for answers.

Councilor Matthews -- the one who votes no on everything -- dropped it in passing during an argument about why the city shouldn't spend money on rental assistance: 72 people in the school department received layoff notices the day before this meeting. Not 78 positions "being considered." Not a proposal pending a March 2 workshop or a May 5 vote. Actual pink slips, delivered on March 18, while I was giving Mia a bath. And he paired it with a number I hadn't heard before: $8.4 million deficit, not the $7.2 million that's been the fixed reference point since December. The crisis grew while nobody was publicly updating the scoreboard.

That revelation was the only substantive mention of schools in four hours of city council business. The same pattern I've documented since December 1 -- schools raised in a passing comment, no follow-up from any councilor, back to other business -- held tonight with new, darker weight. It's not just that the council isn't engaging. It's that the most consequential education event in months happened the day before this meeting, and it surfaced as a rhetorical aside in a debate about something else.

The budget calendar at least gives me dates to hold onto: April 7 public hearing, April 14 workshop with schools first, May 5 vote, June 9 referendum. But those dates now feel almost ironic. The layoff notices went out before the public hearing. Families are being asked to show up in April and May for a process where the administrative decisions are already in motion.

The sewer confirmation is its own kind of gut punch. Twenty-two percent rate increases annually for three years. I identified the triple tax stack in January -- school budget increase, Mahoney bond, sewer rates -- and nobody would calculate it as a combined number. Now one of those three components has a confirmed price, and Councilor Matthews said it plainly: "our tax rate is gonna go way up between the city and the school department needs." That's the closest thing to an honest combined accounting I've heard in three months of meetings, and it came from the person who voted no on helping immigrant families stay housed.

The rental assistance passing 6-1 is the one thing that doesn't feel like loss. After watching the eviction moratorium fail 4-2 in February, the council approving $100,000 for ICE-affected families -- the same families whose school absences have been documented since January -- suggests something shifted. It's not enough. But it's different from what came before.
