# Voter Guides: Types, Sources, and Platforms

*Source: Ballotpedia — https://ballotpedia.org/Voter_guides*

## Overview

Voter guides help voters understand what and who is on their ballot before they vote. They range from official government publications to nonprofit and community-produced guides.

## Types of Voter Guides

### Official Voter Guides
Publications by state government offices — specifically entities like the Department of Elections or the Secretary of State. They provide authoritative information about ballot measures at the state level and are legally required in many states.

### Unofficial Voter Guides
Come from alternative sources including:
- Partisan organizations (must be clearly identified as such)
- Newspaper publishers
- Nonprofit entities
- Civic organizations like the League of Women Voters

## Key Platforms and Resources

### Vote411.org
Launched by the League of Women Voters Education Fund. Provides nonpartisan information to the public with both general and state-specific information. A one-stop-shop for election-related information.

### Guides.vote
Produces nonpartisan voter guides showing where candidates stand, produced by experienced editors, researchers, and veteran journalists, with links to credible sources.

### Ballotpedia
A free online encyclopedia of American politics that provides:
- Overview of candidates' stances on key issues
- Sample ballot lookup for upcoming elections
- Coverage of federal, state, and local races
- Comparative analysis of features of official voter guides by state

### BallotReady
Offers personalized, nonpartisan information to voters in all 50 states and helps voters make a plan to vote.

## Why Voter Guides Matter

People often don't vote because they don't know where candidates stand and are confused about how to find out. Widespread political cynicism, disinformation, and spin compound this problem. Nonpartisan candidate guides provide:
- A concise and credible way to compare candidates' stands
- Clear explanation of why voting matters
- Access to information that was previously difficult to find

## The Civic Literacy Purpose

An official voter guide is an **information device**, not an engagement device — receiving one alone probably won't change a non-voter's mind. However, it may be the one nonpartisan source of information that many people see and should be used as a tool for civic literacy.

## Nonpartisan Standards for Unofficial Guides

For nonprofit or civic organizations producing voter guides:
- Conduct voter engagement and education in the context of your educational and civic mission
- Do not conduct voter education in a way intended to support or oppose a specific candidate
- When circulating candidate positions, **keep your opinion out of it**
- Let voters use the information to make their own decisions
- Include a disclaimer that the publishing organization does not support or oppose any political party or candidate

## Wide Distribution

Distribute voter guides as widely as practicable:
- Online in accessible formats (PDF, web page)
- In multiple languages where the community warrants
- In partnership with libraries, schools, community organizations
- With clear disclaimers about the guide's nonpartisan nature
