# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 9, 2025 -- 2025-12-09

### Interpretation

This is the third city council meeting in Lila's journey — and by now she knows exactly what to look for: did anyone say Dyer's name? The answer is no. Two hours. Not once.

This meeting sits one day after the December 8 school board meeting where Lila first heard the words "spending freeze" and "$1.9 million shortfall" — the meeting that ended her "before." She's already scared. Her parents are already watching. And her mom apparently sat through this entire city council session hoping to hear something useful, something reassuring, something about her school. What she got was house-price talk and a building argument.

The one moment that mattered came from a school board member — Ms. DeAngelo, the same person who would later formally request the reconfiguration proposal in January — who stood up and said budget season starts *this month* and that the schools eat up 61 cents of every tax dollar. She invited people to watch for meeting postings. That's it. No mention of spending freezes. No mention of the $1.9 million shortfall Lila had just learned about the night before. No mention of Dyer. Just: budget season is starting, come to meetings.

There's something about the silence that cuts differently now, knowing everything that follows. By March, the board will be debating nine scenarios — one of which lists Dyer as "CLOSED." Teachers will resign. Parents will flood workshops. A 85% opposition survey will get documented and politely set aside. But in December, the city council spent an hour and a half on a seven-story apartment building and found no time for the elementary schools at all.

The two parents who showed up and said "we had to pay a babysitter to be here tonight" — those are Lila's people. The adults who want to fight for their kids but have to pay forty dollars just to sit in a room and ask for virtual access. The adults who will eventually show up anyway, in March, in huge numbers, too late to change much.

The pattern from December 4 is hardening: the city goes about its business while the school crisis grows underneath. Lila's school is not named. She is not counted.
