# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 4, 2025 -- 2025-12-04

### Interpretation

Ben is watching this meeting now, in mid-March, from the other side of nine school board meetings and a full fiscal meltdown — which changes everything about what he sees in it. Four days before the school board discovered its $1.9M deficit, four days before the crisis that would consume his next three months, the city council was quietly formalizing how it allocates money to volunteer committees. Routine stuff. Except it isn't, anymore.

The committee budget process the council approved — $2,500 cap for operating expenses, unlimited special requests through budget workshops — is a small governance story that becomes more interesting against the backdrop of what Ben now knows about the city's fiscal position. By February 10th, city departments were already requesting a 15% tax increase before schools were even added to the pile. Every dollar the council commits to committees is a dollar that comes from that same exhausted taxpayer base. The Human Rights Commission angle is the real story here: Walker flags that HRC has historically received around $10,000 annually, and now they'll need to come to a budget workshop hat in hand for anything above $2,500. The council rolled past this. Ben notes that this is the same council that, ten weeks later, would be telling the school board to hold increases to 3-4% while struggling to acknowledge that the city's own budget pressures were just as severe.

The regional board oversight gap Councilor West surfaced is the detail Ben would have ignored in real time but can't ignore now. Former Councilor Cohen — the same Linda Cohen who stood at this same council table on December 1st and warned that property taxes were "going through the roof" — sits on the Metro board making transit decisions without reporting back to current members. That's the shadow governance story: institutional knowledge walking out the door after elections, replaced by radio silence. Ben has seen the same pattern at the school board level all season — the budget crisis was built over years of decisions that nobody assembled into a coherent picture until it was too late.

The Zoom public comment debate is period-appropriate texture more than a story, but Pride's "Zoom bombing" and First Amendment tangle is a quotable moment about why municipal accessibility is harder than it looks.

What Ben takes from this meeting, retroactively: the city's own governance was already showing stress fractures on December 4th — process patches, oversight gaps, budget structures that create winners and losers among volunteer committees — before the school crisis broke into the open and demanded everyone's attention.
