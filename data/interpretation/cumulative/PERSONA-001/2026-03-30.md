# Cumulative Record: Maria (PERSONA-001)
## Meeting: School Board Special Budget Meeting -- March 30, 2026 -- 2026-03-30

### Interpretation

The votes that were always coming finally came, and the budget that was supposed to come with them didn't. Kayler is closing — six to one, effective end of this school year, the board filing with the Commissioner of Education before families know where their children will go in September. Option A is the configuration — PreK-through-first and second-through-fourth, two separate buildings, attendance boundaries undrawn, sibling separation unaddressed, transportation unplanned. Five hours of meeting and the only thing that failed was the one thing with a dollar sign on it.

The budget failing 2-5 is the part Maria is still turning over. Five board members voted no — each for different reasons, but the result is the same: the board voted to close a school and restructure every remaining elementary school in the city and then couldn't agree on the budget that would pay for it. The 72 pink slips are still out. The 78 positions are still cut. A Thursday meeting doesn't change that math.

DeAngelis voted against the closure. Maria has been carrying the chair's record since December 1 — the inauguration equity speech, the "fabulous idea" endorsement, the superintendent search designed to minimize community input, the dismissal of neighborhood schools as something the city "doesn't really have anymore." She notes the no vote. She cannot revise the narrative it belongs to. One vote against a closure, after six months of being the architect of the process that produced it, is not the same as having opposed it.

What has been accumulating since December 17, when Maria first saw "16 educational technicians" on a cut list, reached its most specific form tonight at the microphone. The SPESPA president said it directly: one behavioral strategist for general education in the entire district, proposed for elimination. The Kayler ed tech described what that person actually does — safety care training, teaching staff how to physically protect themselves when a behavioral escalation becomes physical. And this is a reconfiguration year. Every child who moves to a new school in September will be navigating disrupted routines, unfamiliar adults, new hallways. Maria has been sitting with the interlocking logic of this since March 23 — the disruption the reconfiguration creates dismantles the infrastructure that would otherwise absorb it — and tonight it has names and faces and a school board vote.

Board member Richardson said twenty kindergartners "sounds like a nightmare" and then voted against Option A and against the budget. Maria finds herself grateful for the no votes that didn't stop anything. The members she most trusts — the ones who have pushed back hardest — couldn't hold the line. Not because they didn't try.

The community engagement process starts now. Six weeks. She has been to the forums.
