# Cumulative Record: Rachel (PERSONA-008)
## Meeting: Regular -- March 9, 2026 -- 2026-03-09

### Interpretation

One week after the most consequential budget workshop in recent memory, the regular meeting served as both a consolidation of what happened on March 2nd and a preview of the battles ahead. The minutes from the February 9th meeting -- confirming the board's formal vote to request the reconfiguration proposal -- are now part of the official record. The nine scenarios presented at Budget Workshop I are no longer a presentation; they're an administrative position with institutional weight behind it.

The parent mobilization I saw on March 2nd was real and sustained. Over 20 parents spoke during public comment at the workshop, and the energy hasn't dissipated. People used language that resonates with my deepest concerns -- children treated as "interchangeable units," the psychological impact of forced transitions, the loss of neighborhood identity. These aren't abstract objections; they're descriptions of what will happen to specific children in specific families. The 85% opposition in the grassroots survey wasn't an anomaly. It reflects how parents actually feel when they understand what's being proposed.

The Skillin HVAC revelation from last week continues to gnaw at me. A $3 million repair bill hanging over a school is exactly the kind of detail that gets used to justify closure regardless of what the board said about opposing it. "We can't close Skillin" becomes "we can't afford to keep Skillin" with one facilities report. I don't trust that the four board members who spoke against Skillin closure will hold firm when the administration presents the HVAC costs as an additional argument.

Two more budget workshops remain -- March 23rd and March 30th -- and those are where final decisions will crystallize. The 78 position cuts, the enrollment collapse, the nine scenarios: they're all converging on a decision point that will reshape elementary education in South Portland for years. I'm bringing everyone I know to those workshops. We've shown the board that parents are paying attention. Now we need to show them that we won't accept disruption as the default answer to a crisis the administration created through years of mismanagement.
