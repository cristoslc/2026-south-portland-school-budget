# Cumulative Summary: Maria (PERSONA-001)

### Current Understanding

As of late March 2026, Maria holds a comprehensive and structurally grounded picture of the South Portland school budget crisis — built across four months of attending meetings across two governing bodies, organizing a parent advocacy network, and doing household tax math no official has done for her.

The FY27 structural gap has grown from the $7.2M first quantified at the February 4 forum to $8.4M as cited by Councilor Matthews on March 19. Seventy-two pink slips were issued on March 18, meaning the layoff process is in execution even as the board has not finalized which reconfiguration option it will pursue. The fund balance is fully depleted. Elementary enrollment has declined 23% in four years (1,401 to 1,080 students), compounded by the district having added 82 positions while enrollment dropped by 300 since 2017. Per-pupil spending stands at $26,651 — highest among comparable districts — driven by costs the board has never transparently disaggregated. The state covers approximately 20% of actual costs against a 55% statutory obligation, a structural underfunding amplified by years of internal management failure: six finance directors in five years, repeat audit findings, illegal budget overages.

The 78-position cut package includes 42 teachers and 16 educational technicians whose elimination removes non-substitutable scaffolding for children with IEPs. Nine reconfiguration options were presented at the March 2 workshop; Skillin School has been effectively protected by board consensus, constraining viable options. Chair DeAngelis's premature endorsement of reconfiguration as "fabulous" before any formal proposal was presented, and the board's unanimous advancement over near-unanimous parent opposition and teacher union pushback, have confirmed for Maria that community input functions as theater, not governance.

City council guidance of 3–6% (mostly 4–5%) undercut the board's own 6% floor, compounding required cuts. Simultaneously, an $87M November bond referendum for police and fire (library eliminated from scope), 22% annual sewer rate increases for three consecutive years, and unresolved Mahoney capital costs all land on the same households. The critical remaining dates are April 14 (school budget workshop), May 5 (council vote on the school portion), and June 9 (public referendum). Maria is mobilizing every parent she knows to be present on April 14 — the last window she believes exists for meaningful input before the math is finalized.
