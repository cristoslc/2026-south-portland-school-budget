# Cumulative Record: Dana (PERSONA-009)
## Meeting: City Council & School Board Joint Budget Guidance Workshop -- February 10, 2026

### Interpretation

The day after the school board's fractious regular meeting, Dana gets what she has been waiting for all season: elected officials openly fighting with each other on camera. School Board Chair DeAngelo telling the city council "I don't think it's fair for you to shirk your responsibility" and calling their guidance "not sufficient, not adequate, not appropriate, and not acceptable" is the soundbite of the budget season so far. That is not policy disagreement -- that is institutional warfare between two elected bodies, and it plays on television like nothing else.

The 19% status-quo tax increase gets restated by the superintendent himself, calling it "crazy," which validates Dana's decision to lead with that number in the February 4 segment. The enrollment-down/staffing-up chart (300+ students lost, 82 positions added since 2017) is shown again, confirming it as the central visual of the budget story. Dana now has this statistic reinforced across four consecutive meetings -- it is the single most durable fact in her coverage.

The new material is the political dimension. The city council's 3% guidance would require "upwards of probably 50 staff reductions" and Assistant Superintendent Prince's warning that "we're punishing our kids" creates the classic local-news tension: fiscal responsibility versus harm to children. Councilor Walker breaking ranks to support at least 6% ("I'm here to say the right thing, maybe not the popular thing") gives Dana the counterpoint voice she needs. And the fixed-income seniors worried about becoming homeless provide the third perspective that makes this a complete story -- kids, workers, and taxpayers all losing.

The elementary consolidation plan (2 primary, 3 intermediate schools, $2M savings, class sizes capped at 20 for K-2 and 24 for 3-4) gets further detail but no resolution. The joint meeting ended without the school board getting the specific tax guidance it demanded, meaning the uncertainty continues and Dana's story has no clean ending -- which, from a coverage standpoint, means more segments ahead.
