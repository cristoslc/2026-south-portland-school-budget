# Cumulative Record: Sheanna (PERSONA-015)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 6, 2026 -- 2026-01-06

### Interpretation

Thirty-seven days after the inauguration where six multilingual families stood up and said *we want to be your friends*, Sheanna sits through forty-five minutes of tank farm advocacy and cemetery maintenance contracts to get three minutes of school budget at the very end of a city council meeting. The pattern she has been tracking since December 9 — when DeAngelos had to walk across the street to announce budget season because the public information vacuum was real enough to require intervention — just ran again, in the same building, with the same result. The school budget is a closing item at the city level. She is writing that down.

But what she carries home is not the burial — it is the architecture that emerged in those final three minutes. February 10 is the joint workshop where the council tells the superintendent what is acceptable. That is the ceiling meeting, the one that shapes every programming and staffing decision she cares about. And the city manager has already named the question that will frame it: *Is this a year to add new positions? Is it a year to add new programs?* She can barely process how wrong that framing is for what she actually needs to argue. She is not asking to add anything. She is asking whether the kids already on the MTSS wait list at two of her three buildings will still have access to services after this budget cycle ends. Those are different conversations, and the one the council is being primed to have has no room in it for the equity of what already exists.

The January 22 forum is the first concrete public engagement date on the calendar, and she is already rehearsing the tension she knows she will feel in that room. She will be there with data — wait lists, specialist travel minutes, the Boundaries and Configurations work that has been sitting unacted on for years — because she is the person with cross-building sight. But she has watched these forums before. They fill with parents from one building who are afraid of closure, and the structural argument — that closing without redistricting makes existing inequities worse — disappears into the noise. The participation barrier she has been documenting since December 4 now has a specific venue to manifest itself in. If the families she serves at her multilingual-dominant buildings don't make it to January 22, the cost-saving conversation will be shaped entirely by people who have only ever seen one piece of the picture.

Councilor West's January 6th anniversary remarks are the only thing in the meeting that gives her breath. *Let's disagree if we need to, but let's share our commitment to a community where we can listen to each other.* That is her language. That is the argument she makes when she tries to move parents across buildings past the "save my school" conversation toward the equity question. Hearing it from an elected official — unsolicited, personal, specific — is the first time since DeAngelos's inauguration framing that the room reflected her convictions back at her. She holds it, and then she starts preparing for January 22.
