# Cumulative Summary: Lila (PERSONA-014)

### Current Understanding

Over nine meetings spanning three months, Lila has watched an abstract budget crisis become a concrete threat to her daily life. What began as a confusing $1.9 million shortfall and a spending freeze that prevented teachers from buying books has escalated into a $7.2 million budget gap, nine formal reconfiguration scenarios for elementary schools, and one option that would close Dyer Elementary entirely. Her school -- the building she's attended since kindergarten, where she knows every corner and every bathroom, where her teacher has looped with her for two years -- appeared on a presentation slide marked "CLOSED."

The reconfiguration plan has evolved from vague rumors to detailed proposals. The district is considering reorganizing all five elementary schools from neighborhood-based to grade-band models, where some buildings would serve pre-K through 1st grade and others would serve 2nd through 4th grade. Under some scenarios, Lila would change schools twice before reaching middle school. Her class size would grow from 16 to 22-23 students. Between 15.5 and 23.5 teacher positions would be eliminated across all scenarios.

The financial picture is dire and fully understood in its broad strokes: the district's fund balance is depleted, state funding covers only 20% of costs instead of the intended 55%, per-pupil spending is the highest among comparable districts, and elementary enrollment has dropped by 300 students in four years. Even implementing all reconfiguration plans leaves a $3+ million shortfall. The board spent $30,000 on a superintendent search firm and set the new superintendent's salary at $165,000, both while claiming to have no money -- contradictions that even a 9-year-old can identify.

The institutional dynamics have crystallized: the school board voted unanimously to pursue reconfiguration, the chair declared it "good for kids" while dismissing children's anxiety as parental projection, the teachers' union pushed back against plans that eliminate colleagues, 85% of surveyed parents oppose reconfiguration, and the school board and city council are in open conflict over budget guidance. A teacher at Dyer has already resigned ahead of any formal decision. The June 9 budget deadline gives the process roughly three more months.

Through all of this, nobody has spoken directly to Lila. She is the person every line item resolves to -- the child whose daily experience changes when a school closes, a teacher is laid off, or a class grows by seven students. But she has no seat at the table, no voice in the room, and no vocabulary for the forces reshaping her world. Her entire understanding comes from overheard conversations, parental stress, schoolyard rumors, and the growing sense that the adults who are supposed to protect her don't entirely know what they're doing.
