# Cumulative Summary: Tom (PERSONA-006)

### Current Understanding

Tom is a South Portland homeowner on a fixed income who has attended or reviewed records from 24 public meetings since December 2025. He tracks the budget crisis as a direct threat to his property tax bill.

His core understanding of the school district's situation: a structural deficit that grew from an initially reported $1.9 million to $7.2 million and then $8.4 million, driven by a 23% elementary enrollment decline over four years while staffing grew by 82 positions, a fund balance drawn from roughly $5.9 million to zero, state funding covering only 20% against a 55% statutory target, and three consecutive years of deficits compounded by documented multi-year under-budgeting of known line items. The finance director has named the causal mechanism — COVID-era hiring masked by federal relief funds, reversed with no policy guardrail to force correction. The FY27 superintendent's budget holds to a 6% local levy increase, producing a per-household cost of approximately $257 per year on a home assessed at the city average. That budget has not yet received a formal board vote. Labor contract structure makes the 6% ceiling mathematically unsustainable in FY28 without renegotiation or accelerated attrition.

Beyond the school budget, Tom is carrying three additional simultaneous cost pressures: sewer rate increases of roughly 22% per year for three years (Pearl Street pump station, no voter approval required), an $87 million police and fire bond targeting a November 2026 referendum, and a Mahoney City Center bond deferred to a 2027 referendum with a stripped-down scope still estimated at $70–76 million. No public body has produced a combined per-household total for all four pressures. Tom considers that the central unanswered question of the entire winter.

His view of governance: the school board has demonstrated both financial management failures and, at the March 30 and April 2 meetings, basic governance incapacity. The city council shows fiscal discipline on low-stakes or politically uncomplicated items but defers on capital decisions and has drawn down its fund balance on back-to-back weeks while the school crisis was active. Councilor West is the one council voice Tom tracks as a credible but isolated fiscal presence. The state is a source of both unfunded mandates and regulatory intrusions — including a housing density law that strips local planning oversight — with the property tax relief task force representing a tentative signal of state-level awareness that has not yet produced local impact.
