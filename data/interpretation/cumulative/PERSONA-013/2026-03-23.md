# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

The thing that hit hardest was band. Since December I've been carrying a vague worry that band might go half-time, that music trips might disappear, that the programs I love could be cut because they're not "required." Now there are actual numbers: four related arts teachers eliminated at the middle school, plus the percussion ed tech — the person who makes band function for kids in grades five through twelve. And someone at the meeting said this same position was almost cut last year too. The community showed up, students spoke, and the board reversed course. Now it's happening again. I don't know how many times kids have to come to a meeting and say the same things before the answer stays changed.

I found out almost by accident that my gifted program is still in the budget — three FTEs, unchanged. I've been carrying that worry since December and I didn't know until now that I could put it down. But band might still go. So I get to keep one thing and lose another, and nobody asked me which one I'd choose.

The school they're actually recommending to close isn't Skillin anymore. It's Kayler. And Kayler is — someone said this out loud at the meeting — about 45% Black and brown students, and 30-something percent kids learning English. Kids like some of my neighbors. Families like mine. And none of those families were in the room. A woman said she looked around and they weren't there because nobody told them in time. I've watched that same pattern since March 2, since February 4, since December 8 when parents showed up angry because they heard about reconfiguration from neighbors, not from the district. The format of "come tell us what you think" only works if you know you're invited.

At Memorial, the ESOL teacher is being cut and the co-teaching model for multilingual learner students is ending. The principal described it as "best practice, but not required." That's one of the coldest sentences I've encountered in all of this. We know what works best for kids. We just won't do it because it costs money.

The student board members spoke and then left for homework. Davidson said he sees the inequality every day — what kids miss in elementary shows up and disrupts middle school classrooms. True things. Real things. Then they were excused at ten o'clock. The adults stayed until midnight, debated for five hours, and voted on nothing. That gap — students sent home with unfinished homework, adults still arguing at midnight with no answer — that's been the whole thing all year.

### Emotional Register

Amira carries the relief about her gifted program and the fear about band in the same breath — she got one answer she needed and it helped, and the other answer is still coming, and nobody asked her which program she would choose if she had to pick. The image that won't leave is the student board members being sent home with their backpacks while the adults argued until midnight and voted on nothing: the institution invited students in, gave them the microphone, applauded them silently because clapping was not permitted, and then excused them before the outcome was decided — and she recognizes that as the whole pattern, compressed into a single Tuesday night.
