# Cumulative Record: Marcus (PERSONA-004)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

Five hours. I stayed for all five hours and the board adjourned at 11:15 without voting on anything. Monday, apparently. We have April 7 for the public hearing and April 14 for the council workshop and the board went home without acting.

But what I can't stop turning over is what Abigail Ketchem said in the financial overview, almost in passing, almost like she was describing the weather. Labor costs, when you include everything, increase at a rate higher than 6% per year. Automatically. Without adding a single position. Which means the ceiling that's been driving every cut since December — the political number that turned a funding gap into a staffing bloodbath — is a ceiling we will structurally break again next year. After all of this. After 42 teachers gone, a school possibly closed, 32 colleagues on a recall list. We are mathematically aimed at the same cliff for FY28. She said it like it was a geological feature of the terrain.

Thirty-two. That's the number I'm carrying home. Not 42 — the 42 breaks down: nine are vacancies or known retirements, eleven were offered different positions. Thirty-two certified teachers have no job next year and are on a recall list, waiting and hoping someone retires before they find work elsewhere and leave permanently. That's not a cut with a path back. That's a cohort of people being asked to put their careers on hold for a district that can't promise them anything.

At SPHS specifically: courses are staying, Glenn said it clearly. I needed to hear that in public. But science average class sizes go from 18.8 to 21.8 — averages, which means some sections will be sitting at 24. And the regular education behavior strategist is gone. Zero full-time remaining anywhere in the district. That doesn't show up on the class-size slide, but every teacher in this building knows exactly what it means when you have 22 kids in front of you and one of them is in crisis and there's no one to call.

Then there's Jen Fletcher at the microphone for the second year in a row. Three hundred seventy-eight days after standing at that same podium opposing the identical percussion ed tech cut. It was restored after community pressure last year. Now it's back. That's not budgeting — that's a test of whether we'll keep fighting.
