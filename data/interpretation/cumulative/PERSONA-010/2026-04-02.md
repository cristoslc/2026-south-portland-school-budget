# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

Five hours and no budget vote — which is the headline until the headline changes mid-meeting. That's the rhythm of this season, and April 2 has it in full. The board defers the FY27 budget again, this time waiting on state figures that could shift the math by a million dollars. A million dollars that nobody knew about at 6 PM, announced by the union president off a text from Augusta during public comment, followed by a board member reading a second text from a state rep in the final hour. Ben has been covering municipal government long enough to know that a budget story that gains a million dollars of new money during the meeting is a different story than the one that started five hours earlier.

But the number he will not stop thinking about is not the state windfall. It's $20,000 to $30,000. That is what the district saved — maybe — by demoting its DEI director and its assistant special education director down to instructional strategist roles. Holman said she expected director-level cuts to "liberate" classroom positions. They didn't. Richardson said she doesn't see the concessions. Ben has been tracking this since March 30, when he first saw the demotion chain and understood it as the equity contradiction of the season — the board that argued equity through every reconfiguration hearing ratified the effective removal of its only BIPOC leadership-level role at negligible savings. Now he has board members confirming the savings were negligible. The community asked for cuts at the top. The district made moves at the top. The moves produced almost nothing recoverable. He needs someone on record explaining why, not to assign blame, but because that is the accountability gap his readers have been earning the right to understand since December.

The finance director's testimony on chronic under-budgeting is the paragraph Ben has been missing since December 8. Four straight years of overspending tuition reimbursement and electricity by six figures, no fund balance to absorb the variance, optimistic projections in year one becoming layoffs in year two. That's it. That's the "how did we get here" his series needed. He is calling her Thursday.

The behavioral specialist statement is the human story inside the RIF class — 60 students, 40 behavior plans, and the argument that eliminating the preventive role does not eliminate the need, it just routes the same kids into special education at higher cost. That is the kind of sentence that makes readers put down their coffee. He is tracking down Jenna Goldstein Walsh next week.

The calendar is now: Monday maybe, April 7 definitely. Five days to confirm a million dollars of state money before the council presentation. Ben has covered enough budget seasons to know that unconfirmed figures from legislative texts in the final hour of a five-hour meeting have a way of being smaller in the morning. He is not writing the $1 million into anything until the superintendent's office confirms it.
