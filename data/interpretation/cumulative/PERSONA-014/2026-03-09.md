# Cumulative Record: Lila (PERSONA-014)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- March 9, 2026

### Interpretation

A week after seeing her school listed as "CLOSED" on a presentation slide, Lila encounters a meeting that is both eerily normal and deeply unsettling. Dyer's principal reports on the Read A Thon, the "Stop, Drop, and Read" event, the Books and Bites night with guest readers and bookmark making. School life is continuing as if nothing is wrong. But Lila knows better now. The cheerful reports about reading events at Dyer feel like descriptions of a place that might not exist in its current form by September.

Then Ms. Beland -- the special education pre-K teacher at Dyer -- submits her resignation effective August 31. Lila's mom told her that when teachers start leaving, it means something bad is happening to the school. Whether Ms. Beland is leaving because of the reconfiguration uncertainty or for other reasons, the signal is the same: people are already making decisions based on the assumption that Dyer is changing. The school is losing staff before any official decision has been made.

The meeting minutes from the March 2 workshop are now part of the official record, and the list of parents who spoke against reconfiguration is long: Kelly, Sarah, Catherine, Zulma, Jenny, Emily, and many more. Lila's mom was among those at the meeting, coming home upset and refusing to share details. The scale of parent opposition -- confirmed by the 85% survey result from the workshop -- is documented but doesn't seem to be changing the board's trajectory.

The revelation that nine different scenarios exist -- nine plans, each with different implications for Dyer -- deepens Lila's sense that the adults don't actually know what they're doing. If they needed nine plans, it means they haven't figured it out yet. Board member Adrian Dowling's story about an elementary school that closed around 2005 -- before Lila was born -- proves that this has happened before. Schools in South Portland have closed and not come back. This isn't just a threat; there's precedent.

The new superintendent's salary was set at $165,000, approved unanimously. Lila doesn't know what that number means in context, but she knows the district is paying someone a lot of money to lead schools it can't afford to keep open. This echoes the $30,000 search firm from December -- money flowing to leadership while classrooms face cuts.

Three months into this crisis from Lila's perspective, the cumulative weight is significant. She has watched the budget shortfall grow from $1.9 million to $7.2 million. She has seen the spending freeze, the depleted reserves, the 42 teacher eliminations, the enrollment decline. She has heard her school called "CLOSED" in a presentation. She has watched her parents come home from meetings tense and upset. She has heard the board chair say reconfiguration is "good for kids" while dismissing children's anxiety as parental projection. She has seen teachers resign. And through all of it, nobody has spoken directly to her -- a 9-year-old whose entire school life is being debated in rooms she'll never enter, in language she can barely understand, on timelines set by adults who insist she'll be fine.

### Emotional Register

Lila exists in a state of anticipatory grief overlaid with forced normalcy. Her school is hosting reading events and making bookmarks while adults debate its closure in official minutes. A teacher is leaving. Parents are fighting but not winning. The precedent of a previous school closure confirms that this can really happen. Lila is carrying three months of accumulated fear, and no adult in any of these meetings has spoken to her experience directly. She is the person the budget resolves to, but she has no voice in the room where it's decided.
