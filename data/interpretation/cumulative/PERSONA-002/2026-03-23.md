# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Budget Workshop -- March 23, 2026 -- 2026-03-23

### Interpretation

The best twenty minutes of this budget cycle came from Abigail Ketchem. She named the root causes without hedging: enrollment fell and staffing didn't follow, COVID money papered over the gap, the district had no minimum fund balance threshold as a guardrail, and six years of finance leadership turnover made it structurally impossible for anyone to watch the whole picture long enough to say stop. It confirms what the February 3 auditor established: this was a systems breakdown with identifiable causes. I appreciated it — and noted immediately that she is the seventh finance director in six years. Nothing in the institutional history I have been tracking since December gives me confidence the analysis will outlast the analyst.

The 6% ceiling is confirmed. That is real. After the February 10 council guidance tightened the band and the board's own members questioned whether the ceiling was achievable, I was not certain it would hold. Approximately $257 per year for the average homeowner, budget growth at 3.3%, described as the lowest in recent memory. For this fiscal year, a genuine success.

But Ketchem's own presentation is why I cannot stop there. Labor costs auto-escalate above 6% per year through step and lane adjustments already written into the contracts. Utilities are up 13 to 14 percent annually. Debt service increases a minimum $300,000 in FY28 for the athletic field bond. The district just confirmed it can meet the council's ceiling once — and its own cost structure will exceed that ceiling in FY28 without a single new decision. Ketchem called it wiping out the credit card balance without fixing the spending behavior. That belongs on the first slide of the FY28 budget presentation, not buried at the end of a five-hour workshop.

Two things I flagged in my spreadsheet that the presentation left unexplained. Transportation is up 17.5% — nearly $500,000 — while the district cuts shuttle routes and reassigns drivers to custodial work. Driver benefits jumped $294,000; I assume health insurance, but that connection was never stated publicly. And the nutrition disclosure adds a new layer: FY26 budgeted $202,000 for the nutrition transfer, actual deficit was several hundred thousand dollars. That is not a rounding error. If that number was off by that magnitude, what else was similarly understated?

The board adjourned at 11:15 PM with zero votes cast on three scheduled action items. The next meeting is March 30. The council gets the budget April 7. Six days to make three defensible decisions after an emotionally exhausting five-hour session. I will be there.
