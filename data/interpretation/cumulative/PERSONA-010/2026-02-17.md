# Cumulative Record: Ben (PERSONA-010)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- February 17, 2026 -- 2026-02-17

### Interpretation

Ben has been carrying the immigration thread since January 12, when children started staying home from school and he filed it as a human story intersecting with the budget. February 17 is where that thread becomes the main event — and then collides, at 10 PM, with the other story he's been building all season.

The public comment is the most specific, most grounded testimony he's heard at a South Portland meeting in years. Cassie Moon drives a woman home on back roads at midnight. The city manager confirms the municipality has no way to know who was taken — no notification, no database, no accountability mechanism. Project Home has handed out $196,000 to 95 households and will be out of money in ten days. Children are missing Julia Edwards' son's first-grade class. And then the council, after sitting through two hours of all of it, votes 4-2 against the eviction moratorium.

Ben knows the vote is the obvious peg but it's not the story he's writing. The story is the gap — the distance between what the council heard and what four of them did with it. Councilor Scott's "shifts the burden" framing is a principled position that he can work with, but it puts a number on the table that doesn't exist yet: if not a moratorium, then how much does a direct assistance program actually cost, and is anyone building that budget?

The enrollment connection is the thread he cannot put down. He's been staring at 1,401 elementary students dropping to 1,080 — a 23% decline in four years — and nobody in that chamber said the two things in the same sentence: fear-based absences and the structural gap the superintendent is trying to explain. That's a call to the district's enrollment office he needs to make immediately.

And then the Mahoney workshop runs until 11:30. He almost missed it. A $57-to-$105 million bond referendum, potentially landing on the same November ballot as whatever school fiscal crisis resolution voters get asked to ratify. He mapped the TIF mechanics in December. He synthesized the combined-tax-bill insight after the January 13 Mahoney meeting. Now the city is actively workshopping the Mahoney bond range while the school crisis remains unresolved — and nobody in that room calculated what both line items mean for a $514,000 South Portland home.

That number is the piece. He's calling the city finance director.
