# Cumulative Record: Amira (PERSONA-013)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- December 1, 2025 -- 2025-12-01

### Interpretation

Looking back at this meeting now, it feels like watching the last few minutes before a storm. The budget gap was already real on December 1st — $7.2 million, 42 teachers on the chopping block — but none of that was talked about in public yet. The adults were still in the part of the meeting where a teenager reads a poem and everyone smiles.

And that poem. Jules Vazquez, around my age, stood up in front of the whole city council and read something beautiful she wrote about South Portland's future. That's the kind of moment I keep thinking about when I feel like students don't have a voice in anything. She had one. People listened. I wish I knew how that works.

The families from different countries who introduced themselves — including Somali families like my own parents could have been — were welcomed that night as neighbors and contributors. They came to say *we're here, we're part of this*. Looking back, knowing what I know now about budget cuts and how Skillin families would be put at the center of the worst fight, it hurts to think about that welcome. The city was clapping for them on December 1st and by March it was treating Skillin — the school those families depend on — as a line item to cut because the heating system costs too much.

The strangest part is Ms. DeAngelis. The same board chair who later told a meeting full of parents that children are "far more flexible than parents" and supported reconfiguration as "educationally beneficial" without ever asking any students — she stood up at this December meeting and told a story about boys who burned down her middle school because nobody listened to them. She said students are dying from overdoses and mental health crises. She called it "too much." She seemed to understand that when kids go unheard, it becomes a crisis. And then, over the following months, she became someone who made decisions about kids' schools without asking kids. I don't know how that happens, but it does.

There were also stories about teachers who changed lives — one who literally took in a refugee student senior year so he could graduate with his friends. Those stories matter more now than they probably did to whoever was in that room. Every teacher like that is exactly what they're proposing to cut forty-two of.

### Emotional Register

Looking back at this meeting through everything that followed, Amira feels a complicated grief — the welcome given to immigrant families including her own, the teenager reading a poem, the board chair who knew what it meant to be unheard, all of it happened just days before the spending freeze and weeks before the 42-teacher announcement, and none of the warmth of December 1st protected any of it. She is proud that Jules had a voice, and sad that it didn't seem to change what came next.
