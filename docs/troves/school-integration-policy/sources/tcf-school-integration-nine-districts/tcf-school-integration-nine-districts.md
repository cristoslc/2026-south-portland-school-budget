# School Integration in Practice: Lessons from Nine Districts

**Author:** Richard D. Kahlenberg, The Century Foundation
**Published:** October 14, 2016

One hundred school districts and charter schools across the United States formally pursue socioeconomic integration, enrolling nearly 4.4 million students in thirty-two states.

TCF commissioned nine district case studies examining policies in: Cambridge, MA; Champaign, IL; Chicago, IL; Dallas, TX; Eden Prairie, MN; Hartford, CT; Jefferson County (Louisville), KY; New York, NY; and Stamford, CT.

## Key Lessons

### 1. Socioeconomic diversity policies produce strong academic outcomes

Districts with well-implemented plans show positive results. Cambridge (plan since 2001) outperforms demographically similar Massachusetts districts. Graduation rates for Black students (90.5%), Hispanic students (88.7%), and low-income students (89.5%) exceed national averages.

Hartford's inter-district non-selective magnet schools show Black/white and Hispanic/white achievement gaps about half the statewide gaps — because all subgroups perform better.

Stamford's low-income students perform above state average. Jefferson County nearly doubled College and Career Ready rates between 2011 and 2015.

Exception: Champaign still has large achievement gaps, possibly due to tracking within schools.

### 2. Choice and incentives smooth the path to community support

Most profiled districts use public school choice and incentives (magnet schools) rather than compulsory busing. Many use "controlled choice" — families choose from options while districts honor choice with an eye to socioeconomic integration.

- Champaign: ~90% of kindergartners receive first-choice school
- Jefferson County: 90% first-choice placement rate
- Louisville: opposition shifted from 98% (1970s busing) to 89% supporting diversity guidelines (2011)
- Hartford: 45 magnet schools draw suburban students into one of the poorest cities in the country; integrated enrollment rose from 11% to projected 46% in 2016
- Cambridge: seeing rising public school enrollment, reversing "white flight"
- Dallas Solar Prep: attracted students from private and charter schools

Free transportation is essential. Without it, choice is not equitable.

### 3. System-wide goals increase likelihood of success

Districts with clear system-wide integration goals (Cambridge, Champaign, Jefferson County, Stamford) achieve broader integration than those targeting a small subset of schools (Dallas, Chicago).

Stamford: 18 of 20 schools fall within +/- 10 percentage points of district average for socioeconomic diversity.

### 4. Breaking down city-suburb walls has greater impact

Hartford and Louisville broke through urban/suburban boundaries — Louisville through consolidation with suburban Jefferson County; Hartford through extensive two-way urban/suburban transfer programs.

Inter-district approaches offer significant opportunities beyond separate and unequal schooling.

### 5. Socioeconomic diversity policies can lead to racial diversity

After the Supreme Court struck down racial integration plans (2007), socioeconomic approaches have achieved racial diversity:

- Cambridge: 84% of students attended racially balanced schools (2011-12)
- Chicago selective enrollment (socioeconomic criteria): 22% white, 30% Hispanic, 35% African-American, 9% Asian
- NYC selective schools (no socioeconomic factor): only 5% Black, 7% Latino in a 70% Black/Latino system
- Minneapolis/Eden Prairie income-based program: 95% of participants are students of color

### 6. Districts have grown more sophisticated in defining disadvantage

Early programs used free/reduced lunch eligibility (185% poverty line). Problems: misses parental education, binary rich/poor split, unreliable as more districts offer universal free lunch.

Newer approaches:
- **Chicago:** Census tract composite — median income, adult education, single-family households, homeownership, non-English speaking, school performance → four economic tiers
- **Dallas:** Adapted Chicago's system
- **Jefferson County:** Three Census tract measures (income, education, race) → three tiers

### 7. Building-level integration is not enough — classrooms matter too

- **Champaign:** Good school-level integration but internal stratification (tracking). Still has large achievement gaps.
- **Stamford:** Eliminated ability grouping in elementary, reduced tracking in middle/high school. Black AP enrollment nearly tripled; Hispanic AP enrollment doubled (2010-2014).

## Districts Profiled

| District | Key Approach |
|----------|-------------|
| Cambridge, MA | System-wide controlled choice since 2001 |
| Champaign, IL | System-wide integration, still struggles with tracking |
| Chicago, IL | Selective enrollment with socioeconomic tiers |
| Dallas, TX | Magnet pilot (Solar Prep), limited scope |
| Eden Prairie, MN | Redrawn boundary lines |
| Hartford, CT | Inter-district magnet schools (45), urban-suburban transfers |
| Jefferson County (Louisville), KY | Consolidated district, controlled choice, 90% first-choice |
| New York, NY | SIG-funded controlled choice pilots, limited scope |
| Stamford, CT | System-wide goals, detracking, +/-10% diversity band |
