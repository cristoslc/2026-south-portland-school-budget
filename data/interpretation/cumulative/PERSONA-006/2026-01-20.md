# Cumulative Record: Tom (PERSONA-006)
## Meeting: City Council Regular Meeting -- January 20, 2026 -- 2026-01-20

### Interpretation

I've been tracking this budget crisis since December and now, in late January, I find out the school board postponed the January 22nd public forum -- the one the city manager specifically announced on January 6th, the one framed as an opportunity for residents to weigh in on cost-saving measures -- because families in Red Bank are afraid to leave their homes due to ICE enforcement at the local car wash and laundromat. I'm not going to pretend immigration policy is my fight, but I will say this: the practical consequence of that situation is that the public budget process just got delayed by two weeks, and I still don't have the community input session I was told was coming. Everything in this crisis keeps slipping.

The Suncor tank farm pitch is the first genuinely new revenue idea I've heard all winter. Four million dollars a year if that Hill Street site gets cleaned up and developed -- more than twice the Maine Mall's contribution. That's the kind of math that actually helps homeowners. The TIF at 170 Ocean Street was supposed to help too, except half the new taxes go back to the developer for thirty years. If the Toss the Tanks people can actually move Suncor out and bring in productive commercial development, that's real relief on the residential side. I'm skeptical it happens fast enough to matter for my next tax bill, but it's worth watching.

What I can't shake is the housing mandate. LD 1829 allows up to four units on any five-thousand-square-foot lot with sewer access. The planning director says that's virtually the entire city. And the state has simultaneously prohibited planning board review for two-to-four-unit developments, so the neighbors don't even get a public hearing. The city has been telling me all winter that commercial development is the key to protecting residential taxpayers from carrying the whole load. Now the state is mandating residential density increases citywide while stripping local oversight -- and nobody in that room connected what that means for the property value argument. If my neighborhood changes character without any public process, the assumption that school quality protects my home's value gets more complicated, not less.

The city council, for its part, approved fee increases and loan programs with minimal drama. The pattern holds: procedural business moves smoothly while the structural questions pile up unanswered.
