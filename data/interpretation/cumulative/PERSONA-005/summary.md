# Cumulative Summary: Priya (PERSONA-005)

### Current Understanding

South Portland's FY27 budget crisis requires eliminating 78 positions — 42 teachers, 16 educational technicians, 14 food service/transportation/facilities workers, and only 2 administrators — against an $8.4 million structural gap. The crisis originates from state underfunding (20% actual vs. 55% statutory obligation), a depleted fund balance, and years of internal mismanagement that degraded equity-designated federal grants (Title I, IDEA, ELL funds) through six finance directors in five years. The TIF sheltering mechanism further suppresses state education aid by reducing the city's apparent fiscal capacity in the state funding formula.

The most diverse elementary schools bear the greatest structural burden. Skillin (60% BIPOC, 54% FRL, 33% MLL) was recommended for closure under equity language — stopped only when four board members named the racial politics explicitly. Kayler (~45% BIPOC, 30–35% MLL) is now recommended for closure without a Title VI civil rights analysis; the board chair could not answer a direct compliance question at the final budget workshop. Option B reconfiguration is explicitly acknowledged by the administration as perpetuating demographic segregation. Middle school ESOL staffing reductions are partly justified by lower enrollment during the January ICE enforcement period — converting documented harm into a rationale for reducing the services those students need. The DEI Director is under review for demotion, and non-biased hiring training has been delayed twice.

At the city level, four distinct council failure modes on equity are now documented: erasure (Montgomery testimony, January 13), inversion (moratorium framing, February 17), weaponization (Matthews deploying school cuts against immigrant aid, March 19), and equity-as-termination (bike share vote, March 10). The structural catch-22 — institutional contact creates enforcement visibility — is compounded by a state GA ruling excluding fear-based income loss from eligibility, producing redundant barriers ensuring the most harmed families fall through every available mechanism. $100,000 for immigrant rental assistance passed 6-1 after months of advocacy; $87 million in public safety bonds passed unanimously. Community engagement exclusion has reached its endpoint: multilingual families were absent from every budget forum, with transportation and translation offered for the first time at the final March 23 workshop.
