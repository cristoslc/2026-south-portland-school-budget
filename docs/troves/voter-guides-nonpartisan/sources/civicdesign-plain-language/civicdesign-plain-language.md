# Plain Language and Design for Voter Guides — Center for Civic Design

*Source: Center for Civic Design — https://civicdesign.org/voter-guides-tips-for-plain-language/ and related field guides*

## Key Principle: Plain Language Is Essential — But Not Enough

Clear writing in plain language helps voters understand elections. However, research shows that plain language alone doesn't guarantee comprehension. Even with plain language, voters can be misled by:
- Confusing concepts
- Irrelevant background information
- Vague descriptions

The guiding question for any voter guide writer should always be: **"What does a voter need to know in order to make a choice?"**

## The Easy Voter Guide (EVG) — A Model

The League of Women Voters of California produces the Easy Voter Guide (EVG), a nonpartisan voter education booklet available in 5 languages. Central to the EVG are plain-language explanations of every statewide ballot question.

Process:
1. Draft plain-language explanations
2. Test with real voters
3. California Legislative Analyst's Office reviews for legal accuracy
4. Translate into Spanish, Chinese, Vietnamese, and Korean

## Testing With Real Voters

Test voter comprehension through:
- Formal usability testing
- Brief conversations with voters in real-world settings (e.g., farmers markets, parks)

Research found that people **voted more accurately with plain language**, and those who saw the plain language version first did significantly better on traditional language versions.

## Design Best Practices

### Language
- Use plain language to bridge the civic literacy gap
- Avoid jargon; explain terms that may be unfamiliar
- Questions and instructions work better as **headings** than noun phrases
- Good headings help voters understand what a page will tell them and recognize the question they need answered

### Visual Organization
- Use illustrations, maps, and diagrams that focus on **one piece of information at a time**
- Eliminate unnecessary details
- Have **one topic per page** to make it easier to scan
- Keep civics information together toward the **front**; ballot-related information in the **second half**

### Structure
- Organize information in an easy-to-follow path
- Present information so it is easy to read and understand
- **Personalize information** for the voter where possible
- Include the right information at the appropriate level of detail — neither too sparse nor too dense

## Design and Shame

"When language or design is confusing, it can inadvertently cause people to feel shame — like there's something wrong with them for not being able to understand what to do."

Organizations have a responsibility to those they serve when it comes to upholding good design principles. Accessible design is not optional — it is a matter of equity.

## The Four Principles (Best Practices Manual for Official Voter Information Guides)

1. **Include the right information** at the appropriate level of detail
2. **Organize information** in an easy-to-follow path
3. **Present information** so it is easy to read and understand
4. **Personalize information** for the voter

## Ballot Measure Explanation

For ballot measures specifically:
- Explain what a "yes" vote and "no" vote would do in concrete terms
- Describe the fiscal impact in plain language
- Avoid legal jargon while maintaining accuracy
- Context is key — explain the problem the measure addresses before describing the measure itself
- Even with plain language, some concepts require additional context to be understood correctly
