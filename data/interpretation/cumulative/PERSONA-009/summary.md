# Cumulative Summary: Dana (PERSONA-009)

### Current Understanding

Dana understands the South Portland school budget crisis as a multi-layered institutional failure with three compounding fiscal pressures converging on the same property tax bill. The school department carries a confirmed gap of at least $7.2 million for FY27 — referenced by Councilor Matthews on March 19 as $8.4 million — which in the status-quo scenario requires an 18.95% tax increase on the school portion alone. FY26 is also running a deficit across all scenarios. The crisis was produced by a documented structural mismatch: elementary enrollment fell 23% over four years while the district added 82 staff positions, generating the highest per-pupil costs ($26,651) among comparable districts. The fund balance is fully depleted. State funding covers only 20% of actual costs against a 55% statutory obligation. An external auditor issued a material weakness finding for two consecutive years, and the department has cycled through six finance directors in five years.

The board rejected its most financially advantageous reconfiguration option — Skillin Elementary closure, worth $19.6 million over five years — on equity grounds after Chair DeAngelos's "families west of the Dairy Queen" speech. No alternative plan has been finalized. Seventy-two employees received pink slips on March 18. Three budget workshops run through March 30, with a city council budget workshop on April 14.

The intergovernmental conflict between a fiscally healthy city (clean audit, $28.8 million fund balance) and a demonstrably poorly-managed school department has not produced agreed guidance. The city's immigration response broke a documented pattern of symbolic inaction on March 19 with a 6-1 vote appropriating $100,000 to Project Home — but Chief Ahern's public apology for endorsing a federal agent's racist texts, and Pedro Vasquez's testimony that his family made contingency detention plans, confirm that the immigrant-community crisis remains unresolved. Sewer rates are projected to rise approximately 22% annually for three consecutive years to finance $51.7 million in wastewater infrastructure, adding a third fiscal pressure alongside the school referendum and the Mahoney bond — which remains unendorsed at $57 million to $105 million — that Councilor Matthews named explicitly in a single dissenting vote on March 19.
