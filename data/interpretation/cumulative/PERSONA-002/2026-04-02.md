# Cumulative Record: David (PERSONA-002)
## Meeting: School Board Regular Meeting -- April 2, 2026 -- 2026-04-02

### Interpretation

Ketchem finally said the thing I have been reconstructing from scattered PDFs since December. Four years of actuals on the same slides: electricity under-budgeted by $138K, then $211K, then $165K, now projecting $368K over budget. Tuition reimbursement under by $41K, then $153K, then $140K. She named the loop explicitly — when you under-budget and have no reserves, the overrun becomes a staff cut the following year. That is the mechanism. That is December 8 in one sentence. I have been building that case in my own spreadsheet for four months and she put it on a slide.

So I will take the win. For the first time in this cycle, the FY27 starting point is built to realistic actuals rather than optimistic assumptions. The FY25 and FY26 tracking columns are in the budget book and they show the reset is real. Feller drilling through the specific figures — $52K per half-point on health insurance, the SRO line item jumping $62K from city billing, the fuel assumptions that hadn't been updated with current diesel prices — is exactly the board behavior I was hoping for when he and Smith joined in December. Someone is doing the math in that room.

But I cannot stay in the win column long. Ketchem said it herself on the slide: FY27 resets the path, it does not fix the core problems. Labor costs escalate above 6% by contract. Utilities keep climbing. The athletic turf bond adds $300K minimum to FY28 debt service. The Skillin boiler is an unclosed obligation. And the fund balance is zero. Not low — zero. Feller got Ketchem on record confirming the standard target is 9 to 12 percent of operating budget. On a $75 million budget that is $6.75 to $9 million. They have none of it and no policy, no timeline, no mechanism to get there. That recommendation has now appeared in three consecutive workshop documents without producing a vote. The $52K from the health insurance basis point reduction went to the chimney stack, not reserves. Someone in public comment caught that contradiction. The board did not resolve it.

And then came the ending. A union president texts a board member mid-meeting with a statehouse rumor: maybe $300K in new state funds. A board member cites a separate text from a representative: maybe another $750K. The board uses these two unverified second-hand numbers to justify not voting on the budget for a second consecutive week. I understand the impulse — you do not want to lock in a position against money that may not exist, or may be one-time, or may carry strings. But nobody asked the prior question: even if every dollar arrives and is real and is recurring, should it go to staff or to starting the fund balance rebuild? That is the decision the board is avoiding, and unconfirmed texts gave them a clean excuse to avoid it one more time.

The April 7 city council presentation is five days away. The board is heading into it with a budget that has failed two consecutive votes, a FY28 structural gap that the finance director named explicitly and nobody has modeled, and a fund balance recommendation that has appeared in three workshops and never reached a motion.
